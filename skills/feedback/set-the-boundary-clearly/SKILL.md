## When to reach for this

You have run the diagnostic, identified that a boundary is needed, and named the type. Now you need to say it out loud to the person who is giving you unwanted feedback. You want to be clear without being cruel, firm without being dismissive, and honest about your feelings without starting a fight.

## The move

**Be transparent: actually tell them.** The biggest mistake in setting boundaries is assuming the other person knows what is going on with you. They may not know you are overwhelmed, struggling, or that their coaching is making things worse. Dave needed a hearing aid and took six months to tell his coworkers. His coworkers, watching him ignore their concerns, concluded he was dismissing them. All he needed to say was: "I got checked. I need a hearing aid. I'm going to get one. It's a hard adjustment. I'll do better if I'm not pestered." The transparency does not fix the underlying issue but it fixes the feedback dynamic around it.

**Be firm and appreciative.** These are not opposites. PJ, about to lecture, is consistently made more anxious when her department head whispers "don't be nervous" just before she begins. Her conversation with the department head: she names the good intention (I know you're trying to help), she names the impact (it makes me more nervous, not less), she holds to her point when the department head pushes back, and she redirects to something that would actually help (tell me how you cope with anxiety when you speak, but on days when I am not lecturing). She does not let the relationship warmth or the good intention invalidate her experience of the impact.

**Redirect unhelpful coaching.** Before establishing a hard limit, consider whether there is a version of the giver's interest that you could welcome. The chapter: "You might experiment with loaning your giver a corner of your acre, and tell them what you'd love to see there." Hunyee to her mother: "I have so much to learn from you. When you visit, would you teach me to make your amazing dumplings?" This meets the giver's need to be useful and valued while redirecting away from the feedback that is not helping. It also lays a foundation for firmer limits if the redirect is ignored.

**Use "and" not "but."** When you reject feedback while affirming the relationship, the temptation is to say "but." "I love seeing you, but if you're going to come to my house you need to stop criticizing everything." "But" signals a contradiction: the love is real, but it is conditional on them stopping. "And" holds both truths as simultaneously true. "I love seeing you and your comments about my weight are incredibly upsetting and I need you to keep them to yourself." Neither feeling cancels the other. The chapter: "Human emotions don't necessarily cancel each other out." The And Stance lets you share the complexity of your feelings without hiding behind a simple bottom-line message.

Raul's use of ands with his parents: "I've been afraid to talk to you about this and it's important to me to be honest." "I've decided to major in music and I know this makes you worry." "I'm also very fearful of the struggle I may face and I need to try." "I know this is hard for you and I hope you will still be supportive of me." Each sentence holds two real truths simultaneously.

**Be specific about three things.** The chapter names three specifics:

1. The Request: What exactly are you asking? A particular topic off-limits (my weight, my new spouse), a behavior off-limits (looking disapprovingly when I step outside for a cigarette), a context off-limits (no coaching right before I lecture)? If the person needs examples of what you're talking about, give them, with their impact on you.

2. The Time Frame: How long is the boundary likely to be in place? Is this a "not right now, I need time to adjust" or an indefinite limit? Let them know if and how they can check in with you about it without violating it.

3. Their Assent: Ask explicitly whether they can honor the request. When they say yes, their identity and reputation are enlisted in living up to the commitment. The chapter: "Don't assume that they understand you or agree."

**Describe consequences as warnings.** Let them know what happens if they cannot or will not honor the boundary. Your goal is a warning, not a threat: you are giving them information about legitimate consequences so they can make an informed choice. The chapter's example: "If you can't leave this issue behind for now, I'll make sure to visit Marv at times when I know you won't be here." You cannot control whether they honor the boundary; you can control your response if they don't.

## Signal it landed

The giver knows specifically what you are asking, how long, and what happens if they do not honor it. They have said yes. You did not hide the complexity of your feelings behind a simple bottom-line message; you used "and" to hold the relationship warmth and the limit at the same time.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "have I actually told them what I need, out loud, clearly, including the time frame and the consequences?" If the conversation has only happened in your head, the boundary has not been set.

After: ask "did I hear them say they would honor the request?" If you did not ask for their assent, you have delivered the message but not yet made it a commitment between you.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without transparency, the giver continues in good faith because they do not know their feedback is damaging. Without firmness, the giver overrides the boundary at the first sign of pushback because they sense you can be persuaded. Without the And Stance, the setting of the boundary feels like an accusation to the giver and triggers all their own truth, relationship, and identity reactions. Without specificity, the request is too vague to honor ("back off" or "stop being so critical" leaves the giver unable to know when they are in compliance). Without consequences, there is no information about what changes if the boundary is not honored.

## Worked micro-example

Hunyee to her mother: "Mom, I love you and I know you want the best for me. And your comments about my weight and housekeeping and clothing are incredibly upsetting to me. If you're going to stay with me, I need you to keep them to yourself. Is that a request you can honor?" This script uses the And Stance (I love you AND this must stop), is specific about the request (weight, housekeeping, clothing, not a vague "back off"), includes an implicit consequence (if you're going to stay with me), and asks for assent. The chapter contrasts this with the weaker version: "I need you to back off" or "Quit being so critical," which set up a fight about whether the label is accurate rather than a clear request about specific behavior.
