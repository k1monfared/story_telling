## When to reach for this

You are reading a section of a draft (your own or someone else's) and feel a low-grade restlessness. The character has just had a real shift: a new relationship, a piece of news, a discovery, a loss. The pages after the shift are spending themselves on the new state. The sentences are not bad, but the section is not earning the space it occupies. Reach for this whenever you can name a beat that has already happened and the prose is still inside it.

## The move

1. Name the current beat in one sentence in the present tense. Saunders's worked example: "Olenka has found a new love (in this case, a little boy) and taken on his opinions and interests and is finally happy again." If you cannot get the beat down to one sentence, the section is doing more than one thing and the diagnosis applies twice.
2. Locate where you are in the prose, relative to that beat. You are inside the beat for as long as the prose is rendering, restating, or atmospherically extending the new state without changing it.
3. Read forward, line by line, and watch for the first sentence that nudges the character or the meaning out of the current state. The nudge can be small. Saunders's example: Sasha's "Please leave me alone!" is the first nudge out of the "finally happy again" beat, because it introduces a new thing the story has not been about yet (the effect of Olenka's way of loving on its object).
4. If you find a nudge: cut what is between the establishment of the current beat and the nudge, or compress it ruthlessly. The section earns its place not by elaborating on the new state but by getting to the next state-change.
5. If you do not find a nudge: you have located the failure. The prose is sitting in the beat because the writer has not yet produced the next escalation. Generate one. Saunders names the kinds of things that count: a new resistance from a quarter the story has not heard from yet, an inversion of an earlier moment ("rebuff instead of praise"), a private/honest version of a public/mediated earlier moment (Sasha awake says "Please leave me alone"; Sasha asleep dreams of violence), a development that changes how an earlier scene reads.
6. Apply the rule recursively. After the next escalation, restart the procedure. Saunders demonstrates this three times within the closing of "The Darling": "still half a page left, what's left to do?" then "five lines left, what's left?" — each time naming the current beat and finding the next escalation that justifies the remaining prose.
7. Stop the story when you cannot find the next escalation. The end of the story is not where the writer ran out of ideas, it is where the next escalation would not add new meaning the story has not already created.

## Signal it landed

You can point to a section of the draft where you cut or compressed prose because it was elaborating on a state-change that had already happened. You can name the current beat in one present-tense sentence at every page-break in the closing third of the story, and you can name what nudges the prose out of each beat. At least one new escalation has been added because the diagnosis surfaced a beat the prose was sitting in without progressing past.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of a stalling section: "What change has just happened, and what change is happening on this page?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the second answer names a new state the prose did not previously reach, and you can name the specific sentence in the section that produces the change.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this procedure, a writer who has earned a real character change is tempted to dwell. The new state is hard-won and feels worth lingering in. So the prose renders the new equilibrium for a page or two, then renders it again under different lighting, then renders it once more in summary. None of these renderings is wrong. All of them are, in Saunders's framing, the same beat. The section reads as a "long dream" the reader is being told. The story stops being a story (a system of escalation) and becomes a still-life of the recently-changed condition. The reader's restlessness is the felt absence of escalation; the writer hears it as "I should make this scene better" and polishes the prose, which deepens the failure. The cure is not better prose inside the beat, it is the next beat.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders applies the diagnosis three times in succession to the closing pages of "The Darling." First, he names the current beat after Sasha's introduction: "Olenka has found a new love and taken on his opinions and is finally happy again." He notes the prose is sitting inside this beat and reads forward to find what nudges out: Sasha's "Please leave me alone!" Second, after a small further move (Olenka returns home contented, Sasha tolerates her, the kitten purrs), Saunders again pauses: "Why not end it there? But there's still half a page left." He locates the next escalation in the late-night knock at the gate that recalls Kukin's death and then turns out to be Smirnin home from the club. Third, with five lines left, he asks again: "What's left to do? Can Chekhov find any more escalation?" The final escalation is Sasha's dream, in which the resistance becomes private, honest, and angry, and which (because Olenka does not register it) reveals that her trait will not be moderated by what it costs the people around her. Three escalations in the closing two pages, each diagnosed by the same procedure: name the current beat, locate the prose still inside it, read forward to find or generate what pushes it out.
