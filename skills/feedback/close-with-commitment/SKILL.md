## When to reach for this

A feedback conversation is ending. The content has been discussed. You are about to go separate ways. Neither of you has explicitly named what was agreed, what the next steps are, or what, if anything, will be different going forward.

## The move

Before parting, spend one to three sentences naming what you have agreed on and what each party will do next. The chapter: "If we're not explicit, we often end up disappointed by the lack of progress, or confused about the other person's lofty expectations."

The closing commitment does not require you to agree with the feedback or promise to change. You can commit to: thinking about what was said and returning to it tomorrow; gathering more information; bringing others into the conversation; seeing how things go over the next two weeks; or describing exactly which parts of the feedback you have decided not to take and why. The goal is clarity.

Depending on the context, the chapter names four types of things to firm up:

1. **Action Plans.** Who does what? What, if anything, is each party going to change or work on, and what do you each agree to do to make that happen?

2. **Benchmarks and Consequences.** How will progress be measured, and when? What are the consequences if benchmarks are not met? Discuss this explicitly rather than leaving it implicit.

3. **Procedural Contracts.** When do we talk again, and about what? Agreements about process: gathering additional input from others, not discussing the matter in front of certain people, giving each other the benefit of the doubt, a method for checking in.

4. **New Strategies.** Where the friction reflects an ongoing difference that is not going to disappear, articulate the ideas generated during the conversation for how to accommodate each other more successfully going forward.

Not every conversation needs all four; a simple one-sentence summary of what each party now knows and will do next is sufficient for most conversations.

## Signal it landed

Both parties leave with a shared understanding of what was agreed. The chapter: "You should both know where things stand." If asked the next day what happened in the conversation and what each person committed to, both would give substantially the same answer.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "do we both know what we each agreed to do next?" If uncertain, the close has not happened.

After: ask "if the other person describes this conversation to someone tomorrow, will they describe the same commitments I would?" If no, the commitment was not explicit enough.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without an explicit close, feedback conversations end when someone gives up, walks out, shuts down, or time runs out. Neither party knows what the other took from the conversation. The giver assumes something will change; the receiver does not know what was expected. Or both parties felt they reached understanding, but with no committed action, nothing happens and both wonder what all that talking accomplished. The chapter: "Both giver and receiver wonder why they spent so much time on the matter to begin with, when nothing ever changes." A one-sentence close resolves this.
