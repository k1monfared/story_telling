## When to reach for this

Your partner is talking about something they're growing toward — a new job, a creative project, a new partner, a course they want to take. You notice your impulse is to be careful, to introduce caveats, to remind them of the costs. Reach for this when the part of you that wants to stay close is overriding the part that wants to support their venturing.

## The move

1. Notice the impulse to limit. Not to suppress it — just to see it. Often it's protective; sometimes it's possessive; often it's a mix.
2. Distinguish concern from withholding. There's a difference between sharing a real concern ("I'm worried about how tired you'll be") and reflexively curbing them ("are you sure that's a good idea?"). The first comes from care; the second often comes from your own fear of change.
3. Encourage. Not flattery — specific encouragement of the venture. "I think you're well-suited for this." "I want to see you do this." "Tell me how I can support you."
4. Don't make their venture about you. Their growth is theirs. Your reactions to it are yours. Mixing the two — making your discomfort their problem — is the failure mode.
5. In nonmonogamy: their venture often includes another relationship. The work of secure-base function applies even when the venture pulls them toward another partner. This is harder; it's the same move.
6. Bring your own concerns separately when needed. If their venture has real costs to you, those costs deserve naming — but in their own conversation, not as a wet-blanket on their ambition.

## Signal it landed

Your partner brings their growing edges to you because they expect to be supported, not curbed. They venture more, and they return to you with what they learned. The relationship has the quality of "you make me bigger, not smaller."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without secure-base function, partners learn to hide their growth from you because each ambition gets met with fear, resistance, or competition. They stop telling you about new ideas; they pursue them in private; they grow apart not because of any specific event but because the relationship couldn't hold their becoming.

## Reflection prompts

- How is each partner already acting as a secure base for you?
- What does growth look like for you? Is it usually a shared or a personal experience, or both?
- How could partners support you in regard to your other relationships?
- How could you better support or encourage your partners' dreams or aspirations to grow and develop?
- How could you better show genuine interest or curiosity in things that are important to your partners?
