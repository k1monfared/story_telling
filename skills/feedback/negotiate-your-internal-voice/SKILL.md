## When to reach for this

You are in a feedback conversation and cannot listen. Your internal voice is too loud: it is making arguments against the feedback, defending your record, cataloguing the giver's faults, or cycling through how unfair this is. Or you are preparing for a difficult feedback conversation and want to give yourself the best chance of genuinely hearing what is said.

## The move

**Do not suppress the internal voice. Give it attention.** The chapter's counterintuitive instruction: your internal voice gets loud because it wants your attention. If you try to tamp it down ("think whatever you want, but keep quiet"), it doubles down. If you give it your full attention, it quiets. The first step is to tune in to what it is actually saying.

**Identify the trigger pattern.** When you are triggered, your internal voice does not say random things. It says specific and predictable things organized around the type of trigger that has fired. The chapter's taxonomy:

- Truth trigger patterns: "That's wrong!" / "That's not helpful!" / "That's not me!"
- Relationship trigger patterns: "After all I've done for you?!" / "Who are you to say?" / "You're the problem, not me."
- Identity trigger patterns: "I screw up everything." / "I'm doomed." / "I'm not a bad person — or am I?"

Recognizing the pattern gives you traction. You know what your internal voice is protecting against and why it's talking.

**Negotiate it toward curiosity.** The chapter prescribes an explicit internal dialogue. Below is the model (the chapter's own dialogue is the illustration):

You: "During this conversation, you're going to be telling me this feedback is wrong."

Internal Voice: "Right. Because it will be."

You: "What might be right about what they're saying?"

Internal Voice: "Well, they might see things I don't. Their interpretation could be different but valid."

You: "So there's something to listen for."

The strategy is to acknowledge and appreciate the internal voice (these are your own thoughts; dismissing them is dismissing yourself), remind it that understanding doesn't equal agreeing, and give it an assignment: "I need you to be intensely curious about what they're saying. Help me dig in and understand. What's right about what they're saying? Why is it that they see things differently?"

**Watch for hot inquiry.** A specific failure mode in the listening phase: when you are upset, your "questions" may become assertions disguised as questions. "Do you actually believe that?" is not a question; it is a frustrated assault with a question mark. Sarcasm and cross-examination ("But isn't it true that...?") are the same. If you notice that your inquiry has gotten hot, stop trying to inquire and assert instead. Replace: "Do you actually think what you're saying is consistent or fair?" with: "What you're suggesting seems inconsistent with the criteria used for others in my position. That doesn't seem fair to me." Clear assertion is more honest than hot inquiry and makes listening easier afterward.

## Signal it landed

During preparation: you have had the internal negotiation, acknowledged what your internal voice is defending against, and given it a curiosity assignment. During the conversation: your questions are genuine rather than heated. You are asking what the giver means rather than cross-examining their credibility.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "if I imagine hearing the worst-case feedback, what does my internal voice start saying?" Naming the anticipated pattern before the conversation gives you advance knowledge of the bodyguard's moves.

After: ask "during the conversation, did I hear anything the giver said that I had not already decided to believe?" If yes, genuine listening happened. If no, the internal voice ran the whole encounter.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without working with the internal voice, triggered listening collapses into the chapter's description: you miss what the giver says after the first triggering sentence because your internal voice is still arguing against it. Your fellow board member says something, you think "that's not true," and while your internal voice is building the counter-argument, the board member has moved to a second point that you now also miss. The chapter notes that this is not a matter of will: the neuroscience research shows that when we feel unfairly treated, our mirror neuron empathy response may be neurologically turned off. Skillful listening during triggered feedback is not natural; it has to be both on purpose and with a purpose.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter provides the internal voice dialogue as the worked illustration. The key exchange: when the internal voice insists "all the usual things will be wrong," the prompt is not to argue with it or silence it but to ask "what might be right about what they're saying?" This invites the internal voice into the curiosity project rather than pitting you against it. The voice offers: "They've been around the block a few times. Their interpretation may be different but valid." That is enough curiosity to begin genuine listening.
