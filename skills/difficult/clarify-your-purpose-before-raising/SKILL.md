## When to reach for this

You are deciding whether to have a difficult conversation. Or you have decided to raise something and are about to start, but you have not named what you are actually trying to accomplish. Or you have had the conversation and it went badly, and you are wondering why.

## The move

**Part 1: Check whether this conversation makes sense.**

Work through the Three Conversations as preparation. As you do, three conditions often emerge that suggest the conversation, at least in its current form, is unlikely to help:

1. **Is the real conflict inside you?** Sometimes what feels like an interpersonal problem is primarily an internal one. Carmen's frustration with Tom over scheduling stemmed from her own ambivalence about working full-time and her need to stay connected to her children. The conversation she thought she needed to have ("let me tell you how you're not being responsible enough") was not the conversation that would help. The real work was inside, not between them. If you find, after working through the Three Conversations, that your own identity issues are driving the frustration as much as the other person's behavior, address that first.

2. **Is there a better way to address the issue than talking?** Sometimes the right move is a change in your behavior, not a conversation about the behavior. Walter spent months feeling he needed to tell his mother to stop asking when he was coming home. When he examined the contribution system, he realized the real issue was connection and belonging — and that he was contributing to the problem by pulling away. He didn't need a conversation about the topic; he needed to change his own actions (call more, visit more, invite her). The conversation became unnecessary. The test: after working through the Three Conversations, is there something you could do that would address the real issue without a direct conversation about it?

3. **Are your purposes off-base?** Three common purposes that will likely make things worse:
   - *Trying to change the other person.* You can influence; you cannot force change. The paradox the chapter names: trying to change someone rarely results in change; engaging them in mutual learning often does. If your primary purpose is to make them change, you are likely to argue and attack rather than listen, which produces defensiveness rather than openness.
   - *Venting or telling them off.* Short-term relief at long-term cost. Janet finally told off the board member who made her feel incompetent. She felt good for fifteen minutes, then regretted it. She had fed the antagonism rather than reducing it.
   - *Hit-and-run.* Saying something important in passing without committing to a real conversation. "Late again, eh?" to an employee who keeps arriving late, then walking off. This produces defensiveness and frustration without the exchange needed for anything to change. Rule: if you're going to talk, really talk.

**Part 2: If you raise it, use the three purposes that work.**

These are the purposes of a learning conversation, not a message delivery:

1. **Learning their story.** Understand their information, interpretations, feelings, identity issues, and what this situation means to them. Not so you can refute it — so you can understand how they see the world. (This is the curiosity stance from shift-from-certainty-to-curiosity.)

2. **Expressing your views and feelings.** Say, as well as you can, what is important for you to say — about your story, your intentions, your contributions, your feelings, your identity. Not to win; to be understood.

3. **Problem-solving together.** Once both stories are on the table, figure out what would improve the situation going forward. Brainstorm. Find options that meet both parties' concerns.

These three purposes accommodate the reality that both people have stories, feelings, and identity stakes. They are what mutual learning looks like in practice.

**If you decide not to raise it: letting go.**

Sometimes, after working through the Three Conversations, the right decision is not to raise the issue. Four assumptions that make letting go possible:
- It is not your responsibility to make things better; it is your responsibility to do your best.
- The other person has limitations too; they may not be able to be different, at least not yet.
- This conflict is not who you are; don't let your identity become defined by the fight.
- Letting go does not mean you no longer care; you can relinquish the bitterness while holding on to the love and the learning.

## Signal it landed

Before the conversation: you can name what you are trying to accomplish, and that purpose is one of the three that work (learning, expressing, problem-solving). You have not launched in order to change them, vent, or say something on the fly. If you decided not to raise it, you have identified why and have a path to let go.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "what would success look like in this conversation?" If success is "they admit they were wrong" or "they promise to change" or "I feel better after venting," reconsider.

After (if the conversation went badly): ask "what were my purposes going in?" If they were off-base, that is likely the root of what went wrong, not your delivery skills.

## Failure mode this prevents

Wrong purposes produce predictable failures. If your purpose is to change the other person, you argue rather than listen, they get defensive, and nothing changes. If your purpose is to vent, you feel relieved for fifteen minutes and the relationship is worse. If your purpose is to say something on the fly, you say something important without the exchange needed to make it useful. The chapter: "If your purpose is to change the other person or their behavior, to vent or tell them off, then having this conversation is quite likely to produce many of the negative consequences you fear."

## Worked micro-example

Janet and Sylvie. Janet finally confronted the board member who had been publicly questioning her competence. Her unstated purpose was to tell Sylvie off — to make Sylvie feel small the way Sylvie had made Janet feel small. The conversation accomplished that purpose. And then it made the situation worse. The chapter walks through what a better-purpose version might look like: curiosity about why Sylvie reacts as she does, learning Sylvie's story, sharing Janet's, figuring out how to work together better. Whether or not that conversation would work, it has a chance. The tell-them-off version does not.
