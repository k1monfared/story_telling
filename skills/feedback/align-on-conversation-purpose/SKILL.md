## When to reach for this

You are at the beginning of a feedback conversation, either scheduled or spontaneous, and you do not yet know: (a) whether what is about to happen is feedback and if so what kind, (b) who the decision-maker is, or (c) whether the feedback outcome is final or still open to influence. Jumping into the content without this alignment leads to arguments that resolve the wrong problem.

Also reach for this when the giver has launched into the middle of a conversation without any framing, and the conversation has already started to go sideways.

## The move

**Ask three orienting questions before engaging with content:**

1. **Is this feedback? If so, what kind?** Some things that look like feedback are not. Not being put on the project team could be a resource allocation decision or it could be feedback; the birthday sweater that is a size too small could be a mistake or a message. If it is feedback, identify whether it is coaching, evaluation, or appreciation. Also ask: what kind of feedback would be most useful to you right now? If you need encouragement rather than critique, say so before the conversation unfolds. And ask yourself what the giver's purpose is: they may be offering coaching when you need evaluation, or offering evaluation when you need coaching.

2. **Who decides?** Some feedback conversations require you to reach consensus; others have a clear decision-maker. Know which. The chicken logo for the Chicken Farmers' Convention: at the impasse between designer and client, the question of who gets the final call changes everything. Also: some conversations do not require agreement at all. If your ex-partner gives you feedback that you were a terrible person and you believe you acted thoughtfully, you do not need to reach consensus. You are each your own storyteller about what happened.

3. **Is this final or negotiable?** If a performance rating is already final, spending your time trying to influence it wastes energy that would be better spent understanding it and discussing what to do next. If it is provisional, you may be able to affect the outcome. Determine which before deciding how to engage.

**You can influence the frame and agenda regardless of how the giver starts.** You do not have to simply react to whoever fires first. The chapter: "Regardless of how the other person starts, you can use your turn to frame the conversation constructively and to offer an agenda." If the giver launches into accusations, reframe: "I want to hear your perspective on this, and then I'll share my view, and we can figure out where and why our views are different." If they are in a "I'm right" frame: "Can we take a minute to step back so I'm clear on our purposes?" This is a process move about the conversation's structure, not about the substance being discussed. The chapter cites Gottman's finding that if the first three minutes of a fifteen-minute conversation are harsh and not corrected, the outcome is negative 96 percent of the time. Getting the opening right pays compound interest.

## Signal it landed

Before the content discussion begins, you and the giver both know what kind of feedback is being given, who is making decisions, and what can and cannot be changed. The chapter calls this getting aligned.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "do I know what kind of feedback this is, whether I can influence the outcome, and who makes the final call?" If you cannot answer all three, the alignment has not happened.

After: ask "did I engage with the content before getting aligned on purpose and status?" If yes, you may have argued about the substance when you should have first clarified what kind of conversation you were in.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without alignment at the opening, the conversation often treats as contentious what could have been settled in advance. You spend energy fighting over a decision that is already final (if you had known, you would have focused on understanding it, not changing it). Or you accept coaching passively when you needed evaluation first (because you never named what you needed). Or you argue about who is right when no one needs to agree at all. The chapter's Gottman finding makes the cost explicit: a harsh opening that is not corrected predicts a negative outcome 96 percent of the time.

## Worked micro-example

The performance rating conversation (the "version four" worked example in the chapter): the receiver opens by flagging what they know (they got a 4, they were told last year that customers would differentiate a 4 from a 5, they have specific data) and what they want to learn (the actual criteria, whether anything has changed). This is alignment framing done as assertion: naming the structure of the conversation you want to have before diving into the content. The receiver does not ask whether the rating can be changed until they have understood the process.
