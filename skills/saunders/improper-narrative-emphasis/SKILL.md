## When to reach for this

You are drafting a scene that contains something that cannot be coherently described. A nose that is also a man in a uniform. An object with a face whose face is unspecifiable. A creature whose anatomy would, if pinned down, contradict itself. A magical event whose mechanics would, if rendered, expose the spell. You can feel that any direct description forces a choice between two specifications that cannot both be true, or would render the impossibility too literally and lose the spell. Reach for this whenever the question "what does this thing actually look like" would, if answered, ruin the scene.

## The move

1. Name the question you are going to sidestep. Be explicit: "Does the Nose have a face?" "Does the talking glove have a mouth?" "How does the disembodied voice produce sound?" Write the question down so you know what you are deflecting.
2. Pick a different attribute to emphasize. Saunders's Gogol-procedure: instead of describing the Nose's form, describe its rank, costume, station, mood, or social bearing. The replacement attribute should be one that the impossibility could plausibly possess, and that carries weight in the world of the story (in Gogol's case, social rank is the load-bearing currency of the world, so the Nose's uniform tells the reader everything the world cares about).
3. Compose the sentence with confident syntax. The prose must not flag its own deflection. Saunders's note: "the confident statement that 'he recognized that it was his own nose' causes our mind to insert, here, a Platonic Nose, one that we couldn't, if pressed, draw." Confidence in the syntax is what licenses the reader's mind to fill in the unrenderable.
4. Use the pronoun the deflection requires. "It" or "him" rather than a full referent. When Kovalyov sees his nose, the prose says "he recognized that it was his own nose" — the "it" both is and is not a person. The grammatical hedge is the deflection.
5. Trust the reader to fill in a Platonic version. The reader will supply a mental image at the level of confidence the syntax establishes. They will not draw it; they will accept it. Saunders's principle: "Yet there it is, rushing into a house."
6. Test by asking the deflected question after writing the scene. If a careful reader could answer the literal question ("the Nose has a face that looks like X") from your prose, the deflection is incomplete. The prose should leave the literal question structurally unanswerable.
7. Hold the deflection across the scene. If you deflect once and then later, in the same scene, render the literal mechanics, you have broken the spell. The deflection must be sustained until the scene closes.

## Signal it landed

A reader cannot, on rereading, answer the literal mechanical question about the strange thing. They have a sense of the thing — they would say "yes, I know what it looked like" — but cannot draw it. The scene reads as confident and clear at the surface, while the impossible element remains structurally undescribable. You can name, in revision, the specific question you sidestepped, and you can locate the sentence that performed the deflection.

## Probe before & after

**Before.** For the impossible element in your scene, write down the literal question: "Does X have a face? How does Y make sound? What is the geometry of Z?" Try to answer the question from your current prose. If the prose answers it, you are still rendering the literal mechanics.

**After.** Same prompt. The prose does not answer the question. The prose has emphasized something else (rank, mood, costume, social station, action) that carries the scene's weight. The reader will accept the impossibility because the syntax around it is confident, but the prose does not commit to a specification that could be falsified.

## Failure mode this prevents

Two opposite failures. (1) Over-specification: the writer renders the impossible thing literally, the reader notices the mechanics cannot work, and the spell breaks. The Nose has a face; the face has eyes; whose eyes? whose voice? — the questions stack and the reader becomes a forensic reader. (2) Under-specification: the writer hedges with "somehow" and "in some way" and the prose loses confidence, signaling its own embarrassment. Improper narrative emphasis is the third path: confident syntax around a deliberate deflection. The literal question is never raised because the emphasis is elsewhere; the impossibility is accepted because the prose has not asked the reader to inspect it.

## Worked micro-example

Kovalyov sees his nose for the first time as a separate being. The prose: "Imagine the horror and at the same time the amazement of Kovalyov when he recognized that it was his own nose!" The reader's mind supplies a Nose without being told what the Nose looks like. The pronoun "it" both is and is not a person. The next sighting: the Nose comes out of a building "wearing a gold-embroidered uniform with a big stand-up collar and doeskin breeches" with a "plumed hat" indicating "the rank of a state councillor." The emphasis is on costume and rank — both load-bearing in this world — and the literal questions (face? size? proportions?) are never asked. The Nose elsewhere is described as having "slightly knitted brows," indicating a face, but the face is not specified. The reader holds, simultaneously, "a nose with a face" and "a nose without a face," and accepts both, because the prose has never paused on the question.
