## When to reach for this

You've recognized that your relationships keep running into the same patterns regardless of who the partner is — and you suspect the through-line is your relationship with yourself. Or you've done a lot of partner-side work without stable improvement and notice the missing piece is internal. Reach for this when you want a coherent practice for self-security rather than scattered self-improvement attempts.

## The move

1. **Diagnose your starting state.** Use `recognize-your-attachment-style` to name the pattern your nervous system runs under stress. Use `spot-your-giving-pattern` to map the specific imbalance in how you give and receive.
2. **Build self-attunement as the foundation.** Practice `attune-to-yourself` daily for several weeks. The point is not perfection — it's developing the capacity to read your own state before reacting. Without this, every other move is built on sand.
3. **Build self-regulation alongside.** Practice `self-regulate-without-others` for low-grade activations. Notice the auto-regulation pattern (`avoid-auto-regulation-via-others`). The goal isn't to stop reaching out — it's to make reaching out a choice rather than a reflex.
4. **Cultivate the inner nurturer.** Practice `cultivate-the-inner-nurturer` deliberately. Catch the inner critic; respond with the kind voice. This takes months, not days. Don't expect immediate transformation; expect gradual revision.
5. **Establish self-rituals.** Build the daily and weekly practices that align you with the version of yourself you're trying to be — a morning practice, a body practice, a sleep practice. Write them down. Keep them small enough to sustain.
6. **Practice turning toward yourself after rupture.** When you let yourself down, practice the inner repair: notice, acknowledge, take responsibility without crushing yourself, recommit. The rupture-and-repair cycle applies internally.
7. **Notice the boundary work alongside.** Whether your skew is `tighten-porous-boundaries` or `soften-rigid-boundaries`, that work is part of the self-security practice — your boundaries are how you treat yourself in relation to others.
8. **Track over months, not days.** This is not a sprint. The goal is a different baseline state of self-relationship within six months to a year, not a different daily mood next week.

## Signal it landed

You can sit with mild distress for ten minutes without outsourcing. The inner critic still speaks but no longer dominates; another voice (steadier, kinder) is reliably available. Your partners notice that you reach less for regulation and more for connection. You experience yourself as a place you can come back to, not just a state you have to escape.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without self-security work, every external relationship carries weight that internal relationship should hold. You exhaust partners with regulation needs they can't meet; you exhaust yourself by trying to outrun your own internal punishment; you make every relationship vulnerable to your own unmet self-attachment.

## Reflection prompts

- What is your current relationship with yourself like? Would you want it as your relationship with a partner?
- Which of the four self-moves (attunement, regulation, inner nurturer, self-rituals) feels most underdeveloped?
- What practices have you tried that worked? What didn't?
- What would become possible in your relationships with others if your relationship with yourself were more secure?
