## When to reach for this

You make a mistake and immediately rehearse what's wrong with you. You feel sad and the inner response is "stop being weak." You have a need and the inner response is "you shouldn't need that." Reach for this whenever you notice the inner critic is the loudest voice — and it almost always is, until you build something else.

## The move

1. Catch the inner critic mid-speech. Even partway. The words are usually some variant of "you're stupid," "you should know better," "what's wrong with you," "you're too much," "you're not enough."
2. Name what is actually happening underneath. The critic is responding to a real moment of vulnerability — a mistake, a need, a hurt. The critic's job, originally, was probably to keep you safe by motivating change before someone else punished you. It's not lying, but it's not nurturing.
3. Ask: how would I respond to a friend in this moment? Not a fantasy friend — a specific real friend you love. What would you say to them about this same shortfall?
4. Address yourself in that voice. Out loud if you can. "Of course you're tired. That was a hard week. You don't have to be more than you are right now."
5. Don't fight the inner critic; let it speak and respond with the nurturer. The critic doesn't have to be silenced, just outvoted by something steadier.
6. Practice it deliberately when stakes are low so the voice is available when stakes are high.

## Signal it landed

When you mess up, you can locate at least one part of yourself that responds with kindness instead of attack. The critic still shows up, but it's no longer the only voice in the room. You start to use phrases internally that you would use with someone you love.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without an inner nurturer, every shortfall is an attachment rupture with yourself. You become exhausting to be around because you're constantly trying to outrun your own internal punishment. Your partners can never love you enough to compensate; the work has to be inside.

## Reflection prompts

- What does your inner critic typically say? Write down its three favorite phrases.
- Whose voice is the critic, originally? A parent? A teacher? A culture? A bully?
- What would the most loving friend you have say about the thing the critic is attacking right now?
- What does it feel like in your body when the inner nurturer speaks instead of the critic?
