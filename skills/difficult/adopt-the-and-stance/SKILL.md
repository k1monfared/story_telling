## When to reach for this

You have been listening to the other person and their story makes sense, but yours still makes sense too, and the two are in conflict. You feel stuck: if you acknowledge their view, you're giving up yours; if you assert yours, you're dismissing theirs. Or you need to deliver bad news or hold a decision the other person opposes, and you are trying to balance being honest about what you need while acknowledging their pain or disagreement.

## The move

**Name the false choice.** The unstated assumption running in most arguments is that one story must be right and the other wrong, and that understanding the other person's story means agreeing with it — which means abandoning your own. This assumption is false. Two stories can both make sense simultaneously. The disagreement is usually not about facts (often both parties already agree on the facts) but about what those facts mean, what rules apply, or what matters most. These are questions where multiple answers can be coherent.

**Hold both stories with "and."** The And Stance means: "I see things this way AND you see things that way, and both make sense." You can feel hurt AND they can feel just as hurt. They can be doing their best AND you can think it's not good enough. You can feel furious with someone AND also feel love and appreciation for them. "And" holds complexity; "but" cancels the first half. "I understand you're upset, but..." translates as "I acknowledged your feelings, now I'm dismissing them." "I understand you're upset, and..." leaves both things intact.

**Apply the And Stance to asserting your view.** You don't have to give up your story to hold theirs. The And Stance gives you a place from which to assert the full strength of your views and feelings without diminishing the other person's. Keiko can say: "Tony, I understand you need this time for your mental health, and I also believe family visits matter. I'd like to figure out together how we honor both." She is not choosing between Tony's need and her value. She is holding both.

**Apply the And Stance to delivering bad news.** When you have made a final decision — you are breaking up, you are firing someone, you are cutting orders — the And Stance is especially important. You can communicate the decision clearly AND acknowledge the other person's pain AND not retreat from the decision AND acknowledge your own contribution AND hold your own uncertainty. The chapter's formulation: "I'm breaking up with you because it's the right thing for me [here's why], and I understand how hurt you are, and that you think we should try again, and I'm not changing my mind, and I understand that you think I should have been more clear about my confusion earlier, and I don't think that makes me a bad person... And, and, and." This is not weakness; it is the most powerful place from which to deliver hard news.

**The And Stance does not require agreement.** Two people can use the And Stance and still have honest, unresolved disagreements. The most useful question after exploring both stories is not "Who is right?" but "Now that we really understand each other, what's a good way to manage this problem?"

## Signal it landed

You can state both your story and theirs in the same breath, in their respective strongest forms, without either one canceling the other out. The word "and" has replaced "but" in your articulation of the disagreement. You are no longer trying to make the other person admit they were wrong, and you are not pretending to agree with them.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "am I treating this as a situation where one of us must be right and the other wrong?" If yes, the And Stance has not been adopted.

After: ask "can I state the other person's view in its strongest form AND state my own in its strongest form, in the same statement?" If yes, the And Stance is running.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the And Stance, disagreements collapse into a binary in which understanding the other person's story feels like capitulation. People therefore stop listening — once you listen, you've lost. This produces the worst possible conversation structure: each party performing understanding while secretly preparing their rebuttal. The And Stance breaks this structure by removing the hidden assumption that understanding = agreeing. It also prevents the opposite failure: having your view in such certainty that you cannot hear the other person at all. The binary forces you into one of two stances — each one inadequate. The And Stance is the third option.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter uses the "open or closed window" as the simplest case: one person prefers the window open, one prefers it closed. There is no question of who is right; it is simply a difference. Both preferences are real. The And Stance: "I need the window open to sleep AND you need it closed to sleep. Both of these are true. Let's figure out what to do." The And Stance in a more charged case: breaking up (the chapter's full example). The person delivering the breakup can hold: my reasons for leaving, your pain, your disagreement with my decision, my awareness that I handled some of this badly, my unwillingness to change the decision, my uncertainty about whether I'll regret it — all simultaneously, without any of them canceling the others. This is harder than it looks, and considerably more honest than the alternative.
