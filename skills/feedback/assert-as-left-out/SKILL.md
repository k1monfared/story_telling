## When to reach for this

You are in a feedback conversation and you disagree with what the giver is saying, or you have information, feelings, or a perspective that has not been introduced. You want to say something that matters. But every version you rehearse either starts a fight ("that's wrong"), signals passive hostility ("it's fine, I guess"), or stays bottled up entirely.

## The move

**Reframe the purpose of assertion.** You are not asserting to convince the giver that you are right. You are adding what is left out. The chapter: "You're adding what's most often left out: your data, your interpretations, and your feelings." The feedback conversation is a puzzle both of you are assembling. The giver has some pieces; you have some pieces. When you don't assert, you withhold your pieces. The puzzle stays incomplete. With both sets on the table, you can see where you agree and disagree, and why.

**Replace truth-assertions with difference-assertions.** The chapter's key move:

- Pitfall: "That advice is wrong."
- Better: "I disagree with that advice."

"That advice is wrong" invites the giver to defend its correctness. "I disagree" is a fact about your view that they cannot argue with. What follows is the productive conversation: why do you see it differently? You can then add the data and context that led you to your interpretation. The chapter: "You can be assertive about your point of view even as you are aware that it's your point of view and not necessarily the entire story."

**Know the three trigger-specific assertion pitfalls.** Each type of trigger produces a characteristic assertion failure:

- Truth trigger pitfall: asserting as objective fact ("that advice is wrong"), which puts the giver on the defensive about correctness. Fix: own your view as yours ("I disagree with that advice, because at my last job we had a different approach and had fewer problems").

- Relationship trigger pitfall: switchtracking or blaming ("you're a self-centered jerk"). Fix: name both topics and give each its own track ("I'm feeling underappreciated, so it's hard for me to focus on your feedback. I think we need to discuss how I'm feeling and the feedback itself"). A related pitfall: asserting "this is not my fault" rather than your contribution alongside your observation about the system. Fix: "I agree there are things I've contributed. I'd also like to look at the bigger picture together, because there are other inputs that matter here."

- Identity trigger pitfall: overdramatic acceptance when overwhelmed ("it's true, I'm hopeless"). Fix: be honest that you are overwhelmed rather than performing collapse ("I'm surprised by all this and it's a lot to take in. I want time to think about it. Let's come back tomorrow"). Inverse pitfall: asserting that the feedback cannot be true because you are not that kind of person ("that's ridiculous, I'm not that kind of person"). Fix: "That's upsetting to hear, because it's not how I see myself or who I want to be." You can signal that the information doesn't fit your self-image without claiming the information is wrong.

## Signal it landed

You have said something specific, in your own words, that the giver did not know before. Not "fine, I suppose" and not "you're wrong," but a piece of your data, interpretation, or feeling that adds to the puzzle. The giver now has a piece they did not have before, and neither of you is simply defending your original position.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "what is the thing I most want the giver to understand about my experience or view?" If the answer is "that they're wrong," you are still in truth-assertion mode. The move is to translate that into: "what do I know that they don't? What piece is missing?"

After: ask "did I say something specific about my own data, feelings, or interpretation that the giver could not have known before I said it?" If yes, the assertion has added a piece.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the "left out" reframe, assertion in feedback conversations escalates into truth fights, passive hostility, or silence. The giver says something; you think it's wrong; you say it's wrong; they say it's right; you repeat more emphatically that it's wrong. No new information enters the room. The chapter's version two of the performance rating conversation is the failure mode made explicit: "I think 4 is a little low, but I suppose it's fine" — unclear and passive-aggressive. The chapter's version one: "this is just unfair" — a truth assertion that triggers the giver's identity. Both failures share the same root: the receiver is either trying to win an argument about who is right or not saying anything real at all. "Here's what's left out" is neither.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's version four of the performance rating conversation: the receiver says "I was surprised I got a 4 instead of a 5. I was thinking that, but as I reflect on it, I realize that wasn't based on very much information." This asserts the surprise and the expectation while holding them with appropriate epistemic tentativeness (I realize I may not have had enough information). Later: "Based on what you've just explained about the criteria, and assuming there were no other factors, I do think I should have received a 5. Do you see it differently?" This asserts a view (5 was warranted under the stated criteria) while opening it to the giver's perspective rather than declaring it a fact.
