## When to reach for this

You have noticed (perhaps after using notice-the-distortion-type or separate-feeling-story-feedback) that your story about the feedback has expanded beyond what the feedback actually addressed. The feedback was about one thing, but your story is about all things. The feedback was from one person, but your story has it from everyone. The feedback was about right now, but your story has it covering your whole past and extending your whole future. You need to pull the story back to actual size.

## The move

The chapter names three "laws of physics" that strong feelings cause us to forget:

- **Time**: The present does not change the past. The present influences but does not determine the future.
- **Specificity**: Being lousy at one thing does not make us lousy at unrelated things. Being lousy at something now does not mean we will always be lousy at it.
- **People**: If one person does not like us, it does not mean that everyone does not like us. Even a person who does not like us usually likes some things about us. People's views of us can change.

When the story has violated one of these laws, use one or more of the three tools to restore the containment.

**Tool 1: Feedback Containment Chart.** Draw two columns. Left column: what IS this feedback about? Right column: what is this feedback NOT about? Write answers in both columns. The left column is the domain where the feedback applies and where you can learn from it. The right column is the domain where the feedback does not apply, where your story has trespassed, and where you need to stop processing the feedback as evidence.

The chapter's example: receiving a rejection from a dream job. The left column is: whether you have the specific qualifications they were looking for, or whether you presented yourself in quite the right way. The right column is: whether you will never get a job you like, whether you will ever work in your chosen field, whether your future is determined by this outcome. Writing the right column plainly often reveals how large the story has grown. Writing the left column plainly reveals how small and tractable the actual feedback is.

**Tool 2: Balancing Picture.** When you know logically that you are over-weighting one negative comment in a set of generally positive feedback, but you cannot feel the proportion, make it visual. Draw a pie chart, a bar chart, a Post-it collage, a literal picture of the proportions. The chapter's finding: Alita, who was devastated by critical comments in a sea of positive patient ratings, was shocked at how different the balance of positives and negatives looked when she drew it. The visual representation does not produce a final truth about the feedback, but it makes the proportion legible in a way that emotional experience alone does not.

**Tool 3: Right-size the future consequences.** When consequences are real (not getting the raise, not getting the job, not being allowed to fly), do not dismiss them. But separate the consequences that will happen from the consequences that might happen. The chapter's warning: in the grip of upsetting feedback, we often process might-happen consequences as if they were will-happen. Your boss confirmed you are not getting a raise. That your spouse will leave you because of the missing raise is only something that might happen, and the chances are small. But in the moment, it feels inevitable.

Right-sizing means developing a realistic and healthy prediction of what is actually likely to follow from the feedback, rather than treating the catastrophic projection as a forecast. The chapter cites Daniel Gilbert's finding that people consistently overestimate how awful they will feel and how long they will feel awful in the wake of loss. And they underestimate their own resilience. What feels like permanent damage is, for most people, a recoverable setback that something new will eventually fill.

## Signal it landed

The left column of the containment chart has the feedback in it and it is smaller than your story was. The right column has the trespass material in it and you can see the boundary. The visual representation of the feedback proportion no longer surprises you; you can hold the proportion in your head. You can name one concrete consequence that will happen and distinguish it from the cascade of consequences your story had appended.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "in my story about this feedback, what domains does it cover?" If it covers all of your skills, all of your relationships, your past, and your future simultaneously, the story has violated all three containment laws.

After: ask "what is the smallest accurate statement of what this feedback says?" If you can write that statement in one sentence and it fits inside the left column of the containment chart, the story has been contained.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without containment, learning is impossible because the feedback has become overwhelmingly large. A person who believes that one critical remark means they are fundamentally incompetent cannot learn from the specific thing the remark addressed; they are too busy defending or mourning their entire competence. A person who believes a single rejection predicts all future rejections cannot investigate what specifically did not work in this case. The containment step is what allows the feedback to be usable: it shrinks the story back to the actual size of the feedback, where the actual lesson lives.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's "dream job rejection" case: Before containment, the story is "I'll never get a job I like." After containment: the left column is "whether I have the specific qualifications they were looking for, or whether I'm presenting myself in the right way." The right column is "whether I will ever work in my chosen field, whether my future is determined by this." The contained version is tractable: you can identify and address one or two discrete things. The uncontained version is paralyzing: you are contending with your entire career.
