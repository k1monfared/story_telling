## When to reach for this

You have considered feedback carefully and decided not to take it. Your children want you to move out of your home; your staff wants you to abandon the reorganization; your neighbor wants you to get rid of your cat. You have heard the concerns and are going ahead anyway. The relationship continues. You cannot simply go your way and declare the matter closed.

## The move

**Inquire about and acknowledge the impact on them.** Ask directly how your decision affects those who live and work with you. Larry decided not to take medication for his ADHD. This affects his family (who have to prompt, prod, and remediate) and his construction crew (who have efficiency and safety concerns). Larry needs to have that conversation: what are the actual impacts, and what can be put in place together to address them? The decision is his; the consequences are shared.

**Coach them to deal with the unchanged you.** Tell them specifically what they can do to manage the situation given that you are not changing. Jackie knows she dominates discussions and has decided, after a year of trying, to stop working on it. Her message to her teammates: "I know I can be overbearing. I tried to change. It was a lot of effort for almost no benefit. So I give you all permission to cut me off. Red-card me. I promise I won't think you're being rude; I need the help." She is not pretending she will change. She is giving others the tools to work with the person she actually is.

**Problem-solve together.** This is not about shutting down the conversation but about jointly inventing ways to minimize the cost of your decision. Mark has offered Steve football tickets for three decades and Steve reliably shows up late or not at all. Steve is not going to change. Rather than Mark continuing to offer and be disappointed, or stopping the invitations entirely, they work out a modified protocol: when Mark extends an invitation, they discuss Steve's specific conflicts, Steve's level of commitment, and what happens to Mark if Steve bails. Sometimes Steve suggests they do something else that better fits what they actually want: time together. The chapter's framing: "The clarity of Steve's boundary made it easier for the brothers to spend time together."

## Signal it landed

You have had a conversation (not just thought about having one) with the affected parties. You know specifically what difficulties your decision creates for them. You have either coached them on how to work with the unchanged you, or you have jointly designed a modified arrangement that reduces the friction. The people affected are no longer simply buffeted by your decision without input.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "have I actually talked to the people affected by my decision, or have I only informed them?" If only informed, the mitigation conversation has not happened.

After: ask "do the affected parties have something they can do to manage this situation, other than wait for me to change?" If yes, the mitigation has given them agency. If no, the duty to mitigate has not yet been fulfilled.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without mitigation, the decision not to change is experienced by others as indifference to its impact on them. The relationship is left with a source of ongoing friction that is known, named, and deliberately unaddressed. The people affected either absorb the consequences silently (which builds resentment), continue to push for change (which generates repeated futile conversations), or leave the relationship. The chapter notes that mitigation paradoxically can make it easier to be together: when the unchanged reality is acknowledged and planned for, rather than denied or resisted, both parties can engage honestly with who each person actually is.

## Worked micro-example

Mark and Steve's thirty-year football ticket conflict. Steve is not reliable. Steve knows this. Mark knows this. Instead of the choices of continued resentment (Mark keeps inviting and is repeatedly disappointed) or withdrawal (Mark stops inviting), they problem-solve: specific check-ins about Steve's conflicts on the day in question, explicit discussion of the cost to Mark if Steve bails, and Steve's offer to sometimes suggest an alternative that serves their real goal (spending time together). Both parties are working with reality rather than the fiction of a changed Steve.
