## When to reach for this

You are in (or preparing for) a difficult conversation where the "What Happened?" conversation is the primary source of difficulty. Both parties are insisting their version is right, arguing about whether the other person had bad intentions, or stuck in a cycle of mutual blame. The conversation keeps generating more heat rather than more understanding.

## The move

**1. Shift from certainty to curiosity.** Apply `shift-from-certainty-to-curiosity`. Stop asserting your version of events and start asking how the other person's version makes sense. What information do they have that you don't? What past experiences or implicit rules lead them to interpret the situation the way they do? Get curious about your own story too: what assumptions are you treating as facts? What information are you ignoring?

Step 1 must come first because all subsequent moves depend on it. If you remain in certainty, you will use step 2's And Stance as a rhetorical posture rather than a genuine stance. You will use step 3's intent disentanglement as a technique for defending yourself while appearing open. And you will use step 4's contribution framework as a way of assigning partial blame rather than genuinely mapping the system. Curiosity is what makes all three subsequent moves authentic.

**2. Adopt the And Stance.** Apply `adopt-the-and-stance`. From curiosity, you now hold both stories simultaneously rather than choosing between them. Your story makes sense. Their story also makes sense. Both can be true at the same time. This posture is what allows you to keep your own view while genuinely hearing theirs — neither capitulating nor dismissing.

Step 2 follows step 1 naturally: once you are curious about their story, you have access to it, and the And Stance is how you hold both. Step 2 before step 1 produces a formulaic stance that does not feel genuine and produces the "you're not really curious, you're just using the right words" response from the other person.

**3. Disentangle impact from intent.** Apply `disentangle-impact-from-intent`. The most common specific error in the "What Happened?" Conversation is the leap from "I was hurt" to "you meant to hurt me." Address this explicitly. If you have been hurt: separate what they did from your interpretation of what they intended; share the impact and hold the intent assumption as a hypothesis. If you have been accused of bad intentions: listen past the accusation for the feelings it contains; acknowledge the hurt before addressing the intent question; reflect honestly on whether your intentions were as pure as you claim.

Step 3 after step 2: the And Stance makes it possible to acknowledge the impact you had (they were hurt) while also holding a different interpretation of intent. Without step 2, the acknowledgment of impact feels like an admission that their intent-accusation was correct.

**4. Map the contribution system.** Apply `map-the-contribution-system`. Replace the blame question ("whose fault is this?") with the contribution question ("how did each of us contribute to this?"). Name your own contribution explicitly and first. Map theirs in behavioral terms, without judgment. Identify the system: how do your respective behaviors form a reinforcing cycle? Name what each of you needs to do differently.

Step 4 after step 3: contribution is how you understand the system now that you have separated impact from intent and can see each party's part without the distortion of the intent-assumption. Without step 3, contribution mapping drifts back toward blame because the intent assumption is still running in the background.

## Chain ordering is intrinsic

- Step 1 (curiosity) → step 2 (And Stance): you cannot hold both stories unless you first became curious enough to actually hear the other story.
- Step 2 (And Stance) → step 3 (intent/impact): the And Stance makes impact acknowledgment possible without conceding the intent accusation.
- Step 3 (intent/impact) → step 4 (contribution): impact/intent separation is what allows contribution to be mapped without the blame frame.
- All four together: this is the complete three-error sequence the book names for the "What Happened?" Conversation, applied in order.

## Signal it landed

Both parties have moved from arguing about who is right to describing how each sees the situation and why. The intent question has been separated from the impact question. Both parties have named at least one thing they each contributed. The conversation is producing new information rather than recycling the same positions.

## Probe before & after

Before step 1: ask "am I still certain that I know what happened and that I'm right?" If yes, step 1 has not happened yet.

After step 4: ask "have both of us named our contributions to the problem?" If yes, the chain has run. If one person still believes the problem is entirely the other's fault, the chain has not completed.

## Failure mode this prevents

The three errors in the "What Happened?" Conversation — the truth assumption, the intention invention, and the blame frame — interlock and reinforce each other. Each one makes the others worse: certainty about your story makes bad-intent assumptions easier to maintain, and bad-intent assumptions make blame harder to avoid, and blame makes the other person defensive and more certain their version is right. The chain runs the remedies in the order that addresses the underlying structure: curiosity first (because it is the substrate for everything else), then holding both stories (because it is the posture that keeps the conversation from collapsing under disagreement), then intent/impact (because it is the most specific and destructive error), then contribution (because it is what replaces blame as the productive backward-looking move).

## Worked micro-example

Jack and Michael's brochure conflict is the book's running case for the entire "What Happened?" part. Jack is certain that Michael treated him badly (truth assumption running). Jack is certain that Michael intended to intimidate him (intent assumption running). Jack sees the problem as Michael's fault (blame frame running). The chapter 12 preparation sequence shows Jack working through all four steps in preparation for his follow-up conversation: discovering through curiosity that Michael probably didn't know Jack had worked through the night and cancelled plans (step 1); accepting that both his story and Michael's could make sense from their respective positions (step 2); recognizing that he doesn't actually know whether Michael meant to intimidate him (step 3); and seeing what he himself had contributed by not raising his feelings at the time (step 4). The preparation transforms Jack from a person going in to hold Michael accountable into a person going in to understand.

## Source

Chain assembled from chapters 2, 3, and 4 of Stone, Patton, and Heen's "Difficult Conversations." The three errors the chain addresses (truth assumption, intention invention, blame frame) are explicitly named as the book's three-chapter framework for the "What Happened?" Conversation in chapter 1 (the overview chapter). Each subsequent chapter provides the remedy for one error. The chain runs the remedies in the same order in which the chapters present them, which is the order of conceptual dependency.
