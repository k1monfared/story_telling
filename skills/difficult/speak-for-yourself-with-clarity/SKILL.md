## When to reach for this

You have something important to say in a difficult conversation and it keeps coming out wrong — the other person defends themselves, doesn't hear what you meant, or argues with your framing. Or you find yourself communicating through hints, leading questions, or sarcasm rather than saying what you mean. Or your authentic feelings are more complex than what you are actually saying.

## The move

**Believe you are entitled to speak.** The foundation: your views and feelings are as legitimate, valuable, and important as anyone else's — no more, but no less. If you don't believe this, the rest of the skills in this chapter will be undermined by the part of you that doesn't think you deserve to be heard. The chapter's formulation: "You are the ultimate authority on you. You are an expert on what you think, how you feel, and why you've come to this place. If you think it or feel it, you are entitled to say it, and no one can legitimately contradict you."

**Start with what matters most.** Whatever is at the heart of the matter for you — share that first. "For me, what this is really about is..." Charlie spent months telling his younger brother Gage what to do, never sharing what was actually true: that he admired his brother, that he felt bad about how he had treated him when they were younger, and that he needed to feel like a good brother who was loved in return. When Charlie finally said those things, Gage was riveted. The things Charlie had been saying before were not wrong, just beside the point.

**Don't rely on subtext; say it directly.** Communicating through hints, ironic questions, offhand comments, or body language gives you the worst of both worlds: you trigger the problems you feared, without getting the benefit of actually saying what you meant. "Is golf so important that you need to play this often?" is not the same as "I miss spending Saturday mornings with you." The first triggers defensiveness and leaves the other person guessing. The second says what is true. Avoid "easing in" — leading through indirect questions that signal there is a message but never quite deliver it. State what you think directly, while signaling that you want to know how they see it differently.

**Use the Me-Me And for complex feelings.** When what you feel or think is genuinely complex — when you feel two things at once about the same person — say both. "I do think you're talented, and I also think you're not working hard enough." "I feel relieved that I finally ended the relationship, and I do miss him." The "and" holds both things as true simultaneously without canceling either. This prevents the oversimplification that makes conversations feel incomplete or dishonest.

**Three guidelines for telling your story with clarity:**

1. **Don't present your conclusions as the truth.** Facts are facts. Everything else is your interpretation, opinion, or judgment. Say "I believe spanking is wrong" not "Spanking is just plain wrong." Say "In my view..." or "The way I see it..." or "From the information I have..." Watch out for words like "inappropriate," "should," or "professional" — they carry judgments that look like facts. Presenting opinions as truths generates "Who are you to say?!" reactions and makes it harder to have a real conversation.

2. **Share where your conclusions come from.** Give the other person the information and experiences that led you to your view. Your wife can't understand your reluctance about private school unless she knows that you spent years feeling like you didn't fit in and that your parents sacrificed financially to pay your tuition. That story gives your view its full weight. Without it, she hears only a conclusion disconnected from any grounding.

3. **Don't exaggerate with "always" and "never."** These words express frustration but invite arguments about frequency and signal that change is impossible. "You always criticize my clothes" invites: "That's not true, I said something nice last year." More effective: describe the specific impact and invite change. "When you tell me my suit reminds you of curtains, I feel hurt and incompetent. I wish I could hear something positive more often — even something simple."

**Help them understand you.** After sharing your story, check whether it landed. Ask them to paraphrase what they heard. Ask how they see it differently — not "do you agree?" (which invites polite evasion) but "how do you see this differently?" The explicit invitation surfaces real reactions rather than the "yes, I suppose so" that means nothing.

## Signal it landed

What you said was clearly your view, not presented as a truth that should be obvious. You said the actual heart of the matter rather than peripheral concerns that circle it. You said it directly rather than through hints. The other person engaged with your actual view rather than defending themselves against an accusation.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "is what I'm about to say the heart of what I actually mean, said directly?" If what you're planning to say is a hint, a leading question, or a proxy for the real thing, rewrite it.

After: ask "did the other person respond to what I actually meant, or to a different interpretation of what I said?" If the latter, something in the expression missed.

## Failure mode this prevents

Subtext and indirect communication produce conversations where the actual issue never gets addressed. The husband talks about the dishes, the housework, and the schedule, never saying "I miss spending time with you" — and the wife has no idea that is what is at stake. Charlie gives advice about debate and drinking, never saying "I need to feel like a good brother who is loved" — and Gage feels only criticized. The chapter: "We say the least important things, sometimes over and over again, and wonder why the other person doesn't realize what we really think and how we really feel."

## Worked micro-example

Charlie and Gage. Charlie keeps giving Gage advice that makes Gage feel patronized. The heart of the matter — Charlie's admiration for his brother, his guilt about how he treated him as a child, his deep need to feel like a good brother — was never said. It wasn't that Charlie said things badly; he said things that were beside the point. When he finally spoke the heart of the matter, the relationship shifted. Gage felt needed rather than patronized.
