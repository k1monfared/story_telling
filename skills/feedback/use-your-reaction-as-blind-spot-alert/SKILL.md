## When to reach for this

The feedback feels off. You can hear it, you can repeat it, and you can already tell the giver is wrong about you. As you sit with it, your mind is not idle. It is constructing an explanation for why this person, specifically, would say something so off base. They must have an agenda. They must be reading their own stuff into you. They must have a personality quirk that warps how they read others. Reach for this skill the moment you notice that the explanation you are constructing is about them, not about the feedback.

## The move

1. Notice the thought form. The chapter names the specific cognitive moves that signal you are in the trap. "What was their agenda?" "What's wrong with them?" "Why are they really saying this?" "Are they projecting?" Annabelle's version, when she received the second 360, was to wonder whether her subordinates were playing politics or shadowboxing absent parents. The chapter is unsparing here: this is not unusual reasoning under triggered conditions. It is the predictable shape the mind takes when a piece of feedback contradicts the self.
2. Treat that thought as the alarm, not the analysis. The chapter's structural claim is that looking harder at yourself does not help, because what you will see when you look harder is that the feedback is wrong and the giver must have an agenda. The verdict of "they are off" is the receiver-side terminus of the very same Gap Map gap the chapter is trying to surface. So when the agenda thought arrives, do not act on it. Use it. Make the next thought a deliberate one: "I wonder if this feedback is sitting in my blind spot."
3. Hold both possibilities open. The skill is not "the feedback must be right." It is "I cannot tell yet, and the feeling that it must be wrong is not, by itself, evidence." Leave the question open long enough to do the next moves the chapter prescribes (look for patterns, ask a friend who will be an honest mirror, ask the giver "how am I getting in my own way"). The agenda story might be true. It usually is not the whole story.
4. Notice the symmetry. The chapter points out that your reaction to wrong feedback runs the same Gap Map in reverse: you upset, you assume intent, you conclude something is wrong with them. Knowing the loop is bidirectional makes it easier to hold the agenda thought as a hypothesis instead of a finding.

## Signal it landed

You can name, in one sentence, the specific pathologizing thought your mind just produced about the giver ("I'm wondering whether her review is really about her own boss"). And you can name, in a second sentence, the question you have substituted for it ("which means I should treat this as possibly sitting in my blind spot, and check"). The case-against-the-giver is on hold. The next move you make is one of the chapter's checking moves, not the dismissal.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "When I think about why this feedback is wrong, am I describing the feedback's content, or am I describing the giver?" If the answer is the giver (their agenda, their character, their issue), the alarm is sounding.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you have moved from "they're off" to "I cannot yet tell, and here is the move I am making to find out." A good test: you can identify the specific blind-spot-checking action you are now taking, and you can articulate that the agenda story might still turn out to be true, but it is no longer your conclusion.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this skill, the receiver mistakes the most predictable possible response (the agenda story) for a careful judgment about the giver. The chapter is explicit that "looking harder" produces the same dismissal verdict, because the dismissal is the asymmetric output of a system that gives you full access to your intentions and almost no access to your behavior. The giver becomes the problem. The conversation closes. The feedback that was actually trying to surface a leaky face, a leaky tone, or a pattern your friends and exes have all noticed gets filed alongside the dismissed others. Six months or three years later, the same feedback arrives again from a different giver, and the receiver is shocked at the consistency, because each giver was processed individually as having their own peculiar agenda. Annabelle's second 360, three years after the first with the same theme, is the chapter's worked case of exactly this failure.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's case is Annabelle, who receives a second 360 in three years naming her as difficult, impatient, and disrespectful. Her mind goes immediately to the candidate explanations the chapter names: maybe her subordinates are playing politics, maybe they enjoy taking anonymous shots at the boss, maybe it is projection, maybe they are working through unrelated developmental issues with an authority figure. The chapter then says: Annabelle is right that something else is going on, but it is not any of those things. It is that her actual feelings (impatience and contempt under pressure) are leaking through her face and tone, and her colleagues are reading the leak accurately. The agenda story was the predictable response, but it was not the truth of the situation. The skill installs the move Annabelle did not make: when the agenda thought arrives, treat it as the alarm that the feedback may be exactly where the chapter says people are blindest, and run the checking moves before drawing a conclusion.

The skill installs the cognitive trigger and the substitution.
