## When to reach for this

You have done the work to understand what the giver is actually saying, and your view still does not match theirs. You are about to settle the disagreement by deciding one of you must be wrong, or by absorbing their view to keep the peace, or by ending the conversation politely with nothing changed. Reach for this before you do, because the chapter's claim is that the disagreement is rarely a right-versus-wrong problem. It is almost always a we-have-different-data and/or we-interpret-the-data-differently problem, and naming which is the move that opens it.

## The move

1. Reframe the disagreement. The default reading is "one of us must be wrong, almost certainly them." The chapter offers a structurally different reading: we are looking at different data, and/or we are interpreting the data differently. Almost any feedback disagreement decomposes into one or both.
2. Ask about their data that you do not have. Where do they sit, what do they see, who do they talk to, what reports cross their desk that do not cross yours? The chapter's framing: different roles, different physical locations, different bodies, different sensitivities, different cares all produce different data even when people are looking at the same situation. Your boss knows what your peers are paid; you do not. The receptionist sees who comes through the door; the CEO does not.
3. Share your data that they do not have. The chapter is explicit that this is not the time to discard your own observations; you are not trying to get their ideas into an empty head, you are trying to get their ideas into your already-full head. Make your data visible to them as you ask about theirs.
4. Surface differences in interpretation. Same data, different stories. The chapter names two specific factors most often embedded in interpretive differences:
    - **Implicit rules**: the unspoken rules in your head about how things should be ("conflict is bad" vs. "conflict is healthy"; "be direct" vs. "be nice"; on time means seated and ready vs. on time means walking in by then). You have rules; the giver has rules; you each treat them as the rules. Naming them as your rules and theirs is the move.
    - **Heroes and villains**: each of you is the sympathetic protagonist of your own version of the story, and may be reading the other as the obstacle. The chapter's surgeon-and-son scene: each is sure they are protecting the father, each sees the other as misguided. Once both heroes are visible, the moral framing softens enough to discuss the underlying disagreement.
5. After the differences are on the table, do one more pass: ask what is right about their feedback. The chapter places this as a deliberate coda. The mindset is not "let me grade their feedback for objective truth" (that slides back into wrong spotting) but "what makes sense about what they are saying, what seems worth trying, where do they have a point." The chapter's image: walking through a forest identifying birds instead of trees. Noticing the birds does not make the trees wrong.
6. If you still disagree at the end of all this, the chapter is clear that you have succeeded at understanding even if you have not converged. You can be transparent about your reaction ("that's upsetting to hear, I'm almost speechless") and still keep the conversation open.

## Signal it landed

You can name, in two parts, what is different about your data and what is different about your interpretation. You can articulate at least one piece of data the giver has that you do not (and ideally one piece you have that they do not). You can name at least one implicit rule each of you is applying to read the same situation. You can list two or three things the feedback gets right, distinct from agreeing or disagreeing with the verdict overall.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "When I think about why we see this differently, do I think 'they're wrong' or do I think 'we have different X'?" If the answer is "they're wrong" (and the X has not been named), wrong spotting is still running underneath your understanding work.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can complete the sentence "we see this differently because we have different ___ and we are interpreting the data through different ___." If you can do that, the disagreement has shape and the conversation can move.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without difference spotting, two failure modes alternate. In the first, you understand the feedback (you did the work) and conclude that the giver is, on balance, wrong. The "on balance, wrong" verdict feels like a careful judgment but is in fact the wrong-spotting habit re-entering through the back door, now armored with the gloss of "I really tried to understand." The receiver loses the chance to learn from the parts of the difference that were genuinely informational about the world. In the second failure mode, you absorb the giver's view because you respect them or want to avoid conflict, and you give up your own observations and rules without testing them. The chapter is explicit that neither swallowing nor dismissing is the move; the goal is each of you understanding both views, with the differences named at the level of data and interpretation, not at the level of right-versus-wrong. Without that named structure, joint solutions are unreachable, because the parties cannot tell which differences are about facts they could share and which are about rules they may need to negotiate.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's case is Mavis and Davis. Mavis is a lawyer on a cross-functional team; Davis, the account manager, gives her blunt feedback at her annual review: "You don't understand the business side of the business. Your laborious legal reviews slow the sales process and we lose out to fleeter competition." Mavis's first read: Davis is wrong, he doesn't see what I see. The chapter walks through the data each has (Mavis: the litigation reports, the general counsel's mandate, the cost of settlements; Davis: the customer conversations, the pitch-to-close ratio, what other firms are willing to offer) and the implicit rules each is applying ("legal's job is to rein in sales" vs. "no deal, no firm"). Neither is wrong on the data they have or the rules they hold; the puzzle is that each has only part of the picture, and difference spotting is what gets the parts on the table together. The chapter's coda case (the surgeon and the son in the postoperative corridor) makes the heroes-and-villains version: each is protecting the father from suffering, each sees the other as misguided, and the morality play has to soften before the medication question can be discussed.

## Source

Chapter 3, "First Understand," lines 1420-1545 ("Shift From Wrong Spotting to Difference Spotting" through "Ask: What's Right?"). Stone and Heen offer the explicit decomposition (different data and/or different interpretations), name the two most common interpretive factors (implicit rules, heroes-and-villains), and place the right-spotting coda as a deliberate further step rather than the conclusion. The skill installs the chapter's second-stage move: once you understand the feedback, surface the differences at the levels of data and interpretation rather than at the level of right-versus-wrong.
