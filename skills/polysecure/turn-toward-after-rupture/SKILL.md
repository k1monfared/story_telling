## When to reach for this

A rupture has happened in the last hour, day, or week, and the silence is starting to set. You can feel the partner is not okay, you are not okay, and the next move is unclear. Reach for this whenever the impulse is to ride it out without speaking. Avoidance is the path that erodes relationships over years; repair is what keeps them alive.

## The move

1. Notice the rupture is there. Don't pretend it isn't. The partner already knows.
2. Initiate. The willingness to be the one who turns back first is itself part of repair. Don't keep score on whose turn it is.
3. Acknowledge impact, not just intent. "I see that what I did landed in a way that hurt you, regardless of what I meant by it." Impact comes first; intent comes only if asked.
4. Take responsibility for your part. Specifically. Not "I'm sorry if you were upset" (which puts the upset on them) but "I'm sorry I dismissed your concern when you brought it up."
5. Listen for what they need. Sometimes it's understanding; sometimes it's a behavior change; sometimes it's just to know you saw the rupture happen.
6. Don't rush to resolution. The repair often takes more than one conversation. The first conversation might just be "I see this is here, I want to come back to it, can we?" The second is the work itself.
7. Notice your own wound from the rupture too. Repair isn't only about offering — it's also about being open to receiving what the other person brings.

## Signal it landed

You and the partner can name what happened, what each of you contributed, and what you each need going forward. The rupture isn't erased; it's metabolized. The relationship feels more solid afterward, not less, because it now has evidence that ruptures can be repaired.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without active repair, ruptures don't heal — they just move underground. The relationship feels increasingly distant without anyone able to point to a single decisive event. By the time someone notices, multiple unrepaired ruptures have stacked up and the work of repair becomes much heavier.

## Worked micro-example

Mar and Jess have a sharp argument about scheduling — Mar feels deprioritized; Jess feels micromanaged. They sleep on it badly. The next morning Mar opens: "Last night went badly. I want to come back to it. I think I escalated when you said you couldn't move the date, and I see now that I was actually upset about something earlier in the week, not really about the calendar. Can we talk about the earlier thing?" The repair takes the conversation back to the actual rupture instead of relitigating the calendar fight.

## Reflection prompts

- Are there themes to the ruptures you have with your partners? Recurring shapes?
- When there's been a rupture, do you and your partners already repair well? How?
- How are you at offering repair? What comes easily? What feels difficult?
- How are you with receiving a repair from your partners?
- What could you do differently to repair better next time?
