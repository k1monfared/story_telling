## When to reach for this

You are revising a unit of a piece of art whose composition you control: a paragraph, a scene, a stanza, a shot, a frame. The unit holds together at first glance. You can feel that some details are doing work and others are decorative or accidental. You have not gone through them one at a time. Reach for this whenever "I just wrote it that way" or "it was already there in the draft" is the only justification any detail has.

## The move

1. Pick the unit. A paragraph, a scene, a shot, a panel, the area inside one frame of attention.
2. Inventory the elements present in it. Not just the protagonists and the named action, but the supporting details: the weather, the background figures, a small gesture, the contents of a glance, the side of a tree the character walks past, the items the character checks.
3. For each element, finish the sentence: "This is here because ___." The blank must name an effect on the reader: it primes a turn, deepens an established mood, registers a relationship, foreshadows, contrasts, ironizes.
4. If the blank stalls on a detail, that detail is not yet your choice. Decide. Either commit to a function for it (and possibly sharpen the detail to serve that function), or remove it.
5. Do not assume that vivid detail justifies itself. A loving couple in the background of a reconciliation scene is doing work; a loving couple anywhere else may be filler. The test is the named function, not the vividness.
6. Run the audit until every element in the unit has either a stated function or an explicit "I am keeping this for atmosphere even though it does nothing structural" tag. Atmosphere is permitted, but as a decision, not as a default.

## Signal it landed

For the unit you audited, you can produce a list of every element in it paired with what that element is doing for the reader. The list contains items you would not have noticed before the audit (a tree position, a glance in the background, a checked wallet) and at least one item that, once named, you decide to cut, sharpen, or move because it was not actually doing the work you thought it was.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, point at a detail in the unit and ask: "Why is this exactly here?" Note your answer. If it is "because it just sounded right" or "because that's how I wrote it," you have not taken responsibility for it.

After applying, ask the same question of the same detail and of two others. The skill landed if every answer names a specific effect on the reader, and at least one of those effects connects forward or backward to another moment in the piece.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this audit, the unit accumulates details by default. Some are doing work, some are filler, and you cannot tell which because you never asked. The reader registers the difference even when you do not, because filler details dilute the ones that are doing work. The piece feels almost-good, "well-written" in a way you cannot improve, because the part you would need to fix is invisible to you. Saunders's diagnosis is direct: an artist is the person who has taken responsibility for every aspect of the work, and a piece feels like art exactly to the degree that this has happened.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders re-watches a sequence from Vittorio De Sica's *Bicycle Thieves* with his class. On first viewing the sequence simply moves them. On repeat viewings they start to notice the pieces. The boy and his father, walking sadly along the river, pass on opposite sides of one tree. At the next tree the boy swerves over and they pass on the same side. (Possible reconciliation pending.) A truck full of celebrating soccer fans goes past, happy in a way the father and son are not. The father, noticing his son notice the truck, and feeling the shame of having slapped him, checks his wallet and proposes pizza. As the reconciliation plays out at the table, behind them in the frame is a loving couple looking out at the river. Saunders names the lesson: it is a lesser sequence without any one of these things. De Sica had taken responsibility for every aspect of every frame, and the sequence's first-viewing effect on the reader is the cumulative result.
