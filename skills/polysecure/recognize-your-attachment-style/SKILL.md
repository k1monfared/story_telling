## When to reach for this

You notice a recurring shape in your close relationships — you reliably escalate when a partner pulls away, or you reliably retreat when they ask for closeness, or you cycle between wanting them and pushing them off. Reach for this whenever you're tempted to explain the pattern by saying "I'm just like that" or "they bring it out in me." The attachment-style frame gives you a more useful description than either.

## The move

1. Notice when your body activates around closeness or distance. Pay attention to specific moments: a partner is late, a partner suggests more time together, a partner mentions another partner, a partner asks for vulnerability you're not ready for. What does your body do in those moments?
2. Map the activation against the four templates. Anxious: hyper-vigilance, urgent need for reassurance, escalating protests when reassurance doesn't come. Avoidant: shutdown, irritation at proximity, sudden need for space. Disorganized: oscillation between wanting and pushing away, often within the same conversation. Secure: notice the discomfort, self-soothe, reach out without escalation, accept what comes.
3. Treat the style as your nervous system's strategy, not your identity. The strategy was useful at some point — it kept you connected enough or protected enough given the conditions you grew up with. It is now mostly outdated, but it's not a moral failing.
4. Notice that style is contextual. You may be secure with friends and avoidant in romance. You may be anxious with one partner and avoidant with another. The nervous system reads each relationship's specific signals.
5. Use the diagnosis to choose interventions, not to label yourself. Anxious work is largely self-soothing and slowing the protest. Avoidant work is largely staying present through discomfort. Disorganized work is harder and often benefits from skilled help.

## Signal it landed

You can describe your activation pattern in nervous-system terms ("I deactivate when she asks for more closeness" or "I escalate within ten minutes of him going quiet") rather than character terms ("I'm cold" or "I'm too needy"). When the pattern fires, you can name it as it happens, which by itself reduces its grip.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this diagnosis, you spend years treating the pattern as either your partner's fault (they're triggering you) or your fixed personality (this is just how I am). Both framings prevent the work that would actually shift it: meeting the underlying nervous-system response with skill.

## Worked micro-example

Aria notices that every time her partner Devon mentions a date with someone else, she experiences a wave of cold, a tightness in her chest, and an urge to pick a fight about something unrelated. She used to call this "him being inconsiderate" or "me being controlling." Now she names it: "anxious attachment activating around perceived displacement." She tells Devon what she observed in herself, and asks for a five-minute physical reconnect when he gets home — not because Devon did anything wrong, but because her nervous system needs the contact to update.

## Reflection prompts

- What is the shape of your activation when you sense distance from a partner? When you sense too much closeness?
- Which of the four styles' descriptions felt most familiar when you read them? Which felt unfamiliar?
- Are there relationships where you function more securely than in others? What's different about those relationships?
- When did this pattern first appear in your life? Who modeled the response?
