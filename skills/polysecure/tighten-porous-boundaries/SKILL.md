## When to reach for this

You catch yourself feeling responsible for a partner's mood, agreeing to plans you'd rather decline, or carrying emotional weight that originated elsewhere. Reach for this whenever the line between you and others is functioning as a sponge instead of a membrane.

## The move

1. Notice the absorption. The first signal is usually somatic: heaviness after a conversation, a sense of having been drained, the feeling that someone else's distress has set up shop inside you.
2. Distinguish your feelings from theirs. Compassion is real; absorption is different. Compassion: "I see you're hurting and that matters to me." Absorption: "I'm now hurting because you were hurting and I can't put it down."
3. Ask: what did I just take on that wasn't mine to carry? Their mood. Their problem to solve. Their emotional regulation. Their expectation. The question "is this mine?" is the basic boundary practice.
4. Practice the no. Small ones first. "I don't have capacity for that today." "I don't want to do that." "That's not mine to handle." The small no makes the big no possible.
5. Notice when you're being intrusive in the other direction. Porous boundaries can also mean taking up too much space — checking on others' feelings unprompted, offering opinions they didn't ask for, inserting yourself into their decisions. The membrane works both ways.
6. Tighten gradually. Boundaries aren't built in a single conversation; they're a slow practice of recognizing and adjusting where the line currently is.

## Signal it landed

You can leave a conversation with someone difficult without carrying their state for hours afterward. You can decline a request without rehearsing the refusal for days first. People around you start handling more of their own state because you stopped doing it for them.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without firmer boundaries, you become exhausted from carrying weight that wasn't yours. You also enable others to under-develop their own capacity because yours is always available. Relationships skew toward you absorbing everything and them under-functioning.

## Reflection prompts

- In what ways do you experience your boundaries as porous — absorbing, intrusive, or both?
- Where do you over-give in relationships? What beliefs about yourself or about others play into this?
- Where do you over-receive or over-take? What beliefs play into this?
- Do you need to focus more on tightening your boundaries, whether on the input or output? How can you do this?
