## When to reach for this

You are revising a stretch of narrative and the events feel arbitrary. A character makes a choice for no traceable reason. A scene ends with a development the previous pages have not earned. You can list what happens but cannot say why each thing happens. A reader you trust has reported feeling unmoored, like following a clueless tour guide. Reach for this whenever your sequence reads as "and then, and then" rather than "and so, and so."

## The move

1. Mark the major beats of the sequence in order. A beat is any unit of action that changes the state of the story: a decision, an action, an encounter, a setback.
2. For each beat after the first, ask explicitly: "What about the prior beat caused this one?" The answer must be statable as a sentence in the form "Because X happened, Y had to follow." If you cannot complete that sentence, the link is missing.
3. If a link is missing, do not paper over it with a transition sentence. Either install causation upstream (give an earlier beat a feature that makes the next beat a natural consequence) or remove the unlinked beat. Saunders's failure case: the lesser writer makes Nikita "just" fall asleep; the better writer arranges for Nikita to be taunted and ignored at a crossroads, so falling asleep is felt as withdrawal, which then enables Vasili (unmonitored) to get them lost.
4. Test each beat against the previous one and the next. The beat earns its place if the previous beat caused it and it causes the next. A beat caused by nothing in particular is filler. A beat that causes nothing in particular is a dead end.
5. For the load-bearing escalation moments — the beats that change the terms of the story — make the causation visible to the reader. Saunders's example on the sled race: the race fires Vasili up (cause shown via direct text "this encounter cheered and enlivened Vasili"); his elation causes him to drive boldly without examining way-marks (cause shown via the next sentence's behavior); that boldness causes the next essential beat (they get lost again). Three causal links across three sentences; none of them transition-words; all of them earned.
6. Use the Forster test on adjacent sentences. "The queen died and the king died" is sequence. "The queen died and the king died of grief" is causation. The added phrase is small; the change in meaning is total. Apply this test to every adjacency where you suspect a missing link.

## Signal it landed

For every beat in the sequence after the first, you can finish the sentence "This beat happens because, in the previous beat, ___." A reader cold to the draft can summarize the sequence as a chain of causes ("he poked the servant about a sore subject, the servant withdrew, and with no one to check him he chose the wrong road") rather than a list of events ("a man and a servant left, then they got lost"). The sequence has acquired the appearance of meaning, which is what causation produces.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, name a sequence in your draft and ask: "If I cut any single beat from this sequence, what about the rest of the sequence would no longer make sense?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer changes from "I'm not sure anything would" to specific downstream consequences ("the loss of the road would no longer have a cause, since this is the beat where the servant withdraws and stops correcting his master").

## Failure mode this prevents

Without causation, a sequence of events does not become a story. It remains a list. Saunders's diagnosis: most beginners can make things happen ("a dog barked," "the house exploded," "Darren kicked the tire"), but few can make one thing seem to cause the next, and the gap between those two skills is what separates writers who go on to publish from those who don't. The reader of a non-causal sequence experiences it as a clueless tour guide gesturing at unrelated rooms — the frustrated, unmoored feeling that asks, "What am I supposed to be noting?" Each detail might be vivid in isolation; none of them earn their place by being load-bearing. Saunders's metaphor: a beautifully hand-painted kite lying on the grass is admirable, but only causation is the wind that lifts it. A pleasing prose sequence without causation is a flightless kite.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders contrasts two versions of the lost-on-the-road sequence. The lesser writer's version: as they leave home Vasili and Nikita pass some things and discuss random topics; at a crossroads Vasili takes the straight road for reasons not provided; Nikita falls asleep just because; somehow they get lost. Each beat occurs but none causes the next. Tolstoy's version: as they leave home Vasili pokes at Nikita about his wife (a sore subject) and asks if Nikita plans to buy a horse (signaling another upcoming cheat); Nikita is irritated. At the crossroads Vasili asks Nikita's opinion (which is wise, given the weather) and ignores it; Nikita, taunted then dismissed, says only "Just as you please" and withdraws. With no one engaged to check his judgment, Nikita falls asleep. Vasili, unmonitored, steers by his own lights and gets them lost. Every beat is caused by the prior; getting lost is felt as the consequence of a chain that started with a poke about a wife. The same plot summary, the same end-state, totally different reading experience — and the difference is "increased causality."
