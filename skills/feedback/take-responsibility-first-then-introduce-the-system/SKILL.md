## When to reach for this

You have genuinely seen your contribution to the problem, and you have also seen real structural or relational factors that contributed, factors you believe the giver has not acknowledged or addressed. When you try to name those other factors, the giver becomes more frustrated rather than less, and says something like "you're making excuses" or "you're deflecting." The conversation is stuck.

## The move

The chapter names two messages that need to be delivered, and a sequencing rule about them.

**Message one: I take responsibility for my part.** State your contribution clearly and specifically, without burying it in qualifications or presenting it as smaller than you believe it is. The giver needs to hear this first, on its own terms. Do not rush past it.

**Message two: We are both contributing to this.** This is your observation about the broader system, the intersections, roles, or structural factors that are also part of the cause.

**The sequencing rule.** Deliver message one fully before attempting message two. The chapter's reason: the two messages are logically consistent, but to the person giving you feedback, they can sound contradictory. If you present them simultaneously, the giver almost always hears message two as a retraction of message one. A "relationship system" observation that arrives before your accountability is settled registers as: "I'm not really taking responsibility; I'm just describing the situation in a way that spreads the blame around."

**Gauge readiness before introducing message two.** After message one is settled, check whether the giver is in a state to hear a systems observation. This is a conversational judgment. If the giver is still visibly angry, still asserting that the problem is entirely yours, or has not acknowledged hearing message one, message two will not land productively. It may need to wait for a second conversation.

**Avoid the vocabulary of "systems."** The chapter says explicitly: avoid phrases like "relationship system" altogether. That vocabulary signals you have been reading a business book and are about to explain why nothing is your fault. The content of message two is the same; the framing is: "I've noticed that when X happens, my reaction to it tends to be Y, and I wonder if that's part of what sets this up. What do you think?" That framing keeps the receiver posture intact while opening the system question.

## Signal it landed

The giver has heard message one and confirmed it (verbally or in posture). The conversation is no longer stuck on "who is really at fault." When you raise message two, the giver engages with it rather than returning to message one. The conversation now includes both what you are accountable for and what the shared system might be contributing.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "have I said, out loud, in this conversation, what specifically I did or failed to do that contributed to this problem, without qualifying it by immediately pivoting to what the other person did?" If the answer is no, message one has not been delivered yet.

After: ask "does the giver now know what I believe I am responsible for?" If yes, message two is now appropriate to attempt.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the sequencing rule, the receiver tries to introduce the systems view as a simultaneous or even preemptive reframe, and the giver reads the whole move as a sophisticated deflection. The conversation shifts from a productive accountability discussion to a meta-argument about whether the receiver is being honest or just clever. The systems observation, which may be accurate and useful, becomes radioactive because it arrived before accountability was settled. The giver's trust in the receiver's honesty drops, and subsequent conversations become harder. The chapter's framing: "They'll assume you're attempting to deflect the feedback and point a finger back at them. They won't be interested in your fancy ideas about systems."

## Worked micro-example

The chapter does not provide a single named worked example for this sequencing move, but the Gil-and-Sandy thread from earlier in the chapter provides the logic. If Gil receives Sandy's feedback about his obsessive monitoring and responds with "You're right, I panic when surprised by spending, and I know I'm overreacting, and here's what would help me," he has owned his contribution (absorber-corrected) before describing the system need. That sequence is far more likely to be heard than if he began with "The real issue is you hide things from me."
