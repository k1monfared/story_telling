## When to reach for this

The feedback you are receiving consists of an evaluative shorthand: "be more proactive," "you need boundaries," "be more assertive," "don't be so selfish," "act your age," "be more affectionate." The shorthand sounds like it has content. You can already picture what to do (or what to push back on). Reach for this before that picture solidifies into a plan or a rejection.

## The move

1. Notice the label. The chapter's image is the supermarket shelf where items are stocked under generic names: SOUP, COLA. A feedback label is the same shape: a topic word with no nutritional content. The chapter says spotting labels is easy once you are listening for them, like counting how many times someone says "and" in a conversation. The hard part is remembering to listen.
2. Resist filling in your own meaning. The chapter is explicit that this is the second step and the harder one. The label triggers your high-definition movie of what the giver "obviously" means, and once that movie is playing you have nothing to ask, nothing to be curious about, no reason to inquire. Catch the auto-fill in the act.
3. Run a quick multiplicity check. Force yourself to generate two or three plausible alternative readings of the label. The chapter's "be more affectionate" example offers four (initiate sex more often / hold hands in public / pitch in around the house / be more playful and cuddly), all of which fit the label and are different in what they ask of you.
4. Ask. Some version of "tell me more about what you mean by [label]" or "can you describe what that would look like" or "show me." The point is not to put the giver on the spot, it is to make the label hand off its content. The chapter's "Spot the Label" section names the work as figuring out what is under the label; without the question, the label stays empty.
5. Hold the label as a placeholder, not a meal. The label is useful as a topic header you can return to later. It is not the meaning. The chapter's line: "the label is not the meal."

## Signal it landed

You can say, in one sentence, what the giver actually means by the label — sourced from what they told you, not from your auto-fill. If asked "what does be-more-X mean to your boss," you would answer with their words and examples, not with your guess. If you decide to act on the feedback or reject it, you are acting on or rejecting their meaning, not the version your own mind supplied.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I had to write down right now what they mean by [the label], could I distinguish my interpretation from theirs?" If the answer is "of course, they mean what they said," you have not yet spotted the label.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can name your interpretation and theirs as distinct, you can articulate at least one plausible alternative reading the label would also fit, and you have asked enough questions to know which reading is the giver's.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without spotting, two failure modes alternate. In the first, you "take" the feedback and act on your interpretation of the label. The giver later reacts with surprise or alarm at what you did. The chapter's case is Nicholas, told by his manager Adrianna to "be more assertive," who interprets that as "push the customer to close the deal today, before they leave the floor," and ends up threatening a customer. Adrianna had meant "show some energy and engagement, let your personality shine through" — almost the opposite. Nicholas was earnest and Adrianna was clear, and the label still hand-delivered the wrong instruction. In the second failure mode, you reject the feedback because your interpretation of the label is unappealing or wrong-headed. The chapter's case is Holly, who hears "you are too enmeshed" and "you need boundaries" and interprets that as "care less," which she rightly refuses; what Irwin actually meant was "watch the specific pattern of giving cash to clients who need real help, because it costs you their trust." Holly rejected an interpretation Irwin never intended.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter offers the multiple-choice exercise on "be more affectionate" (lines 1231-1241) directly. A spouse says it; the receiver auto-fills "she wants me to initiate sex more often." The chapter offers (a) hold hands in public, (b) pitch in more around the house, (c) be more playful and cuddly, (d) tell me you love me at least once a decade. The chapter's punchline: "the correct answer? You won't know until you talk about it, and you won't talk about it if you assume you already know." That is the move in two sentences. In Paul and Monisha's conversation at the end of the chapter, Paul models the move: when Monisha says mid-level managers feel "disempowered and out of the loop," Paul does not nod; he asks "what does it mean that they're feeling disempowered and out of the loop?"

Stone and Heen name the soup-can label image, the high-definition movie that lives in the giver's head but does not travel with the words, and the explicit two-step move (notice the label, resist filling in). The skill installs the surface-form pattern recognition that opens the door to the deeper understanding moves later in the chapter.
