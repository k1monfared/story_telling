## When to reach for this

You received feedback and your emotional reaction is dramatically larger or smaller than the feedback's content seems to warrant. A colleague who received the same feedback appears barely affected, or is devastated while you are unfazed. You find yourself judging your reaction as too sensitive, too brittle, or too dismissive. You believe your distressed interpretation of the feedback is the accurate one.

Also reach for this before entering a high-stakes feedback conversation, as preparation for what your nervous system is likely to do.

## The move

Name your profile on three dimensions. The point is not to diagnose yourself medically but to describe how your nervous system tends to respond so you can stop attributing your reaction entirely to the content of the feedback.

**Baseline**: Your default level of contentment or well-being, the level you return to over time after both positive and negative events. People with higher baselines get a stronger positive lift from good feedback and find negative feedback less emotionally sticky. People with lower baselines get a smaller boost from positive feedback and are hit harder by negative feedback. Ask: "Across my life, would people describe me as typically upbeat or typically more subdued?" The chapter's example: Alita's positive patient ratings give her only a brief lift; the critical comments land hard and stick.

**Swing**: How far you move emotionally from your baseline when feedback arrives, whether the input is positive or negative. High-swing people experience a strong physiological jolt even from comparatively minor inputs; low-swing people remain in a narrower emotional band. Ask: "When I receive unexpected negative feedback, do I tend to have an immediate, intense emotional reaction, or do I stay relatively even?" The chapter's example: Eliza is frantic from the same client message that Jeron reads as merely "a bit more work."

**Sustain and recovery**: How long you ride a positive boost and how quickly you return to baseline after being brought low. These operate independently. A fast recoverer from negative feedback is back to baseline quickly; a slow recoverer is still processing weeks later. A high-sustainer of positive feedback rides the boost for hours; a low-sustainer loses it in minutes. Ask: "After difficult feedback, how many days before I stop replaying the conversation? After a strong compliment, how long before the glow fades?" The chapter notes that recovery time differs by up to 3,000 percent across individuals.

**What to do with the profile.** Once you have named your profile, you have two uses for it.

First, calibrate how much of your current emotional reaction belongs to the feedback's content and how much is your nervous system doing what it was built to do. Alita's devastation at "sometimes runs late" is real, but a significant fraction of it is Alita's baseline-and-swing profile operating as designed, not evidence that the feedback is as catastrophic as it feels.

Second, manage your sustain deliberately. The chapter notes that you can extend positive sustain by recalling positive feedback during a low, deliberately replaying genuine praise to counterbalance a strong negative swing. This works because the same circuits that fire during the original positive experience fire during vivid recall.

**The liberation framing.** The chapter is explicit that wiring is not destiny and is not blame-worthy. It is also not fixed: meditation, exercise, and deliberate practice can raise baseline over time. What matters immediately is that labeling a strong reaction as "this is how I'm built" is more accurate than labeling it as "this is how terrible the feedback must be." Your reaction is not evidence about the feedback's content.

## Signal it landed

You can name your approximate profile (e.g., "moderate baseline, high swing, slow recovery"). When you notice a strong emotional reaction, you can say "part of this is my wiring" in the same sentence as "let me also take the feedback seriously." The two clauses are no longer contradictory.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "am I treating my current emotional state as the accurate read of what the feedback means?" If yes, and the reaction feels extremely strong, the wiring profile is likely shaping the story more than the content warrants.

After: ask "can I separate 'what they actually said' from 'how I happen to experience that kind of message'?" If yes, the wiring profile has been named and does not dominate the interpretation.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the wiring profile, people whose nervous systems respond strongly to negative feedback consistently over-weight the feedback's severity, while people whose nervous systems respond weakly consistently under-weight it. Alita interprets her devastation as evidence that the feedback is catastrophic; it is actually evidence that her sustain-recovery profile makes criticism sticky. Krista interprets her equanimity as evidence that the feedback is not important; it is actually evidence that her fast-recovery profile makes criticism slippery. Both are making the same error: using their emotional reaction as an accurate thermometer of the feedback's content rather than as a report on their nervous system. The chapter's framing: Alita's reactions are "not due to a lack of courage or surplus of self-pity" and Krista's are not evidence of genuine imperviousness. "Your reactions are not due to a lack of courage."

## Worked micro-example

Krista and Alita receive structurally similar feedback in analogous professional situations. Krista: her patients honk in support; the negatives she misses. Alita: her patients give glowing reviews; the negative comments about wait times render the entire survey toxic and sit unopened for two months. Same category of feedback, radically different emotional response. The chapter uses both cases to illustrate that the difference is not in the quality of the feedback or the character of the receiver; it is in their wiring profiles.
