## When to reach for this

You received feedback and your story about what it means is escalating past what the feedback literally said. You notice thoughts like "I can't do anything right" or "this is how it has always been" or "this will never get better" or "everyone on the team thinks this about me." The feedback was specific and contained, but your story about it is not. Or you find yourself replaying every past failure since childhood in response to a comment about one recent meeting.

## The move

Name which of the three distortion patterns is running. The chapter's framing: "Strong feelings push us toward extreme interpretations." Naming the pattern does not fix it (the remedies are in the next chapter), but you cannot dismantle what you have not identified. The three patterns:

**Google bias (the past is being distorted).** Upsetting feedback triggers a search of your memory that returns only the confirming evidence: every past failure, every bad choice, every thing you regret. You feel as though your entire history is a record of incompetence or failure, even though nothing new has happened to that history. The chapter's formulation: "When you feel lousy about yourself, you are effectively Googling 'Things that are wrong with me.'" Signal of this pattern: you are thinking about past events that have no direct connection to the specific feedback you just received. The current feedback is an occasion for a search, and the search is returning results across all of your history rather than being contained to the relevant domain.

**Flooding (the present is being distorted).** Negative feedback on one thing overflows its container and floods into your general self-image and your read of all your relationships. "I sing off key" becomes "I can't do anything right." "My colleague raised a concern" becomes "everyone on the team hates me." Specific and partial feedback becomes total and universal. Signal of this pattern: the scope of your story has expanded beyond the domain of the feedback. The feedback was about one skill, one interaction, one project, and your story is now about who you fundamentally are or how everyone sees you.

**Forever bias and snowballing (the future is being distorted).** A current bad feeling is projected forward as permanent: you feel humiliated now, so you will always feel humiliated. A specific piece of negative feedback snowballs into a chain of disasters: "I had mayonnaise on my cheek during the date" becomes "I will die alone." Signal of this pattern: you are narrating a sequence of future events, each one following from the last, that did not happen and that the feedback did not predict. Or you believe your current emotional state will continue indefinitely. The chapter: "When we feel bad, we assume we will always feel bad."

Once you have named the pattern, note it plainly: "I'm Google-biasing," or "I'm flooding the scope," or "I'm snowballing." This creates enough distance between you and the story to allow the next step.

## Signal it landed

You can name the pattern in a single phrase. The story no longer feels like an accurate read of the feedback; it feels like a recognizable distortion type that your nervous system is running. The feedback is still visible underneath the distortion: you can say what the feedback actually said, separately from what your story made of it.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "is my story about the feedback larger in scope, darker in history, or more dire about the future than the feedback itself?" If yes, at least one distortion type is running. If you are unsure, restate the feedback in its original words and compare it to your current story.

After: ask "which pattern is distorting which time frame?" If you can name one of the three (past, present, or future) and label it, the diagnostic has landed. Note that two or all three patterns can run simultaneously, especially when the wiring profile involves high swing and slow recovery.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming the distortion type, the distorted story continues to feel like the accurate interpretation of the feedback. The chapter is explicit: "What's so amazing about these distortions is how real they appear to us in the moment." The gap between thoughts and reality does not make itself visible; you are inside the story, not observing it. A person in the grip of the Google bias genuinely cannot remember anything they've ever done right. A person flooding cannot hold onto the fact that the feedback was about one specific behavior, not their entire character. A person snowballing feels that their catastrophic projection is calm analysis. Naming the type does not make the emotion stop, but it creates the vantage point from which it can eventually be dismantled.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter uses Marc's account of the Google bias: "The feedback could be small, but if I'm feeling vulnerable, it's as if I fall through the floor, plunged into the basement where all the things I've ever regretted are collected... I feel guilty about the people I've hurt and ashamed of the selfish things I've done. When I'm not in the basement I literally don't think about it. But when I'm there, it's the only reality." Marc's report shows the diagnostic signal clearly: the current feedback is small; the memory search it triggers is the entire ledger of his past. The pattern is Google bias. The chapter also offers the flooding description (singing off key becoming "I can't do anything right") and the snowballing chain ("I had mayonnaise on my cheek during the date" to "I will die alone") as two additional named examples.
