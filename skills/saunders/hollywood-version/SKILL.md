## When to reach for this

You are about to discuss a draft in a workshop, give an editor's notes, or revise your own piece. The conversation is heading toward suggestions: cut this, expand this, change that. You can feel that the people in the room hold different ideas of what story this is. Reach for this before any change is proposed.

## The move

1. Demand a one-sentence summary. Ask, "What story does this story appear to want to be?" Get someone to answer in one or two sentences. Saunders calls this the Hollywood version.
2. Treat the first answer as a ranging shot, not a verdict. It is the opening artillery round, deliberately broad.
3. Iterate. Add one piece of specificity per pass. Each pass narrows by ruling something out. "An unhappy woman is going somewhere in a cart" becomes "A schoolteacher, unhappy because she's been teaching too long, is on her way home." Each addition is a fact the next pass cannot contradict.
4. Stop iterating when an additional specification no longer changes what the story would do next. At that point you have the Hollywood version.
5. Use the result as the standard against which suggestions are measured. A proposed change either serves the story this story appears to want to be, or it proposes a different story (which is fine, but must be named as such).

## Signal it landed

The room (or you, alone) can finish the sentence "This is the story of ___" with a single specific clause that everyone present accepts as the working description. Subsequent suggestions can be evaluated by asking whether they serve that description or replace it.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I made the changes I am about to propose, would I know whether the story is still the same story?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer flips from "I'd be tinkering" to "I'd be either keeping it the same story (and these changes serve that) or proposing it become a different story (and these changes substitute one for another) — and I can tell which."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, critique converges on a Frankenstein draft. Each reader, holding a different unspoken Hollywood version in their head, suggests changes that would serve their version. The writer, trying to honor everyone, ends up with a draft that is consistent with no story at all. Saunders frames the rule plainly: you would not start altering a complicated machine in your yard before you had some idea of its intended function. The same applies to a draft.

## Worked micro-example

In the chapter, Saunders walks through the Hollywood version of "In the Cart" as it would emerge in his own classroom, mid-read at the appearance of Hanov. The first ranging shot is "An unhappy woman is going somewhere in a cart." Successive passes add "schoolteacher," "unhappy because she's been teaching too long," "alone in the world, teaching only out of necessity," and finally the meet-cute formulation "Lonely woman encounters possible lover." Each pass rules something out: she is not unhappy because of love, not unhappy because of grief, and so on. By the time the sentence is "Lonely woman encounters possible lover," everyone in the room can predict what kinds of next moves would be on-story (some development between Marya and Hanov) and which would be off-story (a spaceship abducts Semyon).
