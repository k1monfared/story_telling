## When to reach for this

You are sitting down to critique a draft, your own or someone else's, alone or in a workshop. The room (or your own notes) is about to start producing comments. You can already feel the conversation trending toward interpretation (what the story means, what a scene symbolizes), or toward proposed changes that assume a target nobody has named, or toward defending scenes because they read pleasantly in isolation. Reach for this when you need a workflow that turns the diffuse energy of "responding to a draft" into a stack of decisions a writer could apply to the page on Monday, in the order the decisions need to be made.

## The move

1. **Re-pose interpretive talk as strategic.** Apply `ask-strategic-not-interpretive` to every comment on the table. Replace "what does X mean" with "what is X doing for the reader" or "why do we need X." Direct each strategic question at a specific element on the page, named by location. A comment that cannot be re-posed strategically is a comment the writer cannot act on; mark it so the discussion knows to set it aside or convert it. The output of this step is a list of strategic questions, each pointing at a specific element of the draft.

2. **Establish the Hollywood version before any change is proposed.** Apply `hollywood-version`. Demand a one-sentence summary of the story this draft appears to want to be, narrowed by successive ranging shots until further specification would not change what the story would do next. The Hollywood version is the standard against which every proposed change will be judged. This step must come before step 3 because the Cornfeld test in step 3 depends on a destination for "advances the story." Without the Hollywood version, "advances the story" has no referent and the audit collapses into taste.

3. **Run the Cornfeld test on each structural unit the strategic questions named.** Apply `cornfeld-principle` to every unit (scene, chapter, section, digression) that step 1 surfaced as potentially in question. For each unit, finish two sentences. "This unit is entertaining because ___." "This unit advances the story by ___." Where "the story" is the Hollywood version named in step 2. A unit that fails either test is marked for cutting, replacing, or rewriting. A unit that passes both is provisionally kept but is now eligible for step 4 if it still feels almost-good.

4. **Element-audit any unit that passes Cornfeld but still feels almost-good.** Apply `take-responsibility-for-every-element` at scope. For the suspect unit, inventory the elements present (background figures, weather, side gestures, the contents of a glance, which side of a tree someone walks past) and finish "this is here because ___" for each. Elements that stall on the test are not yet the writer's choice. Decide for each: commit to a function, sharpen the detail, or remove it. This step runs at finer scope than Cornfeld, on units that survived Cornfeld but where the dilution lives at element scope rather than at unit scope.

The chain's order is intrinsic. Step 1 is the disposition that makes the rest of the discussion actionable. Step 2 supplies the destination that makes step 3 testable. Step 3 surfaces the units worth auditing in step 4. Doing them out of order produces specific failures (see Failure mode this prevents).

## Signal it landed

The session ends with three artifacts. (a) A list of strategic questions, each tied to a specific element on the page, every comment on the table convertible to one. (b) A one-sentence Hollywood version that everyone in the room (or you, alone) accepts as the working description of the story this draft wants to be. (c) For each structural unit named in step 1, a Cornfeld verdict (entertaining? advances the story?) and an action (keep, rewrite, replace, cut). For any unit that passed Cornfeld but felt almost-good, a list of its elements paired with what each is doing for the reader, and at least one element marked for cutting, sharpening, or moving. The writer leaves with a stack of revision decisions, in priority order, that they could apply to the manuscript without a translation step.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of the draft on the table: "If I made the changes the room is about to suggest, would I know whether the story is still the same story, and which suggestions to act on first?" Note your answer. The answer is likely "I'd be tinkering" or "the changes pull in different directions."

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer flips to "yes — the Hollywood version tells me which changes serve the story this story wants to be; the strategic questions tell me which elements are in question; the Cornfeld verdicts tell me which units to keep, cut, or rewrite; and the element audit tells me what to do inside the units worth keeping."

## Failure mode this prevents

Out-of-order failures are specific.

**Skipping step 1 (no strategic re-posing):** the conversation produces ever more sophisticated readings of what the story means while the writer leaves with nothing to do. Symbolism talk is especially seductive because it sounds like analysis, but it does not tell you whether the second return to the village is earning its place. The story gets understood and stays unrevised.

**Skipping step 2 (no Hollywood version):** every reader holds a different unspoken target story in their head and proposes changes that would serve their version. The writer, trying to honor everyone, ends up with a draft consistent with no story at all — Saunders's Frankenstein-draft failure.

**Skipping step 3 (no Cornfeld test):** suggestions are made and accepted by feel, and the writer has no audit for which units survived because they earn their place versus which survived because they read pleasantly in isolation. The dog runs in and out, the ray of sunlight passes through the window, plates break, all in the manner of a real scene, none of it doing the work of a story.

**Skipping step 4 when needed:** a unit passes Cornfeld but the polish-level dilution remains. The unit feels almost-good, "well-written" in a way the writer cannot improve, because the part they would need to fix is invisible to them.

**Doing the steps out of order:** step 3 without step 2 produces verdicts against an unspoken destination that may differ across readers. Step 4 without step 3 polishes the elements of scenes that should not survive. Step 2 without step 1 produces a Hollywood version that the room argues over interpretively rather than testing strategically.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders does not walk all four steps on a single draft in one chapter. He demonstrates step 1 in "We Begin" (the strategic-vs-academic reframing), steps 2 and 3 together in "A Page at a Time" (the Hollywood version of "In the Cart" emerging in the classroom mid-read at Hanov's appearance, then the Cornfeld test on the teahouse scene), and step 4 in "Afterthought #1" (the element-by-element audit of De Sica's *Bicycle Thieves* sequence). The composition of the four into one workflow is implicit in his teaching practice: by the time he has demonstrated all four, his classroom has been running them as a stack on every draft they discuss.
