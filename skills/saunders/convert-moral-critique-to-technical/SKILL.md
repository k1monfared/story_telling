## When to reach for this

A story has been called sexist, racist, classist, homophobic, transphobic, pedantic, appropriative, or derivative. Maybe a student said it, or a workshop, or a reviewer, or your own quiet reading. The accusation is general. As long as it stays general, two bad things happen: defenders dismiss it, accusers escalate, and nobody fixes anything. Reach for this whenever a moral concern about a story is real enough to take seriously and abstract enough that you cannot point to a paragraph and say what to change.

## The move

1. Refuse both the verdict and the dismissal. The accusation is not the answer, but it is data. Treat it as a noticed effect on a reader whose source in the prose has not yet been located.
2. Ask "Where, exactly?" Get specific citations. If the accuser cannot supply them, ask them to re-read with the question in hand. Two or three concrete passages are enough.
3. For each citation, name the technical maneuver that produced the moral effect. The most common shape is inequitable narration: one character was granted a narrative resource that another, at a parallel moment, was denied. Resources to check: interior monologue, omniscient access to thought, time on the page, narrator's investment in the character's inner life, freedom from authorial joke at their expense, full rendering of pivotal scenes (homecoming, death, the moment of decision).
4. Audit parallel moments. Find the structural twins in the story (both die, both come home, both face the test, both react to the same event) and lay them next to each other. The technical inequity is now nameable as a sentence of the form: "At moment X, character Y is denied [narrative resource Z] that character W received at parallel moment X'."
5. Follow the inequity downstream. If a saved or transformed character is followed past the pivotal event, check whether the prose grants them the interior life to register the event in subsequent action. A saved person who never thinks of the saver, never reflects on the sacrifice, walks off unchanged is the strongest tell that the inequity is structural, not local.
6. Write the corrected version. Imagine and draft the rebalanced passage. Grant the denied character the interior monologue at the parallel moment. Render their death with the same omniscient lens. Skip the narrator's joke at their expense. Hold the corrected version next to the original and confirm the change is, in fact, an improvement (two-version-comparison handles this verification step).
7. State the fix as a craft instruction, not a moral grade. The accusation "the story is X-ist" becomes "at moments A, B, C, character Y needs the same narrative resources character W got at parallel moments." The first is unactionable; the second is a revision plan.

## Signal it landed

You can produce a sentence of the form "In (at least) N places, character Y is denied [resource Z] that character W received at parallel moment(s)." You can name the parallel moments. You can describe what the corrected version would look like. The moral charge has not gone away, but it has moved into a form the writer can act on, the critic can verify, and the next reader can check.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, write the moral concern as a single sentence ("the story is X-ist toward Y characters"). Try to derive a revision instruction from it. Notice that you cannot, or that the instruction you derive ("be less X-ist") cannot be applied to a specific paragraph.

After applying, write the converted observation. Try to derive a revision instruction from that. The skill landed if the new instruction names a specific moment, a specific resource, and a specific imbalance the writer could address in one sitting.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this conversion, two failures replace each other indefinitely. The writer or defender treats the moral accusation as something to argue against on its own terms ("I didn't mean it that way," "you're reading too much in"), and the prose stays exactly as it was, because no specific change has been proposed. The accuser, frustrated, escalates the moral framing, which is now even less actionable. Saunders's diagnosis is direct: the moral concern is real, and it is also incomplete; the prose is doing something specific to produce it, and that something is fixable. The conversion does not soften the critique; it sharpens it into a form the writer cannot wave away and the critic does not have to keep restating in louder versions of the same sentence. A reader's "this is sexist" and a writer's "I didn't mean to be" are both true and both useless. "Here, here, and here, the woman gets a narrator joke where the man gets a thought" is the version both can use.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders is teaching Gogol's "Nevsky Prospect" and a student says she does not like it because it is sexist. Saunders asks where. The student locates two passages: one where a man is insulted, where Gogol drops into the character's head and we hear his interior response; one where a woman is insulted, where the third-person narrator steps in and makes a joke at her expense. Saunders then asks the class to imagine the story if Gogol had distributed interior monologue equally. There is a pause and what Saunders calls a collective sigh/smile as the class sees, at once, the version that would have been "just as dark and strange, but funnier and more honest." The accusation has been converted: from "the story is sexist" to "the story has inequitable narration at the moments of insult." The first is a verdict; the second is a revision instruction Gogol could have followed.

The same procedure applied to "Master and Man": the floating accusation "Tolstoy is exhibiting class bias toward Nikita" gets located. Section VI gives Vasili an extended pass through his coping methods (cigarettes, disparagement, bragging, money, victorious thinking) failing in the freezing storm. Section VII gives Nikita the parallel pass: less interesting, less detailed, his fears soothed by the mere thought of God. Section IX closes Vasili's death with a long interior monologue. Section X gives Nikita's twenty later years and his death in bare facts ("He died at home as he had wished... under the icons with a lighted taper in his hands"), with the explicit deferral "we'll have to wait for our own deaths to see whether Nikita was disappointed or found there what he expected." The downstream consequence: Nikita, twenty years later, never reflects on the sacrifice, never asks why his master did that, walks off as if the sled night never happened. The conversion: "in at least two places — Nikita's homecoming and the two death scenes — Nikita is denied the interiority Tolstoy gave Vasili at parallel moments." The corrective exercise Saunders sets is: rewrite Section X with Nikita granted narrative resources equal to Vasili's. Write it like Tolstoy. Use, you know, a lot of facts.
