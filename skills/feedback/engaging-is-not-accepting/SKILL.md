## When to reach for this

You are about to do one of two things and feel they are your only options. Option A: take the feedback because rejecting it would prove you cannot handle criticism. Option B: dismiss the feedback because if you engage with it you will end up agreeing to something you do not actually agree with. The pressure of the binary is what to notice. Reach for this before you commit to either.

## The move

1. Separate two questions that have collapsed into one. Question 1: am I willing to engage this conversation skillfully (listen, understand, ask, consider)? Question 2: do I intend to use this feedback to change?
2. Commit to question 1 independent of your answer to question 2. Engaging fully does not commit you to taking the advice; it commits you only to understanding it.
3. Inside the engagement, do the work: hear out what is being said, surface what you do not understand, look for what might be true even if the headline is off, name what you see differently.
4. Only after the engagement decide question 2: take the feedback in full, take part of it, set it aside, or decline it. "I have understood, and I am choosing not to use this" is a legitimate landing.
5. If the answer to question 2 is no, you may still owe a response (acknowledgment, your view, a boundary). Engagement-with-decline is not silent dismissal.

## Signal it landed

You can describe the feedback fairly enough that the giver would say "yes, that's what I meant" before you say what you intend to do with it. Your decision to use or not use the feedback is made on the merits, not driven by the felt need to prove you are open or to prove you have backbone.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "Am I treating disagreeing with this feedback and refusing to hear it as the same thing? Am I treating hearing it out and accepting it as the same thing?" Note where the collapse is happening.

After applying, ask the same questions. The skill landed if the two questions are now visibly separate and you can name what you would do with each independently.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the separation, two failure modes alternate. In the first, you treat engagement as commitment, so to protect your judgment you refuse the conversation, dismiss the giver, or argue everything down. You skip the part where some of it might have been useful, and you leave the relationship strained. In the second, you treat refusal as defensiveness, so you accept feedback you do not believe, change behavior on inputs you have not actually weighed, and store resentment that surfaces later as withdrawal or counter-attack. The binary makes both failure modes feel like virtue.

## Worked micro-example

The book opens the "Benefits of Receiving Well" section by spelling out the disambiguation directly. Receiving feedback well does not mean you have to take it. It means engaging skillfully and making thoughtful choices about whether and how to use what you are hearing, including, where appropriate, setting a boundary and saying no (the boundaries chapter, chapter 10, is where the no gets its own treatment). The intro plants the disambiguation so the rest of the book can teach engagement skills without the reader hearing them as instructions to capitulate.

The skill installs that disambiguation as a moment-of-decision move.
