## When to reach for this

Someone has just offered you a craft model: a rule about openings, a theory of plot, a structural template, an aphorism about voice, a workshop slogan. You feel pulled to argue for or against it, or to adopt it because the source is trusted. Reach for this before you commit to or reject the model on grounds of correctness.

## The move

1. Name the model in one sentence so its claim is on the table.
2. Name the state of mind you are actually trying to reach: the mode in which you could write the kind of story you want to write. This is "the moon."
3. Ask the criterion question: "Is this model helping me get there?" Not: "Is it correct?" Not: "Is it widely accepted?" Not: "Did a great writer say it?"
4. Try the model on a real piece of work, your own or someone else's, for a bounded period. Watch what happens to the work and to the state of mind you bring to the page.
5. Decide: keep it (it is helping), discard it (it is not), or hold it loosely (sometimes helps, sometimes does not, and you can name the conditions of each).
6. If you keep it, say what it is helping you do. If you discard it, do so without arguing it is wrong, only that it is not helping you here.

## Signal it landed

For every craft model you currently hold, you can finish the sentence "This is helping me ___" with a specific effect on a specific kind of writing problem. Models that fail this test are gone, downgraded, or scoped to the situation in which they actually work. You no longer hold any model purely because it is correct or prestigious.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, list the craft rules and theories you currently hold and feel attached to. For each, ask: "Why am I holding this?" Note any that you cannot connect to a concrete effect on your writing.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if any model has moved from the "true and prestigious" column to the "actively helping me with X" column or has been let go.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this test, craft theory accumulates into a museum. You collect models because they are taught, because authoritative writers endorse them, or because they sound right, and you defend them as correct. Two hazards follow. First, you spend energy on theological arguments about which model is true rather than on the work the model was supposed to serve. Second, you mistake the finger for the moon: you confuse fluency with the model for the state of mind that produced the stories you actually love. The signal you are in this failure mode is that your craft talk gets richer while your drafts do not.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders frames every model he will offer in the rest of the book as a "rhetorical trial balloon" and invokes the Buddhist image of "a finger pointing at the moon": the finger is not the moon, and a teaching is to be judged not by whether it is right but by whether it directs you toward enlightenment, here defined as the state of mind from which you could write a story like the ones you love. He explicitly tells the reader: if a model appeals to you, use it; if not, discard it. The criterion is "Is it helping?"

## Source

Chapter "We Begin," lines 188-278 (especially 271). Saunders sets up the disposition with which to receive every craft model in the book that follows, including the ones he himself proposes, by naming the goal as a state of mind and the test as utility, not correctness.
