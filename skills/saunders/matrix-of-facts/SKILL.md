## When to reach for this

You are drafting or revising prose and want the reader to believe something about a character (that he is cheerful, that she is honestly convinced, that the marriage has gone cold, that the host means it) without writing a sentence that asks for that belief on the writer's authority. Or you have a passage of prose that reads as elevated, poetic, philosophical, or aphoristic, and you can feel that the reader is reading it skeptically: the prose has slipped from showing into telling, and the telling is being noticed. Reach for this whenever a sentence is asking the reader for trust the prose has not yet earned.

## The move

1. On the page in front of you, do the color-coding exercise. Mark in one color every sentence (or clause) that reports a concrete fact: an action, a gesture, an object, a sense impression, a quoted speech, a measurable detail. Mark in another color every sentence that asserts a judgment, a feeling, an opinion, a generality, an aphorism, or any interior state described abstractly.
2. Read the proportions. If the page is mostly the second color, you have a register problem: the reader is being asked to accept assertions on your authority. Aim for a page that is mostly the first color, where any second-color sentence sits in a matrix of factual ones.
3. For each second-color sentence you want to keep, rewrite it into the syntax of the first color. The form is short, plain, declarative, free of qualifier and metaphor. Saunders's example: Tolstoy does not write "Vasili was a hypocrite who deceived himself"; he writes "honestly convinced that he was Nikita's benefactor" — a fact about Vasili's mind, in the same factual idiom as "the bridle is leather and tasseled."
4. For each scene's basic action ("woman carries samovar to table"), embroider with concrete factual additions that make the same action more particular: the apron flick at the boiling top, the effort of the carry, the lift before the set-down, the thud. Each addition is a fact, not a judgment. The cumulative effect is a more particular world without a more abstract narrator.
5. When a character's interior must be reported, render the thought "succinctly and precisely, using simple objective sentences that seem factual in their syntax and modesty of assertion." A thought becomes another fact about the character, observable in the same way the bridle is observable. Avoid the lyrical interiority that announces itself as poetry.

## Signal it landed

The reader's belief in your characterizations no longer depends on the reader's trust in your authority. You can point to any subjective claim on the page (he is cheerful, she is honestly convinced, the host means it) and show the matrix of factual sentences around it that produced the involuntary buy-in. A reader stopped at any sentence cannot easily tell whether the sentence reports an external fact or an internal state, because the syntax is the same.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, pick a paragraph and ask: "How many sentences here would a stranger, reading this paragraph cold, classify as 'the writer said so' versus 'the world said so'?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the count of "world said so" sentences has gone up and the few "writer said so" sentences left have been recast in the same factual syntax as the world-sentences around them.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this register, prose drifts into one of two failure modes. The first is the editorialized version: the narrator names emotions and judgments directly (he was furious, she was a hypocrite, the situation was unbearable), and the reader, sensing the prose is asserting rather than showing, holds it at arm's length. The second is the lyrical version: prose reaches for elevated metaphor or aphorism to do the work of characterization (his rage was the rage of a thwarted king), and the reader, sensing the writer's hand on the wheel, stops believing in the world. Both modes break the involuntary buy-in that comes from factual statement. Saunders's diagnosis: the page becomes a referendum on the writer rather than an encounter with the world the writer is reporting from.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders contrasts a generic version, "The maid carried the samovar to the table," with Tolstoy's version, "After flicking with her apron the top of the samovar which was now boiling over, she carried it with an effort to the table, raised it, and set it down with a thud." Every addition is a concrete factual detail: the apron flick, the boiling top, the effort, the lift before the set-down, the thud. None is a judgment about the maid. Yet by the time the sentence ends Saunders, as reader, is supplying details he was never given (her red cheeks, the sweat-stained underarms, the strand of hair) — produced by his reading mind in response to facts, not in response to assertion. The same procedure applies to interior facts: Vasili is "honestly convinced that he was Nikita's benefactor," not described as a self-deceiving hypocrite. The hypocrisy is felt by the reader because the omniscient narrator has, a moment earlier, factually reported that Vasili routinely cheats Nikita; the gap between the two factual reports does the work an aphoristic sentence would have ruined.
