## When to reach for this

You catch yourself declining help that was sincere, deflecting affection, or refusing to share a difficulty even with someone you trust. Reach for this when the boundary is doing more protecting than the situation actually warrants.

## The move

1. Notice the reflex to wall off. Someone offers support and you say "I'm fine"; someone reaches for closeness and you create distance; someone wants to know how you're doing and you give a surface answer. Notice without judgment.
2. Distinguish the protective function from the cost. The wall did real work once; it kept you safe in conditions that warranted it. The cost now is that it also blocks what you need.
3. Pick low-stakes situations to practice. Don't start with the highest-stakes relationship. Practice receiving help with the friend who offers, accepting a compliment without deflecting, sharing a small worry instead of containing it.
4. Stay with the discomfort of being seen. Receiving feels exposing if your nervous system is used to walling off. The discomfort isn't a signal to retreat; it's the sign that you're doing the new thing.
5. Notice when you're under-giving in the output direction too. Rigid boundaries also block what you could offer to others — a vulnerability you could share, a compliment you held back, a request that would deepen the relationship.
6. Build slowly. The wall can come down brick by brick. It doesn't need to come down all at once; it just needs to start.

## Signal it landed

You can let a partner help you when you're struggling without immediately deflecting. You share things you used to handle alone. People around you experience you as more reachable. The relationships have more two-way traffic.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without softening, partners experience you as opaque — they can be near you for years without ever feeling they really know what you're carrying. They eventually conclude either that you don't trust them or that there isn't much there to know. Both are wrong, but both feel true to them, and both produce distance.

## Reflection prompts

- In what ways do you experience your boundaries as rigid — blocking, restraining, or both?
- Where do you under-give in relationships — withholding what you could offer? What beliefs play into this?
- Where do you under-receive — refusing what's offered? What beliefs play into this?
- Do you need to focus more on softening the rigidity, whether on the input or output? How can you do this?
