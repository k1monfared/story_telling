## When to reach for this

You have received feedback and your emotional reaction is running strong. You cannot tell whether your distressed interpretation of the feedback is accurate or distorted. You find yourself unable to say, plainly, what the feedback actually said: your sense of what it means has already overwritten your memory of what was actually said.

Also reach for this when you notice that a small piece of recent feedback has triggered a disproportionately strong reaction, and you suspect the reaction belongs partly to older, unhealed material.

## The move

Ask yourself three questions in sequence and write down the answer to each before moving to the next.

**What do I feel?** Name the emotion as precisely as possible: anxiety, shame, anger, sadness, surprise, humiliation, embarrassment. Then describe how it feels physically, not metaphorically. Seth's example: "I feel a jolt of adrenaline that is by now very familiar to me. It's what I imagine an electric shock feels like. And then I often feel sick to my stomach and slightly faint." The physical description anchors the feeling without amplifying it and keeps it from merging with the story.

**What's the story I'm telling, and inside the story, what's the threat?** Listen to your story about what the feedback means without yet evaluating whether it's true or reasonable. The chapter: "Pay special attention to the threat." Common threats include: a bad thing that might happen as a result (I will lose this job), how others now see you (everyone knows I'm incompetent), or how you must now see yourself (I have always been a mess). Seth's account: "I thought, 'I knew it!' and then my thoughts snowballed: 'This job is the best opportunity I'll ever have and I've messed it up. I mess up everything.' So there are a few threats in there: that my boss disapproves of me, that I will lose my job, that I won't be able to live with myself."

**What's the actual feedback?** Peel back the story and ask only: what was said? What were the exact words? Resist embellishing or inferring. Seth's feedbeck was his boss's single comment about "everyone" being disorganized, including Seth. Everything else in Seth's story (assumptions about the boss's attitude, fears about losing his job, self-loathing) was Seth's addition.

**Check for shadowboxing with the past.** Once the three strands are visible, ask: is the current threat connected to a longer story? Sometimes a small piece of feedback is the last straw in a series of accumulated comments, or it aggravates a wound that never fully healed. The chapter's example: a neighbor's lawn comment triggers a disproportionate reaction because it is the latest chapter in a lifelong story about social obliviousness. A colleague's suggestion to speak more authoritatively detonates old material from childhood bullying and a breakup. Recognizing that the current feedback has activated older material does not make the older material less real. But it separates what belongs to this feedback from what belongs to older fights.

**Assess alignment.** Once the strands are separated, ask whether your story is reasonably aligned with the actual feedback, or whether and how it is distorted. The goal is not to dismiss the emotion or the story, but to see how each element is affecting the others, and to distinguish what was added from what was said.

## Signal it landed

You can write three separate answers, in distinct sentences, for each of the three questions. The answer to "what was the actual feedback" is shorter and more contained than the answer to "what's the story." The threat is named explicitly rather than hovering vaguely. You can identify at least one thing your story added that the feedback did not say.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "if I had to write down exactly what was said in one sentence, what would I write?" If you cannot do this without the sentence ballooning into your story about what it must mean, the strands are merged and need separating.

After: ask "what did the feedback actually say, separately from what I told myself it must mean?" If you can answer in plain words without the story re-entering, the strands have been separated.

## Failure mode this prevents

When feeling, story, and feedback are merged, the distorted story substitutes for the feedback in your memory and your evaluation. You process your story as if it were the content of the feedback, and your learning is based on what you told yourself rather than what was said. The chapter: "Our mind takes what was said and immediately tells a story." The longer the story runs unchecked, the more difficult it is to find the actual feedback underneath it. Shadowboxing with the past compounds this: the older material fills in the story's threat content with material the current feedback did not generate and cannot address.

## Worked micro-example

Seth's boss made one comment, with humor, in a meeting. Seth's merged response: nausea, shame, despair, certainty that his boss has disapproved of him all along, conviction that he would lose his job and fail at everything, decision to cancel a flight to a meaningful family celebration. After separating the strands: one offhand comment (actual feedback), fear of disapproval and global incompetence (story), a strong jolt of adrenaline and nausea (feeling). In retrospect Seth cannot understand how he got from the comment to canceling the flight. That gap is the distance between actual feedback and merged story.
