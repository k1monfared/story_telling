## When to reach for this

You are writing or revising a passage that bridges two events. Something just happened in the story (a death, a decision, a discovery) and something else is going to happen later (a new relationship, a return, a confrontation). The stretch between them, in the world of the story, contains real elapsed time. You are working on prose that occupies that elapsed time and feel the writing going inert. Reach for this whenever you catch yourself rendering days, weeks, or months because they happened in the world rather than because the story has work to do during them.

## The move

1. Name the event the bridging passage starts after, and name the event it ends at. Saunders's worked example: starts after Kukin's funeral, ends at "Three months later Olenka was returning from Mass" with Vasily about to walk in.
2. List, in one or two short phrases per item, what the bridging passage is currently doing in the prose. Mourning rituals, household tasks, small observations, seasonal description, minor character notes. Do not soften the list; write it flat.
3. For each item, ask: is this item delivering meaning the story is built to carry, or is it merely covering elapsed time because the time elapsed? The criterion is the story's standard, not the world's. Saunders is direct: "By whose standard? The story's." A scene where Olenka finds Kukin's spare glasses and weeps may be true to bereavement but not meaningful to a story that is about her trait of becoming-whatever-she-loves; the trait is in stasis during mourning, so the days are not the story's days.
4. For items that fail the test, cut. Replace the passage with a single time-stamped sentence that lands the reader in the next meaningful moment. Saunders's instance: the entire post-funeral interval reduces to "Three months later Olenka was returning from Mass." The reader registers the elapsed time and arrives where the story has work to do.
5. For items that pass the test, keep, but on the same justification you would apply to any scene: name what work this item does for the story, not what it shows about the world.
6. Re-read the bridge. The leap should feel decisive, not negligent. The prose around the leap should signal, by its specificity on either side, that the writer has chosen the leap on purpose. Saunders's framing: a short story is "a radically shaped, even somewhat cartoonish (when held up against the tedious real world) little machine that thrills us with the extremity of its decisiveness." The thrill comes from the decisiveness; bridging filler erodes it.

## Signal it landed

A stretch of in-world time in your draft is now covered by a single time-stamped sentence (or by silence) instead of by paragraphs of small daily detail. You can articulate, in one sentence, what the story was doing during the leaped time (in Saunders's example: "nothing meaningful, by the story's standard"), and you can articulate, in one sentence, what the moment after the leap is doing (introducing the next meaningful event). The cut prose was not bad on its sentence-level merits but was failing the story's-standard test.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of a bridging passage: "What is this passage delivering that the next meaningful event would not deliver on its own?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if you were unable to answer the first question with anything specific to the story's purpose, and the cut left the next meaningful event undamaged or sharper.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this procedure, a writer who has just delivered a real event feels obligated to honor the world's continuity. The character has just been bereaved, lost their job, won the contest, so the writer renders the days that followed because in life those days did follow. The prose accumulates calendar-tending detail: on Wednesday she did the washing, on Thursday she found a glove, by Friday she felt slightly less heavy. None of this is wrong as observation. All of it is wrong as story, on Saunders's diagnosis, because the story is not a documentary and not a fair-minded accounting of time. The reader feels the bridging passage as inert and starts skimming, and skimming becomes the reading-mode the rest of the story has to overcome. The cure is not to write better filler but to admit the days are not the story's and to leap.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders pauses at the moment Chekhov has killed Kukin and is about to introduce Vasily. He poses the writer's question: "There are so many possible paths. Which is the best?" Then he reads what Chekhov actually does, which is to skip past the entire post-funeral interval and land the reader at "Three months later Olenka was returning from Mass." Saunders proposes a counterfactual filler version (Olenka does the washing on Thursday, finds Kukin's spare glasses, bursts into tears) and asks why we do not get it. His answer is the criterion the skill operationalizes: those days do not matter by the story's standard, the story signals as much by skipping them, and the leap teaches that the short story's decisiveness about what is and is not meaningful is part of how it generates its effect on the reader.
