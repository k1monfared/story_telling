## When to reach for this

You are reading or revising and a non-event element in the story keeps drawing your attention. A setting detail, a description of a place, a flash of birds, a piece of weather, a minor digression, an image. It feels like it matters. The story is not telling you why. Reach for this whenever you sense an apparently decorative element is structurally loaded but you cannot articulate the load.

## The move

1. Identify the heart of the story (the central event the rest serves). If you have not done this, do it first.
2. Pick the suspect element. The ravine, the birds, the description of the town in its former glory, the small image at the close.
3. Hold the two side by side in mind. Not literally on the page, in mind: the suspect element on one side, the heart of the story on the other. Saunders calls this "holding up against."
4. Watch for the immediate, pre-articulate response. Either a feeling arises ("there is a relation here, and it is not random") or a non-feeling arises ("Meaningful Relation Not Found"). Saunders's emphasis: this initial response is the data, prior to articulation. Do not skip it.
5. If the response is "not random," now articulate. Try to name what relation links the two. Often it will be a binary, a structural pattern, a contrast, or a foreshadowing. The ravine cleaves the town in two; the contest pits two singers against each other; the story is full of binaries.
6. Once you have one relation, sweep for others. The cleft town, the two singers, the doleful birds vs. the spunky birds, the technical singer vs. the emotional singer, the sparrows that fly up vs. the rooks that stay grounded, the noble narrator vs. the lowly rustics. The story turns out to be organized by the pattern.
7. If the response is "Meaningful Relation Not Found," the element is a candidate for cutting in your own draft, or evidence of looseness in someone else's, depending on what you are doing.
8. Trust the immediate response over your articulation. Saunders is explicit that the mind is at its most intelligent in the instant before it explains, and that the articulation is always partial. The instant of recognition is what art is for; the articulation is the receipt.

## Signal it landed

For each suspect element you held up, you can produce a sentence of the form: "Held against the heart, this element produces the feeling that ___, and once articulated, the relation is ___." The relation points at a structural pattern (a binary, a recurrence, a contrast) that the rest of the story turns out to also be organized by, and you can list at least one other element in the story that participates in the same pattern.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, point at the suspect element and ask: "Why is this here?" Note your answer. It will likely be "atmosphere" or "good detail."

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer becomes a specific structural relation (a binary, a foreshadowed contrast, a pattern the heart resolves), and you can name one other place in the story where the same pattern appears.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, you discount apparently static elements as "atmosphere" and miss the level at which a great story does its real organizational work. The story feels mysterious in its rightness. You can feel that the ravine matters but you cannot say how, and so you cannot reverse-engineer the move for your own writing. In your own draft, the inverse failure: you preserve elements because they "felt right" without checking whether they hold up against the heart, and the draft accumulates motifs that point in inconsistent directions. Saunders names the underlying craft fact: a story's controlling sensibility lives in the meaningful relations between its non-event elements and its heart, and these relations are felt before they are explained. A reader who never holds them up never sees them. A writer who never holds them up never makes them.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders takes the ravine, an apparently static piece of geography described early in "The Singers," and holds it up against the singing contest. The first response is a feeling: there is a relation, and it is not random. He stays in that feeling for a moment, then articulates: a ravine is a thing that splits something into two; a contest is a thing that stages two against each other. The story, once that pattern is noted, reveals itself to be saturated with binaries: town's former glory vs. its present ruin, doleful rooks vs. spunky sparrows, technique vs. emotion, Yashka vs. the contractor, the comfortable narrator vs. the lowly rustics, even the closing call between the two boys across the valley. The ravine, held against the heart, unlocks the story's organization. He runs the same exercise on the burst of birds early in the story, holding them up against the two singers, and finds the contractor in the chirping sparrows and Yashka in the more doleful rooks, with the further complication that the sparrows (like Yashka) are also the ones capable of brief ascension. He notes that the tracking is imperfect ("a wild animal that refuses to get into the box we've made") and treats the imperfection as a feature, not a bug, of the move.
