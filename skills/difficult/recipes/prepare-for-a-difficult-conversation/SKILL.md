## When to reach for this

You know a difficult conversation is coming — or you need to decide whether to initiate one — and you are anxious, unclear about what you want to accomplish, or worried you will lose your footing. Do this preparation before the conversation begins.

## The move

**1. Name which conversations are live.** Apply `identify-which-conversation-is-running`. Ask: which of the three conversations is most likely to generate heat? The "What Happened?" conversation (who is right, who caused this, who is to blame)? The Feelings conversation (emotions that are not being addressed)? The Identity conversation (what this says about who you are)? Almost always, all three are running in parallel. Identify the dominant ones and note which ones you have already done work on and which need more preparation.

Step 1 must come first because all subsequent preparation is targeted. If you do not know that your identity is at stake, you will not do the identity stabilizing work. If you do not know the feelings conversation is live, you will not prepare to express or acknowledge feelings. The three conversations are different problems with different preparation requirements.

**2. Ground your identity.** Apply `ground-your-identity-before-the-conversation`. Using what you learned in step 1 about which identity question is at stake (Am I competent? Am I a good person? Am I worthy of love?), do the acceptance work: you will make mistakes, your intentions are complex, you have contributed to the problem. Build the grounded identity that can receive hard information without collapsing.

Step 2 must come before step 3 because you cannot make a clear-headed decision about whether to raise the conversation if your identity anxiety is distorting your thinking. If Walker cannot accept that he was not always a good father, he will avoid the conversation with his daughter indefinitely — not from deliberate choice but from fear. The identity work allows the purpose decision to be made on its actual merits.

**3. Clarify your purpose and decide whether to raise it.** Apply `clarify-your-purpose-before-raising`. Ask three questions: Is the real conflict inside you? Is there a better way to address the issue than talking? Are your purposes sensible (learning their story, expressing your views, problem-solving) rather than off-base (changing them, venting, hit-and-run)? If you decide to raise it, name the three purposes you will bring. If you decide not to, use the four liberating assumptions to let go.

## Chain ordering is intrinsic

- Step 1 without step 2: you identify which conversations are live but have not stabilized your identity, so the conversation destabilizes you when it starts.
- Step 2 without step 1: you do generic identity work without targeting it to the specific stakes in this conversation.
- Step 3 without steps 1-2: you try to decide whether and how to raise the issue while still in the grip of anxiety and unclear about what is actually at stake.
- Steps 1-2 without step 3: you are prepared personally but have not clarified your purposes, so the conversation begins without a learning stance; your preparation goes to waste because you launch in the wrong direction.

## Signal it landed

Before the conversation, you can name: which Three Conversations are most live; which identity question is at stake and what you have accepted about yourself in relation to it; whether raising the conversation makes sense; and if so, what your three purposes are. You are going in open to learning, not looking to win.

## Probe before & after

Before beginning the preparation: ask "am I so anxious about this conversation that I can barely think about it?" If yes, the identity work in step 2 is urgent.

After the preparation: ask "am I clear about why I am raising this and what I want to learn, or am I still going in to prove I was right?" If the latter, repeat step 3.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without preparation, the conversation begins from inside your story, with your identity at risk, and with purposes that are off-base. You are certain you are right (step 1 skipped), you feel threatened by any challenging information (step 2 skipped), and you are there to make the other person see they were wrong (step 3 skipped). The chapter summarizes the resulting conversation: "More likely, you'll talk about understanding, and they'll talk about who's right. You'll talk about contribution, while they're stuck in blame." But often both parties are stuck in blame.

## Source

Chain assembled from Stone, Patton, and Heen's "Difficult Conversations," chapters 1, 6, and 7. Chapter 1 provides the framework for diagnosing which conversations are live; chapter 6 provides the identity preparation; chapter 7 provides the purpose-clarification and decision-making framework. The authors present these as sequential preparation steps in chapters 12's preparation checklist (lines 5027-5082), which explicitly runs: (1) sort out the Three Conversations, (2) understand your emotions and ground your identity, (3) check your purposes and decide whether to raise it.
