## When to reach for this

The other person has just expressed strong feelings — hurt, anger, fear, sadness — and you want to respond. Your impulse is to explain your own perspective, correct a misunderstanding, or immediately move toward solving the underlying problem. Or the other person does not feel heard and keeps returning to the same emotional ground even as you try to move forward.

## The move

**Before problem-solving, acknowledge.** Acknowledgment means letting the other person know that what they have said has made an impression on you, that their feelings matter to you, and that you are working to understand them. It is a step that simply cannot be skipped.

What acknowledgment sounds like: "Wow, I never knew you felt that way." "I kind of assumed you were feeling that, and I'm glad you felt comfortable enough to share it." "It sounds like this is really important to you." "Before I give you a sense of what's going on with me, tell me more about your feeling that I talk down to you."

**Acknowledgment is not agreement.** You can acknowledge the importance and reality of the other person's feelings without agreeing with the conclusions they draw from them. You can say, "Sounds like you feel really hurt and betrayed. I can see why that would be upsetting" — and then, separately, address the substance: "I do think this transfer is the right decision, but I understand why you're feeling what you're feeling and I want to talk through it." The chapter's example: a supervisor can acknowledge a subordinate's feeling of betrayal without agreeing that the transfer was unfair.

**Don't "fix" the feelings.** A common short-circuit: in response to someone's expressed feelings, you immediately try to make them feel better. "Well, if you're feeling lonely, I'll try to spend more time with you." This may be the right long-term action, but jumping there immediately signals that you want to get feelings out of the way rather than understand them. Don't leap to solutions. The other person needs to feel heard before they can receive any solution you offer.

**Don't short-circuit with "you're right."** Saying "you're right" in response to someone's feelings can also be a way of prematurely closing the conversation. If the other person feels hurt and you say "you're right, what can I say?" — that may be honest, but it can also feel dismissive, as if you've checked a box rather than truly engaged with the weight of what they said.

**Direct the conversation back to understanding.** If the other person is trying to shortcut to your defense (or you feel the urge to shortcut to yours), redirect: "I'm not saying you intended to hurt me. I don't know whether you did or not. What's important to me is that you understand how I felt when you criticized my work in front of the department." Before problem-solving, you have a responsibility to ensure: (1) that they appreciate the importance of this topic to you, (2) that they truly understand your feelings, and (3) that they value your having shared them.

## Signal it landed

The other person has stopped returning to the same emotional ground. They feel heard rather than processed. The conversation can now move to other topics — problem-solving, your own perspective, clarifications — without the feelings pulling everyone back.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "has the other person been heard, or am I ready to move on before that has happened?" If they keep returning to the feeling, it has not yet been acknowledged.

After: ask "did the other person seem to feel that I understood what they were saying and that it mattered?" If yes, acknowledgment has happened.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without acknowledgment, problem-solving fails. The other person cannot receive or engage with any solution while they feel unheard. The teenage boy who wanted to share his story with police officers and community members: "I don't want to change their minds. I just wanted to share my story. I wanted to tell my story, to share my feelings. Because now I know: they care enough about me to just listen." The failure mode is treating feelings as an obstacle to be cleared before getting to the "real" conversation. In fact, the feelings often are the real conversation, and skipping acknowledgment means skipping the thing that needed to happen.

## Worked micro-example

Max and Julie's wedding conversation. Every time they tried to talk about the logistics and budget, the conversation felt difficult and contentious. Once Max shared his feelings of loss and pride — and they were acknowledged — the logistical issues resolved easily. The troubling subtexts stopped getting in the way. Acknowledgment did not require agreement about money or choices. It required that each person's emotional reality be heard as real and important before moving to practical matters.
