## When to reach for this

You have a piece of feedback you want help thinking about. The person you are about to take it to is close to you, knows you well, and would normally line up on your side. You are about to lay out the feedback and the giver in a way that makes it easy for your friend to dismiss it ("the guy in Purchasing was overreacting"). Or, the inverse situation: a friend has come to you with their own such feedback and you are about to give an honest read where they were hoping for support. Reach for this before either of those exchanges starts.

## The move

1. Recognize the two kinds of mirror. The chapter's distinction. A **supportive mirror** shows your best self under flattering light. Its function is reassurance, restoration of the relationship, reminding you that you are a good person and that the bad picture is not the real you. A **honest mirror** shows you what others actually saw on the day in question, including the leaky face and the bedhead. Its function is to surface a blind spot. Both are legitimate. Both are needed at different times. Confusing them is what produces the trouble.
2. Notice your default. Your default ask, especially with a long-standing close mirror, is supportive. Your default phrasing telegraphs it ("right?", "this can't be true?", "the giver was being unreasonable, wasn't she?"). The chapter's image is the wicked queen in Snow White, who is not actually asking the mirror for an honest assessment. Catch the default before you ask.
3. Name the kind you want. The chapter gives explicit scripts. For honest: "Here's feedback I just got. It seems wrong. My first reaction is to reject it. But I wonder if this is feedback in a blind spot? Do you see me doing this sometimes, and if so, when? What impact do you see it having?" For supportive (the chapter validates this is also legitimate): "I just need to vent for a minute, I'm not asking you to litigate it." Either is fine; both being unspoken is what causes crossed wires.
4. Name what you are not asking for, when useful. The chapter notes that long-standing supportive mirrors can feel betrayed when honest feedback suddenly arrives. The reverse is also true. If you are switching modes, say so. If you are staying in a mode but the situation looks borderline, say that too.
5. Apply in reverse when you are the mirror. Before answering a friend's "tell me this isn't true," ask which kind of mirror they need today. Sometimes ask out loud. Sometimes infer and check ("do you want me to tell you what I actually see, or do you want me to be on your side for an hour?"). The same script works in both directions.

## Signal it landed

Both you and the friend can name, in one sentence each, which kind of mirror this conversation is. You asked for honest, they are giving honest, and you are receiving with the curiosity the request implied; or you asked for supportive, they are giving supportive, and you are not later disappointed that they did not push back. The asymmetry-trap (you wanted honest, got reassurance; or you wanted reassurance, got an unprompted honest read) does not occur.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If my friend gives me an answer that aligns with the original feedback rather than against it, do I actually want to hear that, or am I about to be mad?" If the answer is "I'd be mad," you are asking for a supportive mirror. Ask for it on purpose, or change what you are asking for.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if the question your friend just heard, and the answer your friend is preparing to give, are the same kind of thing. If you can predict roughly the shape of the answer (honest read, with examples; or warm reassurance, possibly without specifics) and you welcome that shape, the mirror is matched.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming, two failures recur. In the first, you ask the implicit supportive ask, your friend correctly delivers reassurance, and you walk away with confirmation that the original feedback was wrong. The blind spot stays in the dark. Worse, you have now triangulated against the giver of the original feedback, and the mutual friend or colleague who gave it next time will be processed as having an agenda (per the use-your-reaction-as-blind-spot-alert skill, this is the same failure looped). In the second, you ask for one mirror and the friend gives you the other. You wanted reassurance after a hard day; your spouse delivered an honest read of where they think the original feedback was right; the conversation about the feedback merges with a fresh injury about the relationship, and now there are two upset things to deal with instead of one. The chapter notes the betrayal-by-mirror-shift directly: when someone has been a supportive mirror, an unexpected shift to honest can feel like an ambush even when the honest read is true and offered with care.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter walks through the move at lines 2100-2124. The script for inviting an honest mirror is given verbatim ("Here's feedback I just got. It seems wrong. My first reaction is to reject it. But I wonder if this is feedback in a blind spot? Do you see me doing this sometimes, and if so, when? What impact do you see it having?"). The contrast case is the implicit supportive ask ("He's overreacting, right? He just doesn't understand I've got bigger things to worry about, right?"), where the friend is invited to be on your side and is bound to either oblige (giving you reassurance you already half-distrust) or break the implicit contract (delivering honesty you did not ask for). The chapter's broader point is that close people are hesitant to switch from supportive to honest not out of cowardice but out of confusion and care, so the burden of naming the mirror falls on the receiver who wants the honest read.

## Source

Chapter 4, "See Your Blind Spots," lines 2100-2124, the section "Get a Second Opinion" together with the "Honest Mirrors Versus Supportive Mirrors" subsection. Stone and Heen distinguish the two mirror types, name the implicit-default problem, give the explicit script for inviting honest reflection, and validate supportive reflection as a legitimate but separate request. The skill installs the explicit-naming move on both sides of the conversation.
