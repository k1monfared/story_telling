## When to reach for this

You came out of a feedback conversation worse than you went in, and the puzzle is that nothing the giver said was obviously wrong or unkind. The headline content sounded reasonable, the giver seemed to mean well, and yet you feel unseen, undirected, or more uncertain than before. Reach for this before you decide the giver is bad at feedback or that you are bad at receiving it.

## The move

1. Pause the content debate. Whether the feedback was correct on the merits is a separate question and you are not on it yet.
2. Run the cross-transaction check. There are two patterns; either or both may be firing:
    - **Pattern A — kind-mismatch on want vs. got**: you wanted one kind of feedback, the giver delivered another. The chapter's three round-robin cases: someone wanted appreciation but got coaching, someone wanted coaching but got evaluation, someone wanted evaluation but got appreciation. In each, the content was thoughtful; the kind was wrong.
    - **Pattern B — kind-mismatch on intent vs. hearing**: the giver intended one kind, you funneled it into another slot in your head. The most common version is the structural one: every coaching message carries an implicit "so far you are not doing this as well as you might," and the receiver hears the evaluative echo as the loudest part. The other common version: a casual remark intended as coaching ("call your mother") lands as evaluation ("you are a bad child").
3. Name which pattern is firing, in one sentence each, in your own words. "I came in wanting evaluation; I got appreciation, which is why I am more anxious, not less." "Dad intended coaching; I heard 'you are uncoordinated,' which is evaluation."
4. Decide what the diagnosis changes. If pattern A: the next move is to ask explicitly for the kind you wanted (see negotiate-which-kind-you-need), not to dismiss the feedback you got. If pattern B: the next move is to surface the funneling ("I notice I am hearing this as evaluation; was that your intent?") rather than treat the evaluative reading as fact.

## Signal it landed

You can complete the sentence: "I am dismayed because I wanted X but got Y" or "I am dismayed because I am hearing Y in what they meant as X." The dismay no longer feels like a generic bad feedback experience; it has a specific shape, and the shape suggests its own next move.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I had to say in one sentence why this conversation went badly, would I name the content, the giver's manner, or a kind-mismatch?" If you would name only the content or only the giver, you have not yet checked for the cross-transaction.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can name a kind-mismatch (pattern A or B, or both) and the conversation's failure now reads as a structural problem rather than a personal one.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the diagnosis, you misread a kind-mismatch as a content problem and either argue the content endlessly (the coaching is bad, the evaluation is unfair, the appreciation is hollow) or absorb the conclusion that you cannot handle feedback. The giver, who was acting in good faith with a different kind in mind, reads your reaction as ungrateful or fragile and stops offering anything. The relationship loses both the kind that was actually exchanged and the kind that was actually wanted, and the underlying need (for appreciation, coaching, or evaluation) does not get met because no one names that it was the need at stake.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's law-firm scene runs the round-robin: April wants appreciation but Donald gives her coaching on time management, and she leaves feeling more invisible than ever; Cody wants coaching on his research memo and gets a reassuring "you are right on track for a first-year," which leaves him feeling more lost; Evelyn wants evaluation about partnership prospects and gets warm thanks, which leaves her parsing the appreciation for hidden bad news. None of Donald's individual responses are wrong on the content; all three conversations fail because the kind is mismatched. The chapter also names the structural version through Elsie at the batting cage: Dad is delivering coaching, but the implicit evaluative echo ("so far you are not doing this as well as you might") is the loudest part for her.

## Source

Stone and Heen identify two patterns of crossed wires in the "Beware Cross-Transactions" and "A Complication: There Is Always Evaluation in Coaching" sections: want-vs.-got mismatch (April, Cody, Evelyn) and intent-vs.-hearing mismatch with the structural evaluative echo inside coaching (Elsie, the steering-wheel comment, the Motor Vehicles employee). The skill installs the diagnosis that distinguishes these mismatches from content problems.
