## When to reach for this

You are about to enter or are already in a feedback conversation — scheduled or spontaneous — and you want to handle it well. You want to genuinely learn something from it, say what needs to be said from your side, and end with clarity about what happens next. Reach for this as a mental framework before any high-stakes feedback conversation, or as a course-correction map during one that is going sideways.

## The move

**1. Open by getting aligned.** Apply `align-on-conversation-purpose`. Before engaging with content, clarify three things: Is this feedback, and if so what kind? Who decides? Is the outcome still open to influence? If the conversation has already started without this framing, step outside the content to establish it: "Can we take a minute to step back so I'm clear on our purposes?" This is not about substance; it is about the conversation's structure.

Step 1 must come first because without it you cannot make conscious choices about how to respond. You may argue against a decision that is already final, or accept coaching passively when you needed evaluation clarity first. The Gottman research makes the cost of skipping explicit: a harsh opening that is not corrected predicts a negative outcome 96 percent of the time.

**2. Listen by negotiating your internal voice toward curiosity.** Apply `negotiate-your-internal-voice`. Before and during the conversation, work with your internal voice rather than against it. Give it attention (it quiets when heard). Identify its trigger pattern (truth: "that's wrong!"; relationship: "after all I've done for you!"; identity: "I screw up everything."). Ask it: "What might be right about what they're saying?" and give it the assignment to find out. During the conversation, watch for hot inquiry (questions that are actually heated assertions) and replace them with clear assertions.

Step 2 must come after step 1 because knowing the type of conversation you are in (step 1) gives the internal voice negotiation in step 2 a specific target: what is there to listen for in this kind of conversation? The negotiation becomes "what might I learn about my performance in this role" rather than an undirected instruction to be curious.

**3. Assert what's left out, not who's right.** Apply `assert-as-left-out`. When you have something to say, frame it as a missing puzzle piece rather than as a replacement truth. "I disagree with that advice" rather than "that advice is wrong." "What's left out is my data / my interpretation / my feeling." For relationship-trigger assertions: name both topics on separate tracks. For identity-trigger assertions: be transparent about being overwhelmed rather than either collapsing ("it's true, I'm hopeless") or denying ("that's ridiculous, I'm not that kind of person").

**Rescue move A: When the conversation is stuck on process, make a process move.** Apply `make-a-process-move`. Step outside the conversation's content and diagnose what is stuck (both arguing without listening; two tangled topics; perspectives treated as facts; emotional flooding; deadlock). Name it out loud. Propose a next step. This is the emergency rescue that converts a stuck escalation back into a productive exchange. Use it whenever you notice the conversation is cycling rather than progressing.

**Rescue move B: When the conversation is stuck on solutions, dig for interests.** Apply `dig-for-interests-then-generate-options`. When you have reached agreement that a problem exists but are deadlocked on what to do about it, move from positions to interests. Ask what the giver is actually trying to accomplish (three buckets: helping you, helping themselves/the relationship, helping others/the system). Then generate options that address those interests. This rescue move applies specifically after the core conversation has produced understanding and reached a solution impasse.

**4. Close with commitment.** Apply `close-with-commitment`. Before parting, spend one to three sentences naming what was agreed and what each party will do next. It does not require agreeing with the feedback or promising to change. It can commit to gathering more information, returning to the conversation, or stating which parts of the feedback you have decided not to take and why. The goal is that both parties leave with the same understanding of where things stand.

## Chain ordering is intrinsic

- Step 1 fails if skipped: you engage with content before you know what kind of conversation you are in or whether the outcome is open to influence. You may argue against a decision that has already been made.
- Step 2 fails if skipped: your internal voice runs the encounter unmanaged, turning every trigger into a bodyguard response and every question into hot inquiry. You listen to defend rather than to understand.
- Step 3 fails if skipped: you withhold your pieces of the puzzle. The giver is left with only their view and no reason to update it. The conversation produces no new information for either party.
- Step 4 fails if skipped: both parties leave unclear about what was agreed, which is the chapter's explicit warning: "Both giver and receiver wonder why they spent so much time on the matter to begin with, when nothing ever changes."

## Signal it landed

After step 1: you both know what kind of conversation this is and who makes decisions. After step 2: you can name something the giver said that you did not already know or had not already decided to believe. After step 3: you said something specific about your data, interpretation, or feeling that the giver did not know before you said it. After step 4: both parties can name, in the same words, what was agreed and what comes next.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "do I know what kind of feedback this is, whether I can influence the outcome, and who decides?" If you cannot answer all three, step 1 has not been done.

After: ask "did I learn something in this conversation that I did not already know?" If yes, step 2 worked. "Did I say something the giver did not know before I said it?" If yes, step 3 worked. "Do I know what we each agreed to?" If yes, step 4 worked.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the chain, feedback conversations end in one of the three failure modes the chapter's version-one/two/three case illustrates. Version one: you assert that the outcome is unfair before you understand the criteria, triggering a fight about fairness. Version two: you accept passively and passive-aggressively ("I think 4 is a little low, but I suppose it's fine"), producing nothing. Version three: you ask whether the outcome can be changed before you understand the reasoning, and hear "no" before you have the information to argue for anything. The chain produces version four: you ask questions to understand, assert your view after gathering data, and close with a specific next step.

## Worked example

The chapter's performance-rating version-four conversation walks through all four steps. Open (step 1): "I'm surprised I got a 4 instead of a 5. I don't know much about the decision-making process or the criteria." Internal voice managed (step 2): the receiver does not say the rating is unfair before hearing the criteria; curiosity is maintained throughout. Assert left out (step 3): "Based on what you've explained and assuming no other factors, I do think I should have received a 5. Do you see it differently?" Process move (rescue A): when the boss seems confused about what the receiver is trying to do, the receiver explains: "I'm not asserting the rating is unfair — I don't have enough information to judge yet. Can you tell me what factors are weighed?" Close (step 4): "For the moment, I'd like to leave the rating alone. Would it be okay if I talked to someone on the committee who has concerns about my commitment? I'd like to learn more before I decide whether to pursue changing the rating."
