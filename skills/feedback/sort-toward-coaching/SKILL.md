## When to reach for this

You received feedback and your identity has been hooked. You are asking yourself "am I enough?" rather than "what can I learn?" The feedback may have been offered as coaching, but you heard it as evaluation. Or the feedback genuinely contained both evaluation and coaching, and you got stuck on the evaluation part. Or you are simply unsure whether there is coaching in the feedback at all.

## The move

**Run the sorting exercise.** Take the feedback and imagine hearing it, first as evaluation, then as coaching. The two interpretations produce different experiences and different questions:

- If heard as evaluation: what does this say about who I am? What label does it assign? Am I competent, attractive, responsible, a good parent? Identity is triggered; the question is about a verdict.
- If heard as coaching: what is there to learn? What could I do differently, understand more fully, or work on next? Identity is not triggered; the question is about a next action.

The chapter's case: Elsbeth is told to "amp up the energy level" during a presentation break. As evaluation, she hears: "Am I boring everyone? Am I not up to an audience this senior?" Her attention goes to defending her self-image. As coaching: "I should have another cup of coffee and make this next segment more interactive." Her attention goes to the second half of the presentation. Same words, opposite effects on learning.

**Default to coaching.** The chapter prescribes a default: when you are in the presence of feedback that could reasonably be heard either way, hear it as coaching. This is not self-deception; coaching IS the charitable and often accurate interpretation of what the giver intends. Even evaluation feedback usually contains an implicit coaching message.

**Find the coaching in evaluation.** When feedback is explicitly evaluative and the evaluation is unflattering, there may still be coaching at the core. The chapter's key diagnostic: ask what the giver actually wants. Margaret hears Lisa saying "you were a bad mother." Lisa wants three things: to be understood, to receive acknowledgment that her mother's choices contributed to her pain, and to have a better current relationship. The coaching is at the core; the evaluation is the surface packaging. Margaret can reach past the surface packaging by asking: what is she trying to help me learn or understand?

**Notice your oversort patterns.** After running the sorting exercise several times with feedback from your past, the chapter predicts three discoveries: you can usually hear most feedback either way with some effort; identity reaction diminishes when you hear coaching; and you have identifiable tendencies toward oversort. If you oversort toward evaluation once out of ten times, that is one identity crisis that did not need to happen. If you oversort eight out of ten times, you have a consistent pattern that is blocking learning.

## Signal it landed

After receiving feedback, you can articulate what action you would take based on hearing it as coaching. The identity question ("am I good enough?") has moved to the background. Elsbeth's signal: she has a coffee, thinks about how to make the next segment more interactive, and delivers the second half. She did not need to resolve whether she is the kind of person senior audiences find engaging.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "what am I currently asking myself about this feedback?" If the question is about your worth, competence, or whether you are a certain kind of person, you are in the evaluation bin.

After: ask "what is the action this feedback is suggesting, if I hear it as coaching?" If you can answer this in one sentence, the coaching frame has been found.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without sorting toward coaching, identity-relevant feedback consistently triggers a defensive reaction, and the content gets lost in the reaction. The chapter's label for this: "we snatch defensiveness from the jaws of learning." The receiver spends the energy defending their self-image that they would otherwise spend engaging with the content. In the Lisa/Margaret case, Margaret's identity reaction (I am a good mother; this feedback says I am a bad mother) prevents her from hearing what Lisa actually wants, which is acknowledgment and connection. The feedback fails for both of them. The chapter notes that "placing the conversation in a coaching frame" is important not because it reduces Margaret's pain (though it might), but because it accurately reflects what Lisa intends.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter offers a self-administered exercise: take a real piece of feedback you received recently and run it through both interpretations. "Your friend asked why you let your children stay up so late." As evaluation: you are an overpermissive parent. As coaching: this is a conversation about what your friend has noticed and what they're concerned about, something worth considering alongside your own observations. The exercise surface two things: the evaluation interpretation made you defensive; the coaching interpretation opened a question you could actually engage. Notice which one you defaulted to.
