## When to reach for this

You are revising or drafting and have arrived at the stretch after the central event. The story has produced a real coil, an unresolved question the reader is now waiting for the protagonist to answer. The protagonist's life, at its natural pace, would not answer the question quickly. The honest rendering of the next months or years of that life would be either a long slow dilution of the charge or, more likely, no resolution at all. You sense the story is over once the question is answered, but the story has no machinery to force the answer in the time it has left. Reach for this whenever you can name the charged question the story has raised and cannot see how the protagonist's life, left to itself, would ever answer it.

## The move

1. Name the charged question in one present-tense sentence. Saunders's worked example: "What is Alyosha going to do about that injustice?" Or, equivalently: "How is the injustice Alyosha has experienced going to come home to roost?" The sentence should be a question the prior pages have made the reader carry forward, not a thematic abstraction.
2. List, in one or two phrases per item, the variants the natural pace of the protagonist's life would produce. Saunders's worked variants for Alyosha: outwardly-same-but-inwardly-bitter, eventually-confronts-father, bitterness-dissipates-and-never-causes-anything. Each variant is a faithful rendering of how this specific character would actually live out the coiled energy.
3. Read the variants and ask: which one does the character's nature most plausibly point to? Saunders's diagnosis of Alyosha is the third variant: the bitterness never causes anything, and "this seems the variant Alyosha is fated to live." The natural variant is often the least dramatic.
4. Decide whether the natural variant resolves the charged question on the page. If it does, the story does not need the move; render the natural variant. If it does not (because the dilution is the answer, or because the answer arrives only outside the story's frame), the story needs an event that compresses the time available for the question to discharge.
5. Introduce a compressing event. The event must be of a kind that forces the discharge into the remaining text. Saunders's instance is a fatal accident; the same logic applies to any event that limits the protagonist's available time, attention, or location: an arrest, a deadline, a final visit, a forced departure. The event should not be the answer to the question; it should be the foreshortening that makes the answer come into the remaining pages.
6. Allow the event to be uncaused or only loosely caused. Saunders observes that Alyosha's fall is "not caused by anything, really. 'His foot slipped and he fell with his shovel.' He just dies by chance, in a manner equivalent to, say, being hit by a falling tree." A heavily motivated compressing event will read as the story's answer rather than as the device that compels the answer. The reader needs to feel the event's instrumentality without seeing the writer's hand on it.
7. Write the discharge into the remaining real estate. The remaining pages are now structurally obligated to render the answer to the charged question, in whatever form the protagonist's nature produces. The compression is what makes that answer felt rather than dispersed.

## Signal it landed

You can name the charged question in one present-tense sentence at the boundary between the central event and the compressing event. You can name the compressing event and the remaining-page count in which the discharge must occur. The compressing event is uncaused or only loosely caused at the level of plot, but is structurally necessary at the level of the reader's experience: the reader feels the question pressing against the remaining pages and the answer arriving under that pressure. A reader cannot point to the compressing event as "what the story is about"; they can point to the discharge that follows it as the answer the story raised.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of the post-event stretch in your draft: "What is the question the reader is carrying past this point, and how many pages does the natural unfolding of the character's life need to answer it?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the second answer names a compressing event that has reduced the page-count to a feasible remainder, and the discharge fits inside that remainder without rushing or summarizing.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this procedure, a writer who has produced a real charge in the mid-story is faced with two bad options. The first is to render the natural unfolding faithfully, which produces a long slow dilution of the charge across pages the reader experiences as inert; the question the prior pages raised becomes the question the prior pages used to raise, and the reader registers the loss as the story going slack. The second is to drop the question and let the protagonist's life go on without it, which betrays the prior pages by treating the charge they produced as not real. Both failures share a root: the writer is honoring the world's continuity at the cost of the story's structure. Saunders's diagnosis: a story knows what the reader wants to know and aims to give it to them as quickly as possible; the late-art writer's tool for that aim is to foreshorten the time available for the answer, by introducing an event that compresses the remaining stretch. The compression is not a cheat; it is the structural decision that lets the prior pages' charge resolve.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders walks the closing structural decision in "Alyosha the Pot." The story has produced a real charge: Alyosha has burst into tears on being told to forget the marriage, the reader knows the cheerful obedience has cost him something this time, the question pressing against the remaining text is "What is Alyosha going to do about that injustice?" Saunders surveys the variants Alyosha's actual nature would produce, names the third one as the most likely (the bitterness dissipates without ever causing anything), and observes that this is the variant Alyosha is fated to live. Then he names what Tolstoy does: knock Alyosha off the roof. The fall is barely caused at the plot level — a foot slips, a shovel falls — and Saunders parallels it explicitly with Akakii Akakievich's death in "The Overcoat," where Akakii's collapse is the natural continuation of a lifetime of abuse and Alyosha's is not. The function of the fall is structural: it foreshortens the time available for the discharge, and the remaining page is structurally obligated to render whatever answer Alyosha's nature can produce within it. The answer that arrives — the small protest of the burst-into-tears, the un-resolved final amazement, the still-cheerful obedience held even on the deathbed — is the discharge the compression made possible.
