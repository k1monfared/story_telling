## When to reach for this

You are working on a scene whose meaning depends on the gap between what people say and what they know. A landlord telling a peasant they "deal straightforwardly." A mother praising her daughter's husband while privately appalled. A doctor delivering bad news with practiced kindness. A defendant maintaining innocence to a jury. Reach for this whenever a scene's moral weight lives in the difference between a character's public stance and private knowledge, and your prose currently shows only one of them.

## The move

1. List the characters present in the morally loaded exchange. Two is enough; more works the same way.
2. For the exchange, name the objective truth — what the omniscient narrator could state outright if asked. (Vasili routinely cheats Nikita.) The truth must be statable factually, not as a judgment.
3. For each character, draft three short renderings: their public stance (what they say in dialogue), their private stance (what they think, in the same factual register the rest of the prose uses), and how the omniscient narrator describes their position from outside. You will not necessarily use all three for every character, but you will need to know all three before you choose what to show.
4. Compose the scene by rotating among these vantages. Saunders's worked Tolstoy passages cycle through five shifts in three to four paragraphs: objective truth, character A's speech, character A's thought, character B's speech, character B's thought, and a closing objective frame. The order is not fixed; the rotation is.
5. When you go into a character's mind, render the thought as a factual report (matrix-of-facts), not a confession or soliloquy. The reader must feel they are observing a thought from outside, not being lectured by an interior monologue.
6. Hold the disposition that makes this work: you have an inner version of every person you are writing. Not a special one for each, but the same one — yours, in their situation. The thoughts you ascribe to them are thoughts you would have, were you initiator, host, kid, servant. This is what the magician-and-saw analogy is about: a passable version of God, sustained for the duration of the scene.

## Signal it landed

The reader can name, after the scene, the gap between what each character said and what each character privately knew. The reader does not feel the writer judging anyone, and yet the moral architecture of the exchange is unmistakable. The hypocrisy, the resignation, the small dishonesty are all visible without anyone being labeled.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, pick a morally loaded exchange you have drafted and ask: "Could a reader, reading this scene cold, tell which character is closer to the truth and which is spinning?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer changes from "the reader has to take my word for it (or guess from tone)" to "the reader sees the gap because the rotation has shown them speech-vs-thought for both characters and an objective frame around the exchange."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this rotation, morally loaded scenes fail in one of two predictable ways. The single-mind version: the scene stays inside one character's head, and the reader, having access to that character's interpretation only, cannot tell whether they are being lied to. The dialogue-only version: the scene shows only what is said, and the reader, lacking interior access, must read tone and gesture for clues to private knowledge — a brittle method that fails as soon as the characters are practiced liars. Either way, the scene reduces to the writer asserting (through narrative position) that someone is the hypocrite or the truth-teller. Saunders's diagnosis: what reads as a writer's saintly compassion is actually a technical rotation among vantages, sustained by the writer's confidence that they are inside every person on the page.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders walks the Vasili-Nikita wage exchange across three paragraphs. The omniscient narrator opens by stating an objective fact: Vasili routinely cheats Nikita. Vasili then says aloud to Nikita, "We deal straightforwardly. You serve me and I don't neglect you" — a public stance the reader, freshly informed, recognizes as untrue. The next sentences move into Vasili's mind, where he is "honestly convinced that he was Nikita's benefactor." This is critical: a "bwa-ha-ha" version of Vasili (knowingly lying to an idiotic servant) would be a different character; the un-self-aware hypocrite is what the rotation produces. Nikita then answers in dialogue, with his own spin ("I serve you and take as much pains as I would for my own father"), which the reader doubts in the same way they doubted Vasili. Finally Nikita's thoughts confirm he is "quite aware" he is being cheated and resigned to taking what he can get. Five shifts, three paragraphs. The reader leaves the exchange knowing the moral architecture without anyone having labeled it.

## Source

Chapter "And Yet They Drove On," lines 4253-4293. Saunders walks the Vasili-Nikita wage exchange and the Grishkino departure as two worked instances of mind-to-mind movement, names the disposition that powers the technique (a confidence that the writer has an inner version of every character), and gives the magician-and-saw analogy for how the reader receives the result as a passable version of God.

James Wood, *How Fiction Works* (2008), sections on "Narrating" and "Free Indirect Style." Wood gives the formal name and lineage for the interior-rendering half of the rotation. When the prose enters Vasili's mind and reports him "honestly convinced that he was Nikita's benefactor," the technique is free indirect style, and Wood's treatment (Flaubert, Austen, Joyce) supplies the vocabulary and historical context Saunders does not theorize. Useful for a reader who wants to recognize and tune the in-the-mind register on its own, separate from the rotation pattern.
