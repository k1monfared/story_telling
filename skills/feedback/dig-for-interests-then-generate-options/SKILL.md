## When to reach for this

A feedback conversation has reached agreement on what the problem is (or at least that there is a problem), but is now stuck on what to do about it. The giver has a position (their prescription for what you should change), you have a position (usually a different or less drastic prescription), and neither position is acceptable to the other. You are arguing about solutions rather than exploring possibilities.

## The move

**Distinguish positions from interests.** Positions are what people say they want: "Be on time," "Cut your hair," "Stop micromanaging." Interests are the underlying needs, desires, fears, and concerns the stated position is meant to address. The chapter, drawing on Fisher and Ury: "Often interests can be met by a variety of options, some different from what anyone sees at the outset."

**Listen for the interests behind the position.** Advice typically arrives as a position that represents the giver's best current idea for what to do. The interest behind it is usually broader. Earl's supervisor said "clean up your appearance." The interest behind it: "We want families to feel comfortable with you more quickly." Once Earl had heard the interest rather than just the position, he could generate an option the supervisor had not considered: have the supervisor introduce him as a semiprofessional bluegrass banjo player. This put his appearance in a context that turned surprise into intrigue. The interest was met; the position was bypassed.

**Identify which bucket the interest falls into.** The chapter names three types of underlying interest:

1. **Helping you.** The giver sees ways you could improve, or wants to protect you from problems they see that you don't. If this is the bucket, options that accelerate your growth or protect you from the named risk address the interest.

2. **Helping themselves or the relationship.** The giver is telling you that your behavior is affecting them. "You travel too much" may mean "I feel neglected." Options that address the relational impact, not just the stated behavior, meet the real interest.

3. **Helping the organization, team, or someone else.** The feedback is motivated by concern for a third party or system. Your boss cannot give you a higher rating because it would be unfair to others; your friend is raising an issue on behalf of a mutual friend who is uncomfortable raising it themselves. Options that address the third party or systemic concern address the real interest.

**Generate options.** Once you have identified the interests involved (including your own), generate options that address those interests rather than fighting over positions. The chapter: "The number one reason we don't come up with good options is that we simply don't think to try." Explicitly invite joint option-generation: name the different interests out loud and invite the other person to think with you. Some options will fully resolve the issue; some will be process options (we try this, then reassess; we trade off; we each try our approach and show both to the relevant audience). A process option does not require final agreement; it requires a next step that both parties believe is fair.

## Signal it landed

The conversation has moved from "your position vs. my position" to "what are you actually trying to accomplish here, and what are some different ways we could accomplish it?" At least one option has been generated that neither of you had before the conversation shifted to interests.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "do I know what the giver is actually trying to accomplish, or do I only know what they say should change?" If only the latter, the interests have not been heard.

After: ask "is there an option on the table now that addresses what the giver is trying to accomplish while being acceptable to me?" If yes, the move has generated new possibilities.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, feedback conversations about solutions produce position fights. Each party argues for their version of what should change, and neither version is adopted because neither party understands why the other is proposing it. The chapter's example: Alita's patients complain that she runs late (position: run on time). Their underlying interest is not merely punctuality; it is to understand what is happening and to feel acknowledged. A sign explaining why appointments run late meets part of this interest without Alita being able to solve the structural problem that causes the delays. The position fight (run on time vs. I cannot change the structure) could not be resolved because neither party had articulated the actual interest.

## Worked micro-example

Earl's case: supervisor's position = "cut your hair and trim your beard." Underlying interest = "families should feel comfortable with you more quickly." Earl's counter-position = "I won't change my appearance; people who judge by appearance are no different from those who judge by disability." Impasse on positions. Earl identified the underlying interest (comfort, quickly) and generated a new option (preface his introduction with his banjo playing). The interest was met; the position fight was bypassed. Earl and his supervisor were then working together on the shared interest rather than against each other's positions.
