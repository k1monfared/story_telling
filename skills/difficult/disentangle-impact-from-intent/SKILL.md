## When to reach for this

You are in a conversation where (a) you feel hurt by something the other person said or did and find yourself making attributions about their motives, OR (b) the other person is accusing you of bad intentions and your immediate impulse is to defend yourself. Either side of this cycle is a sign that impact and intent have been conflated.

## The move

The chapter names two distinct mistakes, one for each role, and the remedy for each.

---

**When you are the one who was hurt: avoid assuming you know their intentions.**

The automatic leap: "I was hurt → they intended to hurt me." This leap is almost always wrong or incomplete, and it is never the full story. Intentions are invisible. You make them up from the impact their actions had on you. You then treat your invented story as fact.

Three questions to break the leap:
1. What did they actually say or do? (the action, in plain terms)
2. What was the impact on me? (what I felt, what it meant to me)
3. What assumption am I making about what they intended? (name it explicitly as a guess)

Once you have separated these three, hold the third (your assumption about their intention) as a hypothesis — a guess you are checking, not a conclusion you are asserting. The hypothesis is not nothing; it is based on real information. But it is not the same as fact.

Share the impact and inquire about the intention. Rather than opening with an accusation ("you were trying to hurt me"), open with the impact and your hypothesis: "When you said X, I felt hurt. And what I'm wondering is whether you meant to embarrass me. I don't know that that's what you intended, but that's what I'm sitting with."

Lori's better opening with Leo: "When you said 'why don't you lay off the ice cream,' I felt hurt. Then I found myself wondering whether you said it on purpose to embarrass me. I don't know why you'd want to do that, but that's what I'm thinking when it happens." This opens the conversation rather than closing it.

Also consider: your reaction may say as much about you as about them. Past experience can give an action special meaning it would not otherwise have. The chapter: "Perhaps you've had a past experience that gives their action special meaning to you."

---

**When you are the one being accused: good intentions don't sanitize bad impact.**

The automatic mistake: once you clarify that your intentions were good, you expect the other person's hurt to be resolved. "I didn't mean to hurt you, so you should be fine now." This is a second and equally costly mistake.

When someone says "you intended to hurt me," they are actually communicating two separate messages: (1) "I believe you meant to hurt me" (the intention claim) and (2) "I was hurt" (the impact). When you hear the accusation, you focus only on the first message and defend against it. In doing so, you miss the second. The other person walks away feeling that you are more interested in defending yourself than in caring about their experience.

Three moves for the accused role:
1. **Listen past the accusation for the feelings.** Before responding to the intention claim, acknowledge the feeling. "It sounds like what I said really hurt" comes before any explanation of intent. This is not conceding the intention claim; it is hearing the full message.
2. **Then address the intention question.** Once feelings are acknowledged, you can share your actual intentions. This is more likely to be heard now because the other person feels that you heard them first.
3. **Be open to reflecting on the complexity of your intentions.** "My intentions were pure" is rarely the whole story. Maybe there was also a desire to control, to protect yourself, to be seen a certain way. Saying "I was thinking I was helping, but I wonder if I was also embarrassed by the situation" is more honest and usually produces better results than insisting on purely good intentions.

Leo's better response to Lori: "Wow. It sounds like what I said really hurt." Then, later: "I was thinking I was helping you with your diet. But I can see how saying something in front of everyone would be embarrassing. I wonder why I didn't see that? Maybe I was embarrassed myself, and that got in the way."

---

The chapter's formulation: "Often we say 'You intended to hurt me' when what we really mean is 'You don't care enough about me.'" That is the real hurt at the core of most intent accusations — and it only becomes visible after the impact has been heard.

## Signal it landed

In the hurt role: your opening frames the impact as impact and the intention as a hypothesis, not a verdict. The other person is not immediately defending themselves.

In the accused role: you have acknowledged the hurt before defending yourself. The person who was hurt feels that you understood what they were communicating, not just what you needed to clear up.

## Probe before & after

Before (hurt role): ask "am I about to lead with what they intended, or with what I felt?" If intentions first, the leap has been made.

Before (accused role): ask "is my first impulse to say 'I didn't mean to hurt you'?" If yes, you are about to miss the feelings half of the accusation.

After (hurt role): ask "did I separate what they did, what it did to me, and what I guessed about their motive, in that order?" If yes, the disentanglement has happened.

After (accused role): ask "did they seem to feel heard about the hurt before I started explaining my intent?" If yes, the listen-past-the-accusation move worked.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the disentanglement, conversations about intent cycle in a classic pattern: one party accuses, the other defends their good intentions, the first party feels their hurt is being dismissed, the second party feels falsely accused, both feel they are victims, both escalate. Lori accuses Leo of controlling or hurting her on purpose; Leo defends himself; Leo accuses Lori of starting fights on purpose; Lori escalates. Months later the relationship is strained and the pattern continues. The chapter: "This is how well-intentioned people get themselves into trouble." Both parties in the cycle are doing their best; both are making the same pair of mistakes. The disentanglement short-circuits the cycle before it starts.

Critically: people almost never change without first feeling understood. Good intentions are not enough; what matters is being clear about impact and genuinely curious about the other person's experience.

## Worked micro-example

Lori and Leo. The cycle version (what actually happens): accusation → defense → counter-accusation. The better version: Lori shares the impact and holds the intention as a hypothesis. Leo acknowledges the hurt before explaining his intent. Leo reflects honestly on whether his motives were pure. Both arrive at a more genuine understanding and end with a constructive question ("Let's think about what I should do or say in situations like that") rather than another cycle of the same argument.

Margaret and her surgeon provide the single-direction case: Margaret's angry story about the doctor cavorting in the Caribbean was her intent-attribution. When the actual story emerged (he was setting up a hospital in Bosnia), the impact (having to wait longer) was unchanged but her emotional state changed dramatically. Knowing intent matters; but guessing it wrong, as Margaret did, is both common and costly.
