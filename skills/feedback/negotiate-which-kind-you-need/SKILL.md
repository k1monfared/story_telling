## When to reach for this

You can feel the kind-mismatch coming on, or it has already started. Either you are about to walk into a conversation where the giver thinks the topic is one kind of feedback and you actually need another, or you are mid-conversation and the giver is offering, say, coaching while what you need is evaluation (or appreciation, or vice versa). You are about to let the misalignment ride and absorb the wrong thing. Reach for this instead.

## The move

1. Run the three alignment questions, before or during the conversation:
    1. What is my purpose in receiving (or asking for) this feedback?
    2. Is that the right purpose from my point of view?
    3. Is that the right purpose from the other person's point of view?
2. Name the kind you want, out loud, in your own words. "I am looking for evaluation, where I stand against my peers." "I would like coaching, specific suggestions on how to do this more efficiently." "I really need appreciation right now; I have been working hard and want to know it is seen."
3. Ask the giver to say what kind they think they are giving (or were planning to give). If they have not thought about it explicitly, the question itself is the alignment.
4. If you each want different kinds, surface the difference rather than absorb the wrong one. Use the chapter's principle: "explicit disagreement is better than implicit misunderstanding." Negotiate one of three landings: shift to the kind you need now, hold this conversation for the kind they planned and schedule a separate one for the kind you need, or get a brief version of the kind you need first so you can take in the kind they planned to give.
5. Use the take-the-bull-by-the-horns scripts when you suspect the kind without being sure: "You are offering coaching, but it would help to get a quick evaluation: am I doing all right overall?" or "You are saying this is coaching, but I am hearing it as evaluation, too. Are you saying I am falling behind?"

## Signal it landed

You and the giver can each say, in a sentence, what kind this conversation is and what kind you need, and the answers either match or are visibly negotiated. You stop trying to extract the kind you need from feedback that is not delivering it (no more reading appreciation for traces of evaluation, no more arguing the content of coaching when you are starving for "you are seen").

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If the giver and I each had to name the kind of feedback this conversation is for, would we give the same answer?" If you do not know, or if you suspect the answer is no and you have not surfaced it, the misalignment is implicit.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if both of you can name the kind, and any disagreement is now an explicit topic rather than the silent reason the conversation feels off.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming the kind you need, you accept the kind on offer and try to translate. The translation does not work; appreciation does not answer where-do-I-stand, coaching does not answer am-I-seen, evaluation does not answer how-do-I-improve. You leave the conversation with an unmet need and a giver who feels rebuffed (they offered something thoughtful and you walked away unhappy). Worse, the misalignment becomes a pattern: you stop asking for feedback because "feedback" never gets you what you need, when the actual problem is that you have been asking for the wrong kind, or asking for the right one in language that does not specify it.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter closes its alignment section with Elsie at the batting cage. Dad has been pitching coaching all morning; Elsie has been melting down. The fix arrives when Dad stops pitching, asks "Elsie, what's up?" and the negotiation surfaces: Elsie has been practicing all week and came to the park expecting to wow him with her improved swing. What she actually needed was appreciation for the week of practice and acknowledgment of her disappointment, not more coaching on stance. Once the kind is named, the conversation becomes possible. The chapter's law-firm round-robin is the same pattern in reverse: April, Cody, and Evelyn each got a kind they did not want because none of them named the kind they did want, and Donald, meaning well, defaulted to whichever kind he found easiest in the moment.
