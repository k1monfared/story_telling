## When to reach for this

You are working on the ending of your own draft and unsure whether it is finished, or you are reading a published story whose ending feels inevitable but whose work you cannot articulate. Reach for this whenever you suspect the difference between "narrative" and "story" lives in the last move and you want to make that difference visible.

## The move

1. Pick the cut. Choose a place near the end of the story but before its actual conclusion. A natural break, a scene end, the close of the section before the final one.
2. Read the story up to the cut. Pretend it ends there. Read with that ending in mind.
3. Ask: "Is it a story yet?" Saunders's variant: "Is it soup yet?" Note the felt answer. It is usually no, but interestingly so.
4. Name what is missing. What questions are still in the air? What energy has been built up that has nowhere to go? What moment of change has not yet occurred? Be specific about what the truncated version fails to deliver.
5. Read the actual ending. Now ask: what does the actual ending supply that the truncation lacked? Does it deliver the moment of change the story has been preparing? Does it answer the questions still in the air? Does it discharge the accumulated energy?
6. The answer to step 5 is what the ending is doing. You can now use that knowledge two ways: as a diagnostic for your own draft (does my ending do this?) or as a learning observation (this is what the form requires of an ending here).

## Signal it landed

For the story or draft in question, you can finish two sentences. "Truncated at point X, the piece feels ___ because ___ is still missing." "The actual ending supplies ___, which discharges ___ and produces the feeling of completion." The blanks are filled with specific elements (an event, a memory, a recognition, a return), not with vague evaluations.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of the ending: "What is this ending doing that the rest of the story has not already done?" Note your answer. It will likely be vague.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if your answer becomes specific and points at concrete material the ending introduces (a memory, a glance, a detail) and concrete questions or expectations from earlier in the story that the new material settles or transforms.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this test, you judge endings holistically and cannot revise them. Your ending feels off but you cannot say where the off lives, so you tinker at the level of word choice or pacing while the structural problem (an unanswered question, a built-up energy with nowhere to land, a moment of change that does not arrive) remains untouched. Equally, when learning from another writer's ending, you absorb the satisfaction without identifying its mechanism, and cannot transfer it. Saunders is direct: a story is not a lively narrative that briskly continues then stops; the ending is what makes it story rather than narrative, and you can only see what makes it story by isolating the work the ending does.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders truncates "In the Cart" at the end of the river-fording section, two pages before the actual ending, and asks the reader to feel the result. The truncated version: lonely teacher, possible suitor who turns out to be no possibility, near-humiliation at a teahouse, ruined purchases, end. He names the feeling: anecdotal, harsh, a series of bad things happening to a nice lady; not yet a story. Then he reads the actual ending, in which a glimpse of a stranger on a passing train looks like Marya's dead mother and unlocks a flood of vivid childhood memory, and then dissolves. By comparison, he can name what the actual ending supplies: a moment of change, a flickering-back-alive of something long-dead, the discharge of all the accumulated pity into the sudden glow of restored selfhood. That is the move that converts narrative into story.
