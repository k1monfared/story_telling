## When to reach for this

You snapped at your partner over the dishes and realized later it wasn't about the dishes. You went silent at dinner and couldn't say why. You agreed to plans you didn't want and then resented the plans. Reach for this whenever your reaction feels mismatched to the trigger — that mismatch usually means an inner state went unobserved long enough to come out sideways.

## The move

1. Pause before responding. Even five seconds. The reaction wants to leave your body immediately; let it sit.
2. Name what's happening physically. Where do you feel it? Throat, chest, stomach? Tight, hot, hollow? You're not interpreting yet — just describing the sensation.
3. Distinguish what you feel from what you think about what you feel. "I feel anxious" is different from "I'm being needy." The first is a state; the second is a judgment.
4. Find the bundle of feelings, not just the loudest one. Underneath anger there's often hurt; underneath shutdown there's often fear; underneath busyness there's often loneliness. The dominant emotion crowds the others out.
5. Ask what state is being signaled. Not "what should I do about this?" but "what does my body think is happening here?"
6. Only after the state is read, choose a response. The response from a read state will be more accurate than the response from an unread state.

## Signal it landed

You can name your inner state in present tense before acting on it. The phrase "I'm noticing I feel..." becomes available to you instead of just the reaction itself. Over time, the gap between feeling and reacting widens, and the reaction becomes a choice.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without self-attunement, you treat your reactions as accurate reports about external reality. You're convinced your partner did something wrong because you feel hurt — when actually you feel hurt because of an unrelated trigger that the partner happened to brush. The relationship absorbs the cost of your unread inner state.

## Reflection prompts

- What does self-attunement look like for you in your strongest moments? In your weakest?
- What state are you most likely to overlook in yourself? Sadness? Anger? Need? Fear?
- When you notice a strong reaction starting, what's your typical pattern — push it down, act on it immediately, or pause and check in?
- Who in your life has modeled self-attunement well for you? Who has modeled its absence?
