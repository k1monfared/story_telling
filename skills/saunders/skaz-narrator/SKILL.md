## When to reach for this

Your story has a narrator who currently behaves like an invisible camera. Events happen, the narrator reports them, the prose is competent, and the meaning lives entirely at the level of what happens to the characters. You suspect there is more meaning available — about the world the narrator inhabits, about the kind of person who would tell this kind of story — and that the transparent narrator is leaving it on the table. Reach for this whenever the narrator's competence is itself making your story too clean, or whenever the events you are reporting are strange enough that a transparent-omniscient voice feels dishonest about who could be telling this.

## The move

1. Audit your current narrator. Is the voice neutral, omniscient, transparent? If asked who the narrator is, can you only say "the writer"? If yes, the narrator is doing no work as a character.
2. Compose the narrator as a person. Give them a station, a level of education, an idea of themselves they cannot quite live up to, a literary aspiration they cannot quite execute, a relationship to the events being narrated (above them, beside them, condescending toward them). Saunders's Gogol-narrator: a functionary, "stiff but imprecise literary formality," "pedantic and superior," who "overestimates his intelligence and charm." Make the narrator specific the way you would make any character specific.
3. Build the voice on three signature failures. The skaz-narrator's prose is recognizable by characteristic misfires: (a) digressions that set out to prove one thing and prove the opposite; (b) misplaced emphasis (the narrator stresses what does not matter and skims past what does); (c) failure to register adequate amazement at the strange events being narrated. Identify which of these your narrator is going to perform, and where in the story.
4. Write the misfired digression. Saunders's worked example from "The Nose": the narrator pauses to assure us Ivan is "in many ways a respectable man," then proves the opposite by listing his drunkenness, bad clothing, and stinking hands, plus a "cynic"-defining moment that is actually evidence of normal barbering. The digression is "executed with a straight face and a semblance of authority but seems unable to do what it sets out to do." The misfire is the technique.
5. Write the non-registering reaction. When something extraordinary happens in the story, have the narrator fail to comment on it, skim past it with a phrase like "and so on," or get distracted by the wrong detail. The Gogol-narrator does not step in to reassure us when the dialogue veers into illogic; the absence of comment is the register choice.
6. Hold the writer above the narrator. Saunders's framing: this is "a great writer writing a graceless writer writing." You, the writer, must be in control of every misfire. The narrator's incompetence is your competence. The test: can you point to a sentence and say what the narrator is failing to do, and why that failure is the right shape for this story?
7. Allow the voice to drop. Saunders flags Gogol's "But there is nothing enduring in this world..." passage as a moment where "the clumsiness of Gogol's skaz narration suddenly falls away and the prose becomes beautifully articulate." Skaz is not a uniform mode; it is a register the writer drops into and out of for effect. The drops are themselves load-bearing.

## Signal it landed

A reader can describe who the narrator is, in plain language, as if describing a character. Sentences in the prose can be flagged as the narrator's misfires, not the writer's. The strange events of the story now also reveal something about the kind of person who is willing to narrate them in this way. Trust in the narrator declines without trust in the story declining; the reader, sensing the gap, becomes complicit with the writer rather than with the narrator.

## Probe before & after

**Before.** Read three paragraphs of your draft and ask: who is this narrator? If your only answer is "an omniscient narrator" or "the writer," the voice is neutral. If you can describe the narrator's social station, education, self-image, and the gap between what they are trying to sound like and what they actually sound like, the voice is already doing work.

**After.** Same prompt. The narrator has a recognizable personality, a misplaced emphasis, a digression that misfires, and a relationship to the characters the reader can name. The reader trusts the writer more, the narrator less, and the story is denser as a result.

## Failure mode this prevents

The transparent-camera narrator that lets the events through cleanly while leaving the meaning of the world flat. In a story whose events are strange or whose moral claim depends on the system being shown rather than asserted, a neutral narrator wastes the surface. By making the narrator a character who unconsciously reveals the system that produces him, the prose carries a layer of meaning that no event-level rendering can supply. The opposite failure (writing actual gracelessness rather than great-writer-writing-a-graceless-writer-writing) is also prevented: the discipline of holding the writer above the narrator means every misfire is deliberate.

## Worked micro-example

A story is about an impossible event: a man's nose disappears. The events alone could be told by an omniscient narrator and the story would be a fable. Gogol instead invents a narrator who is himself a minor functionary, full of literary aspiration he cannot quite execute. He pauses to deliver a digression on the nose-finder's "respectability" and proves, via the digression itself, the opposite. He fails to remark on the scale change when the nose becomes a full-sized man in a uniform. He misplaces his emphases throughout (focusing on the rank of the Nose's costume instead of on whether the Nose has a face). The reader sees the events and also sees the kind of person who could narrate these events without being broken by them — a society in which the metaphysical strangeness is metabolized into pedantic ranking. The narrator is the system made audible.
