## When to reach for this

You are in a feedback conversation about something that went wrong, and the accountability discussion feels off in one of two ways. Either the conversation ends very quickly because you accepted all the fault and the other party has nothing more to say, and you feel a private resentment building later. Or you find yourself explaining at length how each factor that contributed was outside your control, and the other person keeps insisting you have not addressed the core problem.

Also reach for this when you notice the pattern across multiple conversations: you consistently feel responsible for things that go wrong around you, or you consistently notice that others are to blame before you notice your own contribution.

## The move

**Identify which pattern is running.** Two profiles:

**Absorber pattern.** When things go wrong, your first move is to accept responsibility, often quickly and completely. Signals: the feedback conversation ends fast because you agreed; you carry responsibility for outcomes that clearly required other people's actions too; you find yourself privately resentful that others are not stepping up; you are prone to staying in situations longer than seems warranted because you believe changing yourself will fix the dynamic. The absorber's learning obstruction: cutting the conversation short by accepting all responsibility prevents you from exploring what actually caused the problem, which means the next occurrence is no more tractable than this one. The chapter's specific caution: absorbers can remain in abusive relationships longer than is safe, because the feedback they receive ("you provoked me") accurately describes their contribution while omitting the harm being done to them.

**Shifter pattern.** When things go wrong, your first move is to identify external causes and other people's contributions. Signals: feedback bounces off because you can always identify a reason why the outcome was not yours to control; you feel victimized, powerless, or assaulted by other people's incompetence or bad intentions; you feel angry or depressed rather than curious when things fail; you explain each failure with a unique external cause (the VCs are fools, the markets are impossible, the team didn't deliver, the brief was wrong). The shifter's learning obstruction: a victim stance makes feedback impenetrable. Because the causes are all external, there is nothing to learn and nothing to change, and the same failures recur.

**Interrupt the pattern.** Once you have named the pattern you are in, apply the systems view from take-three-steps-back. For absorbers: the instruction is not to stop taking responsibility but to insist on looking at the full system, and to hold space for the other people and structural factors that contributed. Accepting all responsibility before exploring the system is not more accountable; it is less accurate and prevents durable learning. For shifters: the instruction is not to find someone else to blame but to locate your own contribution in the cycle. The chapter is explicit: seeing your own contribution to your circumstances makes you stronger, not weaker, because it means there are things you have the power to change.

## Signal it landed

You can name, in one sentence, which pattern was running. You can identify the specific thing you accepted too quickly (absorber) or the specific factor you attributed to others that you could have influenced (shifter). The accountability conversation does not end with you holding all the blame or none of it.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "when the conversation about this failure ended, did it end because the other person ran out of things to say, or because you ran out of explanations?" The first pattern is absorber; the second is shifter. Many people have a default, and situations of emotional stress or fatigue tend to intensify it.

After: ask "can I name both something I am accountable for and something others or the structure contributed to?" Both halves should be answerable. If only one is, the pattern is still running.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming the pattern, absorbers continue to accept blame fully and quickly in ways that feel virtuous but obstruct both learning and fair distribution of accountability. Over time resentment builds and the absorber's capacity for change is exhausted, while others never look at their part. Shifters continue to reinterpret every feedback signal as evidence of others' inadequacy, remaining stuck and increasingly isolated. Both patterns are self-reinforcing: absorbers attract more blame because they accept it; shifters attract more unsolicited feedback because nothing sticks.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter describes Benny, who accepts Alice's feedback that he must bring projects in on time and on budget, and then fixes the problem on his own by inflating timelines and budgets. Benny absorbed the feedback without asking about the board's behavior, Vince's communication failures, or Alice's incomplete briefings. The solution works briefly and then generates new problems, because the system was never examined. This is the absorber version. The chapter does not pair it with a named shifter case, but the structural description is clear: a person who received feedback on the same project failures and explained them by board changes, Vince's silence, and Alice's missed hand-offs, without asking what they themselves might have done differently, would be exhibiting the shifter pattern.
