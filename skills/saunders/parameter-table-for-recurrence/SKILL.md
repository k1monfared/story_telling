## When to reach for this

You have a story (your own or someone else's) whose structure includes an obvious repetition: a sequence of similar scenes, a series of relationships, a recurring ritual, a returning visitor. You sense the repetition is doing structural work but cannot say what. Or you are drafting and the second instance of a recurrence is starting to feel like a copy of the first instead of a meaningful variation, and the reader is about to hit the "long-dream-someone-told-me-about" feeling. Reach for this whenever a story repeats and you need to know what each recurrence is supposed to add.

## The move

1. Identify the repeated unit. Name what is recurring at the same structural level: a relationship (each lover), a scene-type (each daily visit to the Big Slot), a ritual (each pre-battle prayer), an event (each knock at the gate at night). The unit must be the same kind of thing each time, not the same exact thing.
2. From the first instance, extract a list of parameters the story actually specifies for that unit. In Saunders's worked example on Olenka-Kukin: his job, the flavor of their courtship, the flavor of their relationship, how he refers to her, how she refers to him, duration in pages, duration in time, method of parting, period of mourning, the status of the wing of the house. Use what the story specifies, not what you imagine could be specified.
3. Make a row for each parameter and a column for each recurrence. Fill in the first column from the first instance.
4. Walk through each subsequent instance and fill in its column using the same parameters. If the story does not specify a parameter for a later instance, that absence is itself data: the story declined to vary that row, or declined to specify it. Note absences explicitly.
5. Read across each row. Variation across a row is the meaning that recurrence is generating. Read "how he refers to her" across all four columns and you see whether the address-form is intensifying, decaying, or inverting. Read "duration of mourning" across all four columns and you see whether the losses are getting harder to shake. Read "status of the wing" across all four columns and you see a structural undertow the surface narration does not name.
6. For drafting: as you write each recurrence, fill in the table for it before writing. If a row would copy the prior column verbatim, change something on purpose. The variation must be telling, thoughtful, non-robotic. Saunders's failure case is the "9 on a scale of 10" neutralized version, where every recurrence has identical parameters and the story collapses into anecdote.
7. For critique: a row that does not vary across recurrences is either a deliberate constant (a character trait persisting through changing circumstances) or a missed opportunity (the writer forgot to service that parameter). Decide which, and act on it.

## Signal it landed

You can name, for any row in the table, what its variation pattern across the recurrences is telling the reader. You can point to at least one row whose variation generates a felt arc (decline, escalation, accumulation, reversal) that the surface plot summary does not name. For a draft, you can point to at least one row whose variation you adjusted on purpose during the latest pass.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "What is the difference between the first recurrence and the last? What does the story gain by repeating?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer changes from a single thematic abstraction ("she keeps falling in love") to a list of specific row-level variations whose juxtaposition generates the abstraction by inference.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this procedure, repetition reads either as filler (the same beat, a long dream) or as random variation (changes happen but cannot be summed). The writer adds details to the second and third recurrence to keep them from being identical, but adds them by feel, with no schema, so some parameters drift and others stay flat without intent. The reader, sensing the recurrence is supposed to mean something but unable to locate the meaning, drops out. Saunders's diagnosis: a pattern story without controlled variation is a halftime show in which the people on the field drift around in randomly colored street clothes; the difference between a choreographer and a hack is attention to the details of internal dynamics, and the parameter table is what makes those details auditable.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders builds Table 2 from the first relationship in "The Darling" (Olenka and Kukin), specifying eleven parameters: his job, flavor of courtship, flavor of relationship, how he refers to her, how she refers to him, duration in pages, duration in time, method of parting, period of mourning, status of the wing of the house. When the second relationship (Olenka and Vasily) begins, he starts a new chart and notices the column headings stay the same; he then tabulates Smirnin and Sasha against the same headings and lays the four columns side by side. Reading across "how he refers to her" yields the arc Darling → Darling → Don't butt in → Leave me alone, the address-form decaying as the relationships move from worship to insult to silence. Reading across "duration of mourning" yields three months → over six months → some number of years → none required (still together), surfacing both an arc of deepening loss and the fact that the final relationship has not yet ended. Reading across "status of the wing" yields a structural undertow of who is moving in, who is empty, and who Olenka herself migrates to. None of this is in any single sentence of the story. All of it is in the across-row reading the table makes possible.
