## When to reach for this

You are in a feedback conversation and something has gone wrong with the conversation itself rather than with the content. Both of you are repeating yourselves, the heat is rising without resolution, you cannot agree on what you are even arguing about, one of you has shut down, or you are deadlocked and need a fair way to break the impasse. You are not stuck on the substance; you are stuck on the process.

## The move

Step outside the conversation and function as your own referee. The chapter: "Supercommunicators had an exceptional ability to observe the discussion, diagnose where it was going wrong, and make explicit process interventions to correct it. It was as though they were functioning in two roles at once: They weren't just players in the game, they were also referees."

**Three steps:**

1. **Diagnose.** From outside the conversation, name specifically what is stuck. Common patterns:
   - Both are arguing and neither is listening.
   - The conversation has two intertwined topics that need to be separated.
   - Both parties are treating a difference of perspective as a question of truth.
   - The emotional intensity is too high to continue productively.
   - You are deadlocked and need a process to break the tie.

2. **Describe.** Say out loud what you have diagnosed. The chapter's worked examples:
   - "We're both making arguments, trying to persuade the other, but I don't think either of us is listening to, or fully understanding, the other."
   - "I see two issues here, and we're jumping back and forth between them."
   - "I'm not saying my view is right and yours is wrong. I'm saying we see it differently."
   - "I'm shocked by this. My internal voice is saying this simply did not happen that way. I'd like to take a break and come back in a couple of hours."
   - "We're deadlocked. We both need to agree, and we don't."

3. **Propose.** Offer a specific next step or invite the other person to problem-solve about how to go forward. Do not demand; propose or invite. The worked examples continued:
   - "Tell me more about why this is so important to you."
   - "Let's focus on one issue at a time. Which do you want to talk about first?"
   - "I wonder if there are aspects of how you see this that I'm not fully understanding, as opposed to simply disagreeing with? What would you add?"
   - "I'd like to take a break and come back to this in a couple of hours after we've both had time to calm down."
   - "What's a fair and efficient way to decide when we don't agree?"

**Expect it to sound slightly awkward.** The chapter: "All of these examples sound slightly awkward—not how regular people talk. And paradoxically, that's one of the reasons these kinds of interventions can be so powerful." The slight formality is the signal that you have stepped outside the flow of the conversation to name its process. That pause is the point.

The process move is not about the substance: you are not telling the giver what they can or cannot say. You are describing the state of the conversation and suggesting a direction.

## Signal it landed

You have said something out loud about the conversation itself (not its content), and both parties have a shared observation of what was stuck. The conversation has moved off the escalation track and onto a different track. The giver is engaging with your process description rather than continuing to drive the previous direction.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "what is happening to the conversation, not what it is about?" If the answer is something like "we keep repeating ourselves" or "it's getting louder without getting anywhere," a process move is called for.

After: ask "did anything shift in how we were engaging with each other?" If yes, the process move has altered the conversation's trajectory.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without process moves, a stuck conversation stays stuck. Both parties continue doing more of whatever they were doing that was not working: arguing more loudly, listening less, withdrawing further, or repeating the same point more emphatically. The chapter notes that skilled communicators are distinguished precisely by this referee capacity; it is a learnable skill, not a personality trait. The failure mode is treating the stuck conversation as a content problem (trying to say the content differently or more forcefully) when it is actually a process problem (the structure of the exchange is preventing anything new from entering).

## Worked micro-example

The chapter provides five distinct worked examples of process moves, covering five different stuck patterns. The most structurally complete is: "Okay, we're deadlocked. We both need to agree on this, and we don't. Your solution is that I should give in. As a process, that doesn't feel fair to me. On the other hand, I don't know how to break this deadlock, so we've got to figure it out. What's a fair and efficient way to decide when we don't agree?" This contains all three steps: diagnose (we're deadlocked, your proposal is that I give in), describe (that doesn't feel fair as a process), propose (let's figure out a fair and efficient way to break this kind of tie).
