## When to reach for this

A partner finally tells you they've been struggling for weeks and you realize you didn't notice. They were less themselves, sleeping differently, quieter on calls — and you read each as a one-off rather than as a pattern. Reach for this whenever you find yourself surprised by news a partner gives that an attuned reader of them would have already known.

## The move

1. Notice shifts. The partner who is normally chatty has been quiet at three meals in a row. The partner who usually texts in the evening hasn't this week. These are the data of attunement.
2. Don't immediately interpret. The shifts mean something, but not necessarily what you'll first assume. Hold them as questions, not conclusions.
3. Ask. "I've noticed you've been quieter than usual. What's going on for you?" The asking itself is part of attunement — it signals you saw something.
4. Listen for what's underneath the answer. The first answer is usually a surface answer. The deeper state often emerges only when the surface is met with patience.
5. Don't rush to fix. Attunement is the work of seeing accurately; problem-solving is downstream and only sometimes wanted. "Tell me more" is almost always more useful than "have you tried..."
6. Track over time. Attunement isn't a single conversation; it's a practice of continuously updating your model of who this person is right now.

## Signal it landed

Your partner says some version of "I feel really seen by you" or "you got me." When something difficult happens, you noticed before they had to escalate to tell you. The relationship has a quality of being known.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without attunement, partners have to escalate to be seen. Distress becomes shouting; quiet becomes withdrawal; subtle requests become explicit demands. The partner exhausts themselves managing both their actual state and their performance of that state strongly enough for you to notice.

## Worked micro-example

Lila notices that her partner Reese has been less playful for about ten days — replies are shorter, jokes don't land, plans get rescheduled. Instead of waiting for Reese to bring it up, Lila says: "You've been a little quieter than usual. I'm not sure if something's going on or if it's just a tired week, but I wanted to ask." Reese pauses, then says they've been struggling with their other partner and didn't know if it was appropriate to bring it up. Lila listens. The fact of the noticing is itself most of the work; the conversation deepens from there.

## Reflection prompts

- How do you experience your partners as attuning to you?
- How do you know that your partners "get" you and care about your experience?
- In what ways do you already attune to your partners? Where does it come naturally?
- How could you better or differently attune to your partners to support them in feeling even more seen and understood?
