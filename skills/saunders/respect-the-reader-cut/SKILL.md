## When to reach for this

You are revising at the line level. A passage reads acceptably and you cannot point at a wrong word, but it feels heavy. The narrator is naming what the action implies (he was shocked; she was furious; it was unbearable). Or the prose explains a fact the reader has already inferred (their ages, their relationship, what the dropped object means). Or details accumulate that the reader could supply for themselves (the color of the couch, the room someone walked into, the door someone passed through). Reach for this whenever you suspect the writer is doing work the reader would happily do.

## The move

1. Pick the passage. A paragraph or short stretch of prose where the heaviness lives.
2. For each sentence and each clause inside each sentence, ask: "If I cut this, would the reader still know it?" The reader is your imagined intelligent equal, not a beginner. Saunders's frame: a smart person of good taste, a person you would not want to bore.
3. Identify three categories of removable material:
   - **Stated reaction**: language that names what an action would already produce in any attentive reader. ("It was shocking that..." after a shock has been rendered.)
   - **Restated context**: facts the reader can infer from prior pages or from the action itself. (Two characters' relative ages when the relationship was established earlier.)
   - **Default detail**: descriptors and stage directions that do nothing structural. (The color of a couch nobody returns to, a "came into the room" before someone sits.)
4. Cut. Then re-read the cut version, not the original, and run the same audit again. Cuts often expose further cuts: once "it was shocking" is gone, the buildup that justified it may also be gone, leaving a much shorter and cleaner sentence.
5. Test what is left. Does the action still carry the reaction? Does the context still arrive in time? Does the surviving detail do work for what comes next? If yes, the cut earned its place. If no, you cut too much; restore the smallest thing that recovers the missing function.
6. Continue down to a sentence so spare it is at risk of being too thin. Saunders takes "Jane came into the room and sat down on the blue couch" all the way down to "Jane." This is partly a joke and partly a real demonstration: the question "do we even need this sentence" is a real question. If the answer is no, cut the whole sentence and let the next one carry the load.
7. Trust the leap. Saunders cuts "Jim couldn't believe it" because the action of dropping the keys carries that. He cuts the parenthetical that Jim and Sara are the same age because the reader will assume it. The leaps are not heroic; they are routine, made on the assumption that the reader is on the production end with you.

## Signal it landed

For the passage you audited, you can produce a before-and-after pair where the after is materially shorter, the cuts fall into the three categories above, and the reader's experience of the scene has not lost anything they will register. Roughly: the passage is shorter by a noticeable fraction (Saunders cuts thirty-seven words from his Jim/Sara example, about half the original), and you can name, for at least one cut, the work the surviving prose does that previously was being doubled.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, point at a heavy passage and ask: "Why does this feel slow?" Note your answer. It will likely be vague ("the rhythm is off," "it's overwritten").

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if your answer becomes specific: "It felt slow because the narrator named the reaction that the action already produced (here and here), restated the context (here), and added detail that did nothing for what came next (here). The cut version trusts the reader to supply those, and the scene moves at the speed of action rather than the speed of explanation."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this audit, prose accumulates explanation. The writer, anxious that the reader will miss the shock, names it. Anxious that the reader has forgotten an earlier fact, restates it. Anxious that the room is unfurnished, decorates it. The reader, sensing the anxiety, drifts; their experience becomes one of being told things rather than being trusted to take them in. Saunders's diagnosis of why this is fatal goes through the relationship: a story is a frank conversation between equals, and the reader keeps reading because she continues to feel respected by the writer. Every sentence the writer keeps that the reader could have supplied is a small withdrawal of respect. Many small withdrawals compound into a piece the reader leaves before the ending.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders writes "Jane came into the room and sat down on the blue couch," reads it, and starts cutting on the test "do we need this?" "Came into the room" goes (why does she have to enter?). "Down" goes (you cannot sit up on a couch). "Blue" goes (we do not care). "Sat on the couch" — and now, why is it meaningful that Jane sits on a couch? Cut. The sentence becomes "Jane." Saunders calls this a joke, but a serious one: by cutting all the way down, he has preserved the hope of being original; the world of excellence still lies before the writer. Then the second example, less playful: a passage about Jim seeing his ex-wife Sara with a much younger man, in which Jim's reaction is rendered three ways (he could not believe it; it was shocking; it was so shocking that he dropped his keys). Saunders cuts every reaction-naming sentence and trusts the dropped keys to do the work. Thirty-seven words go, about half the passage. He notes the principle: the latter version has more respect for the reader built into it, because the ideas "Jim couldn't believe it" and "it was shocking" are contained in the action of the keys hitting the floor.
