## When to reach for this

You are working on the ending of your own draft and unsure whether it is finished, or you are reading a published story whose ending feels inevitable but whose work you cannot articulate. Reach for this whenever you suspect the difference between "narrative" and "story" lives in the last move and you want to make that difference visible. The same procedure also fires on internal structural units (a digression, a setting passage, a frame device) when you suspect a unit is doing structural work but its plot contribution is invisible.

## The move

1. Pick the cut. Choose a place near the end of the story but before its actual conclusion (the ending variant), or pick an internal structural unit you suspect is doing structural rather than plot work and prepare to cut it from the middle (the pop-out variant).
2. Read the story up to the cut, or read across the cut, ignoring the popped-out unit. Pretend the cut version is the real one.
3. Ask the diagnostic question. For the ending variant: "Is it a story yet?" Saunders's variant: "Is it soup yet?" For the pop-out variant: "Is the seam visible?" If the prose reads continuously across the cut, the seam is invisible — and the seamlessness is data, not a verdict. Note the felt answer.
4. Name what is missing or what was not lost. For the ending variant: what questions are still in the air, what energy has been built up that has nowhere to go, what moment of change has not yet occurred. For the pop-out variant: log the unit on the TICHN cart with the question Saunders gives it, "what were you for?", and read forward looking for the work the unit enabled — characters introduced, complications staged, scenes that exist only because the unit happened.
5. For the ending variant, read the actual ending and ask what it supplies that the truncation lacked. For the pop-out variant, read the rest of the story and watch for moments that retroactively justify the unit: a counterweight to a thematic position, a swimming scene that complicates a speech, a panel of judges set up before a contest, a maid whose presence asks a question the rest of the story will answer.
6. The answer to step 5 is what the unit is doing. You can now use that knowledge two ways: as a diagnostic for your own draft (does my ending do this work? does my digression earn its place?) or as a learning observation (this is what the form requires of an ending or an internal unit here).

## Signal it landed

For the story or draft in question, you can finish two sentences. "Truncated at point X, the piece feels ___ because ___ is still missing." "The actual ending supplies ___, which discharges ___ and produces the feeling of completion." The blanks are filled with specific elements (an event, a memory, a recognition, a return), not with vague evaluations.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of the ending: "What is this ending doing that the rest of the story has not already done?" Note your answer. It will likely be vague.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if your answer becomes specific and points at concrete material the ending introduces (a memory, a glance, a detail) and concrete questions or expectations from earlier in the story that the new material settles or transforms.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this test, you judge endings holistically and cannot revise them. Your ending feels off but you cannot say where the off lives, so you tinker at the level of word choice or pacing while the structural problem (an unanswered question, a built-up energy with nowhere to land, a moment of change that does not arrive) remains untouched. Equally, when learning from another writer's ending, you absorb the satisfaction without identifying its mechanism, and cannot transfer it. Saunders is direct: a story is not a lively narrative that briskly continues then stops; the ending is what makes it story rather than narrative, and you can only see what makes it story by isolating the work the ending does.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders truncates "In the Cart" at the end of the river-fording section, two pages before the actual ending, and asks the reader to feel the result. The truncated version: lonely teacher, possible suitor who turns out to be no possibility, near-humiliation at a teahouse, ruined purchases, end. He names the feeling: anecdotal, harsh, a series of bad things happening to a nice lady; not yet a story. Then he reads the actual ending, in which a glimpse of a stranger on a passing train looks like Marya's dead mother and unlocks a flood of vivid childhood memory, and then dissolves. By comparison, he can name what the actual ending supplies: a moment of change, a flickering-back-alive of something long-dead, the discharge of all the accumulated pity into the sudden glow of restored selfhood. That is the move that converts narrative into story.

The pop-out variant is worked on the rain-caused digression in "Gooseberries." The digression sits between Ivan announcing his story and Ivan beginning to tell it, and it covers eighty-two of the story's three hundred forty-two lines. Saunders performs the cut, splicing across the digression: "Ivan Ivanych heaved a slow sigh and lit his pipe before beginning his story. [two-page cut.] We are two brothers, he began, I, Ivan Ivanych, and my brother, Nikolay Ivanych." The seam is invisible. The cut version reads continuously. Saunders does not infer that the digression is unneeded; he logs it as "a quirk, a goiter" and reads forward, asking what the rest of the story is about to do that the digression made possible. The deferred redemption arrives several pages later: the digression is what allows the swimming scene, the maid Pelageya, the host Alyohin, the rainstorm-as-mood, and the friend Burkin's grumpy referee role — all of which complicate what would otherwise be Ivan's unchallenged anti-happiness lecture. The pop-out test located the unit; the read-forward located its work; the writer's understanding shifts from "this digression is bloat" to "this digression is the entire counterweight machinery the story needs not to collapse into polemic."
