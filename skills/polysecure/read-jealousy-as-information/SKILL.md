## When to reach for this

Jealousy fires — a partner mentions another partner, a date is happening, a connection seems intense — and you feel the whole-body activation: chest tight, mind spinning, urgency to do something. Reach for this before you act on the activation. The activation is information; what it points to is what you need to address.

## The move

1. Treat the signal as accurate at the level of the body, not the level of interpretation. The body has detected something. That doesn't mean your interpretation of what it detected is right.
2. Don't suppress and don't immediately act. The two failure modes are "this jealousy is shameful, I have to hide it" (suppression) and "this jealousy means we have to change something now" (acting on the spike). Neither reads the signal.
3. Ask what the signal is pointing to. Common candidates:
   - An attunement gap: your partner has been less present lately and the new connection feels like further displacement
   - A self-security gap: your own internal scaffolding is thin and the absence of the partner exposes it
   - A history echo: something about this configuration matches an earlier experience that hurt
   - A real concern: there's something in the actual current situation that needs addressing (a broken agreement, a pace mismatch, an unmet need)
4. Address the actual underlying thing. Don't make the partner's other relationship the target if the real signal is "I haven't felt seen by you in a week."
5. Tell your partner what you found, when you can. Not "you're making me jealous" but "I noticed jealousy, here's what I think it's pointing to, here's what I need."

## Signal it landed

You can experience jealousy without immediately treating it as a relationship emergency. You can name what specifically the signal is pointing to (a need, a gap, an echo) and address that. The jealousy spike still happens, but it's data, not a verdict.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this read, jealousy spikes either get acted on (rules, restrictions, ultimatums that don't address the underlying need) or get suppressed (resentment that builds for months until something blows). Neither path resolves what the body is signaling.

## Worked micro-example

Carlos feels a sharp jealousy spike when his partner Min mentions buying a new piece of art with Min's other partner. His first interpretation: "they're building a life together that excludes me." He pauses. Notices what's happening underneath: he and Min haven't done anything special together in two months. The signal isn't really about the art purchase — it's about a lapse in their own ritual of doing meaningful things together. Carlos tells Min: "I noticed jealousy when you mentioned the art. I think it's actually pointing to us not having done anything special in a while. Could we plan something this weekend?" The jealousy resolves; the underlying need gets named.

## Reflection prompts

- When jealousy fires, what's the first interpretation your mind reaches for? Is that interpretation usually accurate?
- What kind of situations reliably trigger jealousy for you? What's common across them?
- Looking back at past jealousy episodes, what was the underlying need? Was it ever actually addressed?
- What does jealousy feel like in your body? How is it different from other forms of distress?
