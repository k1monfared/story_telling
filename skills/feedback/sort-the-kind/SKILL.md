## When to reach for this

You are inside a feedback exchange (or about to start one), and you notice yourself thinking, asking for, or rejecting "feedback" as a single thing. You are about to ask for "more feedback," complain that you "do not get any feedback," or tell yourself this conversation is "feedback" without naming what kind. Reach for this before you proceed.

## The move

1. Pause and ask the categorizing question: which of the three kinds is in play right now?
    - **Appreciation**: communicates "I see you, I know how hard you have been working, you matter." It is fundamentally about relationship and connection, not improvement.
    - **Coaching**: aimed at helping the receiver learn, grow, or change. Two flavors. The first responds to the receiver's need to build capability for a new challenge. The second is prompted by the giver's own feeling of imbalance or hurt in the relationship, where "you should change" is really "this situation should change for me."
    - **Evaluation**: tells you where you stand. It is an assessment, ranking, or rating, always implicitly or explicitly comparative (against others, against a standard, against expectations).
2. Sort the three by what each is for. Appreciation motivates and connects. Coaching accelerates learning, or addresses a relational imbalance. Evaluation aligns expectations and informs decisions.
3. If the feedback in front of you is more than one kind at once (it usually is, especially because every coaching message carries some evaluation), name the mix and which kind dominates.
4. Now decide what to do with what is in front of you, with the kind explicitly named. The needs and the response differ by kind, so the same move does not cover all three.

## Signal it landed

You can label, in one sentence, the kind (or the mix) of feedback you are giving, getting, or seeking. You can answer "what kind?" without falling back on "feedback." The follow-up move you choose (ask for clarification, accept the thanks, push back on the rating, request coaching) is selected for that kind, not for the generic category.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I had to write down which of the three kinds this is, could I?" If the honest answer is "I do not know, it just feels like feedback," you have not yet sorted.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can label the kind in one sentence and, if more than one kind is present, name which kind is loudest or most urgent for you right now.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the sort, three failure modes alternate. You ask for "feedback" generically and receive one kind when you needed another, then walk away dismayed without knowing why. You react to incoming feedback as if all three kinds had the same weight, so a piece of evaluation that should have settled an anxiety gets treated as coaching to argue with, or a piece of coaching that was meant to help you grow gets treated as evaluation that wounds. You complain about a "feedback shortfall" that is really a kind-shortfall: you are getting plenty of one and starving for another, and the lump-category word hides the real gap.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter opens with the post-merger scenario where a colleague says "you do not get feedback?" but the boss has been publicly thanking you in meetings. The friend hears that as feedback (appreciation); the receiver is starving for coaching about a brand-new role (Pacific Rim) and later for evaluation (am I a stopgap or a successor?). The same word, "feedback," covers three different things; each conversation only resolves once the kind is named.
