## When to reach for this

You are about to solicit feedback from a colleague, a manager, a partner, a direct report, or a friend. Your phrasing is forming as something general: "Got any feedback for me?" "How do you think it's going?" "Anything I should be doing differently?" The other person is about to give you a polite answer that costs them nothing and gives you nothing. Reach for this before that exchange happens.

## The move

1. Replace the noncommittal question with the chapter's specific one. Ask: "What do you see me doing, or failing to do, that is getting in my own way?" Or close variants ("...that's getting in our way as a team," "...that's holding back my development on this project"). The phrasing is the move.
2. Notice why each piece of the wording does work. "What do you see me doing" names behavior, observable to them and not to you. "Or failing to do" opens omissions, which are otherwise easy to miss. "Getting in my own way" names the kind of honesty you want (the unflattering kind) and lowers the social cost of saying it (you are framing the request as for-your-own-good, not as inviting attack). The whole question is narrower than "any feedback?" and easier to answer because the topic and the kind are pre-specified.
3. Receive the first answer with curiosity. The chapter notes that the answer often comes timidly: "Well, on occasion I suppose that you sometimes..." If you respond with appreciation and follow-up curiosity ("say more, what does that look like?"), the giver will fill in the picture. If you respond with self-defense or a smile that says you'd rather not hear more, the conversation closes and you taught them not to answer honestly next time.
4. Use the answer as data about your impact, not as a verdict. The skill is about eliciting; what you do with what comes back uses the other blind-spot skills (look-for-pattern-not-counterexample, separate-impact-from-intent, name-which-mirror-you-need for second opinions).
5. If you actually want appreciation, ask for that instead. The chapter is honest that "any feedback?" is sometimes a covert request for appreciation, and the giver may correctly intuit that and supply appreciation. Naming the kind you want (per negotiate-which-kind-you-need) is the cleaner move; this skill is for the case where what you want is blind-spot information.

## Signal it landed

You walk away with described behavior or omission, with described impact, in the giver's words. Not "you're doing great" and not "fine." A test: you could write down two specific things the giver said you do, with one example each, and the impact each is having. If you cannot, the question did not land or the receive-side did not invite the follow-up.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I had to predict the most useful answer this person could give me, can I picture what shape that answer takes?" If the answer in your head is "they'll tell me I'm doing fine, generally," your question has already collapsed it. The shape you should be picturing is "described behavior, with described impact, in their words."

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can replay the answer and identify behavior and impact in it. If the answer was "you're great" or "I can't really think of anything," the question failed; either you asked the wrong version, or you visibly preferred reassurance and the giver gave you what you preferred.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the specific question, the eliciting move runs into two failures the chapter names. In the first, you ask "how am I doing?" and your giver, sensing no real invitation to honesty, gives you appreciation. You walk away comforted and unenlightened. The blind spot is no closer to closing. In the second, your giver tries to be honest but does not know what you want feedback on (this project? our relationship? your leadership? your life?) and produces something so general that you can't act on it. The chapter's analogy: "how was your day?" returns "fine" from a nine-year-old. You taught them, by asking the empty question, that empty answers were what you wanted. By the time you decide you wanted something more, the conversation is over and the next one will start the same way unless you change the question.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter contrasts the noncommittal phrasing ("how am I doing?", "do you have any feedback for me?") with its narrower replacement ("what do you see me doing, or failing to do, that is getting in my own way?") at lines 2018-2023, and walks through the receive-side of the answer: timidly at first, then more clearly if the asker responds with curiosity rather than defense. The chapter places this skill among the practical responses to the prior "you can't see yourself by looking harder" point: since the receiver cannot self-illuminate, the eliciting question must do the work of inviting a specific kind of answer, and the giver needs the topic narrowed before the honesty becomes possible. Annabelle's situation, where two 360s three years apart surfaced the same theme, is the larger frame: she did not have a way to ask the question that would have surfaced the leak earlier in any one relationship.
