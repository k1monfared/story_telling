## When to reach for this

You have already talked to someone about feedback that upset you, and they have supported you. Now the sting has faded and you want to check whether you have missed something worth learning. Or you are about to vent to a friend and want to get more than validation from the conversation.

## The move

**Explicitly request the shift.** Tell the person you already vented to (or are about to vent to) that you want them to function as an honest mirror rather than only a supportive one. Name the difference out loud: "I've been venting and you've been supportive and I appreciate it. But I'd like you to help me sift the feedback for what I might learn. I want you to be honest with me, not just reassuring." Amy made this shift with Hank: "I can usually see where people are coming from, but with Ivan there's something going on that I don't get. I need you to help me with that."

The shift is a request, not a command. Your friend may not have seen enough of the situation to offer a useful honest read. But they are almost always positioned to see more than they have offered while in the supportive mode.

**Use the two-column technique.** Take a piece of paper and draw two columns: what's wrong with the feedback, and what might be right or valid or reasonable. Write down the concerns about the feedback (Ivan's delivery was unprofessional; his comment about being late was in front of eighteen people) in the first column. This acknowledges the legitimate problems without dismissing them. Then focus on the second column: what might be right? The chapter's formulation: "Writing down and discussing what's wrong frees Amy to see what might be right."

Ground rules for the two-column exercise: The two sides do not net each other out. You are not trying to reach a verdict on who was right. You are digging to learn. Each column can have true things in it simultaneously; the point is to separate the delivery problems from the content, and the content problems from the content that might be valuable.

Hank's column for Amy: In "what's wrong," Amy's concerns about Ivan's public scolding and his dismissal of her prior knowledge about signatures. In "what might be right," a pattern Hank had noticed: Amy's habit of needing to have the last word, evidenced by the text exchange that ran during the conference call and her insistence at the end of the phone call with Hank that she really was only two minutes late.

## Signal it landed

You have heard something from your honest-mirror partner that you would not have heard in the purely supportive mode. The second column has at least one item in it that you consider worth examining, even if it is uncomfortable.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "am I getting validation and comfort, or am I also getting a perspective on what might be true in the feedback?" If only the first, the mirror is supportive only.

After: ask "is there anything in the second column that I believe might be worth acting on?" If yes, the honest-mirror mode has produced something useful.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the shift, venting to a supportive mirror ends in relief and validation but no learning. The chapter: "Too often we stop there. We ask our friends to be supportive mirrors so that we can get recentered and feel better. But we miss the opportunity to also ask them to help us sift the feedback itself for anything we might learn." Amy ends her conversation with Hank having gotten reassurance but not the observation that her need-to-be-right pattern was contributing to the Ivan conflict. The honest-mirror version of the same conversation gave her that.
