## When to reach for this

You feel agitated or depleted in a feedback relationship. The feedback may be coming from someone you care about (parent, partner, mentor, boss) who seems to genuinely want to help you. But the cumulative effect is that you feel like you can never do anything right, that your self-acceptance is being eroded, or that the feedback is making things worse rather than better. You are unsure whether to draw a limit or whether drawing one would be overreacting.

## The move

**Step one: Run the diagnostic.** Six questions identify when a boundary is genuinely needed. A single yes does not automatically mean a boundary is required, but a cluster of yes answers is a strong signal.

1. Do they attack your character, not just your behavior? The feedback addresses not what you did but what is fundamentally wrong with you: you are not attractive, ambitious, or good enough, you are not worthy of love and respect the way you are now.

2. Is the feedback unrelenting? You have told them it is not helping, but they continue. They have asked you to stop or adjust and they have not. Unhelpful feedback is useless; relentless unhelpful feedback is destructive.

3. When you do change, is there always one more demand? There is no threshold of improvement that ends the feedback loop. The next critique is always queued. The giving of feedback is an end in itself, not a means to improvement.

4. Does the feedback giver take the relationship hostage? They link your acceptance of their feedback to their acceptance of you. "If you don't do what I say, it means you don't love me." This strips you of autonomy while appearing to leave you free.

5. Are they issuing warnings or making threats? A warning is a good-faith communication of legitimate consequences ("if your people management doesn't improve, we can't keep you in this position"). A threat is a manufactured consequence designed to induce fear or dependence ("if you leave me, no one will ever love you"). The chapter: "A warning is when someone tells you the other shoe may drop; a threat is when they make sure it will squash you."

6. Is it always you who has to change? In every conflict or problem between you, you are the only one who takes responsibility. You apologize, you give in, you absorb. The relationship has no mutual accountability.

An additional criterion: Are your views and feelings a legitimate part of the relationship? Regardless of anything else, does the giver listen to how the feedback is affecting you? Do they care? Are they willing to adjust how they give feedback based on its impact on you? If your feelings are not part of the equation, something is wrong. The chapter quotes Rollo: "It's fine to try to figure out whether the giver is too critical or you're too sensitive, but if the other person isn't listening to you and your feelings, the answer is beside the point."

**Step two: Name the type of boundary.** If the diagnostic confirms that a boundary is needed, identify which of the three types applies:

1. **I may not take your advice.** The softest boundary. You are willing to listen and consider the input. But the choice is yours, and you may not act on it. Use this when the feedback is offered with good intentions and in the absence of any of the diagnostic warning signs, but the giver does not recognize that taking the advice is optional.

2. **I don't want feedback about that subject, not right now.** You are not only asserting the right to decline; you are asserting the right to be free of the topic altogether. The subject is off-limits for now (and possibly permanently). Use this when the subject is one on which you have reached a saturation point, when you have already thought it through, or when you need emotional space to sort it out yourself before receiving anyone else's input.

3. **Stop, or I will leave the relationship.** The starkest boundary. Simply being in the relationship while subject to the ongoing feedback is doing damage to your sense of self. Use this when the diagnostic shows multiple yes answers across the warning signs, when you have already tried softer boundaries and they have not been respected, or when the relationship has become genuinely harmful.

## Signal it landed

You can answer the diagnostic questions and name which ones apply. You can name which of the three boundary types fits the situation. You are no longer unsure whether you are overreacting; you have specific evidence (the yes answers) and a specific response (the type).

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "do I feel like I can never do anything right in this relationship, or do I feel agitated but basically seen?" The first points toward a boundary; the second may not.

After: ask "can I name, in one sentence, which type of boundary I need and why?" If yes, the diagnostic has produced a clear answer and you are ready for the set-the-boundary-clearly skill.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the diagnostic, receivers either stay in genuinely damaging relationships by telling themselves the feedback is legitimate, or draw unnecessary boundaries that damage functional relationships by labeling ordinary difficulty as a problem. The chapter names three common patterns (constant critic, hate-love-hate cycle, renovation relationship) where the absence of a clear diagnostic keeps the receiver stuck: Hunyee absorbs her mother's criticism because she believes it must be legitimate; Henry is too preoccupied with pleasing Isabella to notice she is not listening to him; Brodie dreads work but has not named what is happening as something he can address. The diagnostic provides the vocabulary for naming these patterns and the three-type taxonomy provides the vocabulary for responding.

## Worked micro-example

Henry's case (renovation relationship): Isabella's feedback started with wardrobe suggestions and expanded to cover every aspect of his life. He is genuinely unhappy and has told her so, but she responded that he is too sensitive to feedback. Rollo's diagnosis: "Is the feedback giver not listening to you and your feelings?" For Henry, the answer is yes. That is the decisive signal. The diagnostic question about feelings legitimacy has answered the "is a boundary needed?" question. The type: at minimum, boundary type 2 (I don't want coaching about these subjects); realistically, type 3 (stop or I will leave or change the terms of the relationship).
