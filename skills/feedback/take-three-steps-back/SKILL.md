## When to reach for this

Feedback has arrived and something about it feels unfair or off. Your first thought is "but you are the one who..." or "if you had just..." You can articulate the other person's contribution to the problem more easily than your own. Or you have received the same feedback from this person many times and it never resolves, because every conversation ends in competing accounts of whose fault it is.

Also reach for this when you are the giver and the conversation keeps ending in stalemate, both parties genuinely believing they see the real cause.

## The move

The three vantage points are applied in order from close to wide.

**One step back: You + Me intersections.** Ask what the combination of your tendencies and theirs is producing. The framing is not "what is wrong with you" but "what is the specific you-and-me configuration that creates the friction?" Observe the cycle: your reaction to their behavior produces a behavior in you that reinforces their behavior. Sandy copes with financial stress by spending; Gil copes by monitoring. Each one's coping intensifies the other's coping, producing a downward spiral. Neither is wrong in isolation; together they generate the problem. Locate your own part in the cycle before naming theirs.

**Two steps back: Role clashes.** Add the layer of the roles each person occupies. Ask whether the conflict would exist between strangers cast in these roles, regardless of personality. Two people at opposite ends of a rope doing their jobs are in a tug-of-war before they have said a word to each other. Common role tensions arise from structural design (compliance and trading, sales and legal, surgeons and anesthesiologists), from role confusion (three people who each believe they own task A, and no one who believes they own tasks B through D), and from role change (yesterday we were peers; today you are two levels above me). For each, ask: how much of the friction is role structure, and how much is the person? You cannot always answer, but asking changes the conversation.

**Three steps back: The big picture.** Add other players, processes, policies, the physical environment, timing and decision structures, and the coping strategies people have developed to work around all of the above. In the refinery injury example, the worker's behavior is one factor. The maintenance records, the overtime policy, the performance evaluation incentive, the shift-change communication protocol, and the training budget are also factors. In the Benny/Alice/Vince/board chain, Benny's late delivery is the presenting problem; the board's habit of changing requirements, Alice's failure to pass those changes on, and the absence of any honest accounting of how changes affect timelines are the structural contributors. Ask: what other players are shaping the choices I and the other person are making? What processes or structures are reinforcing the pattern?

**Circling back to yourself.** After all three steps, ask: what am I doing, or failing to do, that is contributing to this dynamic? This step returns the frame to something you can act on without waiting for the other person to change.

**Using the system to create change.** Once the contours of the system are visible, look for structural changes that do not require anyone to change their personality. Shift roles, revise processes, change the environment. The chapter notes that Me + Everybody patterns (feedback that recurs across relationships) are among the most tractable: when one person changes, multiple systems improve simultaneously.

## Signal it landed

You can draw the cycle: my behavior (A) produces their reaction (B), which reinforces my behavior (A). You can name one role factor that would produce friction between any two people in these roles, regardless of their characters. You can name at least one other player, process, or structure that is constraining the choices both of you are making. The feedback no longer feels like an accusation aimed only at you, nor like something you can dismiss by pointing at the other person. It is a system problem with multiple levers.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "whose fault is this?" If you answered without hesitation, you have not yet seen the system. A felt sense of clear, one-directional blame is the exact signal that stepping back is needed.

After: ask "what would need to change for this to get better?" If the answer is only "they would need to change," the system view has not yet landed. The skill has landed when you can name something you can change, something they could change, and at least one structural thing that could change, and all three feel genuine.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the system view, feedback conversations about recurring problems cycle through two competing accounts of who is really at fault. Each party genuinely sees the other's contribution and genuinely does not see their own. The cycle ends in stalemate, mutual resentment, and a coping strategy (Sandy hides the Grape-Nuts) that introduces a new set of problems. The chapter's label for the downstream version of this failure: "fixes that fail." When feedback is aimed at one component of the system and the other components are left unchanged, solutions produce new problems (Benny doubles his budget estimates; the board uses the extra time to pile on more requirements). The system generates the same outcome through a different path.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter opens with a snoring husband and a noise-sensitive wife. From inside the problem, each person sees only the other's contribution. The systems view at one step back shows an intersection: his snoring plus her sensitivity together produce the sleeplessness. Neither is "bad"; together they create the problem. The chapter's framing: "It takes the two of you being the way you are to create the problem. That's how systems work." At two steps back: if they also have a role dynamic (one partner has medical needs, one has a history of minimizing medical concerns), that adds to the tally. At three steps back: the white noise machine he bought is a coping strategy that failed because it was imposed rather than agreed on; that history now colors her refusal to try solutions he proposes.
