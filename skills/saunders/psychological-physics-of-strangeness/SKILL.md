## When to reach for this

Your story has, or is about to have, an event that could not happen in the real world. A talking object, a body part that walks off, an arrival from outside the consensus world, a premise the reader will recognize as impossible. You can imagine writing the strange event itself, but you cannot say what it is going to mean. You suspect, correctly, that the meaning is not in the strangeness but in the reactions to it, and you have not yet decided what those reactions will be. Reach for this whenever the strangeness is the easy part and the world's response is the hard part.

## The move

1. Locate the strange event. The nose in the bread, the talking gloves, the body part with a mind, the impossible arrival. State it as one sentence and place it in the manuscript.
2. Refuse to defend the strangeness. Do not write paragraphs justifying how the impossible thing got there. The reader will accept any premise, briefly, on offer. The defense is not where the meaning is.
3. Compose the immediate reactions of the characters present. For each character in the scene, ask: how does this person respond to the impossibility? Their response must be specific, particular to who they are, and disproportionately concerned with something other than the impossibility itself. Saunders's worked beat: Praskovya is not dumbfounded by the nose; she leaps to the (skewed) explanation that her husband cut it off a client, and Ivan, instead of pointing out the impossibility, accepts the accusation and worries about the police finding it.
4. Read the reactions across all the characters together and ask: what rule do these reactions imply? Not what symbol or theme, what rule. (In Gogol's nose-world, the rule is something like: the social calculation of blame outranks the metaphysical fact of the impossibility.) The rule is the world's psychological physics.
5. Test the rule against our own world. Saunders's criterion: a strange-premise story feels truthful "to the extent that the psychological physics of the fictive world are felt to be similar to the psychological physics of our own." If the rule you have surfaced ("when something disastrous happens, people worry about being blamed before they ask what happened") is recognizable as a way our world actually works, the strangeness will mean. If the rule is arbitrary or merely whimsical, the strangeness will read as a stunt.
6. Compose the reactions of the wider world. The bystanders, the officials, the offices Kovalyov visits. Their mildness, their strict professionalism, their inability to see this as their problem are themselves the meaning-making engine. Saunders's note: "Their reactions are strangely mild, as if Kovalyov has lost not his nose but a suitcase." The mildness is the rule made visible.
7. Accustom the reader by stages. Each later strangeness should land inside the system the earlier reactions established, so that by mid-story the reader will accept (without remarking) something the opening would have rejected. Saunders names the moment: by the newspaper-office scene "we're now arguing earnestly for the ad to be run, having accepted not only the reality of a nose's conversion into a person but the idea that a notice in a newspaper might help us find it."
8. Do not aim for a closed system. The shards-not-vase principle: the story does not have to add up on inspection. It must read as coherent on the first pass, the way snapshots sell as continuous motion. The procedure is to compose tile-by-tile, holding only the local reaction as your obligation, not the long-form audit of consistency.

## Signal it landed

A reader (or you, freshly) can describe what the world's rules are, in plain language, without using the word "absurd." The reactions to the impossibility produce specific, recognizable claims about how power, blame, mildness, or social calculation actually work. The strangeness no longer reads as a stunt; it reads as the most efficient way the story could have surfaced the rule. When asked "what does this story mean," your answer is a sentence about how a kind of system actually behaves, not a sentence about the symbolism of the impossible event.

## Probe before & after

**Before.** Pick the strangest event in your draft. Write down, in one sentence, what it means. If your sentence is a paraphrase of the event ("the nose disappears, which represents alienation"), the strangeness is doing the work the world should be doing.

**After.** Same prompt. The sentence is now about a rule of the fictive world that resembles a rule of our own world. ("In a system where blame is socially distributed before facts are established, an impossibility produces not investigation but defensive scrambling.") The event is the occasion; the rule is the meaning.

## Failure mode this prevents

Strange-premise stories that read as merely whimsical, surreal-for-its-own-sake, or "absurdist" in the dismissive sense. The writer puts the meaning into the strangeness ("a talking nose represents X") and writes the reactions perfunctorily. The reader feels the strangeness without feeling the truth claim. The story collapses to a one-line premise plus narrative filler. By placing the meaning at the response and calibrating the response so its rules resemble our own, the strange-premise story becomes (in Saunders's phrase) a form of supreme realism rather than a stunt.

## Worked micro-example

A nose appears in a loaf of bread. The husband, a barber, accepts his wife's accusation that he cut it off a client, even though the nose was not there yesterday and the bread was made this morning. He decides, with her, to ditch the nose. He worries about being blamed by the police. He fails to ditch it cleanly because the world he goes out into is full of people who will see him and policemen who will stop him. None of these reactions investigate the impossibility. All of them accept it as the local fact and react to its social consequences. The rule of the world surfaces: in this system, the metaphysical strangeness is irrelevant; the social calculation of who gets blamed is what counts. That rule is recognizable; it is how parts of our own world actually work. The strangeness, by being treated as ordinary, becomes a vehicle for an exact realism.
