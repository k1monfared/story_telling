## When to reach for this

Someone is giving you feedback (or has just given it), and you notice your attention has drifted from "what is this person saying" to "what is wrong with what this person is saying." You are mentally cataloguing inaccuracies, exceptions, outdated assumptions, missing context, the wrong people consulted, the wrong frame applied. The feedback may indeed have something wrong with it. Reach for this before you let that something become your reason for closing the case.

## The move

1. Notice that the question driving your attention has become "what is wrong with this feedback" rather than "what is this person saying." This is the chapter's named habit, wrong spotting, and the chapter's claim is that we are very good at it because there is almost always something to find: it is 2+2=5 wrong, or different-planet wrong, or used-to-be-right, or right according to the wrong people, or right for you but wrong for me, or right but unhelpful.
2. Do not deny that the something-wrong exists. The chapter is explicit that wrong spotting works because feedback usually does have parts that are off, vague, or based on incomplete information. The move is not to pretend otherwise.
3. Pause the verdict anyway. Separate two questions that you are about to collapse: "do I understand what this person is actually saying" and "is what they are saying correct." The chapter's claim is that the second cannot be answered well until the first has been. Your mind wants to skip the first because the second feels more urgent.
4. Reroute attention to the first question. Concretely: stop building objections, start asking yourself what the giver actually means under their words. The next moves in the chapter (spot the label, separate data from interpretation, clarify where the feedback is going, difference spot) are what you do once attention is rerouted. This skill is the upstream switch that makes them possible.
5. Reserve the right to disagree later. Understanding is not the same as agreeing, and the chapter is clear that you may end up disagreeing more clearly after you understand. The deferral of the verdict is what protects the understanding, not what concedes it.

## Signal it landed

You can name, in one sentence, what the giver is actually saying — not your refutation of it, not the part that is wrong with it, but the claim itself in your own paraphrase. Your follow-up move is a clarifying question, not a counter-argument. If you still want to disagree at the end of the conversation, you can articulate what you understood that you are disagreeing with, rather than dismissing on the strength of the something-wrong you spotted in the first thirty seconds.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If the giver were listening to my internal monologue right now, what would they say I was doing — listening to understand them, or listening to find the wrong part?" If the honest answer is the second, wrong spotting is running.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if the internal monologue has switched from cataloguing what is wrong to constructing the giver's view in their terms, well enough that you could explain it back to a third party who had not heard them.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the catch, two failure modes alternate. In the first, you spot a real wrong, latch onto it as the verdict on the whole, dismiss the rest of the feedback by association, and lose the parts that were not wrong. Wrong spotting, the chapter notes, defeats wrong feedback successfully but defeats learning along with it. In the second, you spot a smaller wrong, decide it would be petty to dismiss the whole over it, and so you swallow the whole feedback uncritically — the same skipped-the-understanding step, just collapsed in the other direction. Both failures share a structure: the verdict was reached before the content was understood. The receiver gives up the chance to learn from feedback that was partially right and partially wrong (which is most feedback) because the binary of right-or-wrong was applied before the content of the message was clear.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter opens with Holly, a public defender hearing from her supervisor Irwin that she gets "too enmeshed" with clients. Holly hears the feedback, decides it is wrong (she grew up on these streets, she knows what a fighter in your corner looks like), and quietly resolves not to act on it. Later the chapter walks through what Irwin actually meant — not "care less" but "watch the specific pattern of giving small amounts of cash to clients who need much more, because it costs you their professional trust" — and Holly's wrong-spot dismissal turns out to have been a dismissal of feedback she might have used. The chapter's wider catalogue of "kinds of wrong" (2+2=5, different-planet, used-to-be-right, etc.) is the diagnostic for catching yourself running this pattern in real time. Paul's preparation before talking with Monisha at the end of the chapter is the disposition installed: "his purpose in the conversation is not to accept or reject, but first to understand."

## Source

Chapter 3, "First Understand," lines 1118-1147 (the wrong-spotting catalogue), 1153-1158 ("Understanding Is Job One"), and 1420-1429 (the explicit pivot to "tell me more"). Stone and Heen frame wrong spotting as the receiver's habitual entry into feedback and name the costs: it succeeds at filtering bad feedback and fails at filtering for learning. The skill installs the upstream catch before any of the chapter's downstream understanding moves can run.
