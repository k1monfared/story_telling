## When to reach for this

You're with your partner but your mind is somewhere else — work, another partner, the phone, an upcoming logistics question. Reach for this whenever you notice the time together has become low-quality background even though the calendar says you're together. In nonmonogamous configurations, this matters extra: time per partner is finite, so each shared interval has to actually be present.

## The move

1. Notice when you're not present. The flag is usually subtle — half-listening, looking at your phone, mental wandering. The first work is just to notice.
2. Decide whether you can be present in this moment. Sometimes the answer is no: you're exhausted, distracted by something urgent, post-stress and not regulated. Naming this is honest. Pretending presence is worse than naming its absence.
3. If yes: put the distractions away. Phone in another room, not just face-down. The visible phone is itself a signal of half-attention.
4. Bring your body into the room. Eye contact, physical proximity, slow breath. Presence is a body state, not a mental decision.
5. Ask one question and listen to the answer. Listen as if the answer mattered — because the listening is what produces presence as much as anything.
6. When you return from another partner, fully arrive. The transition from one connection to another is its own work; don't show up half-residing in the place you came from.

## Signal it landed

The other person can tell you're there. They share something they wouldn't have shared if you'd been half-present. The interaction has the quality of "we just spent meaningful time together" rather than "we were in the same room."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without active presence, time with each partner accumulates as low-grade hours that don't deepen the relationship. The partner reads the half-attention as "I'm not important enough for full presence" and either protests, withdraws, or starts to disinvest. Nonmonogamy can amplify this: when each partner gets less time, low-quality time doesn't compensate.

## Worked micro-example

Tomas comes home after dinner with another partner and finds his nesting partner Erica reading. He sits down nearby and starts checking his email. Erica says nothing but the quality of her presence shifts. Tomas notices, sets the phone in another room, and asks her about the book. She tells him the part she just read; he listens; she puts the book down. Twenty minutes of full presence does what two hours of co-existing wouldn't.

## Reflection prompts

- How do you show that you are here with your partners? How do you show when you're not?
- Are there ways you could be more present with your partners than you currently are?
- How do your partners signal that they are here with you? When that's missing, how do you experience it?
- What rituals around departures and arrivals would help you fully arrive when transitioning between partners?
