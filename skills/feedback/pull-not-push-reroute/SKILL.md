## When to reach for this

You are mid-feedback, or replaying a feedback conversation afterward, and your mind is producing a running list of why the giver is not qualified to evaluate you, what they got wrong about your situation, how badly they delivered it. The list feels accurate. It is also a sealed loop that produces no usable change. Reach for this whenever you notice that loop running.

## The move

1. Notice the inventory. The signal is a string of sentences whose subject is the giver, not the work or the relationship: "she doesn't even understand what I do," "his timing was awful," "they have no standing to say this."
2. Stop the inventory and ask the rerouting question: "Granting everything in that list is true, what is here that I could pull?" Pull means drive your own learning, manage your resistance, find any insight that might help, ask for what you need, or stand up for how you see it.
3. Generate at least one receiver-side action: a question to ask, a piece of the feedback to take seriously even if the rest is wrong, a clarification to seek, a boundary to name, an experiment to run.
4. Decide what to do with the giver-inventory separately. It may be valid, but it is now a separate file (e.g., a relationship issue to handle later, a feedback-quality complaint to raise on its own).

## Signal it landed

You finish the encounter with at least one concrete next move on your side, written in the first person. The giver-inventory may still exist, but it is no longer the only output. You can answer "what did I get from this?" with something other than a critique of the giver.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask yourself: "If I had to write down what I am taking away from this feedback, what would I write?" If the answer is mostly about what is wrong with the giver or how badly they delivered it, you are inside the inventory.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer now contains at least one receiver-side action item, even if the giver-criticism still stands alongside it.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without rerouting, you spend the encounter and the hours after it perfecting the case against the giver. The case may be airtight. It also leaves you with no learning, no clearer asks, no boundaries set, and no decisions made. The receiver controls intake, sense-making, and change, so when attention is parked on the giver's deficiencies, the leverage point sits idle. You walk away validated and unchanged, which is the receiver-side equivalent of the giver pushing harder and getting nowhere.

## Worked micro-example

The book opens the introduction by noting that when people describe difficult feedback, both parties slip into the same move. As givers, we notice the receiver isn't good at receiving. As receivers, we notice the giver isn't good at giving. The intro names the receiver's version of this loop directly: Tom's boss gives him a dressing-down about his organizational skills, and on the drive home Tom silently catalogues his boss's inadequacies, even pulling over to write the list down. The reroute is the move Tom does not make.

## Source

Introduction "From Push to Pull," lines 367-386. Stone and Heen frame the receiver, not the giver, as the leverage point: "the receivers are in control of what they do and don't let in, how they make sense of what they're hearing, and whether they choose to change," and so "the real leverage is creating pull." The reroute is the moment-to-moment application of the book's central reframing.
