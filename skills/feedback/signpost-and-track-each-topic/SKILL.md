## When to reach for this

You have just spotted that a feedback exchange has split into two topics: the giver's original feedback and a relationship concern of yours (delivery, credibility, trust, appreciation, autonomy, or acceptance). You are about to do one of two things, both bad. Option one: keep going on what looks like one topic, telling yourself you can resolve both as you go. Option two: switch onto your own topic without naming the move, and hope the giver follows. The chapter's prescription is neither.

## The move

1. Step outside the conversation and name the split out loud. The chapter is explicit that this is unnatural and that the unnaturalness is part of why it works: it interrupts the reactive pattern by being hyper-explicit about what is happening. Use your own words, but name two things: that there are two topics, and that both deserve conversation.
2. Use the chapter's signposting template, in your own phrasing: "I see two related but separate topics for us to discuss. They are both important. Let's discuss each fully but separately, finishing one before swinging back to the other."
3. State each topic concretely. The giver's topic in their words ("you'd like me to spend less time with Howard and more with the other children"). Your topic in your words ("the way I'm getting feedback this year, including the fact that this is the first piece of feedback I've heard"). Both topics named, neither minimized.
4. Decide the sequence using the chapter's two-factor rule:
    - **Default**: edge to the original feedback. That is what the giver came to discuss; all things being equal, start with their topic.
    - **Override**: if your relationship trigger reaction is loud enough that you will not actually take in their topic, name the trigger and propose your topic first ("I want to engage what you're saying about Howard, and I notice I'm too triggered by the appreciation question to hear it well; can we talk about the second one first, briefly, so I can come back to the first?"). The override exists because the chapter notes that under sufficient activation, you will not actually hear the giver's topic anyway, so processing yours first is in service of getting back to theirs, not avoiding it.
5. Hold the discipline of one track at a time. The point of signposting is that both of you are on the same topic at the same time. When you finish one, swing back to the other deliberately. The signposting does not work if you signpost and then immediately re-tangle.

## Signal it landed

The conversation now has a visible structure both of you can name: there are two topics, you are talking about one, you have agreed to come back to the other. The giver knows what you have agreed to discuss now and what you have agreed to discuss next. You are no longer covertly steering the conversation toward your topic; you are openly proposing the order.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "Does the giver know that there is a second topic on my side, and does the giver know which topic we are discussing right now?" If the answer to either is no, the conversation is still tangled, regardless of how the words are landing.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can answer yes to both: the giver knows there is a second topic, and both of you know which one is on the table at this moment.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without signposting, two failures alternate. In the first, you stay tangled: both of you continue on parallel tracks, each hearing the other's words through your own topic's filter, each accumulating evidence that the other is missing the point. The conversation does not resolve because resolution requires being on the same topic at the same time, and you are not. In the second failure, you switch unilaterally. You quietly drop the giver's topic and pull onto yours without naming the move. The giver feels unheard on their topic and unfooted on yours, because you arrived without warning, and the conversation either escalates or collapses. The chapter notes that signposting is unnatural; that is the cost of the move. The cost of skipping it is that the conversation defeats the feedback in both directions.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's worked case is Ella, a teacher's assistant who has spent eight months designing activities, collecting supplies, and devoting extra time to a class of children with disabilities. Her supervising teacher's first piece of feedback all year is "you're spending too much time focused on Howard. There are nine other children in this class." Ella's switchtrack is silent: she leaves the room with her objections trapped in her head ("after eight months, the first piece of feedback I get is that I'm caring too much?"). The chapter walks through what would be different if she signposted instead. After calming down, Ella returns and says (paraphrased): "Let's talk about Howard and how I'm spending my time. That's important. This is also the first time I've gotten feedback. So after we talk about Howard, I want us to come back to the question of how I get feedback and what you notice in my work with the kids that is positive." Both topics named, sequence proposed, original feedback given the edge, second topic preserved for after. The chapter also offers Louie's hypothetical signposting move at the end of the chapter as the same template applied to the rose scene: "Okay, I forgot you don't like roses. You should remind me again why. And then I have to say that I'm feeling a little underappreciated for my effort. We should talk about both."

## Source

Chapter 5, "Don't Switchtrack: Disentangle What from Who," lines 2622-2648 ("Give Each Topic Its Own Track" and "Signposting"). Stone and Heen offer signposting as the operational response to a spotted switchtrack: name both topics out loud, propose a sequence, and discuss each fully and separately. The chapter explicitly notes that signposting is not a natural conversational move because it requires stepping outside the conversation; that absence of flow is what gives it its corrective power. The decision rule for sequencing (default to the giver's topic, override only when your trigger is loud enough to prevent hearing) is the second half of the move and is what turns the script into a procedure.
