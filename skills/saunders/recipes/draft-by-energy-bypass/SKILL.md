## When to reach for this

You are a writer whose recent work has been coming back as "interesting" or "ambitious" rather than producing laughter, tears, hunger to read more. You have read enough craft advice to name the deficit. When you sit down at the page, you channel the writer you imagine yourself to be, and that writer's habitual mode is the slack one. Audit-shaped revision skills have not helped because the writer running the audit is the writer who produced the slack. Reach for this when you need the full sequence: diagnose the trap, bypass it in a single session, sustain the surfaced register into a real draft, and revise that draft to a highly organized state.

## The move

1. **Run the energy probe.** Apply `energy-not-aspiration`. Set aside the question of whether your prose is good in the abstract. Ask whether your readers (friends, editors, your own honest re-reads) react with energy or with respect. List the off-license modes your writer-self-image has been keeping out of the real work (humor at awkward moments, a working-class register, a corporate voice, a raunchy impulse). Identify, by recent evidence, which mode actually generates energy — laughter on a kitchen table, a friend reading aloud unbidden, a piece of writing you almost threw out because it was not "real" writing. The output of this step is the named off-license mode and the commitment to follow whatever produces the energy. Without this step you have no way to know which voice the next step's bypass should sustain.

2. **Bypass the writer-self-image with a constraint exercise.** Apply `escalation-by-vocabulary-constraint`. Set a 45-minute timer; write a 200-word piece using only 50 unique words; pre-install a read-aloud constraint. The conscious mind is busy counting words, which lets a less conscious, more playful part of your mind run the prose. The constraint makes elaboration uneconomic and rising action cheap. Read the result aloud and write down two answers in plain prose. What was different about this draft compared to my usual drafts? What does the writer who produced this draft do that the writer I usually channel does not do? The output of this step is a 200-word piece with rising action that your habitual writer-self-image would not have produced, plus a named catalogue of what the surfaced writer does differently. This step depends on step 1 because without the energy probe you cannot tell whether the surfaced register is the one to carry forward or one of several you would have to choose among.

3. **Sustain the surfaced register into a real draft.** Apply `follow-the-voice`. Use the constrained piece (or a fragment of language whose register matched what step 2 surfaced) as the seed. Imitate the voice. Write a paragraph or a page sustaining the voice with almost no other goal in mind. Let the world emerge — vocabulary forces idiom, idiom forces setting, setting forces situation. Apply the escalation engine: keep putting new events in front of the character so the voice has to find new registers in response. Beat back conceptions of "outcome" or "message" as they arrive. The output of this step is a draft of meaningful length whose setting, cast, and situation you can describe but did not plan, and whose voice is recognizably the off-license one step 2 surfaced. This step depends on step 2 because without the bypassed-self-image draft, the writer trying to "follow the voice" is the same writer-self-image step 1 diagnosed; you'd be following an aspirational voice, not the one that produces energy.

4. **Revise per-decision by P/N reaction.** Apply `iterative-preference-revision`. Read the draft top-to-bottom as a first-time reader; watch the meter on your forehead drop to N; wait for the fix to present itself; mark the change; continue. Aim for a single full pass per session, dozens or hundreds of passes over the life of the draft. Apply your editing basis without defense or rationalization. The terminal product is a highly organized system: pronounced causation, precisely selected elements, decisive escalation, everything to purpose. This step depends on step 3 because iterative revision needs prose to react to; without a sustained-voice draft, the per-decision loop has nothing to operate on.

## Signal it landed

You have four artifacts at the end of the chain. (a) A named off-license mode plus an energy reading on it — at least one piece of recent work in that mode produced an energetic response (laughter, tears, eagerness, alarm) that your old mode was not producing. (b) A 200-word constrained piece with rising action where your slack drafts had none, plus a written catalogue of what the constrained-mode writer did differently from the writer you usually channel. (c) A draft of meaningful length whose voice you can trace back to the constraint-surfaced register, with at least one moment when a new event ("the horse") forced the voice to find a register it had not used before. (d) A revised version of (c) in which you can name the four-axis terminal product (causation, selection, escalation, to-purpose) of the iterative method, and you can describe a recent revision session in concrete reaction terms (where the needle dropped, what fix you made, where it dropped next).

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "Why is my work coming back as 'interesting' rather than alive, and what would I do differently?" Note your answer. It will likely be theoretical (a definition of energy, a quote about voice) or fatalistic ("this is just how I write").

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer becomes a procedural account of what you did at each of the four steps, with reference to specific artifacts you produced (the constrained piece, the sustained-voice draft, the revised pass).

## Failure mode this prevents

The writer who has named the deficit but cannot escape it stays loyal to the writer they imagined being and produces inert work indefinitely. Each individual step has its own failure mode that the chain's ordering prevents.

**Skipping step 1 (no energy probe):** the constraint exercise in step 2 surfaces some voice, but the writer has no criterion for which voice to keep, and may sustain a voice that is merely novel rather than energetic. The writer reverts to the writer-self-image after the exercise because they did not first commit to following whatever produces the energy.

**Skipping step 2 (no constraint bypass):** the writer who has identified the off-license mode in step 1 still cannot generate from it because the writer who sits down to draft is the one channeling the writer-self-image. Step 1 supplies the diagnosis but not the route past the trap; step 2 is the route.

**Skipping step 3 (no sustained voice):** the writer has a 200-word constrained piece and a named catalogue of moves but no draft of meaningful length. The constraint exercise's value is the proof of capability, but a 200-word piece is not yet a story. Without follow-the-voice's escalation engine and world-emergence, the writer has a fragment, not a draft to revise.

**Skipping step 4 (no iterative revision):** the writer has a sustained-voice draft but the prose is at first-draft strength. The four-axis terminal product (causation, selection, escalation, to-purpose) cannot emerge from a first-draft voice-piece; it emerges from cumulative micro-decisions. Without step 4 the chain produces an interesting draft but not a highly organized system.

**Doing the steps out of order:** step 4 before step 3 line-edits a 200-word fragment to perfection; step 3 before step 2 follows whatever voice the writer-self-image is channeling; step 2 before step 1 surfaces a voice but loses it after the session because no commitment was made to follow energy over aspiration.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders does not walk all four steps on a single project in one chapter. He walks step 1 in "The Heart of the Story" (the autobiographical case of abandoning Hemingway-mountain after his wife laughs at the Seussian poems on the kitchen table). He walks step 2 in "Appendix B" (the practice protocol with the read-aloud intensifier and the Doug Unger on-the-spot anecdote as parallel evidence). He walks step 3 in "Afterthought #5" (the worked autobiographical case for "Jon" — a line from a student paper becomes the seed, he riffs on the voice, and within a page has "Privacy Tarps" and "Gender Areas" and the confined-teenagers facility). He walks step 4 in "Afterthought #2" (Stan and the railroad; the P/N meter; lather/rinse/repeat across hundreds of drafts; the apartment-redecoration analogy). The composition of the four into one workflow is implicit in his career arc: the post-Hemingway-mountain Saunders runs steps 2-4 on every project, and the *CivilWarLand* book is the worked instance of all four steps composed.
