## When to reach for this

You are reading closely, revising, or teaching, and you have arrived at a craft choice that is doing real work but resists analysis. A description that gives a scene a specific tone. An adjective that changes how the rest of the paragraph reads. A weather choice that colors a meeting. Reach for this whenever you find yourself saying "I can feel it but I can't say what it's doing." The same procedure scales up to whole structural units (a digression, a sub-plot, a frame device) when you suspect the unit is doing complicating work but cannot name the complications.

## The move

1. Identify the choice. The actual sentence, image, adjective, weather, location, or detail on the page; or, at the structural scale, the digression, sub-plot, framing device, or section you suspect is keeping the story from collapsing into a position paper.
2. Write the inverted version. Compose, briefly, an alternate that makes the opposite choice while keeping everything else the same. The road getting better instead of worse. A cheerful teahouse instead of a rough one. A character laughing instead of crying. At structural scale: the same story without the digression, without the sub-plot, without the frame. Stay roughly faithful to the surrounding context.
3. Hold the two side by side. Read the actual paragraph (or section, or whole story), then the counterfactual. Notice which feels different and where the difference lands.
4. Name what the actual version does that the counterfactual does not. Try to put it in concrete terms: a sense of foreboding, a registering of social distance, a setting up of a later turn. At structural scale: a list of specific complications the inverted version loses (a counterweight character; a mood the storm sets up; a maid whose presence asks a question the story will answer; a swim that contradicts a speech).
5. Check that the named effect connects to something else in the story. The actual version, once named, should turn out to be doing work for what comes next: priming a turn, deepening an established mood, placing a character in a position the rest of the story will use. At structural scale: the lost complications should turn out to be exactly what keeps the story from collapsing into lecture or anecdote.
6. If the counterfactual is genuinely just as good, the original choice is arbitrary. That is also useful information: arbitrary choices are candidates for cutting or replacing.

## Signal it landed

You can produce a sentence of the form "The actual version does X; the counterfactual does Y; the story uses X for the following purpose at this later moment." The purpose is concrete and points at a specific later moment in the story, not at a general mood.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of the choice: "Why is this version better?" Note your answer. It will likely be something like "it just is" or "it sets the mood."

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer becomes specific and connected to the rest of the piece. "It is better because the steadily-worsening road primes the reader for the later moment when the river ruins her purchases; an improving road would have read those same purchases as bounty rather than loss."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this comparison, craft talk stays at the level of taste. You report that a passage is "well-written" or "evocative" without being able to say what it is evoking and why that evocation matters two pages later. You cannot transfer the move to your own work because you never named it. In revision, you cannot tell which of your own descriptions are doing structural work and which are decorative, so you preserve them all and the draft bloats.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders pauses at Chekhov's description of the road as the cart enters the woods: "the road was growing worse and worse." He composes the counterfactual: a road that widens welcomingly, low-hanging flowers brushing the schoolteacher's cheeks softly as the cart passes a joyful peasant wedding. He holds the two versions up. The worsening road, he names, indicates a steadily degrading situation, primes the reader for foreboding, and connects forward to the moment when the river ruins Marya's purchases. The cheerful counterfactual would have made that same later moment land as random misfortune rather than as the next beat in a darkening sequence. He runs the same exercise on the rough teahouse, contrasting Chekhov's actual description (vitriol, banging door, drunkenness) with a cheerful variant of the same scene to make visible what Chekhov needed the teahouse to be doing in the story.

The structural-scale variant is worked on the rain-caused digression in "Gooseberries." Saunders writes the digression-free version explicitly: in bright sunlight, Ivan tells his brother's story to just Burkin, crossing non-rain-dampened fields, and his closing supplication ("There is no happiness and there should be none... Do good!") is addressed only to Burkin. He lists what is lost when the digression is removed: the rainstorm, the host Alyohin, the maid Pelageya, the swimming scene, Burkin's grumpy reaction to the swimming. He names the consequence: "Ivan lays out his case against happiness and there's nothing in counterweight. The result is an unchallenged lecture from Ivan with a simple takeaway: Happiness — not as great as you think. A lecture worth hearing, but not the complex experience that is Gooseberries." The structural comparison locates exactly what work the digression was doing — supplying a counterweight machinery the story needs to avoid collapsing into polemic — and the procedure is identical to the road and teahouse comparisons, only scaled up.
