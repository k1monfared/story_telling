## When to reach for this

You are working on a story whose thematic charge is real and intentional: a story about happiness, about whether to forgive, about the cost of ambition, about whether a belief is worth holding. You can name the position the story is moving toward. Each scene you have written votes for it. The story is becoming the thing the writer thinks about the question, delivered as fiction. Reach for this whenever you can summarize your story in one sentence of the form "X is good" or "X is bad" and feel the summary is faithful to what the draft is doing.

## The move

1. Name the position the story is currently expressing. One sentence, in the form of a verdict. Not "the story is about happiness," but "the story expresses the view that happiness is decadent" or "the story expresses the view that the small kindness redeems."
2. Identify each aspect of the story (character, scene, image, voice) that votes for the position. Make a list. The list is the inventory of one-way votes.
3. For each item on the list, find a counterweight aspect. The counterweight is not a refutation. It is an aspect of the same world that, when held next to the voting aspect, complicates the verdict without erasing it. If a character delivers a passionate condemnation of happiness, find the moment that character is also susceptible to happiness (a swim in cold water, a noticed beauty, a thoughtless pleasure with a smelly residue). If a scene argues that the small kindness redeems, find the cost the kindness imposed elsewhere.
4. Write the counterweight in. Render it with the same fullness as the voting aspect. The two readings must coexist on the page. Saunders's criterion: "the two readings coexist, making a truth bigger than either would have alone."
5. Do not rank. Refuse the temptation to make one reading override the other. The story is not delivering a verdict; it is staging a conversation. If you can summarize the story by quoting only the voting aspects, the counterweight is too quiet. If you can summarize it by quoting only the counterweights, the original position has been erased.
6. Recurse. Once a counterweight is in, look at it. Is it now expressing its own one-sided position? "The brother's passion for gooseberries is trivial; on the other hand, at least he loves something; on the other other hand, his frugality killed his wife." Saunders runs the chain three deep on Alyohin and on the gooseberry-lover. Each new "on the other hand" is a new aspect that complicates the prior layer.
7. Stop when further qualification would no longer add new dimension. The stop condition is not "the story has reached a conclusion" but "the next on-the-other-hand would be redundant or pedantic." The story has, in Saunders's phrase, "qualified itself right out of the business of judgment."
8. Test the result with the question schema. Read the draft, then ask of its central concern: "For whom? On what day, under what conditions? Are there unintended consequences? Is there good hidden in the bad? Is there bad hidden in the good?" The story should answer "tell me more" rather than supplying a verdict.

## Signal it landed

A reader cannot summarize the story as endorsing or condemning the central concern. Asked what the story thinks about X, they have to list pairs of aspects that pull in different directions, and they cannot say which pair the story prefers. The summary they can give is procedural ("the story shows X from these angles, and refuses to settle which angle is right") rather than verdictive ("the story shows that X is bad"). At least one aspect of the draft has, on this pass, been complicated by a counterweight you wrote in deliberately, and you can name the counterweight's specific function.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, write your one-sentence summary of what the story is currently saying. Make it a verdict. Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the verdict-shaped summary no longer fits and you have to give a list of pairs instead. The summary becomes "for whom, when, at what cost, with what unintended consequence" rather than "X is bad."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this procedure, a thematically charged story slides into polemic. Each scene votes the same way because the writer believes the verdict and is unconsciously stacking the deck. The reader, sensing the stacking, either agrees and finishes converted, or disagrees and finishes annoyed; in both cases the story has functioned as essay rather than fiction. Saunders names the underlying craft fact: a story is not the place to tell the reader what to think but a structure to help them think. The structure works by staging the thought it would otherwise deliver as conclusion. If every aspect of the story votes the same way, the story has nothing for the reader to think with; it has only a position to accept or reject. The reader reads to be enlarged, not converted, and persistent self-contradiction is what does the enlarging.

A second failure mode the procedure prevents: the false counterweight. A writer who has noticed the polemic problem may add a token character who voices the opposite view, then defeat that character. The reader registers the token as a token; the story's vote is unchanged. The procedural test is whether the counterweight aspect coexists with the voting aspect without being defeated. If the counterweight loses, the writer has performed the polemic with extra steps.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders walks "Gooseberries" through the procedure. Ivan's speech against happiness votes against happiness. Saunders identifies the counterweight: Ivan's exuberant swim in the rain, in which the same man who just delivered the anti-happiness sermon plunges joyfully into a cold river, kicks up waves, dives to the bottom, swims across to chat with peasants. The two readings coexist: Ivan is against happiness; Ivan is also for happiness, perhaps more than his friends. Saunders names the result: "the two readings coexist, making a truth bigger than either would have alone." He recurses. Alyohin lives without striving, which votes for the moderate-life view; on the other hand his dirtiness and lostness vote against it (something has shut down, he lives beneath his potential). The maid Pelageya is so beautiful she stops the men in their tracks, supplying gratuitous beauty as a vote for the reality of joy; on the other hand she does all the work while the men sit and philosophize, supplying labor-cost as a vote for Ivan's position. The rain votes both ways at different points: it makes the men miserable on the road, refreshes them in the swim, and at the close taps on the windows in a way Saunders explicitly leaves irreducible. The recursion stops not at a verdict but at the point where Saunders himself notes that any further articulation of what the rain is "saying" would be reductive and insufficient. The story has qualified itself out of the business of judgment, and the reader is left thinking rather than concluding.
