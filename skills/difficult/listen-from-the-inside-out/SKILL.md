## When to reach for this

You are in a difficult conversation and you cannot genuinely hear the other person. Your internal voice is arguing against them, preparing your rebuttal, or replaying earlier grievances. Or you are asking questions but they are really assertions in disguise. Or you notice that the other person does not feel heard — they keep repeating themselves, becoming more insistent, or saying "you're not listening."

## The move

**The core shift: from "I understand" to "Help me understand."** The single most important thing Greta did in the better conversation with her mother was this: she stopped trying to persuade and shifted her goal to learning. From the outside, this looked like asking questions, paraphrasing, and acknowledging. On the inside, the decisive change was this shift. Everything else follows from genuine curiosity.

Authentic listening requires that you actually care about what the other person thinks and feels. People can read, not just your words, but what is going on inside of you. If your internal stance is "I'm asking questions to be polite while I wait to rebut," the other person feels it. If your internal stance is "I genuinely want to understand," that comes through too.

**Manage your internal voice.** Good listening is blocked not by a lack of technique but by an internal voice that runs a contrary commentary. Your internal voice is saying: "That's wrong," "I have good intentions," "This isn't my fault," or "I'm so angry right now." As long as it runs unchecked, you are at most half-listening. Two approaches:

1. Negotiate your internal voice toward curiosity before the conversation (see ground-your-identity-before-the-conversation and clarify-your-purpose-before-raising).
2. When it runs during the conversation: give it attention — it quiets when heard, not when suppressed. Then negotiate: "What might be right about what they're saying? What don't I know about their perspective?" The chapter: "The single most important thing Greta has done is to shift her internal stance from 'I understand' to 'Help me understand.'"

**When you can't listen: say something first.** If your internal voice is too loud to listen through, don't fight it. Instead, express it — briefly, with care — and then listen. Tell the other person that you want to hear them and that right now you need to say something first: "I'm surprised to hear you say that. I think I disagree, but say more about how you see it." Or simply: "I have to admit that as much as I want to hear what you have to say, I'm feeling a little defensive right now." Once what's distracting you is on the table, listening becomes much easier.

**Three listening skills.**

1. **Inquire to learn.** Ask questions only to learn — not to persuade, cross-examine, or signal your position. The test: ask yourself "Why am I asking this?" The only good answer is "to learn." Common violations: statements disguised as questions ("Isn't it true that...?" "Don't you think...?"), leading questions ("So you admit that...?"), sarcasm ("Oh, so you think that's a good idea?"). These are assertions with question marks, and the other person knows it. Better: "What did you mean when you said...?" "How did that come across to you?" "Help me understand what happened from your point of view." Ask follow-ups to go deeper: "Can you say more about what you mean by X?"

2. **Paraphrase.** Restate in your own words what you understood the other person to say. Two purposes: it lets you verify that you understood correctly, and it lets the other person know that you understood (which helps them feel heard, which makes them more likely to hear you). Greta: "So keeping to the regimen would feel like accepting something that you don't really totally accept yet?" Her mother's response confirmed the paraphrase and opened further. Don't parrot the words back literally; put them in your own language. Don't interpret or judge — just reflect back what you heard. If you got it wrong, the other person will correct you.

3. **Acknowledge.** Let the other person know that what they said made an impression on you, that you understand why they see it or feel it the way they do. Acknowledgment is not agreement. You can acknowledge that their feelings make sense given their experience without agreeing with their conclusions: "I can see why you'd feel that way, given what you've described." The chapter: "It's not hard to see why this is an unproductive exchange: there's virtually no information being transferred. Ross isn't asking why his boss sees his behavior as lacking dedication — he's just defending."

## Signal it landed

The other person has stopped repeating themselves. They are not escalating or becoming more insistent. They seem to feel understood rather than merely heard. You can state what they said in your own words and they confirm you got it right.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "am I genuinely curious about what they will say, or am I preparing my response while they talk?" If the latter, the internal stance has not shifted.

After: ask "did I learn anything in this conversation that I did not already know?" If yes, genuine listening happened.

## Failure mode this prevents

Listening without genuine curiosity is just waiting for your turn to talk. The other person feels it. They escalate, repeat, or shut down. The conversation produces no new information and no movement. Greta's first conversation with her mother went in circles precisely because Greta was in "persuade" mode. Once Greta shifted to "learn" mode, she discovered things she could not have known: her mother's fear of the diagnosis (her grandmother died of diabetes), her longing for her husband, her unwillingness to follow the regimen because it would mean accepting that she was sick. None of this was available in the persuade conversation. All of it became available in the learning conversation.

## Worked micro-example

Greta and her mother. Persuade-mode conversation: Greta asserts, explains, problem-solves. Mother shuts down. Learning-mode conversation: Greta asks questions and paraphrases. Within the same conversation: the mother reveals her fear (grandmother died of the condition), her grief, her loneliness, and the actual practical obstacle (she doesn't understand the regimen because the doctor hasn't explained it). Greta offers to help. The mother accepts. The actual problem becomes solvable because Greta now knows what it actually is — something she could not have learned without genuine listening.
