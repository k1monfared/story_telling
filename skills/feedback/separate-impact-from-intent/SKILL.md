## When to reach for this

Feedback has just landed about your impact. They felt bossed around. They felt disrespected. They thought you were difficult, contemptuous, controlling, dismissive, self-obsessed. The feedback does not square with your sense of what you were trying to do. You were trying to help, to be efficient, to support, to give space. Reach for this before you collapse the gap by discarding either the feedback or your sense of your intentions.

## The move

1. Hold both layers as separate facts. The chapter's structural claim: you have privileged access to your intentions (arrow 2 on the Gap Map) and almost no direct access to your behavior (arrow 3) or your impact (arrow 4). Others have it the other way around. They cannot read your intentions; they can only read the impact, and they often guess the intentions from the impact. Treat your intentions as your data, and treat their feedback as data about your impact. Neither datum cancels the other.
2. Notice the leaks that produce the gap. The chapter spends most of the first half on the anatomy of why this gap exists: your face is visible to everyone and not to you, your tone is processed by your STS only when others speak (turning off when you speak), and even big patterns of your behavior live in your blind spot (the cellphone-pacing parent who works hard to minimize phone time and is still mimed by his five-year-old). The leaks mean others' feedback is your only way to access the part of yourself you cannot observe.
3. Notice the three amplifiers that widen the gap. The chapter names three systematic asymmetries between your story and theirs.
    - **Emotional math**: you subtract certain emotions from your self-description ("I wasn't really angry, the situation was tense") while others count them double. The angry exit you don't remember as anger is the heart of how the room remembers you.
    - **Situation versus character**: you attribute your missteps to the situation; others attribute the same actions to your character. You took the last piece of cake because no one else wanted it; they read it as selfishness.
    - **Impact versus intent proper**: you judge yourself by intentions; they judge you by impacts. You assume good intentions produce good impacts; they reason backward from impact to intent and conclude bad impact means careless or bad intent.
    All three converge to make your self-story systematically more flattering than the story being told about you, and to make their feedback systematically more about character than yours feels.
4. Use the chapter's verbal move. When the gap is on the table, do not collapse it: surface it. The script: "I was working hard to be patient [arrow 2, my intentions]. And it sounds like that's not the impact I'm having [arrow 4]. That's upsetting. Let's figure out why." Both layers are named. Both are now discussable. Neither has been discarded.
5. Decide whether to change behavior or feeling. The chapter's "from the inside out" move. If the gap is wide and the feedback consistent, surface-level behavior change will keep leaking through your face and tone, because your underlying state will keep transmitting. The deeper choice is between (a) talking explicitly about the underlying state ("I get frustrated when X happens; here is what's behind it; let's problem-solve") and (b) working to change the underlying state itself (developing actual empathy, getting to know people better as people, finding what they are doing well). Either is a fuller response than "I'll act more X." Pretending will not work because your face will not pretend along with you.

## Signal it landed

You can describe, in two sentences, your intent and their impact as separate, both real, neither cancelled by the other. You can name at least one of the three amplifiers (emotional math, situation/character, impact/intent) operating in this specific case. You have moved from "the feedback is wrong because my intentions were good" or "I guess my intentions were bad after all" to "my intentions were X, the impact was Y, those are different facts, what now?"

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "When I think about this feedback, am I treating it as a verdict on what kind of person I am, or as data about what others experienced?" If the answer is "verdict," the layers are still collapsed.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can complete two sentences in the giver's words and your own. "What I was trying to do was ___" (your intentions, your access). "What landed was ___" (their impact, their access, in their words). The truthfulness of each sentence does not depend on the other.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without separation, two failures alternate. In the first, you hold onto your intentions as a defense and discard the impact data: "I had good intentions, therefore the negative impact you describe must not have happened, must not have been mine, or must say more about your sensitivity than my behavior." The chapter's case is Annabelle, whose response to "you're difficult" is essentially "I have positive intentions and therefore positive impacts." The feedback gets dismissed; the leak stays open; the next 360 surfaces the same theme. In the second failure, you swallow the impact and discard the intentions: "I tried to be respectful and they say I'm not, so my self-perception must be a lie." This is not actually correction; it is identity collapse, and it produces either over-correction (performing respect while still feeling contempt) or paralysis (afraid to act because every action might be a leak you cannot see). The chapter's broader point in "from the inside out" is that surface-only correction without inner work does not close the gap; the leaks resume as soon as you stop policing yourself, because the underlying feeling is the source.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's anchoring case is Annabelle. Her thoughts and feelings about her team (impatience, contempt, the underlying belief that they should figure out questions on their own) are real, accruing over years of high-standards experience and positive reinforcement for being quietly resourceful. Her intentions, formed downstream of those feelings, are different: she wants colleagues to feel respected and appreciated. Her behavior is downstream of both, and the chapter is precise that her face, tone, and body language are leaking the feelings, not enacting the intentions. Her colleagues are reading the leak accurately. Annabelle, hearing the feedback, registers her intentions ("I am trying so hard to act respectful") and rejects the impact data ("they must be wrong, or playing politics"). The chapter walks through what would be different if she held the layers separately: she could name the gap, problem-solve the underlying frustration with the team rather than performing a respect she does not feel, and either explicitly discuss the feelings ("I get frustrated under pressure, please point out my reaction in the moment") or work to change them. Sasha and the conference-room outburst are smaller cases of the same separation: in each, the receiver's intent is real, the impact is real, and collapsing one onto the other forecloses the conversation.
