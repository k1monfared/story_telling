## When to reach for this

You are revising your own draft. You catch yourself trying to settle big questions before touching the page (what is this story about, what should the structure be, what is the meaning). Or you are stuck because the first draft seems too poor to bother revising. Or your sessions feel like deliberation: each change requires an argument, and arguments are exhausting. Reach for this whenever the work is sitting there waiting and you are sitting there thinking instead of reacting.

## The move

1. Print the draft, or open it fresh. Read it from the top, trying to read as a first-time reader would, without hope and without despair, in Saunders's phrase. The first-time reader is yourself, freshly. Saunders sharpens this in Afterthought #3: the work is "not so much trying to perfectly imagine another person reading my story, but to imitate myself reading it, if I were reading it for the first time." The whole skill is the ability to lapse into a reasonable impersonation of yourself encountering the prose as new.
2. Imagine a meter on your forehead: P on one side for positive, N on the other. As you read, watch which way the needle points. The reading is what generates the data, not the analysis.
3. When the needle drops into N, stop and admit it. Do not argue with the reaction. The reaction is the data. Saunders's diagnosis of an N-drop, in Afterthought #3: "this tells us that, in the instant of the writing, we weren't there." The writer was on autopilot, executing rather than perceiving. Revision is the chance to be there now.
4. Wait the brief moment for the fix to present itself. A cut, a rearrangement, a swapped word, a moved sentence. The fix usually arrives as an impulse, with at most a quiet internal yes, not as a deliberation. Saunders's image: it is like the model-railroad hobbyist who turns a plastic figure a quarter-turn because something in him just liked it better that way.
5. Mark the change. Continue reading until the next N drop, fix it, continue. Aim for a single full pass per session, three or four passes per writing day, dozens or hundreds of passes over the life of the draft.
6. Treat each change as reversible. The next pass can undo it. This is what permits the impulse to be acted on without negotiation: nothing committed today cannot be revisited tomorrow.
7. Trust block-level signals as they emerge. When a passage finally reads through with no needle drops, mark it as undeniable. Once two or three blocks are undeniable, listen for what they say to each other: what order they want, which ones can be cut so that two others abut, which ones cause the reader to ask which question.
8. Do not pre-plan the meaning. The big thematic decisions are produced by the accreted micro-decisions, not the other way around. When you sense the story trying to be about something, note it and return to the meter; the theme will be carried by the line-level work whether you name it or not. The same anti-top-down stance applies to general theory: refuse to ask "what makes a good sentence?" in the abstract. Saunders's framing in Afterthought #3: "I find I can't complete that sentence, not in any general way. And I don't need to. To be a writer, I only need to read a specific sentence of mine, in its particular context, on a given day, pencil in hand, changing the sentence as it occurs to me to do so. Then do that again, over and over, until I'm pleased."
9. Repeat across days, weeks, months. The story turns slowly, like a cruise ship, by way of thousands of small adjustments.

## Signal it landed

You can describe a recent revision session in concrete reaction terms: where the needle dropped, what fix you made, where it dropped next. You did not deliberate any single change for more than a few seconds. The session produced specific line-level changes, not a list of intended-but-deferred decisions. At least one block has crossed into undeniable, and you can name what it now does for the story that it was not doing before.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of your current revision session: "Am I changing the page, or am I deciding what to change?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer flips from "deciding" to "reacting and changing," and you can name three line-level changes the session produced, each tied to a place where you felt the needle drop.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this method, revision becomes architecture: you sit above the draft trying to settle what the story is, what it means, what its structure should be, and you delay the work of changing the page until those decisions feel earned. They never feel earned, because the decisions that would actually settle them are made line by line by the writer who has been reacting to the prose for a long time. So the draft sits, polished only at the level you can articulate, with the deeper micro-level work undone. Or you suffer the inverse failure, refusing to revise because the first draft is bad, not yet seeing that the first draft is not the work, only the substrate the work happens to. Saunders is direct about the alternative: a piece written this way, like a seed crystal, starts small and devoid of intention and grows into something neither writer nor reader could have planned, and is recognizably the writer's because of what they did to any old text by way of the iterative method, not because of what they decided in advance.

A second failure mode the method prevents, sharpened in Afterthought #3: arriving at the page with a fixed plan and merely executing it. Saunders's image is the anxious dater consulting index cards ("7:00 p.m. Inquire re childhood memories"); the dater's plan substitutes for moment-to-moment responsiveness, and the date feels the substitution as disengagement. A draft executed against a plan reads the same way: a lecture or a data dump, not a story. The reader registers care or its absence as a felt difference, intuiting (in Saunders's framing) "the many less-cared-for versions of a sentence behind the one the writer let stand." The iterative method is what produces evidence of care, because each sentence visibly stands as the survivor of many considered alternatives rather than as the default.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders opens the Afterthought with Stan, a hobbyist arranging a model-railroad town. Stan places a hobo under a bridge, notices the figure is gazing back toward town, then turns a tiny plastic woman in a Victorian window so she is gazing toward the bridge. He has not exactly decided to turn her: it occurred to him to do so, in a split second, with no language attached, except a quiet yes. Saunders names the moment as the instant of intuitive preference and says all art begins there. The same impulse, scaled up, is the P/N meter at work over hundreds of drafts. He extends the picture with the apartment-redecoration analogy: a room he decorates for you in one day will reflect only one of the people you are, while a room you redecorate one swap per day for two years will reflect hundreds of you, all contributing, and will end up more yours than either of you could have planned. That is what iteration does to a draft.
