## When to reach for this

You catch yourself uncertain whether to feel hurt that a connection didn't show up the way you expected — because you weren't sure they were supposed to. Or you notice you're investing more (planning, coordinating, emotionally) than the other person seems to be. Reach for this whenever the implicit type of the relationship hasn't been named, especially in nonmonogamous configurations where the defaults aren't shared.

## The move

1. Distinguish the major options. Attachment-based partnership: I'm one of your people; we expect to show up for each other; we'd notice each other's absence. Friendly-sexual or casual: connection without the expectation that we're each other's safe haven. Close friendship: significant connection that may or may not include attachment dimensions. Comet, satellite, or seasonal: configurations specific to nonmonogamous practice.
2. Bring it up with the actual person. Not with friends, not in your journal — with them.
3. Ask, don't impose. "I want to check in about what we're building. I've been treating this as attachment-based; I want to know how you're holding it." Leave room for them to disagree or want something different.
4. Listen for the gap between what they say and what they do. Words can be aspirational; behavior tells you what they're actually capable of right now. If they say "yes attachment-based" but reliably can't show up, the named type and the lived type don't match.
5. Renegotiate when needed. Relationships shift. The partner you were attachment-based with can become a close friend over time; a friendly connection can deepen. Naming the type is an ongoing practice, not a one-time conversation.

## Signal it landed

Each significant relationship in your life has a type you and the other person could each name aloud. There are no surprised feelings of "wait, I thought we were..." because the conversations have already happened.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming, one partner over-invests and gets hurt; the other coasts on what felt like a casual connection and is shocked by the other's expectations. Both feel betrayed by the other's behavior, when actually they were operating from different unspoken templates the whole time.

## Reflection prompts

- For each significant relationship in your life: what type do you think it is? What type does the other person think it is?
- Which relationships in your life would you like to be attachment-based that aren't currently?
- Which relationships have you been treating as attachment-based that the other person hasn't been treating that way?
- What does commitment mean to you, specifically? What aspects (structural, emotional, public) matter most?
