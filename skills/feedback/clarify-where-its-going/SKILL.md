## When to reach for this

A feedback conversation is happening or has just happened, and the giver has handed you something forward-looking: a piece of advice you are supposed to apply, a rating that determines what comes next, a piece of news that changes your situation. You have the headline. You do not yet have the actionable forward content — what specifically to do, what to expect, what is now expected of you. You are about to leave (or to nod) anyway. Reach for this before you do.

## The move

1. Notice that the feedback has a forward-looking component and that you do not yet have it. The chapter splits feedback into a coming-from layer (data, interpretation) and a going-to layer (advice, consequences, expectations). Most feedback has both. The going-to layer is what you act on; without it, the feedback is just news.
2. If the feedback is coaching, run the actionability test. The chapter's exact form: "If I did want to follow this advice, would I know how to do so?" If the honest answer is no, the advice is not yet usable. Ask for examples, contrasts, demonstrations. The chapter's Tony-speech example: "Sparkles? Describe what you mean. Show me some examples of speeches that sparkle. And show me some examples of speeches you think fell flat." The contrast is what makes the advice take shape.
3. Stay in the inquiry until you can visualize doing it. The chapter's claim: when you discuss advice in this kind of detail, you can start to visualize it, and once visualized, advice that sounded useless ("you need to learn to say no") often turns out to be useful after all. The signal that the inquiry is done is that you can picture the next concrete action.
4. If the feedback is evaluation, switch to the consequences-and-expectations question set. The chapter is clear that evaluation makes the inquiry harder: you are still vibrating from the impact, and you are not in a curious state of mind. Even so, the questions are the actionable part. The chapter's three illustrative cases give the shape of the questions:
    - On a piece of news (Max's hearing loss): What caused this? What can I do to slow or stop further loss? How does this compare to others my age? Will it get worse, how fast?
    - On a verdict (Margie not getting the department head role): What were people seeing as gaps? Are there suggestions for how to fill them? How does this affect my project mix, my compensation, the next opportunity?
    - On a relational evaluation (the live-in girlfriend who will not marry you): What are you assuming about the future? What would need to change? Are you unsure about marriage or about me? Is there a path or is there not?
5. Carry a short list of forward-looking questions in your back pocket. The chapter calls this the rip-cord image: you already have the skills, the trick is remembering to use them when the impact has just hit. The list does not have to be long; it has to be ready.
6. If you missed the moment, come back later. The chapter's note: unlike forgetting to pull a parachute rip cord, you can usually have the follow-up conversation. The asking is not closed when the meeting ends.

## Signal it landed

For coaching, you can describe the next concrete action you would take if you decided to follow the advice — not in the giver's label, in your own visualized behavior. For evaluation, you can answer "what does this mean for me, what will happen next, what is now expected of me, what should I do given where I stand" with sentences sourced from the conversation rather than from your own guesses.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I left right now, what would I actually do differently tomorrow because of this feedback?" If the answer is "I am not sure" or "I will figure it out," the going-to layer has not been clarified.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if the answer is a specific behavior (for coaching) or a specific set of consequences and expectations (for evaluation), and the specifics came from the conversation, not from your own filling-in.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the clarification, two failure modes alternate. In the first, you accept vague coaching, walk out with the label, and either do nothing different (because you do not know what to do) or do the wrong thing (because you filled in the meaning yourself). The chapter's case is Tom, whose friend Liz tells him to "learn to say no," which Tom dismisses as unhelpful — until he asks Liz how she actually does it, at which point her concrete practices (sharing the dilemma with the team, using sorting questions, deferring the answer) turn an annoying label into something he can try. In the second, you accept evaluation, walk out with the headline, and discover only later what it actually meant for you. The chapter's cases are Max, who hears that his hearing has dropped 80 percent and only later thinks of all the questions he wishes he had asked; Margie, who hears she did not get the role, says "that's disappointing — who got it," and only later thinks to ask what gaps she could fill; and the unmarried partner who says nothing in the moment and weeks later realizes there was a whole conversation to have had. In all three, the actionable forward content was reachable in the room and was lost.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter offers two parallel scenes, one per kind of feedback. For coaching, Tom and Liz on "saying no": the label is empty until Liz describes her own practice (sharing the dilemma with her team, using sorting questions, declining to commit on the spot), at which point Tom can visualize it and decide whether to try it. For evaluation, the chapter runs three vignettes — Max and the audiologist, Margie and the promotion, the live-in girlfriend's silence — each pairing what was said in the moment with the questions the receiver later wished they had asked. The pattern is the same across kinds: the receiver had access to the forward content in the room, the impact of the news displaced the inquiry, and the rip cord went unpulled.

## Source

Chapter 3, "First Understand," lines 1347-1414 ("Ask Where the Feedback Is Going," with the subsections "When Receiving Coaching: Clarify Advice" and "When Receiving Evaluation: Clarify Consequences and Expectations"). Stone and Heen split the forward-looking layer by kind of feedback (coaching wants advice you can act on, evaluation wants consequences and expectations you can navigate), give the actionability test for coaching ("would I know how to do it?") and the rip-cord image for evaluation, and supply scripted question sets for each. The skill installs both flavors as one move: when the feedback has a future, ask for it, in the right form for the kind.
