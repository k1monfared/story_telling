## When to reach for this

You have a story with a strong strange premise. The opening pages set it up well. The middle pages feel oddly flat. Each new beat is in the same key as the premise rather than building on it. You sense the story is repeating its strangeness rather than compounding it, and the reader is starting to settle into the tone instead of being pulled deeper. Reach for this whenever a strange-premise story plateaus and you suspect the fix is not "more of the same kind of weird" but "weird in a category that has not been weird yet."

## The move

1. List the tiers of strangeness already in your draft. Saunders's enumeration on "The Nose" gives the categories: (1) the premise itself (an impossible event); (2) the reactions of the characters present (their responses are irrational by our standards); (3) the plans they form in response (the plans are odd in light of the situation); (4) the execution of those plans in the world (the world itself behaves with a slight ornery resistance); (5) the narration (the voice telling the story is itself off). Other categories are possible, but these five are a working palette.
2. For each tier, write one sentence describing what kind of weirdness lives there in your current draft. If a tier is empty (the world your characters move through behaves perfectly normally; the narrator is a transparent camera), name it as a tier with potential.
3. Identify which tier the draft is currently leaning on. If the strangeness is concentrated in one or two tiers and the others are empty, that is the diagnosis. The draft is restating, not compounding.
4. Ratchet up one of the empty tiers. Saunders's procedure on the bridge scene is exemplary: Ivan, having accepted the irrational accusation (tier 2) and formed the odd plan (tier 3), is unable to execute the plan because the world keeps providing acquaintances and policemen — that is the world itself becoming weird (tier 4). The world's resistance is a fresh category; it does not restate the prior tiers, it compounds them.
5. Make each tier add weirdness in a different category. The rule is not "more weird" but "weird in a way the prior tiers were not weird." Repeating the same category louder is the failure this skill prevents.
6. Hold the narrator-tier as a special option. Saunders flags it as the highest-leverage tier because it changes how every prior tier reads. If the prose is currently transparent, the question to ask is whether the story is one in which the narrator's voice could itself be revealed as off, in which case the existing weirdnesses are recoded as filtered through that voice. (See the related skill skaz-narrator for the procedure.)

## Signal it landed

You can name, for your draft, four or five distinct categories of strangeness, each operating at a different level. A reader cannot collapse the strangeness into a single phrase; each tier supplies something the others do not. The story does not feel like one weird premise extended; it feels like a system whose oddness compounds.

## Probe before & after

**Before.** For your current draft, complete the sentence: "The strangeness in this story is X." If you cannot expand that sentence into four or five categories that are each genuinely distinct (premise, characters' response, plan they form, world's resistance, voice telling it), the strangeness is concentrated.

**After.** Same prompt. The answer is a list of distinct categories, each filled. If you cut any one of them, the others would still hold but the compounding would weaken.

## Failure mode this prevents

The draft that has one good weird premise and then several pages of restating it. The middle goes flat because the reader has accepted the premise on offer and is now waiting for the story to do something with it. The writer, not having decided what to compound, intensifies the same key (more talking objects, louder surreal description, the same kind of impossible event repeated) and the strangeness loses its purchase. By naming distinct categories and ratcheting fresh ones up, the strangeness keeps producing new ground rather than re-decorating ground already taken.

## Worked micro-example

The premise: a nose appears in a loaf of bread. That is one tier of weirdness. A weaker version of the story would make the nose more vivid, give it more lines, render the bread more strangely. Gogol does not. He moves to the next tier: the couple's reaction is irrational (Praskovya leaps to the conclusion that her barber husband cut it off a client; Ivan accepts this rather than asking the obvious questions). Then the next tier: the plan they form is to ditch the nose, which is the wrong response for either an innocent or a guilty party. Then the next tier: out in the world, Ivan cannot execute the simple act of dropping a wrapped object, because acquaintances and policemen are everywhere. Then the next tier: the narrator who has been telling us all this is himself unreliable, pedantic, and digresses about Ivan's "respectability" in a way that proves the opposite. Five distinct categories, each weird in a different way. None of them restates the others. The compounding is what makes the story feel infinite.
