## When to reach for this

A piece of feedback you do not like has just landed. Within seconds, your mind has a roster of counterexamples ready: people who said the opposite, awards you have won, contexts where you behaved nothing like that. You are about to use the roster to file the feedback as an outlier and move on. Reach for this before you do, because the chapter says counterexample-hunting is exactly how blind spots stay hidden.

## The move

1. Catch the contradictory-evidence reflex. The chapter's image: "you say I'm self-absorbed? Then how come I won the community service award last year? You think I interrupt? Let me stop you right there..." The reflex is fast and feels like fairness ("look at all this contrary data"), but it is structurally an avoidance move. The skill begins with naming the reflex.
2. Look for consistency, way one: same behavior, different interpretation. The chapter's table pairs the receiver's self-description against others' read of the same behavior: shy / aloof, upbeat / phony, spontaneous / flaky, truth-teller / nasty, passionate / emotional, smart / arrogant, high standards / hypercritical, outgoing / overbearing, quirky / annoying. Run the feedback through this filter: is the giver and you describing the same observable behavior, with one of you using a flattering label and the other using an unflattering one? If yes, the disagreement is not about what happened; it is about what to call it. The chapter's framing: others may be misunderstanding you (shy read as aloof) or you may be unaware of your impact (outgoing landing as overbearing). Both produce the same paired pattern.
3. Look for consistency, way two: where have I heard this before? Run the feedback against your history. Has another person, in another context, at another time, said something that lands in the same general region? The chapter's punchline is brutal: if your first-grade teacher and your first wife both complained about your hygiene, it might be time to listen. The point is not that any single piece of feedback is decisive; it is that consistency across time, source, and context is the strongest evidence you have access to as a receiver, and counterexamples cannot dissolve it.
4. Hold the consistencies separately from the verdict. The skill is not "I must be guilty"; it is "the pattern is real, regardless of whether I accept the giver's interpretation of it." The data is the recurrence. The interpretation is still up for negotiation (per separate-data-from-interpretation and difference-spot-not-wrong-spot, applied across multiple feedback events instead of within one).
5. Notice the silence that has been hiding the pattern. The chapter's "we collude to keep each other in the dark" point: people often don't tell us, and the receiver mistakenly reads silence as confirmation. The pattern has been visible to others for years; the question is what they were willing to say and when.

## Signal it landed

You can name, in two parts, the consistency the feedback is part of. Part one: which behavior on the chapter's table (or a similar pair) is being described, with the flattering label you use for it and the unflattering label others use for it. Part two: at least one prior instance, ideally from a different relationship and a different period of your life, where the same theme surfaced. If you cannot name any prior instance, you have either checked carefully and this really is novel (rare), or you have not yet looked.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I had to write down right now whether this feedback connects to anything I've heard before, would my first instinct be to list reasons it does not?" If yes, the counterexample reflex is running.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can complete two sentences. "The flattering label I have for this behavior is ___; the unflattering label they have for it is ___." "Where I've heard this before, from a different source, is ___." If both blanks fill in, the pattern has shape, even if you and the giver still disagree on what to do about it.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, the counterexample reflex produces a stable but misleading verdict: each piece of feedback is a one-off, no piece coheres with any other, and the receiver concludes there is no pattern. The chapter is explicit about why this is wrong. First, the receiver is selectively recalling counterexamples and not pattern-confirmations, by virtue of being the receiver. Second, the people who saw earlier instances of the pattern often said nothing (the colluding-silence point), so the absence of prior corroboration is not evidence; it is censorship in the politest possible form. Third, the same observable behavior is often paired with both flattering and unflattering labels, and counterexamples in the flattering vocabulary do not contradict feedback in the unflattering vocabulary; they confirm it. Annabelle's case is the worked instance: two 360s three years apart, same theme, no recognition of the pattern; she instead processes each 360 as a separate puzzling event explainable by ad-hoc theories about her subordinates' motives.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter at lines 2029-2098 lays out both consistency-checks and gives the table directly. The shy/aloof, high-standards/hypercritical, outgoing/overbearing pairings are the chapter's own examples of the same-behavior-different-interpretation pattern. The "where have I heard this before" probe is sharpened with the first-grade-teacher / first-wife / hygiene example as the chapter's deliberately uncomfortable case. Annabelle's two-360s-three-years-apart situation, given as the chapter's framing case (lines 1751, 1806), is the larger demonstration: same feedback, separated in time, processed each time as though novel, when the pattern across time was the strongest data available.

## Source

Chapter 4, "See Your Blind Spots," lines 2029-2098 (the section "Look for Patterns" with the same-behavior-different-interpretation table). Stone and Heen prescribe consistency-search as the diagnostic move that contradictory-evidence-search forecloses, give the table of paired labels as a concrete tool, and add the "where have I heard this before" probe as the second consistency check. The skill installs the inversion of the receiver's default reflex: instead of looking for contradictions, look for recurrences.
