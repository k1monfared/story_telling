## When to reach for this

You are at the end of a draft whose middle has accumulated things the reader has been politely ignoring: implausibilities, unexplained gaps, a narrator who keeps misplacing emphasis, structural choices that do not quite cohere. The reader has been tracking these on a private cart (the TICHN cart, in the existing skill's vocabulary). You sense the ending needs to engage the cart, but you also know that explaining away the irregularities would dissolve the spell that made them work. The lesser-writer move is to write a paragraph that resolves the loose ends. Reach for this whenever the temptation is to explain and you can feel the explanation would ruin the story.

## The move

1. Inventory the cart. List, briefly, the implausibilities, narrator-quirks, and structural irregularities the reader has been silently tracking. Be specific: which gaps, which improper emphases, which unexplained transitions. The list is for you, not for the prose.
2. Refuse the lesser-writer move. Do not write a paragraph that retroactively explains how the impossible thing happened, what the narrator really meant, or how the irregularities resolve into a coherent system. Saunders's note: "if the story's crazy logic gets explained away, then so does that revelatory feeling it has produced in us, the sense that the story's logic is actually the logic of the universe."
3. Have the prose acknowledge the oddness directly. The narrator (or the prose itself) names the irregularities as irregularities. Saunders's Gogol-line: "Only now, on second thoughts, can we see that there is much that is improbable in it." The acknowledgment can be performed through the narrator's voice (consistent with the voice you have been using) or through a closing meta-comment, but it must be an admission, not a solution.
4. Address none of the concerns. The crucial constraint: the confession must not actually answer any of the questions it raises. Saunders: "He addresses exactly none of the concerns he's raised... but the flailing gestures he's making in that direction have the effect of emptying out my TICHN cart." The reader's residual suspicion drops away because the irregularities have been seen, not because they have been explained.
5. Stay in voice. If your story has a skaz narrator, the confession arrives in skaz. If your story has a transparent narrator, the confession is a sudden honest moment from that narrator. The voice consistency is part of why the move works.
6. Let the irregularities remain. The story does not become coherent. The reader does not, on rereading, find that the gaps now close. The shards-not-vase arrangement stays as it was. What changes is the reader's relationship to the gaps: they have been named and let stand.

## Signal it landed

A reader who would have, before the ending, complained that the story does not add up, instead reports that the ending is satisfying. The complaint is not that you have explained the irregularities; the satisfaction comes from the prose's having seen them. The reader's residual suspicion is gone but the gaps remain. On a second reading, the reader notices that the confession resolves nothing and is not bothered by this.

## Probe before & after

**Before.** Read your current ending and ask: am I explaining the loose ends? If yes, you are performing the lesser-writer move. Write down what you would lose by deleting the explanation. (Usually: the spell.)

**After.** Same prompt. The ending acknowledges the loose ends without resolving them. The prose admits the story does not add up. The reader, instead of being confused or annoyed, feels seen. The irregularities are now load-bearing rather than evidence of failure.

## Failure mode this prevents

The endings that destroy their own stories by explaining them. The writer, anxious about coherence, writes a final paragraph that retroactively resolves the impossibilities into a dream, a hoax, a misunderstanding, a metaphor. The strangeness that gave the story its truth-claim collapses into a tidy fable. The opposite failure is also prevented: endings that simply trail off, leaving the reader irritated by the cart of unresolved suspicions. The confession is the third path: the prose names what it has done, and the act of naming it does the work that an explanation could not.

## Worked micro-example

A story has accumulated by its closing pages: a nose found in bread, a couple's irrational reaction, an odd plan to ditch the nose, a world full of policemen, a Nose-as-man with a face that may or may not exist, a series of offices that respond to the impossibility with strangely mild civility, and a narrator who has been digressing, misplacing emphasis, and failing to register the strangeness. The lesser-writer move is to write a final paragraph explaining the dream / spell / hoax / chemical hallucination. Gogol instead has the narrator turn directly to the reader: "Only now, on second thoughts, can we see that there is much that is improbable in it." The narrator goes on to flounder: he criticizes the wrong things (Kovalyov used "an improper method to advertise for one's nose"), digresses into "it's improper, embarrassing, not nice!", asks "how did the nose come to be in a newly baked loaf?", and admits "I simply don't know what to make of it." None of the actual questions are answered. But the cart empties. The reader's residual suspicion drops away. The irregularities remain, and they now feel like the right shape.
