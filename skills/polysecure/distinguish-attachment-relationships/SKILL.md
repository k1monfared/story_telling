## When to reach for this

You're confused about why a connection feels disappointing — they didn't show up the way you wanted, but you can't quite say why you wanted it. Or you're investing HEARTS-level work in a connection that the other person seems to be holding casually. Reach for this whenever the type of relationship hasn't been distinguished from the activity it currently involves.

## The move

1. Sort your significant relationships into rough types:
   - **Attachment-based partnership**: we expect to show up for each other in ongoing, body-felt ways; we'd notice each other's absence; we're each other's safe haven and/or secure base
   - **Friendly-sexual connection**: real, valued, but without the expectation that we are each other's people in attachment terms
   - **Close friendship that may be partially attachment-based**: significant connection that meets some attachment functions
   - **Casual or comet connection**: occasional, valued, but not configured for daily presence or repair work
2. Apply the right expectations to each type. The friendly-sexual connection's failure to text every morning isn't a failure — that wasn't the contract. The attachment partnership's failure to be available during a crisis IS a failure — that was the contract.
3. Where the expectation and the type don't match, get curious about which to update. Sometimes the type is right and the expectation needs revising. Sometimes the type was misidentified and a real attachment is forming that you both should name.
4. Don't downgrade or upgrade unilaterally. The type of a relationship is something both people hold. Re-categorizing it in your head without saying so produces the same drift you started with.
5. Recognize that types can change over time — and changes deserve a conversation. The friend who became a partner. The partner who became a friend. The casual connection that deepened. Each shift is a real moment of renegotiation.

## Signal it landed

You can describe each of your significant relationships in terms of its type. Your expectations are calibrated to the type each one actually is. You stop being disappointed by casual connections for not being attachment partnerships, and you stop being relieved that attachment partnerships are "low-maintenance" because that's not actually what was wanted.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this distinction, two patterns recur: investing attachment-level energy in connections that aren't configured to receive it (and getting hurt when they don't reciprocate), and under-investing in actual attachment partnerships (because you're treating them like the casual ones).

## Reflection prompts

- For each significant relationship in your life: what type is it from your perspective? From theirs?
- Are there relationships where you've been investing attachment-level work without it being explicit?
- Are there relationships you've been treating as casual that have actually become attachment-based?
- When you read this list of types, which one describes each of your current connections most accurately?
