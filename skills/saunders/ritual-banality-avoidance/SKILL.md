## When to reach for this

Your draft has built up a clear question or hope in the reader's mind. The early pages have set it up so well that the reader can already see the resolution coming. The lonely person will meet someone, the test will be passed, the secret will be confessed, the lost item will be found. Reach for this when the story's first-order solution is the one you and the reader can both already see, and you suspect that delivering it will leave the story feeling thin.

## The move

1. Name the first-order solution. Write down, in one sentence, the resolution the early pages have set the reader up to expect. The crappo version, in Saunders's word.
2. Notice that the reader has already received the benefit of imagining this resolution. It is in their head whether you write it or not.
3. Remove the means of producing it. Take the relevant character offstage, foreclose the conversation, ruin the object, miss the train. Do this on the page, visibly enough that the reader registers the loss.
4. Sit with what is left. The story now owes the reader a resolution that is not the one they were waiting for. This is uncomfortable, and the discomfort is the point: the story has to find a more honest answer, because the easy one is no longer available.
5. Push subsequent reveals to the knot, in Saunders's phrase. When you know where the story is going, do not hoard the reveal. Make it land now, then ask the harder question: now what?
6. Trust that the second-order answer, the one you find by foreclosing the first, is the one the story actually wants. If you cannot find any answer at all once the first-order solution is gone, that is information about the draft.

## Signal it landed

The story produces a resolution the reader did not pre-imagine, but that, once it lands, feels inevitable. You can name what was foreclosed and what arrived in its place, and the arrival is more specific than the foreclosure was. The draft no longer feels like the story everyone could see coming.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "What does the reader already think this story is going to do?" Note the answer.

After applying, ask: "What does the reader still think this story is going to do?" The skill landed if the second answer is "I don't know, but I want to find out," and you, as the writer, have a different and more honest answer to give them than the first one.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, your draft delivers what the reader already pre-imagined and the story collapses to the exact arc the early pages telegraphed. The reader's experience becomes confirmation rather than revelation. Saunders is blunt about why this is fatal: a story that resolves in the plane of its original conception is the story the reader has effectively already read, in their head, and the act of reading it on the page becomes redundant. Hoarding the obvious reveal as your big finish is the same mistake at slower speed.

## Worked micro-example

In "In the Cart," Chekhov spends five pages building up the reader's hope that Hanov, the wealthy landowner who keeps appearing in the lonely schoolteacher's vicinity, might assuage her loneliness. Then he removes Hanov from the story. Saunders tracks the move: the door labeled "Hanov Might Assuage Marya's Loneliness" has not just been shut, it has vanished. The story has no choice now but to ask a deeper question, "What if a lonely person can find no way out of her loneliness?" The actual ending Chekhov produces, a flash of restored childhood memory at a railway crossing, is a resolution the reader could not have pre-imagined but that, once it arrives, feels exactly right. Saunders adds a process metaphor for the discipline: when you know where the story is going, do not hoard the destination, push the bead to the knot and let the next bead find itself.
