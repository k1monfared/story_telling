## When to reach for this

You have decided to implement feedback and act on a change, but you keep finding yourself reverting to old behavior. Or you have started a change and things feel worse, not better, and you are wondering whether to abandon it. Or you are planning a change and want to increase the probability that you will follow through.

## The move

**Recognize the J curve.** When implementing any feedback-driven change, a common trajectory is: you start somewhere in the middle (your status quo), things get worse as you begin to change (it is awkward, you are less skilled, the discomfort is new), and then, eventually, things get better than the starting point. From the inside of the dip, you cannot tell whether you are at the bottom of the J about to rise or heading further down. This uncertainty is what causes people to abandon changes just as they are about to become beneficial. The chapter: "You are likely to get worse before you get better. And more important, you are likely to feel worse before you feel better." Knowing this is true in advance changes how you interpret the dip.

**The two-decision-maker problem.** Your Sunday Night self makes a commitment; your Monday Morning self faces a choice that your Sunday Night self would not have approved of. Sunday Night guy is gone when Monday Morning guy arrives, and Monday Morning guy prioritizes immediate comfort over the commitments made the night before. The question is: how can Sunday Night guy bind Monday Morning guy to the agreement? There are two strategies: increase the appeal of change (make it easier or more rewarding), or increase the cost of not changing (make reversal more expensive).

**Increase the appeal of change.**

1. Make it social. Doing the change alongside someone else makes it less unpleasant, adds accountability (you might let yourself down but not your partner), and provides appreciation for your efforts. Online communities can provide the same accountability benefits without requiring in-person contact.

2. Keep score. Tracking progress turns behavior change into a game. Miyamoto charted his jogging and swimming on the bathroom wall; Bernardus tracked the number of customers successfully entered in the new database. The chapter draws on video game design: intersperse new challenges with moments of mastery to avoid exhaustion and maintain motivation. Ask: can you make your progress measurable in some way, playful, or competitively engaging?

**Increase the cost of not changing.**

Tie yourself to the mast (precommit). Change the terms of the choice before you arrive at the temptation, so that Monday Morning guy faces a different cost structure than you could have predicted on Sunday Night. Schelling stopped smoking by threatening himself with a donation to an organization he found morally repugnant if he continued. Doctors with drug addictions wrote confessional letters to the medical board, sealed them, and entrusted them to a friend who would mail them if they relapsed. The stakes change the siren song of the muffin.

**Commit to a specific duration.** When starting the change, commit in advance to trying it for a specific period that reaches past the J curve's dip. The chapter: "Give it two weeks, thirty days, a fiscal year—whatever seems like a reasonable duration to test whether this new behavior might actually help." This is the structural form of riding out the J: you are committing to reaching the upswing before evaluating the change's value.

## Signal it landed

You have designed at least one precommitment strategy before beginning the change (social partner, score-keeping mechanism, or raised cost of reversal). You have named a duration commitment. When you hit the dip and feel like abandoning the change, you can name the J curve and remind yourself that you are in the dip, not heading indefinitely downward.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "what is the most likely moment when I will want to abandon this change?" If you can name it (it's when I get to day 5 and it still feels awkward), you can design a precommitment for that moment.

After: ask "when things got worse, did I keep going?" If yes, you may be in the upswing. If you abandoned the change, ask whether you gave it the duration you committed to.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the J curve framing, the dip looks like failure and gives you rational grounds to stop. The chapter notes that the dip is predictable, not evidence that the change was wrong. Bernardus's first few weeks with the database were a minor disaster; six months later he could not imagine working without it. If he had used the early performance drop as evidence to return to paper notes, he would have made a decision based on data from the worst phase of the adoption. The J curve gives you a frame to discount that data appropriately and commit to reaching the upswing.
