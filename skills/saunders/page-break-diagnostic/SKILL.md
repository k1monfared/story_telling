## When to reach for this

You are reading a story to learn from it, revising your own draft and trying to feel where the reader is at each turn, or workshopping someone else's draft. You suspect the writer's choices later in the piece are responding to expectations the earlier pages set up, but those expectations are invisible to you because you read straight through. Reach for this whenever you want to see the story as a sequence of pulses rather than a finished whole.

## The move

1. Pick a pause. Treat each natural break (a chapter end, a scene break, a literal page) as a stop, or, in a draft, the points where you sense a new beat is starting.
2. At the pause, before reading on, answer three questions in writing, briefly. (a) Look away from the page and summarize what you know so far in one or two sentences. (b) What are you curious about? (c) Where do you think the story is headed?
3. Be specific. If your curiosity is "I want to know what happens," sharpen it: which character, about what, by when.
4. Note the expectations your answers imply. Anything the next section does will land against this list. Saunders calls these the bowling pins in the air.
5. Read the next section. Run the same three questions again.
6. Compare the two answers. What did the new section confirm, complicate, defuse, ignore, or replace?
7. Treat anything the next section ignored as a question the rest of the story still owes you. Treat anything it complicated as evidence the writer is working with what the earlier pages set up, not against it.

## Signal it landed

You can produce, for a given pause, a short list of specific questions and expectations the reader has at that moment, plus a record of how the next section spoke to each one. The list contains items you would have missed if you had read straight through, including expectations the story raised and then chose not to satisfy.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, pick a pause in the story or draft and ask: "What does the reader know, want, and expect here?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the second answer is more specific, names questions you did not see the first time, and lets you predict (and then check) which of those questions the next section will address.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, you read the story as a finished object and judge the whole. You miss the moment-by-moment pulse Saunders calls the way a story makes meaning at speed, and you cannot reverse-engineer the writer's choices because you are not reading from the seat of the reader whose expectations the writer was actually managing. Late-in-the-story decisions look arbitrary or "well-crafted" without you being able to say what they are crafted in response to. In a draft, you defend a scene because it is good in isolation, never noticing that the reader, at that point, is asking a different question than the scene answers.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders pauses at the end of the first page of "In the Cart" and runs the protocol. What do we know? An unhappy schoolteacher is in a cart, sick of her monotonous life. What are we curious about? How she got here. Where is the story headed? Some change in her situation. He shows that the story's second page opens with the answer to exactly that "how she got here" question (her parents died, and necessity forced her into teaching), demonstrating that the structure of the story is an organized response to questions the prior page caused the reader to ask.

## Source

Chapter "A Page at a Time," lines 279-1349 (especially 329-368, 735-748, 1148-1152). Saunders frames the entire chapter as an exercise: he gives you "In the Cart" one page at a time, and at every break asks variants of the same three questions. The chapter's central claim is that this is how a story makes its meaning, and that paying attention to it page by page is how you learn what the form is doing.

## Related skills

- Contrasts `the-basic-drill`. Use the page-break diagnostic mid-read at chosen pauses when the goal is to surface evolving expectations as the story unfolds. Use the basic drill after a complete reading, when the goal is to register the felt reactions of the finished experience. Both anchor reactions to places on the page, but operate at different stages of the reading.
- See also `truncation-test`, which uses a related cut-and-observe procedure but on a fixed cut near the end (or on an internal unit) rather than at every break.
