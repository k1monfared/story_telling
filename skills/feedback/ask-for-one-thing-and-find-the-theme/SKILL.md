## When to reach for this

You want to improve but you are either getting no feedback, getting vague or label-heavy feedback (below the mean on "engagement"), or getting so much feedback that you cannot act on any of it. Willpower to change is a limited resource: too many changes drain it without producing any. You need one useful thing to start on.

## The move

**Ask for one thing.** Do not say "give me some feedback" or "how am I doing?" Instead, ask: "What's one thing I could work on?" For more precision, use the chapter's specific phrasing: "What's one thing you see me doing, or failing to do, that's getting in my own way?" This question gives the giver permission to be candid, helps them prioritize rather than dump everything at once, and focuses the conversation on your behavior rather than on general assessment. If you receive ten concerns, acknowledge and validate them all, then set priorities: "I'm serious about improving, and it's been my experience that the best way for me to do that is to focus on one thing at a time. Let's figure out a good place to start."

**Go to several people and listen for the theme.** Rather than relying on a single source, go to three to five people who observe you in different roles and ask each the one-thing question. Look for what recurs. Rodrigo went to five people (three close collaborators, his boss, a coworker he found irritating) and discovered that three of five independently mentioned the same thing: he hung back in conversations when he should have weighed in with direction. Without the theme-finding pass, any single response could have been written off as idiosyncratic. With the pattern visible, the evidence was hard to dismiss.

**Ask what matters to them.** A variant of the one-thing question: "What's one thing I could change that would make a difference to you?" This centers the giver's actual concern rather than your self-improvement agenda. Sharon asked this at dinner and received Colin's answer: not Skittles, not anything abstract, but bowling, because that was the only time all four of them did anything together. Sharon had not noticed. The question surfaces the specific concern hiding behind vague or absent feedback.

## Signal it landed

You have identified one specific improvement target, not a list. You have checked whether it appears across multiple sources (theme-confirmed) or only from one (still worth considering but less certain). If you used the "what would make a difference to you" variant, you know what the giver's real concern is.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "do I have one specific thing I am actively working on right now?" If not, the one-thing question needs to be asked.

After: ask "did I hear the same theme from more than one person, or just from one?" If from one, treat it as a hypothesis. If from two or more, treat it as evidence.

## Failure mode this prevents

Asking "any feedback?" produces either silence (too broad, no permission to be candid) or a comprehensive audit (too much to act on). Receiving a comprehensive audit without prioritizing leads to the chapter's cookie-and-radish finding: trying to attend to too many changes simultaneously exhausts willpower without producing improvement in any one area. And relying on one person's view risks acting on idiosyncratic data. The theme-finding pass distinguishes real patterns from one person's particular complaint.
