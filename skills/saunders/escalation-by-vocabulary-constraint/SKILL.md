## When to reach for this

You are a writer whose drafts read as long stretches of exposition, characterization, and atmosphere where the tension never rises. You have read enough advice on rising action to name the deficit (the pot is on the stove and never boils, the beats catalogue rather than escalate, the prose elaborates a state instead of moving past it) but you cannot produce a draft that escalates. The reason is not analytic. When you sit down at the page, you channel the writer you imagine yourself to be, and that writer's habitual mode is the slack one. Audit-shaped revision skills cannot help here because there is nothing yet to audit; you need a draft that escalates so you have a felt sense of what generating-with-escalation feels like. Reach for this when you need to bypass the writer-self-image rather than reason with it, and when you need that bypass to take less than an hour.

## The move

1. Set a timer for forty-five minutes. The timer is not a goal; it is a fence around the session that prevents you from drifting back into your habitual mode by widening the time available.
2. Set the constraints. The piece will be exactly 200 words long, not 199 and not 201. You may use only 50 unique words across the whole piece. Plurals of a word count as the same word.
3. Set up a running list at the bottom of the page. As you write, append each new word to the list with its number. When you hit 50, the list is closed; from that point on you must reuse only words on the list. The list-keeping is not bookkeeping; it is the conscious mind's busywork, the displacement that lets the playful mind run the prose.
4. Pre-install the read-aloud constraint if you can. Tell yourself, or tell someone in the room, that you will read the piece aloud at the end of the timer. Saunders names the effect directly: announcing the read-aloud "intensifies things" by drawing on the natural performative streak almost every writer has. If you are alone, tell a friend you'll text the piece in 45 minutes. The performance deadline is the second pressure that shuts down the writer-self-image.
5. Write. Pay no attention to whether what you are writing is the kind of story you usually write. The constraint will pull you toward escalation because exposition is expensive: cataloguing a state requires lots of nouns and verbs not yet on your list, while rising action lets you reuse the words you've already paid for. The mechanism is not "try to escalate"; the mechanism is "the constraint makes elaboration uneconomic and rising action cheap."
6. When the timer ends, stop, even mid-sentence. Land at exactly 200 words. The hard stop is part of the design. You are not allowed to edit your way to a satisfying length.
7. Read the piece aloud to whatever audience you committed to. Notice your own voice. Notice what is funny, what is dramatic, what has rising action. Saunders's empirical claim: in a class of twenty, almost every one of these pieces will have rising action, and some will be funnier and more dramatically shaped than the writer's "real" work.
8. Write down two answers in plain prose. What was different about this draft compared to my usual drafts? What does the writer who produced this draft do that the writer I usually channel does not do? You are looking for the glimpse Saunders names with the drunk-dancing-and-filming-it analogy: in playback you may catch yourself doing something you do not normally attempt but that you like. Naming what it was is the precondition for replicating it on purpose later.
9. Decide what to do with the result. Saunders gives two after-care variants: chain multiple 50-word passes (each pass uses a different 50-word set but keeps the same characters) into a longer story, or treat the piece as a starter with rising action already in place and rewrite with the constraints relaxed (use as many "new" words as you want). The starter use is the most useful for a writer with an inert draft already on the desk: the constrained piece supplies the rising-action shape, the relaxed rewrite fills it back in with the writer's full vocabulary. The constraint did the structural work; the relaxed rewrite supplies the breath.

## Signal it landed

You have a 200-word piece written under the constraints. Read aloud, it has rising action where your slack drafts had none. You can name, in two sentences, what the constrained-mode writer did differently from the writer you usually channel. If you ran the after-care, you have either a longer chained piece with constant characters across multiple constraint passes, or a relaxed rewrite that retains the rising-action shape the constrained piece supplied. You have at least one move identified that you intend to attempt on purpose in subsequent unconstrained work.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "What does generating-with-escalation feel like for me?" Note your answer. It will likely be theoretical (a definition of escalation, a quote from a craft book) rather than experiential.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer becomes a felt setting you can recognize on the page when you produce it, named with reference to the constrained piece you just wrote, and if you can finish the sentence "I will attempt move X on purpose in my next draft" with a specific X.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without a constraint exercise that bypasses the writer-self-image, the writer who knows their drafts do not escalate revises only by audit: each pass diagnoses the slack, names the missing escalation, prescribes a fix, and produces another inert draft because the writer who sits down to write the next draft is the same writer who wrote the slack one. The audit-shaped skills (refuse-to-repeat-beats, parameter-table-for-recurrence, causality-not-sequence, ritual-banality-avoidance) all assume the writer can produce escalation when prompted; they help shape it once it is on the page but cannot generate it from a writer whose habitual mode is exposition. Saunders names the deeper trap directly: the writer-self-image is what we channel when we sit down, and channeling that self-image changes our brain function so we are less open to what the story wants to do. The exercise's value is not the 200-word piece itself; the value is the proof that the writer can produce escalation under the right conditions, the catalogue of what those conditions did to the prose, and the named moves that can be carried back into unconstrained work. A writer who does not run a constraint exercise of this kind has only the audit route to escalation, used by the same writer-self-image that produced the deficit.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders does not produce a finished 200-word piece in the chapter. He gives the protocol and the example sentence "A cow stood in the field" with its running list (a, cow, stood, in, the, field — six words used, forty-four remaining). The chapter's worked instance is at the level of the pattern itself: in his classroom the exercise "almost always" produces rising action, and "for certain students" the results are funnier and more dramatically shaped than the writer's "real" work. He gives a parallel anecdote: as a student at Syracuse, Doug Unger fed up with the cleverness of the workshop's submitted stories announced after a break that each student would tell a story aloud on the spot during the second half. The stories that came out of that nerve-filled break were "without exception, livelier and more dramatic and more infused with who we really were, richer with our real charms, the way we actually were witty in the world." The Unger anecdote is the same mechanism without the word constraint: a forced-performance condition collapses the writer-self-image and surfaces a register the writer's submitted work was not reaching. The escalation exercise is the textual form of the same effect.
