## When to reach for this

Reframing and listening have not been enough to move the conversation. The other person keeps interrupting every time you try to complete a thought; they are acting as if they are fine while behaving as if they are angry; they keep finding new reasons why the problem is not actually a problem; or they are using intimidating behavior to make you back down.

## The move

Name what you see happening in the conversation itself — not the substance of the disagreement, but the pattern of how the conversation is going. Put the meta-level on the table as a topic for discussion.

The chapter's worked examples:

- **On interrupting:** "I've tried to say what I was thinking three times now, and each time you've started talking over me. I don't know whether you're aware that it's happening, but I'm finding it frustrating. If there's something important about what you're saying that I'm not understanding, please share it. And then I want to be able to finish what I'm saying."

- **On deflecting/acting fine while not fine:** "Here's what I'm noticing. I ask you if you are feeling hurt by what I said, and you say, 'No, no, of course not.' But then you keep acting toward me in ways that people act when they're hurt or mad at me. It seems to me the best thing to do is to try to figure out what I'm doing that might be upsetting to you. Otherwise, I don't think we're going to get anywhere."

- **On intimidating behavior:** "Hang on a second. Several times now, when I've said the things that are important to me, you've gotten very angry to the point where I feel threatened. I don't know what's causing your response. If you're upset, I'm interested to hear why. If you're trying to intimidate me into changing my mind, it won't work. I really do want to know what's upsetting you, and I want us to find a way to talk about it that doesn't feel intimidating to me."

- **On being repeatedly sidelined:** "I've noticed that we keep running out of time whenever we start talking about this. Maybe we should designate an hour when we can both really focus on this and address it then."

The key elements in each: (1) describe what you are observing in concrete behavioral terms, (2) name the impact it's having on you or on the conversation, (3) invite engagement rather than issuing a demand.

**Naming the dynamic pauses the conversation's momentum.** This is intentional. Like a referee stopping a game to make a call, you are stepping outside the action to name what's happening. The pause is the point. The chapter notes that these moves can feel slightly awkward — they are not how regular people talk — and that this very formality signals that you are stepping outside the content to examine the process.

**Use this when reframing has not worked.** Naming the dynamic is more direct and more confrontational than reframing. Reframing translates their framing while staying in the conversation's flow. Naming the dynamic stops the flow and says: this pattern itself is a problem. It can clear the air and draw implicit behaviors into explicit discussion. But it can also increase tension. Use it when nothing else has worked, not as the first move.

## Signal it landed

The other person becomes aware of a behavior they were doing without fully recognizing it. Or the two of you explicitly agree on a different way to continue the conversation. The pattern that was blocking progress has been named and addressed.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "have I tried reframing? Have I tried listening?" If neither has worked, naming the dynamic may be appropriate.

After: ask "did the named behavior change, or did we at least both agree it was happening?" If yes, the naming worked.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming the dynamic, stuck conversational patterns continue indefinitely because neither person is explicitly acknowledging them. The person who keeps being interrupted eventually shuts down or explodes. The person who is acting hurt while claiming to be fine creates an invisible impasse that blocks everything else. The intimidating person continues to rely on intimidation because it has never been called out. Naming the dynamic makes the implicit explicit, which is the first step to addressing it.
