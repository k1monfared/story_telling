## When to reach for this

You are about to have or have just had a difficult conversation in which your feelings were involved but did not get expressed — they leaked out instead (through tone, sarcasm, withdrawal, or explosions) or were avoided entirely. Or you tried to express your feelings and the other person became defensive rather than understanding. Or you realize that your strong feelings are blocking your ability to listen.

## The move

The chapter prescribes three sequential steps: find your feelings, negotiate with them, then describe them without evaluating.

**Step 1: Find your feelings.**

Feelings are often not what they first appear to be. Several things prevent us from seeing them clearly:

- **Your emotional footprint blocks certain emotions.** Every person has a characteristic pattern of which emotions are acceptable to feel and express and which are not. (In difficult conversations, you may acknowledge anger but not vulnerability, or sadness but not anger.) Know which emotions you tend to bypass or avoid.

- **One strong emotion hides the bundle.** In most situations, multiple feelings are present simultaneously. Brad thinks he is only angry at his mother, but when he examines more carefully, he finds: anger, shame, fear, confusion about his own direction, appreciation for her caring, pride in his choices. Expressing only the dominant emotion produces an incomplete picture. To find the bundle: "What are all the things I'm feeling about this situation?"

- **Feelings hide under attributions, judgments, and accusations.** "You are self-absorbed" is a judgment about the other person. "I feel hurt, confused, and embarrassed about where our friendship stands" is a feeling. Emily stated the first and Roz became defensive; when Emily switched to the second, Roz was contrite and apologized. The pattern: we translate feelings into judgments ("you are thoughtless"), attributions ("why were you trying to hurt me?"), characterizations ("you're so inconsiderate"), and problem-solving proposals ("you should call me more often") — none of which are feelings. Find the feeling behind the judgment by asking: "What am I actually feeling when I think that thought?"

- **The urge to blame signals unexpressed feelings.** When the contribution-system conversation still feels unsatisfying, it is usually because the feelings have not been expressed. The drive to say "admit it, this is your fault!" is a clue that important feelings are sitting unexpressed. Those feelings need to be found and named.

**Step 2: Negotiate with your feelings.**

Before expressing a feeling, check whether it is based on an accurate picture of the situation. Feelings follow thoughts; to change your feelings, examine your thoughts. Three checks:

1. What is your story, and what might you be missing? How does the situation look from the other person's point of view? Almost always, seeing more of the other person's story shifts the emotional picture.
2. What assumptions are you making about their intentions? (Apply disentangle-impact-from-intent.)
3. Are you seeing your own contribution clearly? (Apply map-the-contribution-system.)

These checks do not require definitive answers — asking the questions, honestly and openly, is enough to produce more nuanced feelings. The goal is not to suppress feelings but to bring them to "actual size" before expressing them.

**Step 3: Describe feelings without judging, attributing, or blaming.**

Three rules for expressing feelings clearly:

1. **Frame feelings back into the problem.** Feelings are part of the problem, not peripheral to it. Say them, even if they don't seem rational. You can preface them ("I'm not sure this makes sense, but...") but then say them.

2. **Express the full spectrum, not just the dominant emotion.** The more complete picture produces more useful conversations. Brad telling his mother he feels angry, ashamed, hopeful, and grateful produces a different conversation than Brad telling her he's angry.

3. **State pure feelings; keep out evaluation.** The practical test: can the other person argue with what you are saying? "You are thoughtless" is arguable. "I feel hurt" is not. "I feel frustrated — you didn't send the letter out" is a feeling plus a fact, without blame. "It's your fault that I felt hurt" introduces blame and invites a fight about causation. The practical formulation is "I feel..." followed by the emotion, separated from any judgment about the other person's character or intentions.

**Don't monopolize.** Both parties probably have strong feelings. Make room for theirs as well as yours. The goal is to get both into the conversation before moving to solutions.

## Signal it landed

You have said something that begins with "I feel..." followed by an actual emotion, without a judgment about the other person embedded in the same sentence. The other person has not become defensive. What you said is arguably true from your perspective and not arguable as a characterization of them.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "am I about to express how I feel, or am I about to tell them what kind of person they are?" If the latter, you are about to express a judgment rather than a feeling.

After: ask "did they respond to what I was feeling, or to what I seemed to be accusing them of?" If they engaged with your feeling, the expression worked. If they got defensive, a judgment probably leaked in.

## Failure mode this prevents

Expressing feelings as judgments ("you are thoughtless, self-absorbed") produces defensiveness rather than understanding. The other person hears an accusation about their character, not an expression of your experience. They respond to the accusation and the actual feeling never gets acknowledged. The conversation becomes a fight about whether the judgment is accurate. The underlying feeling remains unexpressed and continues to contaminate the relationship. Emma tried to have the conversation with Kathy about her hurt and betrayal by framing it as a rules dispute about professional communication. The feelings were never on the table; the conversation produced no resolution; months of strained relationship followed.

## Worked micro-example

Emily and Roz. Emily tells Roz that Roz is "self-absorbed and thoughtless." This is a judgment; it produces defensiveness; Roz attacks back. Emily tries again with pure feelings: "I feel hurt. I feel confused about the state of our friendship. I feel embarrassed that I put so much work into a friendship that apparently wasn't as important to Roz as it was to me." Roz became contrite and couldn't stop thanking Emily. The same underlying situation, two different expressions, two completely different conversations.
