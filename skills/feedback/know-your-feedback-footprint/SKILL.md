## When to reach for this

You know you are about to receive feedback that will be difficult to hear, or you have realized, after a feedback conversation, that you reacted in your habitual way again (defended, went silent, agreed immediately and quietly resolved never to change, became awkwardly friendly, etc.) and did not actually receive what was said. You want to interrupt the pattern next time.

## The move

**Before the conversation: name your footprint.** Ask yourself, plainly: "How do I typically react when I receive feedback that upsets me?" The chapter offers examples: blaming others, switchtacking, crying, apologizing, chattering, going silent, agreeing while quietly resolving never to change, lawyering up emotionally, becoming awkwardly friendly, panicking. Note that your mind will present each example as an exception to how you actually are. The chapter is direct: those exceptions are not exceptions. They are how you are. If you have trouble discerning your footprint, ask those around you and notice your defensiveness as they describe it.

**Before the conversation: inoculate yourself against the worst.** If you have advance notice that difficult feedback is coming, think through what the worst plausible outcome is. Try it on emotionally, in a balanced and unhurried state, before you are in the conversation. Reason through what you would do if that outcome arrived. The chapter's mechanism: when you've already encountered the scenario once in imagination, the actual arrival is less shocking. Your nervous system has seen this threat before and knows it can handle it. You'll think, "Yes, this is what I feared might happen. I've seen this before. I'll be okay." You also have already thought through what you would do, rather than improvising while distressed.

**During the conversation: notice what's happening.** When feedback arrives and you feel your footprint activating, name it to yourself: "Okay, this is that thing I do. That triggered thought pattern I get into. That sick feeling I get." The chapter's finding: naming your reaction to yourself activates the left prefrontal cortex, which exerts a calming influence on the amygdala's alarm. Seth's account: "And that one thought really helps. I'm not fighting or resisting my thoughts and reactions; I'm just noticing them. Once I think, 'Yep, this is the part where I have my overreactions,' I actually start to calm down."

You are not trying to suppress the reaction. You are observing it, which creates enough distance for the reaction to begin to pass rather than to escalate.

## Signal it landed

Before: you can name your footprint in one sentence (e.g., "I tend to agree immediately and then quietly resolve to change nothing" or "I get panicky and start snowballing"). After inoculation: you can describe what you would do if the worst-case arrived; it no longer feels abstract and terrifying.

During: you can name your reaction to yourself while it is still happening. The naming produces a sense of slight distance from the reaction; you are watching it rather than being entirely inside it.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "if I imagine getting very difficult feedback tomorrow, what is my first instinct?" If the answer is a behavioral reaction (flee, fight, deny, exaggerate) rather than a deliberate process, you have found your footprint.

After: ask "when the reaction arrived during the conversation, did I notice it, or did I only recognize it later in retrospect?" If only retrospect, the during-conversation noticing move needs more practice. If during, it has started to land.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without knowing the footprint, each difficult feedback conversation triggers the same automatic reaction, and the reaction blocks learning. The person who always agrees and quietly resolves not to change never gets to decide consciously whether the feedback is worth acting on. The person who always panics cannot think about what the feedback actually says while the panic is running. The person who always deflects into critique of the giver's delivery never processes the content. Naming the footprint in advance creates a small, reliable gap between trigger and reaction. The chapter: "If you name it, you have some power over it."

## Worked micro-example

Seth, a counselor, receives a single offhand comment from his boss in a meeting and is instantly unable to think. He later recognizes this is his pattern: he is easily triggered, his reaction strong and fast. In retrospect the comment was benign. Seth has since learned to use the during-conversation noticing move and reports that naming the reaction helps him calm down. His footprint is panic plus snowballing; by naming it he gains enough distance to begin recovering.
