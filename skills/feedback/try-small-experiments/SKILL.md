## When to reach for this

You have received feedback and your analysis of pros and cons has not produced a conclusion. The status quo has home field advantage: you are going along reasonably well, the feedback asks you to do something unfamiliar, and you cannot see in advance whether the change would help. Or you are stuck because the change feels all-and-always: if I try this, I'm committing to it permanently and admitting I was wrong all along.

## The move

**Reframe from decision to experiment.** You are not choosing whether to change permanently. You are testing whether the change might help. This distinction removes the home field advantage of the status quo, because the question is no longer "should I change my whole approach?" but "is it worth finding out what this might do?" The chapter: "Experiment. Not because you know that it's right or you know it will help. But because it's possible it will help."

**Try it on (the mental experiment).** Before committing to any real-world change, run the experiment in your head. Harpreet received a student evaluation calling him "arrogant and condescending" and rejected it entirely. His department head suggested: "Try it on. Assume the student is on to something. Not because you already know it fits, but because it's a good way of finding out. If it doesn't, no worries. Take it off." Harpreet sat with the possibility and began to see what the student might have meant. He did not conclude the feedback was true, but found a perspective he could act on. The mental try-on is low-risk: if the feedback doesn't fit after a genuine attempt, you take it off. If it does, even in some small way, you have something to work on.

**Try it out (the real-world experiment).** Run the feedback as a time-limited behavioral test. Emily received five years of coaching to cut her twenty-minute introduction. She heard it as an all-and-always verdict and resisted. When she reframed it as a one-night experiment, she tried it. The result: some parts of the intro were dispensable, some were not, and five minutes with a handout at the end turned out to be better than twenty minutes with nothing. She would not have known this from analysis alone.

**Reframe from all-and-always to some-and-sometimes.** When the feedback asks you to "change your workshops entirely for the rest of time," that is the all-and-always frame, and the answer will always be no. When the question is "try it for one evening and see what you think," the answer becomes accessible. The chapter: "If the question is 'should I go to yoga for the rest of my life?' the answer will always be no. If it's 'should I try yoga for one morning and see what I think?' the costs drop dramatically."

## Signal it landed

You have defined a specific, time-limited experiment: what you will try, under what conditions, for how long, before deciding whether to continue. You have not committed to permanent change; you have committed to finding out. Or, in the mental version, you have spent genuine time trying to inhabit the feedback rather than dismissing it.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "am I stuck because I cannot commit to permanent change, or because I genuinely cannot see any upside to the change?" If the first, the all-and-always reframe applies. If the second, the try-it-on mental experiment is the first step.

After: ask "did I actually try it, or did I just think about trying it?" The experiment produces data only if it is actually run.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the experiment reframe, feedback that you are not sure about produces no action. The chapter's observation: "In any contest between change and the status quo, the status quo has home field advantage. All things being equal, we won't change." Analysis of pros and cons, in the absence of real experience, rarely produces a conclusion about things that are genuinely uncertain. Gawande hired a surgical coach not because he knew he needed one, but because the downside was small and the upside, though unclear, was worth exploring. His complication rates went down. He could not have concluded this from analysis. Emily's five-year resistance to a one-night experiment is the cost of treating an experiment as a decision.
