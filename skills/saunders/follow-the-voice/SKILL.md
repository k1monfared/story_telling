## When to reach for this

You are sitting down to start a story and find yourself trying to choose a topic, theme, or message. Or you are mid-draft and have a clear conception of what the story is "about," and the resulting prose is reading like a position paper or a one-sided performance. Or you have read enough writing advice to know that "no worthy problem is solved in the plane of its original conception" and want a method, not a slogan, for proceeding without a destination. Reach for this whenever the next move would otherwise be to decide what the story should mean before letting any prose generate.

The orientation that licenses this skill: voice is not decoration on top of a propositional content that could in principle be paraphrased. It is the part of the prose that carries the felt truth the propositional content does not reach. "I really enjoyed that and I think I'm in love" is recoverable as a sentence; what Jon actually feels in Saunders's honey/milk LI 34321 passage is not. Following the voice is following the truth.

The skill is one specific instance of a broader move. The general shape is: identify the thing about prose you have strong opinions about — voice, recurring imagery, the look of the words on the page, sound, the minutiae of structure — and proceed by attending to that thing, so that you do not know too well in advance what you are doing. Voice is the instance Saunders walks in detail; the steps below are voice-shaped, but a writer whose strong-opinion thing is sound or structure can adapt them.

## The move

1. Find a seed. Saunders names four sources. (a) A real person whose voice you can hear, sometimes a person you only know by their writing (a student's paper, a colleague's email, a bus driver's announcement). (b) A tendency in yourself, exaggerated past where you would normally let it go (a worry-prone monkey mind ratcheted up; an inarticulateness dial turned past your ordinary self-correction). (c) A fragment of language from elsewhere whose flavor you cannot stop noticing (a sentence, a clause, a misuse). The student-paper line about a reader feeling at a distinct tilt is Saunders's worked instance of the third. (d) A single sentence the writer has perfected by radical preference alone, with no service to any pre-existing host story (Saunders's Appendix C addition; see step 1a). For the first three, the seed is a voice — a continuous register the writer will sustain. For the fourth, the seed is a single artifact whose virtues come from the writer's own taste rather than from imitation; the artifact's implications, not a register's continuation, supply what comes next.

   1a. Detachment gesture (perfected-sentence variant). If the sentence you are starting from was originally written for a host story (your own draft, or a translation exercise where the source is some other writer's story), explicitly detach it. Saunders frames the move as a deliberate act: forget the host story, and perfect the sentence by your own preference until it lands as good as you can make it. Then read it as if you had just encountered it. The world it implies — the character it would suit, the mood it presupposes, the constraint it places on what can come next — is what you follow from here.
2. Commit to imitating it. Try to sound like that sentence. Write a paragraph or a page in the voice. The goal is not to satirize, document, or analyze; the goal is to sustain the voice. Saunders's "Jon" example: he was "just riffing on that sentence in the student paper, trying to sound 'like' that sentence."
3. Keep almost no other goal in mind. Saunders is direct: "I'm giving almost no thought to anything but sustaining the voice — not thinking of the story's themes or what needs to happen next or any of that." If a thought about "what should happen" surfaces, note it and return to the voice.
4. Let the world emerge. The voice will force vocabulary, idiom, register, and reference. Those force a setting: who would talk this way, where, about what. Saunders's "Jon" page produced "Privacy Tarps" and "Gender Areas" by lexical pressure alone; a roomful of confined teenagers and a corporate masturbation-encouragement program then arrived because the vocabulary had nowhere else to go. The path narrows by itself.
5. Apply the escalation engine. As the voice settles into rules the reader can sense, the reader will get restless with sentences that merely abide by those rules. To keep the voice generative, keep putting new events in front of the character — events new to that character — so the voice has to find new registers in response. Saunders's image: "If a character, talking along in a certain voice, has never seen a horse before, and I show him one, his voice has to expand, to accommodate the horse." The horse can be anything the character has not yet had to speak about: a piece of news, an arrival, a small trauma, a confession, a kiss.
6. Let the voice and the writer co-lead. Saunders's framing is reflexive: the voice leads the writer (forcing world, vocabulary, plot), and the writer leads the voice (turning dials, pushing the inarticulateness or the cynicism or the corporate-poetry up a notch when the voice plateaus). Neither is alone in charge. If you find yourself only following the voice with no input, the work flattens; if you find yourself only steering the voice with no listening, the work freezes.
7. Beat back conceptions of "outcome" or "message" as they arrive. The story will start trying to be about something. Note this; it is fine. Saunders's "Jon" came to be "about" corporate capitalism, materialism, language, love, marriage, loyalty — none of which he intended at the start. The aboutness is what arrives, not what you aimed at. When you catch yourself trying to engineer the message, return to the voice.
8. Read what you have. The arc that has appeared is the arc; the world is the world; the meaning is whatever the voice forced into being. Revise from there using `iterative-preference-revision`.

## Signal it landed

The draft contains a setting, a cast, and a situation that you can describe but did not plan. You can name the seed (the student paper, the exaggerated tendency, the real person) and trace specific elements of the world back to vocabulary or register pressure from that seed. You can describe at least one moment when a new event ("the horse") forced the voice to find a register it had not used before, and you can hear in the prose that the voice did expand. The story has acquired what feels like a topic, but you can confirm — by checking your notes or by asking yourself "did I sit down planning to write about this" — that you did not start with it.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "What is this story going to be about?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer changes from a stated topic ("about loneliness," "about corporate language") to a description of a voice and an account of what that voice has so far forced into being. The topic, if there is one, is the byproduct, not the input.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this method, drafting begins from conception: a topic, a theme, a message, an outcome the writer has decided the story will deliver. The writer then tries to engineer prose that gets the reader to that destination. Saunders's diagnosis: this produces a lecture or a one-sided performance and drives the reader away. The reader senses that the writer arrived knowing what to make them feel and has been steering accordingly; the prose registers as advocacy rather than as truth. The writer also freezes, because the gap between current sentence and intended destination is always large enough to demand strategy, and the strategizing is what the writer is doing instead of writing. Saunders's archery image names the corrective: stop aiming at the target, attend to the feeling of the arrow leaving the bow, and let the arrow find its own target.

A second failure mode: writing in voice but treating voice as decoration. The writer composes a paraphrasable proposition ("I really enjoyed that and I think I'm in love") and then dresses it up. The dressing-up reads as ornament; the truth-content of the prose is recoverable as the bare statement. The reader feels nothing the proposition did not already convey. The corrective is the orientation in "When to reach for this": voice carries what propositional content cannot reach, so the voice is not added on top of the truth, the voice is part of the truth.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders is grading student papers on Kafka's *Metamorphosis* and reads the line "Upon perusing this story, I felt myself at a distinct tilt." His first reaction is "Pretty bad. But also: kind of great." He starts trying to imitate the voice, looking for a corollary inside himself, and within a few pages has produced a sentence that begins "Back in the time of which I am speaking, due to our Coordinators had mandated us, we had all seen that educational video..." A roomful of horny teenagers is suddenly there, in a facility where horniness has been judged so problematic that the management has produced a corporate masturbation-encouragement video called "It's Yours to Do With What You Like!" The vocabulary keeps forcing the world. A few lines later "Privacy Tarps" and "Gender Areas" appear because the syntax wants them. Once Privacy Tarps and Gender Areas exist, two of the teenagers (Ruthie and Josh) leave their respective Gender Areas to have sex on the very next page; the path the story is on has narrowed. Saunders had at no point sat down thinking "I'd like to do a little something on the topic of how a group of confined teens deal with their powerful dawning sexuality." He had been riffing on a sentence. Later he turns up a dial labeled "Level of Inarticulateness" and tells himself "do part surfer, part corporate wonk." Sentences arrive whose syntax is defective but whose meaning lands oddly efficiently ("Then came the final straw that broke the back of me saying no to my gonads"). The horse, for Jon, is whatever new event arrives next: a chip in the neck loaded with TV commercials indexed by Location Indicators; a girl named Carolyn; a melting kiss; eventually an exit from the facility. Each forces the voice to expand. The story comes to be "about" corporate capitalism, materialism, the deformation of language by commerce, love, marriage, loyalty. None of that was intended at the start. The aboutness was what arrived.
