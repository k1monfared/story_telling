## When to reach for this

Your story has at its center an event the reader cannot directly access on the page. Singing, a sermon, a kiss, the taste of a dish, a magical performance, a charismatic argument, a speech that supposedly transforms its listeners. You can describe the event but the description alone will not tell the reader whether it is working on the people in the story. Reach for this whenever "the reader has to feel that it landed" cannot be solved by better description of the event itself.

## The move

1. Name the unwitnessable event. The thing the reader cannot fully experience: the singing, the eulogy, the meal.
2. Recognize that the reader's verdict on the event will be drawn from the reactions of bystanders, not from the description of the event. Plan accordingly.
3. Build the panel. Identify or invent multiple side characters who will be present during the event, each with a distinct personality, susceptibility, and credibility relative to the event's domain.
4. Establish each panelist's credibility on the page, before the event. Saunders's pattern: a long static description, even a tedious one, that fixes for the reader what kind of judge this person is. The drunken Idiot Judge whose enthusiasm is too easy. The cynical innkeeper who does not pass false judgment. The locally feared Hercules whose verdict is final. The hardened wife who never cries.
5. Make the panelists differ in at least two dimensions. Susceptibility: how easily they are moved. Authority: how much weight their verdict carries. A panel that is uniform in either dimension cannot grade.
6. During the event, narrate the event minimally and the reactions specifically. A first impression from the lowest-credibility panelist is your "broad indicator." A subtler reaction from a higher-credibility panelist refines the verdict. The highest-authority panelist, held in reserve, finalizes it.
7. Use the order deliberately. Saunders calls this an ascending ladder of credibility: lower-authority reactions come first, higher-authority reactions later. The reader learns to weight earlier reactions against later ones. A premature verdict from the Idiot Judge can be subtly overturned by the silence of the high-authority panelist, then confirmed or replaced when the highest panelist finally speaks.
8. The verdict on the event is the cumulative shape of the panel's reactions, not any single one. Once the panel has spoken, the unwitnessable event has been adjudicated for the reader without the writer ever having to render the event itself convincingly.

## Signal it landed

For each side character present during the central event, you can finish three sentences. "What kind of judge they are: ___." "Where on the credibility ladder they sit: ___." "What their reaction during the event tells the reader: ___." The reactions, taken in order, form a weighted verdict that the reader receives even though the event itself was not directly rendered. Cutting any one panelist would weaken the verdict in a specific, nameable way.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of the central event: "How will the reader know whether it worked?" Note your answer. If it is "I will describe the event vividly," you have not solved the problem.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if your answer becomes "the panel will tell them, in this graded order, with these weights," and you can name each panelist's contribution.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the panel, you try to make the unwitnessable event land through the quality of your description of it, and you cannot. Singing on the page is not singing. A sermon transcribed is not the sermon delivered. The reader's verdict on these events is being constructed from the bystanders whether you plan it or not, so an unplanned panel is a panel that votes randomly. Worse, if all your bystanders react identically (they all weep, or all applaud), they collapse into one undifferentiated voice and the event reads as merely asserted, not adjudicated. Saunders is exact about the alternative: differentiated panelists with established credibilities make the event readable in real time, and the reader leaves the scene knowing both what happened and how to weight it.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders walks through Turgenev's contest scene a second time with the panel-of-judges lens. Booby is the Idiot Judge, a known local drunk whose pronouncement that the contractor has won serves as a broad early indicator and is then subtly overturned by Nikolai the innkeeper, who is "an excellent judge of what a true Russian considers to be either interesting or important." The Wild Gentleman has been set up via a long description as having "enormous influence in all the district," "passionately fond of singing," with "tremendous forces" hidden inside him. He becomes the highest-authority panelist, and his reactions are the ones the others orbit. During the contractor's song he smiles at one transition and softens his gaze, but does not finalize. During Yashka's song the hardened wife cries, Nikolai looks down (hiding tears), Blinker turns away (hiding tears), Booby is stunned silent, the little peasant whimpers, and finally the Wild Gentleman sheds "a heavy tear" and says the single word "Yashka." The verdict is delivered. The singing itself is unhearable. The graded reactions, in order, have made it not just heard but adjudicated. Saunders names the underlying engineering: this is exactly why the eleven pre-contest pages of static description had to exist, so the reader would know how to weight each reaction once the contest began.
