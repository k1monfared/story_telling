## When to reach for this

You have just received a piece of feedback that is phrased as a verdict. It tells you what kind of person you are, what kind of work you produced, what kind of impact you had. You are about to engage with the verdict directly: defending against it, conceding it, or filing it. Reach for this before you do, because the verdict is two layers deep and you are about to act on the surface layer.

## The move

1. Treat any feedback judgment as a two-step product. The chapter's claim: feedback givers arrive at their labels in two steps. They observe data (what they saw, heard, felt, smelled, remembered, read), and they interpret the data (the story they told about what it meant). The verdict you receive is the second step packaged as the whole.
2. Ask for the data first. Some version of "what specifically did you see or hear that led you to that," or "what is an example you have in mind," or "walk me through a time when this happened." The chapter is clear that the data is not facts and figures only; it includes behaviors, statements, tone, body language, work products, even the giver's own emotional reactions ("when you didn't email me back, I was frustrated").
3. Push past the second-order interpretation that the giver may offer in place of data. The chapter flags this directly: when Nicholas asks Adrianna for the data behind "more assertive," her first answer is "what I saw on the sales floor is that you were too laid back." That is still interpretation, not data. The skill calls for one more push: "what about my tone, my body language? Show me." The data you are after is the raw observation, not the next-level-down judgment.
4. Then ask for the interpretation explicitly. "And what did that mean to you? What conclusion did you draw from it?" The chapter is emphatic that the goal is not to dismiss the interpretation. Both layers matter. You are after a clear picture of both, side by side.
5. Notice your own confusion of the two. The chapter folds in the receiver's parallel mistake: even when you describe "data" to yourself you may be storing interpretations as data. Their example: "you barked at the kids" — barking is itself an interpretation; the data is the actual sound of what you said. The skill applies in both directions: when you ask the giver for data, and when you watch your own mind treat its own interpretations as facts.

## Signal it landed

You can describe, in two sentences, the data the giver observed and the interpretation they drew from it, as separate things. If a third party asked "what did your boss actually see," you could answer with the observation, not with the conclusion. If they asked "and what did your boss make of that," you could answer with the conclusion, not pretend it does not exist. The two-layer structure is visible to you and can be discussed.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I had to write down what specifically the giver saw or heard, separately from what they made of it, could I?" If the only answer is the verdict ("they think I'm not a team player"), the layers have not been separated.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can name the data in their words ("she heard me tell Gus I was too busy to help") and the interpretation in their words ("which she read as me prioritizing my own work over the team's"), and you can locate where you agree or disagree at each layer rather than at the verdict alone.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the separation, two failure modes alternate. In the first, you argue the verdict directly: "I am too a team player, here are five examples." The giver experiences this as denial of their lived observation, because the data they actually saw is still sitting underneath unrefuted. The argument heats up and neither of you is talking about the same thing. In the second, you concede the verdict because it sounds plausible or because you do not want to fight, and you end up agreeing to a conclusion whose underlying observations you have never seen. Six months later you discover the data was a single conversation taken out of context, or an event you remember differently, and the verdict has hardened into received truth in your file. Both failures share the same skipped step: the conclusion was treated as the message, when in fact the conclusion was a person's reading of a specific set of observations, both of which deserved their own discussion.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter walks through Nicholas and Adrianna in detail. Adrianna gives the label "be more assertive." When Nicholas asks for the data, her first move is to describe him as "too laid back" — itself an interpretation. Nicholas pushes one more level: "It's my tone? What about my tone? My body language? Show me." Only at this level does the actual observation start to surface. The chapter's broader point lands through the parenting-at-the-next-table example: when we say we "saw people parenting badly," the data was actually the way the woman looked at the baby or how the man responded when the baby wailed; "bad parenting" was the auto-story, not the observation. Paul and Monisha's conversation at the end of the chapter shows the move from the receiver's side: when Monisha says she sees "a genuine attitude problem," Paul asks "what do you mean by messaging versus attitude?" and then asks her to walk through a specific example (the ethics workshops), opening up both the data and the interpretation behind her label.

## Source

Chapter 3, "First Understand," lines 1281-1341 ("Ask Where the Feedback Is Coming From," with the "They Observe Data," "They Interpret the Data," and "They Confuse Data and Interpretation (We All Do)" subsections). Stone and Heen lay out the two-step decomposition, give it the explicit Roger Schank framing about human intelligence being organized around stories rather than data, and provide the Nicholas/Adrianna script as the receiver-side move. The skill installs the unpacking move: under any judgment label sit two layers, and both must be made discussable for the feedback to be understood.
