## When to reach for this

You have just finished reading a story (or a draft, your own or someone else's), and you want to understand how it worked on you. You feel the urge to skip past your felt reaction and go straight to interpretation, theme statements, or a thumbs-up/thumbs-down verdict. Reach for this whenever the next move would otherwise be a literary-vocabulary summary.

## The move

1. Stop before you interpret. Do not yet ask what the story means or whether it was good.
2. Turn your mind to the experience you just had. Run the prompts: Was there a place that moved you? Something you resisted or that confused you? A moment you teared up, got annoyed, thought something new? Were you bored or pissed off? Any lingering questions?
3. For each reaction, point to the specific line, paragraph, or scene where it happened. Anchor every reaction to a location on the page.
4. Write the reactions down in plain language. Refuse the words "theme," "plot," "character development," "metaphor," "symbol," and any other literary-class vocabulary. If a reaction is "I got bored here," that is the right wording.
5. Treat every honest reaction as valid data, including boredom, annoyance, and confusion. Do not edit them to sound more sophisticated.
6. Only after the reactions are listed and located, allow yourself to interpret or evaluate.

## Signal it landed

You can produce a list of at least three reactions, each tied to a specific spot in the text, written in everyday language. The list contains at least one reaction you would not have written down if your goal had been to sound smart (boredom, irritation, confusion, a guilty laugh).

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask yourself: "What did this story do to me, and where on the page did it do it?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. Compare. The skill landed if the second answer is longer and more specific than the first, names places on the page, and includes at least one reaction your first answer skipped or smoothed over.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the drill, you collapse the story into an interpretation ("it's about loneliness," "the theme is grief," "the character arc is well done") and lose access to the diagnostic data your own reactions provide. The interpretation feels like understanding, but you cannot reverse-engineer the technique from it: you no longer know which sentence made you cry, which paragraph lost you, or which line earned the ending. You have summarized the story instead of studying how it worked, and you walk away with nothing you could apply to a draft.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders frames the entire book around this drill: he will give you a story, you read it, then turn your mind to the experience. He explicitly invites reactions like "I got bored here" or "this confounded me" as the starting data of the analysis, not as failures to be hidden. His own essays then walk through his felt reactions to each Russian story line by line and only afterward offer technical explanations for why those reactions happened.

## Source

Chapter "We Begin," lines 188-278 (especially 243, 247, 251). Saunders introduces "the basic drill," names the diagnostic question "What did we feel and where did we feel it?", and frames the book as a workbook designed to push the reader past a casual first reading by training the reaction-locating habit.
