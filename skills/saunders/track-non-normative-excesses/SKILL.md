## When to reach for this

You are reading a story to learn from it, or revising your own draft. The text contains features that call attention to themselves by deviating from what feels normal, an impossible incident, markedly elevated or vernacular language, a long digression at the wrong moment, repeated description of secondary characters when the main event is waiting. You feel either irritation ("this is a flaw") or aesthetic acceptance ("this is the writer's style"). Reach for this before either reaction settles, because both of them stop the diagnostic clock.

## The move

1. Keep a running list while you read. Saunders calls it the TICHN cart, Things I Couldn't Help Noticing.
2. Add an item whenever the text presents something non-normative: a surface oddity (alliteration in three pages, a switch into reverse chronology), a structural feature (eleven pages of digression before the contest starts), a physical implausibility, a tonal break, anything that registers as "presentational excess."
3. Phrase each item neutrally. Not "this is bad" or "this is striking." Just: "Noticed: long static descriptions of every face in the pub." The cart records features, not verdicts.
4. Once an item is on the cart, enter the "waiting to see" stance. You are now in a transactional relationship with the writer: you have allowed the excess provisionally, on the implicit promise that the story will pay it off.
5. As you read on, watch each item. Mark whether the story (a) redeems it by making the excess do work that justifies its presence, (b) leaves it unredeemed, or (c) escalates it (which is usually a sign more redemption is coming).
6. At the end, do a retro-assessment. Items the story redeemed are now virtues, evidence the writer "meant to do that." Items the story left unredeemed are weaknesses, evidence the writer either did not notice or did not solve them. The pattern of redemptions and non-redemptions is your assessment of the story's craft.
7. For your own draft, do the audit on yourself. List the excesses your draft is asking the reader to tolerate. For each, name what later move is going to redeem it. If you cannot name one, you have a problem to solve.

## Signal it landed

You can produce a list of every non-normative feature in the story or draft, paired with whether and how the rest of the work redeems it. The list contains items you would have either dismissed as flaws or absorbed as style if you had not been keeping the cart. At least one item on your own draft's list lacks a redeeming later move, and you know which item it is and what kind of move would redeem it.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "What in this story is asking the reader to extend goodwill, and what is the story doing to repay that goodwill?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if your answer becomes a specific list of features (not "the style"), each paired with a specific later move that earns it (not "the overall effect"), with the unredeemed items separately marked.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this audit, you collapse non-normative features into either "flaw" or "style" and lose the data they carry. Reading: you cannot say what the writer's apparent excesses are doing structurally, so you cannot reverse-engineer the technique. The story's "voice" feels mysterious in a way you cannot transfer to your own work. Revising: you preserve excesses that are not redeemed (they are "your style") or remove ones that are doing real work (they "felt off"). The draft drifts. Saunders is direct about the underlying craft fact: a story that approaches its ending with nothing in its TICHN cart is going to have a hard time ending spectacularly, because it had no apparent flaws to convert into virtues at the last moment, which is one of the most powerful things a story can do.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders opens his class on "The Singers" by collecting his students' first reactions. The students name what is on their TICHN carts: the digressions, the endless physical descriptions, the slowness, the way Turgenev seems to need to tell us everything about everyone. Saunders treats these reactions as the start of the diagnostic, not as complaints to be smoothed over. The rest of his essay then does the redemption audit: for each item the cart accumulated in the first eleven pages, he asks what later work in the story uses it. The descriptions of side characters turn out to be needed because those characters function as a graded panel of judges during the singing contest. The description of the ravine turns out to be needed because the story is structured around binaries that the cleft town cues. The cart's items get redeemed one by one, and the story's apparent excesses become its virtues.

## Source

Chapter "The Heart of the Story," lines 1656-1666. Saunders names the TICHN cart, describes the "transactional" reading stance it produces, and proposes the criterion: a good story is one that, having created a pattern of excesses, notices those excesses and converts them into virtues.

## Related skills

- Contrasts `confess-the-oddness`. Use track-non-normative-excesses while drafting or critiquing, when the question is "is this excess being redeemed?"; the procedure is to log items and watch the rest of the work pay them off. Use confess-the-oddness at the ending of a draft whose middle has accumulated unredeemable excesses, when the question is "how do I close without explaining them away?"; the procedure is to acknowledge them without resolving them. The two share the cart-emptying language Saunders uses across the chapters: TICHN tracks; confession empties the cart by being seen.
- See also `truncation-test` (the pop-out variant explicitly logs cut units to the TICHN cart for deferred redemption) and `holding-up-against-the-heart` (the cart's items are commonly the suspect elements held against the heart).
