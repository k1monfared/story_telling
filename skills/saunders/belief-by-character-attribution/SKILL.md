## When to reach for this

You have a belief, a position, an articulate piece of moral or political thinking that wants to be in your story. You can write it well as a paragraph of essay. The story currently does not have a place for that paragraph that does not feel like a lecture pasted in. Or you are revising and a character, mid-scene, has just produced a blurt of conviction the writer used to hold but no longer does, and the impulse is to either rewrite the line into something the writer still endorses or cut it. Reach for this whenever the question "is this me speaking" is interfering with the work, and whenever a story you are writing is at risk of becoming a sermon.

## The move

1. Render the belief in full conviction. Do not water it down to keep it deniable. Saunders's note on Ivan's speech: "articulate, earnest, precisely expressed, supported with examples, infused with sincere intent." The truth-content of the position is what gives the move its power; if you hedge the speech, the destabilization that follows will have nothing to destabilize.
2. Attribute the belief to a character. The character is not yourself, even if the belief is. Saunders's frame: "we export fragments of ourselves, then give those fragments pants and a hairstyle and a hometown and all of that." The character can be a former self (Saunders writes a sixteen-year-old in "Victory Lap" voicing what he believed at sixteen and no longer believes at fifty-one), a latent voice (an opinion the writer can imitate but does not endorse), or a fragment of self that competes with other fragments. The constraint is only that the character be rendered as a particular person, not as a mouthpiece.
3. Refuse ownership. Once the speech is on the page, take a step back. Saunders's framing: "It's not me, it's him." This is not disclaiming responsibility; it is refusing to use the story as the platform for an authorial position. The writer remains responsible for everything in the story, but the story is no longer required to endorse the speech.
4. Have the story react to the speaker. Ask three diagnostic questions of the character who just delivered the speech. (a) Are there consequences to believing this way that the speaker has not noticed? (b) Are there suspicious overtones in what the character just said — places the conviction got cranky, leapt over a counterargument, addressed an imaginary critic, or revealed personal frustration? (c) Are there latent unforeseen consequences — actions the character will take in the next scene that the conviction makes inevitable but the speaker has not yet seen?
5. Render one of the diagnostic answers as a small concrete detail in the scene or just after it. Saunders's term: a smelly pipe. The detail does not refute the speech and is not framed as commentary on it. It is an observable fact that, by attribution to the character who delivered the speech, makes the speech read differently on rereading. The pipe stinks; the friend cannot sleep; the man who exhorted "Do not cease to do good" has neglected ordinary courtesy. The story does not say so; the prose lets the reader notice.
6. Stage the back-scan. After the destabilizing detail, the reader will scan back through the story re-reading earlier elements through the revised question. Saunders walks his own scan in "Gooseberries": "I find myself scanning back through the story, looking for: Happiness, Things About." Make sure earlier elements support the back-scan. The swimming scene must be there for the reader to find when they go looking; the work the writer does in advance is to plant the countervailing evidence, not to engineer the reread.
7. Let both readings stand. Saunders's criterion: "the two readings coexist, making a truth bigger than either would have alone." Do not write a sentence that resolves the tension. The speech remains true; the speaker remains flawed; the reader holds both.

## Signal it landed

A reader cannot tell whether the writer endorses the speech. The truth-content of the position is felt and remembered; the speaker's flaws are also felt and remembered. The reader's relationship to the speech is "this is true and this is also suspect, and the story does not tell me which to weigh more." You can name, on rereading your draft, both the place where the belief lands with full conviction and the place (often a small concrete detail near the close, often not where you expected it) where the speaker's having delivered it is complicated by an action of theirs the prose merely reports.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, point at the passage where the belief is currently expressed. Ask: "If a reader asked me whether I believe this, what would I say?" If the answer is "yes, this is what I think" or "no, but I want it to be in the story anyway," the belief is currently functioning as authorial position.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer becomes "the character believes it; the story takes the belief seriously; whether I as the writer endorse it is no longer the question the prose is asking." You can also point at the destabilizing detail you wrote in and name what it is doing for the speaker's credibility.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this discipline, a story with a sincere belief in it slides toward one of two failures. The lecture failure: the belief is delivered as authorial commentary, by the narrator or by an unflawed mouthpiece character, and the story functions as a vehicle for the position. The reader, sensing the vehicle, either agrees and feels flattered or disagrees and stops reading; in neither case does the story do work that an essay could not have done better. The hedge failure: the writer, sensing the polemic risk, waters the belief down until it has lost its truth-content; the reader feels the hedge and the story no longer has anything to destabilize. Saunders names the third path: deliver the belief in full conviction, attribute it to a character, render a small concrete fact about that character that complicates the conviction without refuting it, and let both stand. The reader exits with the belief's power intact and the speaker's flaws also intact, and the story has done what essays cannot: held two true things in suspension.

A second failure mode: confusing this skill with skaz-narrator. Skaz-narrator is about a narrator-character whose graceless register is itself the meaning; the reader is meant to see past the narrator's voice to the truth the narrator is missing. Belief-by-character-attribution is the opposite shape: the speaker delivers the belief with full sincerity and the writer believes (or once believed) the belief is true; the destabilization comes from the speaker's flaws as a person, not from the speaker's narrative voice. Conflating them produces either a skaz speech that no one is meant to take seriously (which loses the truth-content) or a sincere speech delivered by a "wise" character with no flaws to complicate it (which loses the destabilization).

## Worked micro-example

Saunders gives a worked instance from his own story "Victory Lap." He set out to write a sweet, nonviolent story like Chekhov's "After the Theatre," in which a teenaged girl waits for her mother to pick her up for dance class and thinks giddily about love. The sweet-monologue version was no good — chatty, anecdotal, static, nothing at stake. But in the inner monologue Saunders had let the girl blurt: "To do good, you just have to decide to do good. You have to be brave. You have to stand up for what's right." Saunders identifies the blurt as a position he held at sixteen and no longer held at fifty-one. The story's response, which Saunders hears as if in dialogue with the character: "Oh, really?" The story is now committed to challenging the girl's facile belief. Saunders does not narrate the challenge as authorial counter-argument; he lets the plot test the belief by what happens next, which (he says without spoiling) is the opposite of the sweet nonviolent story he meant to write. The procedure is the same as Chekhov's: a sincere belief is attributed to a particular character, the character delivers it with conviction, the writer takes a step back, and the story reacts to the character's having said it. The destabilization arrives as event, not as commentary.

## Related skills

- See also `persistent-self-contradiction`. The two are complementary and commonly fire together. Belief-by-character-attribution renders one earnest position with full conviction and destabilizes it by a small concrete fact about the speaker; persistent-self-contradiction is the macro-structural method that builds counterweights across the rest of the story so the destabilization fits a thinking machine rather than a single hedge. The first is the move at one position; the second is the architecture that holds many.
- See also `skaz-narrator` (kept separate from this skill: skaz renders graceless misfire whose voice the reader sees past, while attribution renders sincere belief whose truth-content the reader is meant to feel; conflating them collapses both moves) and `follow-the-voice` (the same disposition applied to drafting: write in the voice of a character who is not you, let the prose generate, and trust the story to react).
