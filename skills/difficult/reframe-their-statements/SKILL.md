## When to reach for this

The other person is: insisting they are right and you are wrong; attributing bad intentions to you; placing all the blame on you; attacking your character; or saying something that would derail the conversation if you responded to it at face value. You want to redirect without dismissing what they said.

## The move

Reframing is translating what the other person says into concepts from the Three Conversations framework — contribution instead of blame, perspective instead of truth, impact instead of intention. You take the essence of their message (which is often real and worth addressing) and rephrase it in a way that moves toward understanding rather than argument.

The technique is to respond not to the aggressive or unhelpful framing but to the legitimate underlying message. The chapter provides worked translations:

- **"I'm right, and there are no two ways about it!"** → "I want to make sure I understand your perspective. You obviously feel very strongly about it. I'd also like to share my perspective on the situation." (Reframes: from truth to perspectives.)

- **"You hurt me on purpose!"** → "I can see that you're feeling really angry about what I did, which is upsetting to me. It wasn't my intention. Can you say more about how you felt?" (Reframes: from intention assumption to feelings + impact.)

- **"This is all your fault!"** → "I'm sure I've contributed to the problem; I think we both have. Rather than focus on whose fault this is, I'd like to look at how we each contributed to the situation." (Reframes: from blame to contribution.)

- **"You are the nastiest person I've ever met."** → "It sounds like you're feeling really badly." (Reframes: from character judgment to feeling.)

The pattern across these: acknowledge the valid emotional content of what they said, redirect the framing to one that both parties can work with, and invite further exploration rather than defending against the accusation.

**Be persistent.** The other person may not immediately accept the reframe. They may return to blame, to certainty about their view, or to accusation. Reframe again. Sydney reframes Miguel three times before he starts to shift — each time, she takes whatever he says, finds the legitimate kernel in it, and translates it back into the contribution frame. The chapter: "Sydney had to be persistent in redirecting him away from blame."

**The key is acknowledging the kernel while redirecting the framing.** You are not dismissing what they said. You are translating it into a form that can be discussed. If you simply ignore or reject what they said, the conversation stalls. If you accept their framing, you get drawn into a blame or truth argument. Reframing takes the real substance — their anger, their frustration, their sense of injustice — and channels it toward understanding.

## Signal it landed

The other person's next statement is slightly less extreme or accusatory than their previous one. They have accepted, at least implicitly, the frame you offered and are now responding within it. You are no longer arguing about who is right or who is at fault; you are exploring what happened from each person's perspective.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "am I about to respond to their framing (argue about blame, defend my intentions, correct their facts) or to redirect it?" If the first, reframing has not happened.

After: ask "did the conversation move toward exploration after my response, or did it stay in argument?" If it moved, the reframe worked.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without reframing, the conversation reacts to the stated framing rather than the underlying issue. "This is all your fault" gets a defense of your blamelessness, which gets a counter-defense of their blamelessness. The cycle continues, each statement generating the counter-defense that generates the next statement. The conversation stays in the loop the chapter describes: "You'll talk about understanding, and they'll talk about who's right. You'll talk about contribution, while they're stuck in blame." Reframing is the move that steps out of the loop.

## Worked micro-example

Sydney and Miguel. Miguel's statements: "A team leader should know how to handle this better" (blame). "American women are oversensitive" (character/cultural blame). "You are overreacting" (invalidating her experience). "You should have brought this up sooner" (blame again). Sydney reframes each time without escalating: acknowledges her own contribution, translates cultural blame into a difference frame ("we are coming from very different cultural perspectives"), asks about what his behavior meant to him rather than arguing about whether it was appropriate. Eventually Miguel himself says, "I suppose I could have also asked you if things were okay... maybe we should have talked about expectations up front." He has moved from blame to contribution through persistent reframing.
