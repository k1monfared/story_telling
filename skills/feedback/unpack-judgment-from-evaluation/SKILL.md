## When to reach for this

You received feedback that is genuinely evaluative: a grade, a rejection, a ranking, a termination. You cannot sort this as coaching because the evaluation is explicit and the stakes are real. Your identity has been triggered, but you are not sure whether it is the rating itself, the consequences, or what the evaluator seems to think of you that is hitting hardest.

## The move

Break the evaluation into three separate components and handle each on its own terms.

**Assessment: where do you stand?** The assessment is the data. At the track meet you ran the mile in five minutes, nineteen seconds, placing you fourth in the forty-to-forty-five age group. It is a number, a rank, a placement. The assessment is usually the least identity-loaded of the three components; it is a measurement. Ask: "Is this assessment accurate?" If you have concerns about the measurement itself (the methodology, the criteria, the data used), raise them. If the assessment seems accurate, accept it.

**Consequences: what happens as a result?** Consequences are the real-world outcomes that follow from the assessment. You qualify for regionals; you do not qualify for nationals. You did not get the promotion. Consequences can be certain or speculative, immediate or longer-term. Ask: "Are the consequences clear? Are they proportionate to the assessment?" Consequences deserve attention because they are often the locus of the most legitimate concern. Distinguish between consequences that are certain (you did not get the raise; there is a dollar amount) and consequences that are speculative (your spouse will leave you because of the raise). The chapter's caution: in the grip of upsetting feedback, we tend to process speculative consequences as if they were certain. Right-size the speculative ones (this belongs to contain-the-story).

**Judgment: what story does the giver tell about the assessment and its consequences?** Judgment is the interpretation. Your coach is disappointed by your time and thinks you should have done better. Your neighbor disapproves of your "home environment." Judgment is one person's reading of what the assessment means. It is not the assessment itself. Ask: "Whose judgment is this? Do they have information I don't have? Is there something to learn from their interpretation? Or is it one reading among multiple possible readings?"

**Locate which component is triggering your identity.** In most evaluations it is the judgment that hooks identity. The assessment (fourth place) is acceptable; the consequences (regionals, not nationals) are manageable; the coach's disappointment is what cuts. Once you have located the trigger, you can respond to it specifically. You may want to discuss why the coach's judgment differs from yours. Or you may decide that their judgment is one interpretation you can weigh and set aside. Either way, you are no longer responding to the whole evaluation as if it were a single undifferentiated blow.

The chapter: "Accurate assessment is valuable and the consequences are important to understand. Others' judgments? You may find certain judgments illuminating; other judgments you'll rightly dismiss. It's one person's interpretation, and you've got your own interpretation, thank you very much."

## Signal it landed

You can write three separate sentences: what was the assessment, what are the certain consequences, and what is the evaluator's judgment. The trigger is in one of those sentences, not spread across all three. You know which one.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "when I think about this evaluation, what exactly is hurting?" If the answer is "all of it" or "I'm not sure," the three components are merged and need unpacking.

After: ask "is the assessment fair? Are the consequences clear? Whose judgment am I reacting to, and what would it mean if I treated that judgment as one interpretation rather than a verdict?" If you can answer each question separately, the unpacking has worked.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without unpacking, the whole evaluation lands as a single undifferentiated identity challenge. The assessment, the consequences, and the judgment arrive together and are processed together. The result is that you either accept or reject the whole package, which means you lose the parts worth accepting and preserve the parts worth contesting in the same move. A receiver who cannot separate judgment from assessment may reject an accurate assessment to protect against an unfair judgment. Or may accept a harsh judgment because the assessment is accurate. Unpacking allows the three components to be handled appropriately rather than as a single all-or-nothing verdict.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's track meet case: the assessment (fifth minute, nineteen seconds, fourth place) is objective. The consequences (regionals yes, nationals no) are specified by the rules. The coach's judgment (disappointed, thinks you should have done better) is one interpretation among possible others: you are delighted by the time because it beats your morning expectation. The identity trigger is the judgment, not the assessment or the consequences. Locating this allows you to respond specifically: why does the coach have a different judgment about your time? Is there something to learn from that discrepancy?
