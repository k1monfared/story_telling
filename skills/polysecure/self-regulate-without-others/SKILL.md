## When to reach for this

You feel a wave of activation — hurt, anxiety, restlessness — and your hand reaches for your phone, the fridge, a bottle, or a partner. Reach for this when the impulse is strong and the trigger is small. The size mismatch is the signal that you're using others/things to do regulation work that needs to happen internally.

## The move

1. Notice the impulse to outsource. The hand toward the phone is the moment to notice; that's the activation you can work with.
2. Slow down. Pause for thirty seconds without acting on the impulse. The activation will probably get more intense. That's expected.
3. Bring attention back to the body. Name three sensations: feet on the floor, breath in the chest, tension in the jaw. The body comes online when the head is busy.
4. Take three slow breaths, longer on the exhale than the inhale. The exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system, which is what's actually doing the regulation.
5. Stay with whatever feeling is there. Don't try to fix it. Five minutes is enough; it doesn't have to resolve, just be witnessed.
6. After regulating, you may still want to reach out to a partner. That's fine. The difference is that you reach out from a more regulated state, with a clearer ask, rather than from full activation that the partner has to resolve.

## Signal it landed

You can sit with mild-to-moderate activation for a few minutes without immediately discharging it through outside contact or substances. You start to notice the difference between "I want to share this" (clean reach) and "I need them to fix this" (auto-regulation request).

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this capacity, your partner becomes a thermostat for your nervous system. They have to pick up calls, respond quickly to texts, be available for crises that aren't really crises. The partnership starts to bend around your regulation needs in a way that exhausts the partner and prevents you from ever discovering you could do this yourself.

## Worked micro-example

Sam feels a wave of distress when their partner texts that the date will run late. The impulse is to text back asking what time exactly, when they'll be home, who they're with. Sam pauses. Notices the activation: tightness in the chest, slightly faster breath, urgency. Sets the phone down for ten minutes. Walks around the block. The activation drops by half. Sam then sends a single text: "Okay, see you when you're home, take your time." The partnership absorbs the night more easily; Sam notices they survived ten minutes of feeling activated without resolution.

## Reflection prompts

- What state are you most likely to outsource? Loneliness? Boredom? Anxiety? Anger?
- What's your typical auto-regulation tool? Phone, food, substance, partner, work?
- Are there moments when you've successfully self-regulated through difficulty? What made it possible?
- What might become available to you if you could sit with mild activation for ten minutes without outsourcing?
