## When to reach for this

You are revising a draft, evaluating a workshop story, or trying to decide whether a scene should stay. The scene is enjoyable in its own right. You can describe what happens. You cannot describe what changes because of it. Reach for this when "but it's a good scene" is the only argument keeping a scene in.

## The move

1. Locate the structural unit you are testing. A scene, a chapter, a section, an interlude, a flashback, a digression.
2. Apply the first criterion. Is the unit entertaining in its own right? Vivid, lively, funny, gripping, unsettling, beautiful — something that holds the reader on the page through it.
3. Apply the second criterion. Does the unit advance the story in a non-trivial way? Name what is different on the other side: a character is in a new state, the story's question has been refined, a possibility has been opened or closed, a stake has been raised.
4. Both must be true. If only the first is true, the unit is a vignette in a story that does not need a vignette there. If only the second is true, the unit is functional but boring and the reader is leaking attention. If both are true, keep it.
5. If only the first is true, your choices are: cut the unit, replace it with one that also does (2), or rewrite it to do (2) while preserving the entertaining quality you already have.

## Signal it landed

For every structural unit in the draft, you can finish two sentences. "This unit is entertaining because ___." "This unit advances the story by ___." Any unit where the second sentence stalls is marked for cutting or rewriting. The marks point at concrete revision actions, not at vague reservations.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, name a scene you suspect is weak and ask: "Why am I unsure about this scene?" Note your answer. It will likely be vague.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer becomes specific: "It is entertaining (this and that work), but on the other side of it the story is in the same state it was on the entry side, so it advances nothing." That answer tells you what to do.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this test, you keep scenes for the wrong reason. The scene is well-written, you remember writing it, your critique partner laughed at the joke in it, so it stays. Meanwhile the reader passes through it without anything in their understanding of the story changing, which is the exact failure Saunders calls out: the dog runs in and out, the ray of sunlight passes through the window, plates break, all in the manner of a real teahouse, none of it doing the work of a story. The draft accumulates such scenes and grows long without growing more story.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders tests Chekhov's teahouse scene against both criteria. Entertaining? Yes — there is a drunken peasant, a confused apology, the door's pulley banging. Advance the story? Yes, in a way that is easy to miss until you ask. Marya has, throughout the story so far, presented herself in interior monologue as a woman fallen among the rabble, and the reader has half-believed her. Inside the teahouse, an insulting peasant and a jeering reaction make her precarious social position visible from outside her head. The reader leaves the scene seeing her differently, and Saunders names the change: "Now we know" how far she has fallen. The scene satisfies both criteria.
