## When to reach for this

The coaching or feedback you are getting is not helping. The feedback is unclear, delivered in a way that triggers you before you can hear it, focused on the wrong things, or simply absent. You keep absorbing feedback that isn't reaching you, or the person who has the most to offer isn't offering it.

Also reach for this in any formal or informal coaching relationship before problems develop: establishing a shared understanding of how you learn best prevents most of the interference.

## The move

**Tell them about "feedback and you."** Tell the person giving you feedback the things they need to know to get through. The chapter's examples:

- Subtle doesn't work with me. Be really explicit and don't worry about hurting my feelings. You won't.
- I tend to get defensive at first, and then I circle back later and figure out why the feedback is helpful. So if I seem defensive, don't be put off.
- I react better when you present your advice as an idea that might help rather than as "the obviously right answer."
- Here's what I've been working on lately in terms of self-improvement. That's where I need the most help right now.
- I'm really sensitive to negative feedback. Don't give it in the middle of a presentation unless it's urgent and immediately actionable.

These are descriptions of your feedback footprint (from know-your-feedback-footprint) and your current growth focus. They are not demands; they are operational information that helps the coach get through rather than get blocked. The chapter: "We're explaining our particular defensive formation not to block givers out, but to help them get through."

**For more formal coaching relationships, discuss preferences, roles, and mutual expectations.** Three topics:

1. Your feedback temperament and tendencies: your wiring profile, your footprint, the kinds of feedback that land and those that tend to trigger.
2. Your current growth areas: what you are working on, what you want more help with.
3. Their philosophy, strengths, and weaknesses: ask what they find challenging about coaching you. Ask whether there is anything in how you receive their input that makes their job harder.

Also establish: whether the coaching is confidential, how often you will meet, how progress will be measured, what your priorities are.

**Raise it in hierarchy carefully.** When the coach is your boss, you may hesitate because admitting learning edges feels risky. The chapter's guidance: frame your learning needs in terms of doing your current job more effectively, not in terms of ambition or past failures. Not "I want to improve my meeting skills because I see myself as VP in five years" but "I want feedback on running meetings because I want to use the team's time well during the upcoming merger." The chapter also notes that workers who seek out coaching on what they can improve tend to receive higher performance ratings.

**Ask your coach what they find challenging.** The conversation is mutual. Your coach has their own preferences and blind spots. Asking them what they find challenging about the coaching work invites a more honest collaboration.

## Signal it landed

The person giving you feedback now knows at least one thing about how you receive it that they did not know before, and that knowledge changes how they deliver it going forward. The interference has been reduced.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "does the person giving me feedback know anything specific about how I receive it?" If the answer is no (they just give it and hope), the coach-your-coach conversation has not happened.

After: ask "is the feedback I'm receiving now landing differently than before the conversation?" If yes, the coaching has improved. If no, the conversation may not have been specific enough.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without coaching the coach, the feedback relationship continues to generate the same interference indefinitely, because neither party names what is blocking the feedback from getting through. Doug's football team lost all eight games because no one asked the coach to explain the "In and Out Zone." The players did not coach the coach about what they did not understand; the coach never learned that the defensive formation had not been communicated. The chapter: "Our colleagues and family aren't even aware that the advice isn't getting through. Or perhaps they are very aware it's not getting through, but they're not seeing that how they're handling it is part of the problem."
