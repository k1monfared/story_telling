## When to reach for this

You are arguing with someone. Neither of you is persuading the other. You are frustrated that they are being irrational, naive, selfish, or stubborn. You have told them what they should do more than once and they are not doing it. The argument seems like it should be resolvable — you are clearly right — but it keeps not resolving. Or you are preparing for a conversation you expect to be difficult, and you realize you have already decided the other person is the problem.

## The move

**Recognize the certainty stance.** You are in certainty when your internal stance is "they are the problem" or "I am right and they are wrong." Certainty is not wrong as a feeling — your story does make sense. The problem is that it blocks you from learning anything about why the other person sees things differently. When you argue from certainty, you trade conclusions: "get a new mattress" versus "stop trying to control me." Neither conclusion makes sense in the other person's story, so it doesn't land. Arguments don't change minds; they produce counter-arguments.

**Shift to curiosity.** The shift is internal, not verbal. Instead of asking yourself "How can they think that?!" ask yourself "I wonder what information they have that I don't?" Instead of "How can they be so irrational?" ask "How might they see the world such that their view makes sense?"

Three specific places to look for what you don't know:

1. **Different information.** What did they notice that you didn't? What information do they have about themselves — their constraints, fears, hopes, history — that you don't have access to? Jack doesn't know that Michael's client blew up that morning over another brochure. Michael doesn't know that Jack worked through the night and canceled a dinner with his wife. Each is missing crucial context for understanding the other's reaction.

2. **Different interpretations.** What past experiences are shaping how they interpret the facts? What implicit rules are they operating from that differ from yours? Caroline's Depression-era childhood makes even a moderately priced meal feel extravagant; Bonnie sees this as cheapness. Thelma's rule is "being late is unprofessional"; Ollie's rule is "obsessing about small things is unprofessional." Both rules are reasonable; neither is the truth.

3. **Self-interest.** How might their conclusion reflect what they want to believe? (And equally: how might yours?) Professor Raiffa's study: buyers valued a company at 30% below market; sellers valued it at 30% above. Both groups sincerely believed they were being objective.

**Also get curious about your own story.** Ask: what information am I selecting and ignoring? What implicit rules am I applying that feel like truths but are actually rules? What past experience is shaping how I'm interpreting this? Jack's implicit rule is "you should always show appreciation no matter what." He experiences Michael's reaction as a violation of this rule, but he hasn't named it as a rule — he experiences it as a simple truth about how people should behave. When you can see your own implicit rules as rules, you can share them as such rather than as facts about the other person's failures.

The shift to curiosity does not require you to abandon your view. It means temporarily holding it loosely enough to learn what the other person knows. Tony's wife Keiko catches herself in certainty ("What kind of person thinks football is more important than family?") and negotiates herself to curiosity: "I wonder what Tony knows that I don't, how he's seeing the world such that his decision seems to make sense." Tony's story (the three hours is his only genuine relaxation of the week, essential for his mental health) was nothing like what she had imagined. She would never have heard it without the shift.

## Signal it landed

Your internal question has changed. You have moved from "why are they being like this?" to "what might they know or believe that I don't?" You can articulate at least one specific question about their perspective, their information, or their interpretive rules that you do not yet have an answer to.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "what is my explanation for why we disagree?" If the explanation is primarily about their character or reasonableness, certainty is running.

After: ask "can I state one thing about their story that I did not know before I started asking questions?" If yes, curiosity has opened something.

## Failure mode this prevents

From certainty, the only tool available is assertion: saying what you believe more emphatically or more often. The chapter: "If the other person is stubborn, we assert harder." But people almost never change without first feeling understood. The more you assert, the more they defend. The argument escalates in volume and repeats in content. Trevor and Karen repeated the same argument about paperwork deadlines until Trevor shifted to genuine curiosity about Karen's experience. Once he understood her story — she was overwhelmed and felt underappreciated — and shared his story about the extra work her lateness created, something changed. No assertion had produced this; only curiosity made it available.

## Worked micro-example

Rory and Aunt Bertha. From certainty, Rory sees Bertha as irrational. From Bertha's side, Rory is pushy and doesn't listen. Neither is wrong about their own story; both are missing the other's story. Bertha's story — the mattress she shared with her husband of forty years, a small constant in a life full of change, her wanting connection not advice — is entirely coherent and makes perfect sense once heard. But it was never available to Rory while Rory was arguing from certainty. Keiko and Tony provide the live demonstration of the shift: Keiko in certainty, then Keiko catching herself and shifting, then Keiko hearing something about Tony's inner life (the game as meditation, his only decompression of the week) that she could not have anticipated from outside his story.
