## When to reach for this

You are drafting and your character feels like a type rather than a person. Or you have a draft where the protagonist exists but the plot keeps stalling, with no particular event seeming necessary. Reach for this whenever you sense that a character has not yet specified far enough for meaningful action to be available to them.

## The move

1. Ask the question: "Which particular person is this, anyway?"
2. Answer with a fact. The fact should rule something out. "She is unhappy" does not rule out enough; "she is unhappy because of the monotony of her life, not because of grief or scorned love" rules out two clichés and points the path narrower.
3. Repeat. Add another fact, then another, each one ruling out more possibilities. How long has she been in this state? Why? What does she do when she encounters relief? What can she imagine for her future? Each fact closes off a generic alternative.
4. After two or three such facts, notice the pull. A specified character generates pressure for particular events. A boy afraid of water makes a body of water feel imminent. A woman who can imagine no other future makes a disturbance to her future feel due.
5. For characters of a recognizable type, cross-paint: set up the type, then add a contradictory trait that makes the person three-dimensional. The kindly examiner who is also a clueless drunk; the warmhearted gentleman who is also a bumbler. The contradiction prevents the reader from filing the character away as "the rich landowner."
6. Continue specifying as the story progresses. A character is not finished when introduced. New events are opportunities to specify further, even at the cost of complicating the reader's view (the noble teacher who is also complicit in her own diminishment).

## Signal it landed

You can write the character's defining facts as a short list, and reading the list, you can predict the kinds of events the story is now ripe for. The list also rules out the obvious clichéd version of this character. The plot, which had been stuck, suggests its own next move.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, write your character in one sentence and ask: "What needs to happen to this person?" Note your answer. If it is "I don't know" or a generic event, the character is underspecified.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer becomes a specific event or class of event that the character's facts make pressure for, and you can name which facts produced that pressure.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this work, your character remains a placeholder. You write "an unhappy woman" and try to put her into a plot, but no particular plot fits, because nothing about her selects for one. You may then try to fix this by adding more dramatic events, but events without specification feel arbitrary; the character is not the kind of person these things would happen to. Or, equally common: you produce a flat type the reader files away on first encounter and stops attending to. The story has nothing out of which to make its meaning, in Saunders's phrase, because before the character was specified, there was nothing in the story that wanted to happen.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders walks through Chekhov's specification of Marya in the opening pages of "In the Cart." She is unhappy. (Type: an unhappy person.) She is unhappy not because of grief or scorned love but because of the monotony of her life. (Cliché ruled out: tragic backstory.) She has been teaching for thirteen years and has done this trip countless times. (Cliché ruled out: she is new to her plight.) She can imagine no other future. (The trap is internal as well as external.) Saunders names the effect: as a particular person gets made, the potential for meaningful action increases. He then demonstrates the cross-painting variant on Hanov: the warm gentleman is set up, then complicated by alcoholism, by bumbling, by the absurdity of giving globes to a school in the middle of nowhere; the result is a person we wonder about rather than have neatly in our pocket.
