## When to reach for this

You are receiving feedback and the size of your reaction does not match the size of the input. Either you are flaring at a small note ("why can't you just accept me?") or you are nodding along now and noticing a stored charge that you expect to surface in a day or a week. Reach for this when the reaction feels too big, too small, or too delayed, and you suspect more than the headline content is at stake.

## The move

1. Pause the content debate. Whether the feedback is true or fair is a separate track and you are not on it yet.
2. Name the learning need out loud (to yourself or in the conversation): "Part of me does want to get better at this and is curious what is here to learn."
3. Name the acceptance need out loud: "Part of me wants to be received as I am, and this feedback is signaling that how I am is not quite okay, which is what is stinging."
4. Hold both as real and simultaneous. Refuse to collapse either one. The acceptance need is not weakness to be argued down; the learning need is not betrayal of your selfhood.
5. Decide what each need is asking for separately. The learning need may want a clarifying question, an experiment, a piece of coaching. The acceptance need may want a reassurance from the relationship, a boundary, a gesture, or simply your own acknowledgment that the sting is legitimate.
6. Then, with both surfaced, return to the content of the feedback.

## Signal it landed

You can state, in a sentence each, what you want to learn and what you want to be accepted for, and the two sentences sit alongside each other without one canceling the other. The reaction-size returns to proportion: either the flare subsides because the acceptance need has been heard, or the suppression releases because the learning need is no longer being smuggled through the back door.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "Is the size of my reaction explained by the content of this feedback?" If no, ask "what else is being touched?"

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if you can answer the second question with a named need ("I want them to know I am already trying," "I want this person specifically to think well of me," "I want the work itself to be seen") rather than a vague "I just felt weird about it."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming both needs, one of them goes underground and runs the conversation from there. If the acceptance need is hidden, you argue the content endlessly because winning the content argument is doing the work the acceptance need is silently asking for. If the learning need is hidden, you defend yourself and miss the coaching the relationship is offering, then notice months later that you are stuck. Either way, the conversation that is happening is not the conversation that is needed, and the unspoken need does not go away; it leaks into withdrawal, resentment, or the next argument.

## Worked micro-example

The book frames the entire intro around this collision. Humans are wired to learn, and learning about ourselves is also where we collide with our longing to be loved, accepted, and respected as we are. The "Tension Between Learning and Being Accepted" section names the bristling out loud: "Why can't you accept me for who I am and how I am? Why are there always more adjustments, more upgrades?" The book's stance is that the tension is not going away and the leverage is in managing it, not resolving it, which is what naming both needs does in the moment.
