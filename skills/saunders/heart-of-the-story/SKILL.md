## When to reach for this

You are looking at a draft, your own or someone else's, that contains stretches you cannot defend or attack precisely. The piece moves but feels long, or moves but feels off-center. You can describe what is happening in each section but cannot say which section is the one the others should be serving. Reach for this whenever "I don't know what this scene is for" comes up and you suspect the problem is not the scene but the lack of a center to test the scene against.

## The move

1. Identify the heart. Ask the story: "What is the heart of you, dear story? Why are you bothering telling me this?" The heart is the central event, scene, performance, or arrival the story exists to deliver. In a contest story, it is the contest. In a wedding ceremony, the exchanging of vows. In a joke, the punchline. Name it as a specific moment in the manuscript, not as a theme.
2. State the heart in one sentence and locate it in the manuscript by page or line range. The rest of the audit depends on the heart being a fixed reference point.
3. Walk through the rest of the story unit by unit (scene, paragraph, descriptive passage, sub-scene). Saunders frames the procedure two ways. The candy factory: every department in the place must be related to making the candy; an office labeled "Center for the Planning of Steve's Wedding" is a sign of inefficiency. Club Story: roam the club as a bouncer, asking each part, "Excuse me, but why do you need to be in here?"
4. For each part, finish the sentence: "This part is here because it ___ the heart." Acceptable verbs: prepares, sets up the criteria for judging, deepens the stakes of, primes a contrast that the heart will resolve, supplies a panel that the heart needs, foreshadows. Unacceptable verbs: "feels nice next to," "happens to be true," "got into the draft and would not come out."
5. If a part stalls on this test, do not yet cut it. Mark it as on-trial and ask one more question: is there a non-obvious way it serves the heart? In Saunders's reading, the eleven static pages of pub description in "The Singers" pass on the second look because they build the panel of judges whose reactions later make the unsinging-able contest readable.
6. Apply the test generously, not mathematically. Saunders is explicit that scrutiny administered too cleanly will produce a story that is too neat. Atmosphere, mood, and texture are valid functions if you can name them as decisions, not defaults.
7. Parts that fail the second look, fix or cut. The fix is to give them a function pointed at the heart. The cut is the same as cutting them.

## Signal it landed

You can name the heart of the story in one sentence pointing at a specific moment on a specific page. Every other part of the story is annotated with what it does for that heart, in concrete terms (preparing this contrast, supplying this judge, raising this stake). At least one part is marked for cutting or rewriting because, on second look, it cannot be tied to the heart.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I cut any random scene from this story, would I know whether the cut hurt the story?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer flips from "I'd be guessing" to "yes — the question is whether the cut weakens what the heart needs, and I can name what it would weaken."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without a named heart, every part of the story is on equal footing and nothing can be tested against anything. Edits become tinkering. You move sentences, sharpen lines, polish description, but you cannot tell whether a whole section is doing structural work or merely existing pleasantly. The draft accumulates parts that pass the local test ("this is a good scene") and fail the global test (no part of it routes energy to the central event). Saunders names the underlying craft fact: a story has a heart in the same way a Mass has communion or a wedding has the vows, and the rest of the ceremony is felt as beautiful and necessary to the extent that it serves that heart. A story that does not know its heart cannot ask its parts to serve.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders identifies the heart of "The Singers" as the singing contest, five pages out of nineteen, beginning on what he marks as the contest page. He then walks through the eleven pre-contest pages part by part and tabulates them: a description of the ravine, of the innkeeper, of the innkeeper's wife, of some birds, of the town, of Booby and Blinker, of the pub, of Yashka, of the Wild Gentleman, of the contractor, of a peasant, plus a few brief active sequences. The tabulation makes the imbalance impossible to overlook. He then runs the bouncer-at-Club-Story test on each item: what is it doing for the contest? The character descriptions earn their keep by establishing a graded panel of judges whose reactions during the contest will tell the reader who is winning. The ravine description earns its keep by cueing the binary structure of the story (a town cleft in two, two singers, Yashka vs. the contractor, technique vs. emotion). The bird descriptions earn their keep by setting up the doleful-vs.-spunky contrast that will read forward onto the contestants. Each pre-contest part, once tested against the contest, turns out to be on-purpose. The procedure is the audit, and the audit is what converts the apparent bloat into apparent virtuosity.
