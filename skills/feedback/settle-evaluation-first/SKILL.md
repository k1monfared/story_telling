## When to reach for this

You are receiving (or trying to receive) coaching or appreciation while an evaluation question is open and unsettled. The open question may be loud (you just got a worse rating than you expected, or a vague comment that might be evaluative) or silent (no evaluation has been offered at all and you are mining everything else for clues). Either way, you can feel that you are not actually absorbing the coaching, even if you are nodding along. Reach for this before you try harder to absorb it.

## The move

1. Pause and name the open evaluation question. "Did I meet expectations or fall short?" "Am I a stopgap or part of the long-term plan?" "Am I still wanted here?" Whatever is taking the airtime in your head while the giver is talking.
2. Treat the evaluation question as prerequisite, not parallel. The chapter's image: when a professor hands back a graded paper, the student turns to the last page first to see the grade; only after that can they read the margin notes. The order is not a flaw of attention, it is structural.
3. If the evaluation is loud and unfavorable (you got the lower rating, or the bad news), do not try to absorb the coaching in the same conversation. Acknowledge the evaluation, sit with it, and schedule the coaching for later — the chapter recommends days, probably longer.
4. If the evaluation is silent (none has been offered and you are guessing), ask for it directly before continuing with coaching or appreciation. The "evaluative floor" needs to be there before the rest can stand on it. Use a script like: "Before we get into how I can improve, I need a quick read on where I stand overall. Am I broadly on track?"
5. Once the evaluation question is answered enough to free attention, return to the coaching or appreciation and absorb it deliberately.

## Signal it landed

You can name the evaluation question that was occupying you, and you have either answered it (asked and gotten an answer, or confirmed it is settled enough) or deferred the coaching until the evaluation is settled. The internal noise (what about the times I bailed you out, what's wrong with me, what does this mean for my compensation) has dropped enough that you can actually hear what the giver is saying about how to improve, or take in their thanks without parsing it for hidden bad news.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "While they are coaching me (or thanking me), what is actually running in my head?" If the answer is an unanswered where-do-I-stand question rather than the content of the feedback, evaluation is not yet settled.

After applying, ask the same. The skill landed if the answer is now the content of the coaching or the appreciation itself, with the evaluation question either answered or visibly parked for a separate conversation.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without sequencing, two failure modes alternate. First, in a loud evaluation: you sit through the coaching nodding while internally relitigating the rating, then leave with neither the rating accepted nor the coaching absorbed, and you misremember the meeting as having been about the rating only. Second, in a silent evaluation: you mine the coaching and the appreciation for hidden evaluative content (why is the boss giving me so much coaching on this — am I in trouble? why was I singled out for thanks in one email but not the next?), so the coaching cannot land as coaching and the appreciation cannot land as appreciation. Both kinds get rerouted to the slot of the unanswered question, and the actual content of both is lost. The receiver concludes feedback is opaque or political when the real issue is that the evaluative floor was never set.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter's "Evaluation Shortfalls" section makes the silent-evaluation case directly: in a workplace where evaluation is absent, the receiver puts an ear to the ground and reads coaching and appreciation as veiled signals about standing. The "Separate Evaluation" section makes the loud-evaluation case: walking into a performance review hoping to learn how to improve, getting a "meets" instead of the "exceeds" you expected, and then being unable to hear any of the coaching that follows because internal voices have taken over. The graded-paper image at line 1067 is the chapter's compact statement of the principle: students cannot read the margin notes until they have seen the grade.
