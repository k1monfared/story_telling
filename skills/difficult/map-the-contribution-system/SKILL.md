## When to reach for this

You are in a situation where something went wrong and you feel certain you know who is to blame. Or you are avoiding raising an issue because you fear you would be blamed for it. Or a conversation has turned into a debate about fault rather than a discussion of what happened and what to do. Or you feel the urge to get the other person to admit they were wrong before you can move forward.

## The move

**Understand why blame fails.** Blame asks three questions simultaneously: Did they cause this? Was their action wrong by some standard? How should they be punished? When you say "it's your fault," you are giving condemning answers to all three. Blame invokes defensiveness and punishment-avoidance, producing arguments rather than understanding. Focusing on blame also keeps you from raising issues: if you need to be blameless to raise something, and you have contributed to the problem (which you almost always have), then you will not raise it.

**Shift the frame: contribution asks different questions.** Contribution asks: how did each of us contribute to bringing about this situation? And: having mapped those contributions, what can each of us do differently? Contribution is backward-looking only to enable forward-looking change. It is not about punishment; it is about understanding the system well enough to change it.

**Map the contribution system.** Before raising the conversation, map out what each party contributed to the problem. Ask three questions:

1. What are they contributing? Name, as specifically as possible, what the other person said or did (or failed to say or do) that contributed to the current situation.

2. What am I contributing? This is the harder question. Four hard-to-spot contributions to look for:
   - **Avoiding until now**: have you allowed the problem to continue by not raising it sooner? Avoidance is a contribution. Complaining to a third party instead of the person involved is a particularly problematic form of avoiding.
   - **Being unapproachable**: does your style (short-tempered, judgmental, hypersensitive, punitive) make it harder for others to raise things with you, creating a system of avoidance?
   - **Intersections**: is the conflict partly the result of a simple difference in background, communication style, or implicit rules? Treating a difference as a character flaw misses the contribution that the intersection itself is making.
   - **Problematic role assumptions**: do you and the other party each have a role in a repetitive dynamic, even if neither explicitly chose it? Changing the dynamic requires changing the role, not just changing the person.

   If you cannot see your contribution, try role reversal: "What would they say I'm contributing?" Answer in the first person as them. Or step back and ask: as a neutral consultant, how would I describe what each person is contributing?

3. Who else is contributing? Are there other players — other team members, family dynamics, organizational structures, past events — whose contributions are part of the system?

**Take responsibility for your contribution early in the conversation.** When you raise the issue with the other person, acknowledge your own contribution up front, before naming theirs. Sydney might say to Miguel: "I apologize for not bringing this up earlier, before it became such a big deal for me. Also, I realize that arranging for us to work together at the beginning of the project may have sent a confusing signal." This signals that you are not there to prosecute; it prevents the other person from deflecting by pointing to your contribution; and it models the stance you want the conversation to take.

**Help them understand their contribution.** Make your observations specific: describe what they did that triggered your reaction, in concrete terms. Explain what you would have them do differently, and link it to how that change would help you change yours. Ask if there are things you are doing that make it hard for them to raise concerns with you.

**Distinguish contribution from blame in ongoing conversations.** If the conversation starts to drift back into blame ("but it was really your fault"), return to the contribution frame: "I think we each contributed to this in important ways. I've talked about what I did. Can we also talk about what you were doing?" If you find yourself with a persistent urge to have the other person admit wrongdoing, ask yourself: what feelings are you failing to express? The impulse to blame often functions as a proxy for unexpressed hurt, anger, or need for acknowledgment. Speaking directly to those feelings (rather than attributing blame) often reduces the need for the other person to admit fault.

**Contribution is not "blaming the victim."** Contribution is about understanding leverage — what did I do that helped cause this, and what could I have done differently? It does not imply you deserved what happened. Nelson Mandela's insight: those who carry almost all the moral blame for a situation can still look for their contribution to understand what they can do to improve it.

## Signal it landed

You can name at least one specific thing you contributed to the problem before naming anything the other person contributed. The conversation has moved from "who is at fault?" to "how did we each get here?" Both parties are treating the problem as a system rather than as something one party did to the other.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "am I raising this conversation to get them to admit they were wrong, or to understand what happened and figure out how to go forward?" If the first, blame is driving; contribution has not been adopted.

After: ask "do we both have a clearer picture of what we each contributed to the situation?" If yes, the mapping has produced something useful.

## Failure mode this prevents

The blame frame produces defensiveness and arguments about fault rather than learning about what went wrong. It also produces avoidance: people don't raise important issues because they feel they need to be blameless to do so. And it can leave the system that produced the problem intact even when the "responsible" person is removed or punished (the Commodity Corp. VP who was fired while the organizational incentives that produced the bad decision were unchanged). Contribution is not a moral position; it is a diagnostic. It produces information that blame cannot.

## Worked micro-example

You and your assistant lost the ExtremeSport pitch. Blame conversation: you tell her it was her fault, she apologizes, you warn her. The underlying system (your abruptness makes her afraid to ask questions; her withdrawal makes you more anxious) is left untouched. Contribution conversation: you ask her to retrace what happened; she reveals that she felt too intimidated to double-check because you seemed in a hostile mood; you acknowledge that you do get like that; you each make a specific request of the other; you end with a system (she asks even when you seem irritated; you try to be less abrupt; both double-check). The second conversation produces durable change; the first produces a promise that is likely to be broken the next time the same pressure arises.
