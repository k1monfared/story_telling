## When to reach for this

A conversation feels difficult and you cannot say exactly why. Or you have just had a difficult conversation and feel that it went badly, but cannot identify where it broke down. Or you are preparing for a difficult conversation and feeling anxious without being able to name what you are anxious about.

Also reach for this as the first move before applying any other skill in this library: knowing which conversation is generating the most heat tells you which set of tools to reach for next.

## The move

Stop and ask: which of the three conversations is running hardest in this situation?

**The "What Happened?" Conversation.** This one is running when the heat comes from disagreement about facts, interpretations, who said or did what, who meant what, or who is to blame. Signals: you and the other person are arguing, each presenting your version of events as the truth; you are defending your interpretation or your actions; the conversation keeps returning to questions of right and wrong, fairness and fault. The three errors that produce heat here: treating your view as the truth rather than as your perspective; assuming you know their intentions; framing the question as blame rather than as contribution.

**The Feelings Conversation.** This one is running when the heat comes from emotions that are not on the table. Signals: strong feelings are underneath the surface but not being named (you feel hurt, underappreciated, angry, betrayed, or afraid); you or the other person is working hard to stay rational and keep feelings out; the factual conversation keeps getting derailed by an emotional subtext neither person is acknowledging. Or the opposite: feelings are erupting in ways that make the factual conversation impossible, but no one has named what the feelings actually are.

**The Identity Conversation.** This one is running when the heat comes from what the conversation seems to be saying about who you are. Signals: you feel your self-image threatened (your sense of being competent, being a good person, or being worthy of love); you feel destabilized or off-balance in a way that seems out of proportion to the factual stakes; you find yourself defending your identity rather than engaging with the other person's point; you feel paralyzed or flooded with anxiety not because the facts are complicated but because the implications for who you are feel unbearable.

**Note: all three are always running.** The three conversations are simultaneous, not sequential. You are not just picking one; you are identifying which one is generating the most heat right now, so you know where to focus. In the Jack/Michael case: the What Happened conversation (who is right about the brochure) is explicit; the Feelings conversation (Jack's anger and hurt at being unappreciated; Michael's anxiety and disappointment) is implicit and unaddressed; the Identity conversation (is Jack incompetent; did Michael make a foolish decision) is running for both of them in the background and is making each of them more rigid in the surface argument.

Once you have named which conversation is running hardest, you can decide which of the subsequent skills to apply: the What Happened conversation tools (Chapters 2-4), the Feelings tools (Chapter 5), or the Identity tools (Chapter 6). If you are preparing for a conversation rather than already in one, name which conversations you expect to be live and prepare for each.

## Signal it landed

You can say, in one sentence, which conversation is running hardest and why. The diagnosis does not resolve the difficulty, but it changes how you approach it: you know what kind of problem you are dealing with.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "when I think about having this conversation, what is the thing I am most afraid of?" If the fear is about who is right or what the facts are, the What Happened conversation is live. If it is about what might get expressed or how the other person might react, the Feelings conversation is live. If it is about what the conversation might reveal about you, the Identity conversation is live.

After: ask "can I name which conversation was generating the most heat?" If yes, the diagnostic has landed.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming which conversation is running, you apply the wrong tools to the wrong problem. You argue about facts when the real issue is feelings that have not been named. You try to resolve feelings when the real issue is a factual misunderstanding. You get flooded by identity anxiety and act as if the conversation is only about whether you are right, missing the impact the conversation is having on the other person's sense of self. The chapter: "In each of these conversations we make predictable errors that distort our thoughts and feelings, and get us into trouble." The first step to addressing those errors is knowing which conversation you are in.

## Worked micro-example

Jack and Michael's brochure conversation. On the surface it is a What Happened conversation: did the brochure need to be redone? But underneath, the Feelings conversation is running: Jack feels mistreated and unappreciated; Michael is anxious about the client relationship. And the Identity conversation is running too: Jack is wondering whether this means he is incompetent; Michael is wondering whether he made a foolish decision to hire his friend. The conversation goes badly in part because both parties treat it as purely a What Happened conversation (about the brochure) while the Feelings and Identity conversations are driving their reactions from underground. Months later, the relationship is still strained — not because the brochure question was never settled, but because the other two conversations were never addressed.
