## When to reach for this

You notice you've been calling a partner three times today not because anything urgent is happening but because the contact itself calms you. Or you can't sit with mild distress for ten minutes without reaching out. Reach for this when you suspect the partner is doing nervous-system work that you could be learning to do yourself.

## The move

1. Notice the impulse to reach. Catch it before it becomes a text. The flag is usually: I'm activated AND the activation is mild AND I haven't tried to settle myself yet.
2. Distinguish auto-regulation from clean reach. Clean reach: I want to share this. Auto-regulation: I need them to fix this. The first is connection; the second is using the partner as a thermostat.
3. Try self-regulation first for low-grade activation. Three breaths. A walk. Sitting with the sensation for five minutes. Often the activation drops and the urgency to reach also drops.
4. After self-regulating, notice if you still want to reach out. If yes, reach out — but from a more regulated state, with a clearer ask. The conversation will be different.
5. For higher-grade activation, do reach out — that's what attachment is for. The work is to distinguish "I genuinely need contact" from "I'm using contact to avoid feeling something."
6. Tell your partners about the pattern. They probably already feel it. Naming it together lets them ask "what kind of reach is this?" without it being an accusation.

## Signal it landed

You can sit with low-grade activation for a few minutes without immediate contact. When you do reach out, you have a clearer sense of what for. Your partner experiences your reaching as connection rather than as nervous-system maintenance.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this distinction, your partner becomes responsible for managing your nervous system. They can't ever be unavailable without it being a small crisis; their bandwidth becomes shaped around your regulation. Over time, this depletes them and prevents you from developing the internal capacity that would actually solve the problem.

## Reflection prompts

- In what ways do you autoregulate, or try to use others to regulate, so that you don't have to self-regulate?
- What does self-regulation look like for you? What would you like more of?
- Are there specific situations where the auto-regulation pattern is strongest? What's common about them?
- What is your relationship with your inner nurturer like? How could that part become more central?
