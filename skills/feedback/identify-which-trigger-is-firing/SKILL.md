## When to reach for this

You are mid-feedback or in the immediate aftermath, and you can feel the reaction (heart pounding, stomach clenched, mind composing rebuttals, or a flat refusal to engage). You are about to do one of two things: push the reaction aside as a distraction so you can keep functioning, or let the reaction drive your next move without naming what set it off. Reach for this before either move.

## The move

1. Pause before responding or suppressing. Treat the reaction as a smoke alarm: it is telling you something is on fire somewhere, and the right first step is to locate the fire, not silence the alarm.
2. Ask the categorizing question: which of the three is firing right now?
    - **Truth trigger**: the reaction is to the substance of the feedback. The internal sentence is "this is wrong, unfair, or unhelpful." The feeling is indignation or exasperation.
    - **Relationship trigger**: the reaction is to the giver, their credibility or motives, or to how you feel treated by them. The internal sentence is "I cannot hear this from you." The feeling is hurt, suspicion, or anger about the person, not the message.
    - **Identity trigger**: the reaction is to what the feedback implies about who you are. The internal sentence is "if this is true, what does that make me?" The feeling is destabilization, shame, threat, or being off balance.
3. Name a mix where one exists. Triggers stack: feedback can hit all three at once (the chapter's example, Miriam at the bar mitzvah, gets truth-triggered AND relationship-triggered by the same comment). Naming the mix prevents you from solving for one and being run by the others.
4. Pick the matching response track. Each trigger has a different remedy, developed in its own chapters of the book. Truth triggers want sorting and understanding. Relationship triggers want disentangling what from who. Identity triggers want stabilizing your story before you can engage. Mismatched remedies (arguing the content when the wound is identity, defending yourself when the issue is the relationship system) deepen the trouble.
5. Only then engage the conversation, having decided which problem you are actually solving.

## Signal it landed

You can name the trigger (or triggers) firing in a single sentence per type, in your own words, before you take your next action in the conversation. The reaction is no longer just felt; it is read. Your next move is selected for the trigger that is actually firing, not for the loudest reaction or the easiest deflection.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask yourself: "If I had to write down right now what is upsetting me about this feedback, would I name the content, the giver, or what it implies about me?" If you cannot answer, or if you would write "everything" or "I don't know, I just feel awful," you have not yet read the alarm.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if you can answer with one or more of the three categories named explicitly, with a sentence locating each one in this specific feedback.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the diagnosis, two failure modes alternate. In the first, you treat the reaction as an obstacle to push past, so you suppress it, perform composure, and lose the information the alarm was carrying. The fire keeps burning; you act calm; the trigger leaks out hours or weeks later as withdrawal, resentment, or a reaction to the next small thing. In the second, you act on the reaction without reading it, applying the wrong remedy: arguing the content of feedback whose real injury was relational, attacking the giver when what you actually need is to steady your identity, or stabilizing your identity when the feedback was just plain wrong on the merits. The mismatch deepens the original trouble and confirms the giver's view that you cannot receive feedback. The receiver loses both the information the trigger was carrying and the encounter the trigger was inside.

## Worked micro-example

The book opens chapter 1 with the metaphor directly: trying to ignore a triggered reaction without first identifying its cause is like dealing with a fire by disconnecting the smoke alarm. Then it introduces Miriam, whose husband Sam tells her she was "unfriendly and aloof" at his nephew's bar mitzvah. Miriam's reaction reads as one big upset, but the chapter shows it is at least two: a truth trigger (the feedback is wrong, was she supposed to tap dance on the table) and a relationship trigger (she rearranged her mother's dialysis to be there and Sam can't even remember his nephew's name). When she switchtracks to the second without naming the first, both topics get tangled. The chapter's whole framework rests on the move of separating the triggers before responding.

## Source

Chapter 1, "Three Triggers That Block Feedback," lines 493-527. Stone and Heen frame triggers as both obstacles and information ("a kind of map that can help us locate the source of the trouble"), and lay out the three categories as the book's organizing spine: truth triggers (challenge to see), relationship triggers (challenge of the we), identity triggers (challenge of being me). The skill installs the chapter's central diagnostic move: before applying any remedy, name which alarm is sounding.
