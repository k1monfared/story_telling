## When to reach for this

You are drafting a story whose engine is one character moving through an arc that ends in transformation. The character is in your head as a type (a miser, a hypocrite, a self-absorbed master, a worry-prone middle-class striver). You want them to land as a particular person whose actions feel inevitable rather than asserted, whose moral architecture the reader feels rather than is told, and whose climactic change reads as redirection of the energy they had all along rather than as a substitution of a new being for the old. Reach for this when the character must carry the weight of the story across all four scopes — situation, sentence, exchange, climax — and you need a workflow that builds each scope on the prior.

## The move

1. **Specify the character until plot becomes available.** Apply `increasing-specification`. Ask "which particular person is this, anyway?" and answer with facts that rule out clichés. "Vasili is a master" rules out nothing; "Vasili is a master who routinely cheats his servant Nikita and is honestly convinced that he is Nikita's benefactor" rules out the simpler hypocrite (the bwa-ha-ha villain) and the simpler saint (the well-meaning patron). Continue specifying until the facts produce pressure for particular events: a specified character generates plot. The output of this step is a list of defining facts on which the rest of the story will rely. Without this step, the prose has nothing to render — every later scope is rendering the character, and the rendering needs a particular person to be of.

2. **Render the world and the character in the matrix-of-facts register.** Apply `matrix-of-facts`. The character's actions, surroundings, and interior thoughts are reported in the same factual idiom: short, plain, declarative, free of qualifier and metaphor. "He flicked the apron at the boiling samovar." "He was honestly convinced that he was Nikita's benefactor." A fact-syntax sentence about an interior state ("honestly convinced that") gets accepted in the same spirit as a fact-syntax sentence about an exterior fact ("the bridle is leather and tasseled"). The output of this step is a prose register in which any sentence about the character — exterior or interior — produces involuntary buy-in rather than skeptical reading. Without this step, the specified character of step 1 cannot be rendered; the writer reaches for editorialized or lyrical assertion, the reader holds the prose at arm's length, and the specification's work is squandered.

3. **Rotate vantages in morally loaded exchanges.** Apply `mind-to-mind-vantage-shifts`. For each exchange whose meaning depends on the gap between what people say and what they know, draft three short renderings per character: their public stance (what they say), their private stance (what they think, in the same factual register from step 2), and how the omniscient narrator describes their position from outside. Compose the scene by rotating among these vantages. The reader leaves the exchange knowing the moral architecture without anyone having labeled it. The output of this step is a scene whose moral weight is felt through structural rotation rather than through authorial assertion. This step depends on step 2 because the rotation only produces involuntary belief if the interior thoughts arrive in the same factual idiom as the exteriors; without matrix-of-facts, the move into a character's mind reads as a confession or a lyrical interiority, and the rotation breaks.

4. **Render the climactic transformation as redirected energy in the character's familiar idiom.** Apply `transformation-by-redirected-energy`. Refuse the soliloquy ("He realized that..."). Place the character in an action they were already capable of taking, with the action's beneficiary or object reset. Borrow the character's familiar idiom from the prior pages — the same resolution Vasili used when striking hands on a profitable purchase. Render only the action in concrete factual sentences (matrix-of-facts again). Allow the character to react to their own action with surprise, but render the surprise after the action. Preserve the character's old register post-transformation. The output of this step is a climax in which the change is visible through action, not announcement, and the post-transformation character is still recognizably themselves. This step depends on step 3 because the rotation produces the moral architecture the climax must carry forward; on step 2 because the climactic action is rendered in the same factual idiom; and on step 1 because "the character's familiar idiom" requires a character who has been specific enough across the prior pages to have one.

## Signal it landed

You can produce four artifacts that share a single character. (a) A list of defining facts that rule out the obvious clichéd version of this character, with the next event the story is now ripe for predictable from the list. (b) A page of prose in which any subjective claim sits in a matrix of factual sentences whose syntax is indistinguishable; a stranger reading the page cold cannot easily tell whether a sentence reports an external fact or an internal state. (c) At least one morally loaded exchange that the reader can summarize as a gap (what each character said vs. what each privately knew) without having been told. (d) A climactic action rendered in concrete factual sentences, in the character's familiar idiom from prior pages, with the character's surprise at their own action arriving after the action and the post-transformation register still sounding like them. A reader of (d) cannot point to a sentence in which the character announces or realizes the change.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask of your character: "Is this person specific enough that the next event has to be a particular kind of event, do my sentences make the reader believe in them without being asked, can the reader feel the moral architecture of any exchange they are in, and does my climax change them by redirecting the energy they already had?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if you can answer yes to all four parts and point at the specific work each step did: the facts that ruled out clichés, the factual register that produced involuntary buy-in, the rotation that surfaced the moral architecture, the redirected action that made the change visible.

## Failure mode this prevents

The character who is rendered well at one or two scopes but not all four arrives broken. Each missing step produces a specific failure.

**Skipping step 1 (no specification):** the character is a placeholder; no particular plot fits; events feel arbitrary because the character is not the kind of person these things would happen to. Saunders names the underlying fact: before specification, "there was nothing in the story that wanted to happen."

**Skipping step 2 (no matrix-of-facts):** the specified character is rendered through editorialized or lyrical assertion; the reader holds the prose at arm's length; the specification's work is squandered because no sentence produces involuntary buy-in.

**Skipping step 3 (no vantage rotation):** morally loaded exchanges depend on the writer asserting (through narrative position) who is the hypocrite and who is the truth-teller; the moral architecture must be told rather than felt; the reader has nothing to think with.

**Skipping step 4 (no redirected-energy climax):** the climax becomes a soliloquy of realization that the prior steps' careful work cannot save. The character realizes a doctrine, articulates it in interior monologue, converts to its program; the prose reads as a sermon and the character as the writer's mouthpiece. Or, the inverse failure: the character is so radically remade that they no longer resemble the person we have been reading; the reader cannot transfer their belief.

**Doing the steps out of order:** step 4 first writes a transformation scene with no specified character to redirect; step 3 before step 2 produces a rotation in the wrong register; step 2 before step 1 polishes the syntax of a placeholder.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders walks the entire chain on Vasili in "Master and Man." Step 1 (specification): Vasili is a master who cheats Nikita and is honestly convinced he is Nikita's benefactor. Step 2 (matrix-of-facts): the prose is "almost entirely facts"; even Vasili's interior thoughts are "rendered as objectively and precisely as Nikita's crossing of the yard." Step 3 (vantage rotation): the wage exchange cycles through five vantages — omniscient narrator (Vasili routinely cheats Nikita), Vasili's speech ("we deal straightforwardly"), Vasili's thought (honestly convinced he is Nikita's benefactor), Nikita's speech (his own spin), Nikita's thought (quite aware he is being cheated). Step 4 (redirected-energy transformation): after Nikita begs forgiveness and announces he is dying, Vasili stands silent for half a minute, then "just acts (or, actually, goes back into action)" with "the same resolution he used to strike hands when making a good purchase." Six factual actions: rake the snow, undo the girdle, open the coat, push Nikita down, lie on top of him, adjust the skirt to tent the body. Then, only then, the surprise: tears come, the jaw quivers, a strange and solemn tenderness arrives. Post-transformation Vasili is "still self-celebratory, still living to feel good about himself" — only now what he is feeling good about is having saved a man's life. Same character, four scopes, one chain.
