## When to reach for this

You received a negative evaluation: a rejection, a poor review, a thumbs-down, a loss. The first score is in, and it is unflattering. Your initial impulse is to either find someone to blame or to collapse entirely. There is nothing you can do to change the first score. What you do next is entirely within your control.

## The move

**Imagine an invisible second evaluation.** After every first score, there is a second score that rates how you handle the first. The first score is for the situation itself; the second score is for what you do with it. You can receive an F for the situation and an A+ for the handling. Both scores are real; in the long run the second is often more important.

**Give yourself the second score.** This is not a consolation prize for failure. It is a genuine separate assessment. Melinda and Mel both received a thumbs-down on their YouTube video. Mel's second score: angry, blamed the audience for being too stupid to understand, withdrew. Melinda's second score: studied the craft, took a class, iterated, eventually reworked the video to a mostly positive reception. Both received the same first score; only Melinda received a second score worth keeping.

**Make the second score part of your identity.** The chapter's prescription: do not just occasionally aim for a good second score. Make it a component of your self-story. "I don't always succeed, but I take an honest shot at figuring out what there is to learn from the failure. I'm actually pretty good at that." Set up a Second Score Scorecard in your mind. This identity investment means the initial evaluation is no longer the end of the story; it is the start of the second story about what you make of the experience.

**Handling it well is not performing wellness.** The chapter is explicit: handling something well does not mean denying pain or emerging unscathed. Heather, whose longtime girlfriend left her, handled it well. Her handling: she got up every morning, went to work, treated everyone respectfully, and when she could not manage on her own, asked for help. That is handling it well. It is not claiming the situation is fine. It is facing whatever you are dealing with head-on. The chapter: "Even as Heather's identity as someone who is worthy of love took a hard hit, she found growth: 'I learned that I could deal with a tough loss with grace and resilience.'"

## Signal it landed

You can name what you are going to do in response to the first score that is within your control. You are not asking "how do I make the first score different" (unanswerable) but "what is the best next thing to do given that the first score is what it is" (answerable). The second score is available to you regardless of what the first score was.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "when I think about this negative evaluation, am I primarily thinking about undoing it, or about what to do with it?" If undoing, the second score has not been identified yet.

After: ask "what is the one concrete thing I am going to do in response to this evaluation?" If you can name it and do it, the second score has been claimed.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without the second score concept, a negative first score has nowhere to go. Mel's response (blame the audience) protects his first score self-image at the cost of any second score. His identity is entirely invested in whether the work was good; since the audience called it not good, there is nothing to do except dispute the audience. Melinda's identity includes how she handles setbacks, which is partly independent of whether any given work is received well. That identity investment gives her something to do after the rejection. It also produces better work: she learned the craft and eventually posted a better video. The first score was a data point; the second score is a choice.

## Worked micro-example

Mel and Melinda post their first YouTube video. It gets savaged. Mel blames the world; Melinda wonders what they can learn. A few weeks later Melinda sees flaws in the execution that she had not noticed before. She studies, takes classes, makes more videos, and reworks the original to a positive reception. The chapter's summary: "Both Mel and Melinda got a thumbs-down first score; only Melinda got a thumbs-up second score. In this example, as is so often the case, a good second score is what really matters."
