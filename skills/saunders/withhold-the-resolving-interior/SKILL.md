## When to reach for this

You are at the climactic moment of a draft. The protagonist is about to die, leave, decide, or otherwise resolve the central question the prior pages have raised. The obvious next sentence enters their interior and tells the reader what they realize, what they understand, what they feel. You can feel two things at once: that you have a position on the question (you want the protagonist's realization to land in a particular direction), and that the prior pages have voted on both sides of the question with enough force that one sentence of explicit interiority will overrule the structure you have built. Reach for this whenever your hand is moving toward "He realized that..." or "She suddenly understood, finally, that..." at the climactic moment.

## The move

1. Locate the climactic moment. It is the place where the question the story has been raising would, if entered, be answered. In Saunders's instance: the death-scene paragraph in "Alyosha the Pot," at the line that would specify what Alyosha's final amazement is amazement at.
2. Notice your own didactic instinct. The instinct is the thing that wants to specify the meaning at this moment. It often arrives as a sentence that begins with a verb of cognition ("He saw that...", "He realized...", "He knew, finally, that...") and ends with a position you could state outside the story. Saunders's two attempted alternatives are exactly this shape: one ends in "feeling himself drawn to that eternal reward he had earned, and to which we should all aspire"; the other in "he had lived incorrectly, been too passive. He should have stood up for himself and for Ustinya."
3. Refuse to write that sentence. The refusal is not a substitution; it is an exit. Get out of the character's head at this moment. Render only externals: a request for water, a stretching of the legs, a held expression, a sound, a small action. Saunders names the procedural form: "Whatever happens inside him in those last seconds, we have to deduce by observation."
4. Hold the externals to the same factual idiom the story has been using. "He didn't talk much. He just kept asking for water and looked like he was amazed at something. Then something seemed to startle him and he stretched his legs and died." No interior, no judgment, no specification of the amazement. The amazement is named as observable; what it is amazement at is left to the reader.
5. Trust the prior pages. The omission only works if the prior pages have built both readings of the question with enough force that the un-rendered interior is felt as un-rendered, not as forgotten. If the prior pages vote one way and the climax declines to specify, the reader will read the omission as the writer's reticence about a settled question, not as the question's irreducibility. The prior pages must have set up the Rubin's-vase configuration before this skill can preserve it.
6. Frame the disposition that produces the decision: you are deferring, not avoiding. Saunders's framing: "an interim decision to not, at that moment, decide." The writer is not refusing to take a position on the question forever; they are refusing to take a position at the moment that would resolve the story's structure into a verdict. The non-decision is the structural decision.
7. Test the result with a strikethrough alternative. Compose the version that fills in the omitted interior — write the cognition-verb sentence you refused — and strike it through. The strikethrough makes the omission visible. What remains is what the story keeps; what is struck through is what the omission preserves the reader from.

## Signal it landed

A reader cannot point to a sentence in the climactic paragraph that tells them what the protagonist realized, understood, or finally saw. The protagonist's interior at the resolution moment is rendered only by externals (asked for water, stretched legs, expression of amazement) in the same factual idiom the story has been using. You can compose the alternative version that fills in the missing interior, strike it through, and confirm that the surviving prose is what the actual story has on the page. Asked what the story thinks about its central question, a reader cannot quote a deciding sentence; they have to reproduce the un-rendered moment and name the question as the prior pages raised it.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, point at your climactic paragraph and ask: "If I cut every sentence that names what the protagonist realizes, understands, or feels at this moment, what is left, and is what is left enough?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the cut surfaces a paragraph that is structurally complete (the externals carry the moment) and the un-rendered interior is felt as un-rendered rather than as missing — that is, the reader knows the moment is interior-shaped and the writer has chosen not to enter.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this discipline, the climactic moment becomes the place where the writer states the story's verdict in the protagonist's head. The reader, who has been holding both readings of the central question across the prior pages, suddenly receives one reading as the protagonist's final understanding, and the structure collapses. The story stops being a structure to help the reader think and becomes a structure that delivers the writer's conclusion. Saunders's diagnosis works in two directions. Toward didacticism: "the story, in declining to answer (in obscuring the place where it might have answered), feels like it's not avoiding the question but irradiating it with increased intensity" — which means the converse, that a story that does answer at the place it might have declined collapses the irradiation into a position. Toward dishonesty: "the most artful and truthful thing is sometimes simply that which allows us to avoid being false: the swerving away, the deletion, the declining to decide, the falling silent, the waiting to see, the knowing when to quit" — which means a writer who specifies the un-known interior is writing past their own knowing, falsifying the moment to satisfy their own didactic instinct. The cure is not to write a more nuanced interior; it is to step out of the character's head at the place that would resolve the question, and let the omission carry the question forward.

A second failure mode the procedure prevents: the false omission. A writer who has noticed the didacticism problem may render an interior that is vague, lyrical, or aphoristic, and call that an omission. The vague interior still votes; the reader registers the haze as one side of the question. The procedural test is whether the climactic paragraph contains any sentence that names a cognition, realization, understanding, or felt position. If it does, the omission has not been performed; the writer has rendered the verdict in soft language.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders walks Alyosha's death scene. The prior pages have built both readings with equal force: Alyosha as holy fool whose cheerful obedience is rewarded, Alyosha as unconsciously crushed victim whose passivity has cost him love and now life. The story arrives at the climactic paragraph. The reader is given one last glance into Alyosha's mind ("In his heart was the thought that if it's good down here when you do what they tell you and don't hurt anybody, then it'll be good up there too") and then one external view of the dying ("He didn't talk much. He just kept asking for water and looked like he was amazed at something. Then something seemed to startle him and he stretched his legs and died"). What the amazement is amazement at, the story does not say. Saunders composes the two didactic alternatives — one in which Alyosha sees that God loved him and approved of his obedient cheerfulness and feels himself drawn to eternal reward; one in which Alyosha sees that he lived incorrectly, was too passive, should have stood up for himself and Ustinya — and strikes them through. The struck-through text is the interior monologue Tolstoy refused to write. What remains is what the actual story keeps. Saunders names the reason for the swerve: Tolstoy did not know, did not yet know, or did not like the answer he was about to come up with, and a lifetime of artistic practice produced the deferral as an "interim decision to not, at that moment, decide." The Rubin's-vase outcome is what the deferral protects: both readings keep moving forward and receding, and the central question is irradiated rather than answered.

## Related skills

- Extends `persistent-self-contradiction`. The macro-structural method builds two readings of the central question across the prior pages with equal force; this skill is the single-moment technical decision at the resolution point that preserves the un-rankability the macro-method has set up. They commonly fire together: persistent-self-contradiction across the body, withhold-the-resolving-interior at the climax. The Rubin's-vase outcome the macro-method aims at is what this skill protects.
- Contrasts `transformation-by-redirected-energy`. Both refuse the soliloquy of realization, but under opposite conditions. Use withhold-the-resolving-interior when the prior pages have built two readings with equal force and entering the head would collapse them into a verdict; the procedure renders only externals and leaves the resolution un-answered. Use transformation-by-redirected-energy when the prior pages have built toward a real change and the character is the kind who would take the hint; the procedure renders the changed action in the character's familiar idiom and lets the character react to their own action with surprise. The first ends in irradiated question; the second ends in changed action.
- See also `compress-to-force-resolution` (the structural device that creates the time-pressure under which the omission or the redirected action discharges).
