## When to reach for this

You received feedback that you believe is wrong, unfair, incomplete, or based on misattribution (they're projecting their own flaw onto you). You find yourself:
- Rehearsing arguments you plan to make to change the person's mind
- Feeling that their view of you now determines your identity
- Spending significant emotional energy on a verdict you had no part in creating
- Unable to move past the feedback because you cannot accept that the person continues to hold the view

## The move

**Accept what you cannot control.** Others' views of you are one thing you cannot change, no matter how wrong and unfair they may be. You can discuss, offer counterexamples, provide supporting evidence. But you cannot make someone think something different about you. The chapter: "Maybe she will and maybe she won't." The energy spent trying to force a change in how someone sees you is drawn from a finite supply. Spending it on an uncontrollable variable leaves less for what you can control: your own assessment of the feedback, your decisions about what to change, and your actual behavior.

**Note: most people are not actually thinking about you.** The chapter's calibration: "Most people are simply too obsessed with themselves to be obsessed with you." Your ex-spouse called you a pathetic excuse for a human being and may still hold that view. But she is not sitting at home dwelling on it. She is watching television. The amount of emotional space the other person's view of you takes up in their mind is usually far smaller than it takes up in yours.

**Receive the view, do not wholesale adopt it.** Others' views of you may be incomplete, outdated, unfair, or simply wrong. They may tell you something accurate about the person holding the view rather than about you. The chapter: "most annoying, they may be claiming something about you that is actually true only about them." At the same time, their view is not entirely dismissible. It may contain something worth examining. The chapter's formulation: "Don't dismiss others' views of you, but don't accept them wholesale either. Their views are input, not imprint."

**Have compassion for them.** This is not a technique for suppressing justified anger. It is a practical mechanism for loosening the grip of an unfair verdict. When someone levels an attack or withholds approval in a way that has a long history, remind yourself what their own history must look like: the wound they are operating from, the patterns they learned before you appeared. This does not excuse behavior. It de-personalizes it just enough that you stop treating their view as an authoritative judgment.

## Signal it landed

You have stopped rehearsing the argument you were going to make to change their mind. You can hold the view they expressed (whether fair or unfair) as one data point rather than as a verdict. You can examine what in their view is worth taking seriously without taking it wholesale. The statement "their view is input, not imprint" feels accurate rather than like a coping consolation.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "how much time in the last twenty-four hours have I spent thinking about how this person sees me, versus thinking about what I want to do with the feedback's content?" If the ratio is heavily weighted toward managing their view of you, this skill applies.

After: ask "am I still trying to make them see me differently, or have I moved to asking what I want to do with what they said?" If the latter, the skill has shifted your focus to the controllable.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, the receiver becomes obsessed with a verdict that is, in part, outside their authorship and entirely outside their control. The obsession consumes emotional energy without producing learning or change. The chapter's framing: you can offer notarized statements from your therapist or your dad or the pope, and you still cannot make her think something different. Every attempt to force the change keeps you anchored to their view as if it were the definitive truth about you. The "input not imprint" framing is what restores the distinction between a piece of evidence (which you can weigh, contextualize, and decide about) and a tattoo (which is permanent and defines you).

## Worked micro-example

The chapter uses the football case: you watch football for the chesslike strategy; your coworker insists it's your adolescent compensation for insecure masculinity. You believe you know your own reasons better than she does. You can offer evidence, but you cannot make her think something different. The chapter's prescription: let it go. You know why you watch football. Her belief about your reasons is her belief. The relevant question is whether there is anything in her view worth examining (is there anything in how you discuss football that a reasonable observer might read as compensatory?). If not, the view is input that has been examined and set aside. That is not the same as dismissing it unseen.
