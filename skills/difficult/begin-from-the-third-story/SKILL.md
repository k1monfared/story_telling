## When to reach for this

You are about to initiate a difficult conversation. Every time you rehearse how to start, your opening sounds like an accusation, a verdict, or a position — even when you are trying to be tactful. Or you have had this conversation before and it immediately descended into argument. Or the other person is likely to become defensive the moment you raise the topic.

## The move

**Step 1: Formulate the Third Story.**

Every difficult situation has at least three stories: yours, theirs, and the Third Story — the one a neutral observer would tell. The Third Story does not judge either perspective as right or wrong. It describes the gap between your stories as a difference, not a verdict. The test: can both parties sign on to this description?

From inside your story: "Do I have to do everything around here? You can't let dishes sit this long — it's a health risk."
From the Third Story: "You and I seem to have different preferences about when the dishes get done."

From inside your story: "I was very upset by what you said in front of our supervisor."
From the Third Story: "I wanted to talk about what happened in the meeting this morning. I was upset by something you said, and I wanted to explain what was bothering me and hear your perspective."

The Third Story removes the judgment from the description. It doesn't eliminate your views — those come later. It provides a starting point that both people can accept as accurate without either having to concede that they were wrong.

You don't have to know what their story is to include it. All you need is: "My sense is that we see this situation differently. I'd like to share how I'm seeing it and learn more about how you're seeing it." That is enough to open a joint exploration rather than a debate.

**Step 2: Extend an invitation.**

After the Third Story description, invite the other person into a joint exploration. This means: (a) describing your purposes (learning their perspective, sharing yours, figuring out what to do going forward), (b) making the invitation genuine rather than imposing, and (c) offering the other person an appealing role.

The role you offer matters. If accepting your invitation requires the other person to acknowledge that they are naive, callous, or at fault, they are far less likely to accept. "Can you help me understand...?" offers the role of advisor. "Let's work on how we might..." invites partnership. "I wonder whether it's possible to..." positions them as potential hero. Sometimes sharing your own internal struggle is the most honest option: "The story I'm telling in my head is that you're being inconsiderate. I know that's probably unfair to you, and I need you to help me put things in perspective."

**If they start the conversation.** You can still move to the Third Story. Treat whatever they say as their half of the Third Story, acknowledge it, and add your half: "It sounds like you're pretty unhappy with how I handle the dishes. I have trouble with how you deal with them too. It seems like we each have different preferences around that — that seems worth talking about."

**For specific situations:**
- **Delivering bad news**: put the bad news first, then invite discussion of both sides' reactions and thinking. Don't lead the other person toward saying it; don't bury it under two hours of preliminary discussion.
- **Making requests**: frame as exploration rather than demand. Replace "I deserve a raise" with "I'd like to explore whether a raise for me might make sense. Here's my reasoning. How do you see it?" This better reflects reality (your boss has information you don't) and reduces the other person's defensiveness.
- **Revisiting conversations that have gone badly**: before trying the same topic again, talk about how to talk about it. Describe the meta-problem from the Third Story: "When I've raised this topic in the past, it hasn't gone well. I don't think either of us was happy with how those conversations went. I wonder if we could talk about how we each experience that conversation and whether there's a better way to address these issues."

## Signal it landed

The other person does not immediately become defensive. They engage with the description of the problem or ask a clarifying question rather than rejecting the framing. The conversation has started as an exploration rather than an argument.

## Probe before & after

Before: ask "would the other person agree with how I just described the opening problem?" If they would argue with your description, you are starting from inside your story.

After: ask "did both parties sign on to the same description of what the problem is?" If yes, the Third Story opening worked.

## Failure mode this prevents

Beginning inside your story triggers the other person's defenses from the first sentence. Even a tactful version of your story — "Jill, we need to talk about your problem with getting the dishes done" — is still your story and still positions Jill as the problem. Her predictable reaction is to defend herself or attack you. The conversation enters the brick wall before it has begun. The Third Story approach short-circuits this: the other person does not feel accused because the description acknowledges that both perspectives are present and that both are part of what needs to be understood.

## Worked micro-example

Ruth and Brian's conversation about Brian's commitment to spending time with their daughter Alexis. Previous conversations have devolved into fights. Ruth opens from the Third Story: "It seems to me that we're having a hard time being clear with each other about how likely it is that you'll make it for your time with Lexi." Brian initially misreads this as an accusation (he starts apologizing for the specific recent incident) but Ruth persists, clarifying that she means the larger pattern: "You and I are misunderstanding each other. I'd like to sort this out because it's hard on Alexis when we get our signals crossed. Can we spend some time figuring this thing out?" Brian accepts: "Sure. I don't want to upset Lexi." Ruth's persistence pays off — she neither backs off nor escalates; she simply keeps clarifying the purpose until he understands what kind of conversation she is proposing.
