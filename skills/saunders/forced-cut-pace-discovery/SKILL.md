## When to reach for this

You are revising prose that reads acceptably but slack. You cannot point at sentences that are wrong, only at a piece that feels long. The category audits (does this earn its place, would the reader supply this, does each element have a function) all return "fine" because you have not internalized the pace those audits should be cutting toward. You also notice that you cannot describe what offends you in prose or what delights you when you protect a phrase. Reach for this when the question you need answered is not "what should I cut here" but "what is my editing sensibility, and what is my natural pace."

The skill has two modes that fire on the same target — surface the writer's editing basis and innate sense of voice — through different operations on someone else's prose. The cut-to-quota mode (the main procedure below) operates by forced compression and surfaces what offends the writer. The rank-among-versions mode (Saunders's Appendix C translation exercise, summarized at the end of the procedure) operates by forced choice among multiple finished alternatives of the same content and surfaces what the writer prefers in arrangement, register, sound, and image-order when nothing is removable. Run the cut mode when the prose you are studying is one piece you can compress; run the rank mode when you have multiple existing translations or versions of the same source content. The two are complementary and frequently fire on adjacent sessions in a writer's voice-finding practice.

## The move

1. Pick a passage you did not write that you can mark up freely. Saunders supplies one in the appendix; any roughly 600-word passage will do as long as it is not your own. Cutting someone else's prose is easier than cutting your own; the exercise's transfer step depends on starting where the resistance is lower.
2. Set a timer for five minutes. In that window, cut exactly twenty words. The quota is the point. You are not cutting because something feels wrong; you are cutting because the constraint says so, and you are watching where your hand goes under the constraint.
3. After each round of twenty, answer three questions in writing. What did I cut. Why did I cut it. Is the piece better or worse than before this round. The second answer is your editing sensibility surfacing: name the offense each cut removed (a stated reaction, a default detail, a clause that restated the obvious, a flourish that drew attention to itself, a word repeated from earlier in the paragraph). The third answer is your taste calibrating: notice the rounds where the piece improves and the round where it begins to lose something.
4. Repeat. Saunders takes the exercise from 600 words to 300 — half — round by round. Do the same. The point is not to land at a particular length but to traverse the range of strictness, noticing what becomes available to cut in each round and at what point the cutting starts costing the piece.
5. Notice the threshold. Somewhere in the descent you will hit a round where you cannot tell what to cut next, because the answer depends on where the piece is going. Saunders names this directly: at that point the question of which detail is wheat and which is chaff requires knowing what later pages will need. Mark the round where you hit that threshold and read back: nearly everything you cut up to that point you cut for reasons you can name without knowing the rest of the piece. Those reasons are your editing basis surfacing.
6. Tally the categories. Write down the kinds of things you cut (reaction-naming, default detail, restated context, throat-clearing, sentence-tail filler, repeated word, flourish, etc.) and the kinds of things you protected against cutting (a particular image, a rhythm, a turn that surprised you). Saunders frames the question explicitly: what, in prose, offends you, and what delights you and inspires you to protect it. The two lists are your editing basis named.
7. Listen for sound, not just sense, in the late rounds. Cuts often leave a partial sentence whose tightened state suggests how it wants to finish. Read the tightened version aloud. Some words come to mind that complete the sentence on rhythm rather than on logic, and those are usually better than what you started with. Saunders demonstrates with a sentence cut down to its first three words and then completed by what the rhythm of those three words wanted next; the completion introduces a fact the original draft did not have. This is the second product of the exercise: cuts make room not only for less, but for new material that arrives because the surviving rhythm asks for it.
8. Now run the same procedure on your own work. Pick a page from a story you currently have in progress (Saunders suggests page 4). Set the timer. Cut at the same quota. Saunders notes the difficulty differential — easier on someone else, harder on yourself — and the practical case for the exercise: students report that the muscle memory built on the practice piece carries over.

### Rank-among-versions mode (Appendix C translation exercise)

When you have multiple existing translations or versions of the same source content (as is the case with any well-translated foreign-language sentence), run this sister exercise instead of, or in addition to, the cut-to-quota procedure.

a. Assemble three to five existing versions of the same content. Saunders supplies five English translations of one sentence from Babel's "In the Basement" — a description of white wicker chairs in a foliated walk. The versions vary on word choice (foliage vs. verdure vs. leaves), syntax (front-loaded vs. back-loaded), register, image-order (chairs first or paths first), and sound.

b. Rank the versions best-to-worst. Do not look up any criterion. The whole point is that no basis is supplied; you supply it, by way of your taste.

c. After the ranking, write down the answer to one question: "On what basis did I just now make that ranking?" Saunders frames the introspection: since no basis was supplied, the basis you used reveals an innate preference — a preference you will be relying on in every sentence of your career.

d. Transfer to your own work. Saunders supplies the second half: write your own version of the stipulated content (in his case, the elements are chairs, light, paths, foliage). Afterward ask three more named questions: "What was I honoring? On what was I relying? How did I decide?" The questions externalize the same per-decision introspection that the cut mode produces from cut categories. Saunders's worked example shows him iterating in real time, each draft followed by a one-line reaction that surfaces what the previous version was missing.

e. Tag the categories the same way the cut mode does: write down what you preferred in arrangement (image-order, sentence shape, sound) and what you preferred in word choice (register, sensory specificity). The two lists are your editing basis surfacing through choice rather than through cut.

## Signal it landed

You can produce three artifacts after a session: a marked-up passage cut from its original length to roughly half; a written list of what you cut, with each cut tagged by category in your own words; and a written list of two or three things you protected, with reasons. You can name where the threshold landed for that piece — the round at which you stopped being able to cut without knowing the destination. Run on your own page, the cuts come faster than they did before the exercise, and the resulting page reads at a pace closer to the one you found acceptable on the practice text.

For the rank-among-versions mode, the artifacts are: a written best-to-worst ranking of three to five versions; a written answer to "on what basis did I rank"; a version of your own composed from the stipulated elements; and a written answer to "what was I honoring, on what was I relying, how did I decide." The basis you wrote down should be specific enough that, given a sixth version, you could predict where it would fall in the ranking before reading it carefully.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "What is my editing sensibility?" and "What is my natural pace?" Note your answers. They will likely be vague or empty.

After applying, ask the same questions. The skill landed if the first answer becomes a list of nameable offenses you cut for and protections you held against cutting, and the second answer is a felt setting you can recognize on a page rather than a number you can state.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without a forced-quota exercise, the writer revises only by diagnosis: each cut requires a reason articulated in advance, so cuts are slow and the writer never reaches the strictness setting that would reveal what slack the prose was carrying. Pace stays at first-draft length because the writer has no felt knowledge of what tighter would feel like. Voice stays generic because, as Saunders frames it, voice is made in revision through cutting, not in the first draft where most of us "sing for too long, in ways that actually sound pretty similar to all of those other writers singing." The two routes to a distinctive voice are putting it in or discovering it by erasure, and a writer who has never erased to a quota has only the first route, used without knowledge of how strict the result needs to be. The exercise produces the felt knowledge that makes both routes work.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders supplies a 600-word DMV passage about a man named Bill, full of explanatory asides, hedged phrasings, and stretches of paranoid interior monologue. The reader is to cut twenty words at a time, asking after each round what was cut, why, and whether the piece improved. He then demonstrates the rhythm-of-completion mechanism on a separate sentence. A loose draft sentence about a college student in a class taught by Professor Vader is cut to a much tighter form; the cut version is described as smarter and more considerate, the writer having sifted through the meandering original and brought forward what he found most vital. He demonstrates the same mechanism on a third sentence: "Sam was this big sort of dumb guy who I knew" cut to "Sam, big and dumb…" and then completed by what the partial sentence's rhythm asked for next. The completion arrives as "could, nevertheless, run." Now Sam is a runner, which the original draft did not yet know, and the new fact entered through the cut. The exercise's second product is named: cutting opens room for material the writer would not have written from a slack draft.
