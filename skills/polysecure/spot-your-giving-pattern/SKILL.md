## When to reach for this

You leave a relationship feeling drained without being able to say what specifically drained you. Or you finish a conversation aware you held back and don't know why. Reach for this whenever the felt sense is "something's off in the give-and-receive" but you can't name the specific shape.

## The move

1. Map the four patterns onto your behavior:
   - **Over-giving**: providing more than you can sustainably offer; saying yes to things you should decline; performing care to earn worth
   - **Under-giving**: withholding what you could offer; declining to share, support, or commit even when capacity is there
   - **Over-receiving / over-taking**: pulling more from others than is fair; expecting time, attention, or labor that exceeds what the relationship can sustain
   - **Under-receiving**: refusing what's offered; deflecting compliments, declining help, treating offers as obligations
2. Identify which pattern is most active for you in your most important relationships. Different relationships might surface different patterns — that's useful data.
3. Identify the belief underneath. "If I don't over-give, I won't be loved." "If I receive, I'll owe something." "If I take less, I'm not a burden." The belief is what makes the pattern feel necessary.
4. Test the belief. Find one moment to behave outside the pattern — give less, receive more, decline a request, ask for support — and notice whether the catastrophe you expected actually arrived.
5. Track over weeks. Patterns are stable; one experiment doesn't shift them. The work is repeated small moves against the gravity of the existing pattern.

## Signal it landed

You can name your dominant pattern in plain language. You can identify the belief underneath. You can describe specific situations where the pattern fires and what it costs you. You start catching the pattern in the moment instead of only noticing it in retrospect.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without naming the pattern, you treat each instance of imbalance as one-off (this particular partner is needy, this particular friend doesn't reciprocate). You miss the through-line: your behavior is shaped by a consistent pattern that produces the same kind of imbalance across many relationships.

## Reflection prompts

- How do you find yourself over-giving in relationships? What beliefs about yourself or others play into this?
- How do you find yourself under-giving? What beliefs play into this?
- In what ways do you over-receive or over-take? What beliefs play into this?
- In what ways do you under-receive? What beliefs play into this?
