# Stone and Heen, *Thanks for the Feedback* (parent context)

This file captures the framework, vocabulary, and lens Douglas Stone and Sheila Heen lay out in the front matter and introduction. Every skill under `skills/feedback/` inherits this framing.

## The authors' stated project

Building on their earlier *Difficult Conversations*, Stone and Heen take an honest look at why receiving feedback is hard and provide a framework for metabolizing challenging input. Their core observation: the field has spent decades training givers to push more skillfully and ignored the receiver. Since the receiver controls what they let in, how they make sense of it, and whether they change, the real leverage is on the receiving side.

## The lens (the central reframing)

The book is read entirely from the receiver's standpoint. The unit of analysis is what happens inside the person being given feedback, not what the giver should do differently. Receiving well does not mean always taking the feedback. It means engaging the conversation skillfully, managing emotional triggers, sorting what is useful from what is not, and choosing whether and how to use what was offered.

## Vocabulary (named concepts to inherit)

- **Push and pull**: training givers to push harder is "push." The leverage the book aims at is "pull," the receiver-side skill of driving one's own learning.
- **Three feedback triggers**, the book's central organizing frame. Each blocks feedback in a different way:
    - **Truth triggers**: the feedback is wrong, unfair, or unhelpful. Challenge: to *see*.
    - **Relationship triggers**: tripped by the giver's credibility or motives, or by how we feel treated. Challenge: the *we*.
    - **Identity triggers**: our self-story is destabilized, whether or not the feedback is right. Challenge: being *me*.
- **Three kinds of feedback**: appreciation, coaching, evaluation. Givers and receivers often want or hear different kinds, and the mismatch is its own source of failure.
- **Switchtrack conversation**: a feedback exchange that quietly splits into two topics, one about what was said and one about how or who said it, each party speaking past the other on their own track.
- **Wrong spotting**: the receiver's habit of scanning feedback for the part that is incorrect and using that to dismiss the whole.
- **Blind spots and the gap map**: the systematic ways our self-perception diverges from how others see us.
- **Relationship system**: feedback in a relationship is rarely about you or me; it is about the dynamic between us. Stepping back replaces blame with joint accountability.
- **Wiring (baseline, swing, sustain/recovery)**: built-in differences in how individuals metabolize feedback emotionally.
- **Distortions** and seeing feedback at "actual size."
- **Fixed vs growth identity** (drawing on Dweck), and the *second score*, how you handle the first score.
- **Boundaries**: three kinds, from "I may not take it" to "stop, or I will leave."

## Structure

Introduction (push to pull, the tension between learning and acceptance), then chapter 1 introducing the three triggers as the book's spine. Three parts follow, one per trigger: truth triggers (chapters 2 to 4), relationship triggers (chapters 5 and 6), identity triggers (chapters 7 to 9). A fourth part, Feedback in Conversation, covers boundaries (chapter 10), the conversation itself (chapter 11), action steps (chapter 12), and feedback in organizations (chapter 13).

## The implicit promise

Receiving feedback well is not an inborn trait but a skill that can be cultivated. The book promises a framework and concrete moves that let any reader, of any temperament, learn from feedback that is off base, unfair, poorly delivered, or simply unwelcome, without either swallowing it whole or shutting it out.
