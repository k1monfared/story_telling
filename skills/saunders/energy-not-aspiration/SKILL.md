## When to reach for this

You have been writing for a while in a mode you chose because of writers you wanted to resemble: a painstaking realist, a Hemingwayesque minimalist, a Nabokovian stylist, a deeply spiritual Robinson-style writer, whichever lineage you admired. The work is coming back to you, from friends, editors, your own honest re-reads, with reactions like "interesting," "a lot going on," "you really worked hard on this." You are not getting reactions like laughter, tears, hunger for more. Reach for this whenever the gap between the writer you intended to be and the responses your prose actually produces has become impossible to ignore.

## The move

1. Run the energy probe. Set aside the question of whether your prose is good in the abstract. Ask instead: when someone reads it, do they react with energy (laughter, alarm, eagerness for more, an immediate physical response) or with respect (patience, "interesting," "you worked hard")? Saunders's image: the wrist-mounted meter measures energy output during dancing; the goal is 1,000 units. Aspiring to dance the way you wanted is irrelevant if the meter shows 50.
2. Look for evidence of a different mode that does generate energy. Saunders's autobiographical case: he was writing minimal Hemingwayesque oilfield realism, eliciting "interesting" reactions, until on a boring conference call he scribbled some Seussian poems with cartoons and dropped them on the kitchen table; his wife laughed out loud reading them. That laugh was the meter going past 1,000 for the first time in years. The non-real-writing thing was the real-writing thing.
3. Catalog the off-license modes. The voices you have been keeping out of your real work because they are not what a serious writer of your aspired lineage sounds like. Humor at awkward moments. A working-class register. An overdriven corporate voice. A raunchy, attention-seeking impulse. A kind of structure your tradition does not approve of.
4. Try one. Write a story (not a fragment) entirely in one of the off-license modes. Pay attention to two specific signals: do you have stronger opinions while drafting (knowing immediately when a line is wrong, in the form of an impulse rather than a deliberation), and does the imaginary reader feel hungrier as the story proceeds rather than more patient.
5. Read the result. Saunders's diagnostic: there will be some "essential me-ness" in it that no one else could have written. The story will likely also be embarrassing, exposing taste you would rather not have admitted to (working-class, raunchy, attention-seeking, sentimental, whatever). The two go together.
6. Make the trade. Saunders's framing: you climbed Hemingway Mountain and could only ever be an acolyte at the top. You stumble back down and find a smaller hill labeled "Saunders Mountain" (your own name, your shit-hill). The hill is little and disappointing. It is also energetic. Decide to stand on it.
7. Commit. Saunders is direct about what makes the small hill grow: not waiting for it to become impressive before committing, but committing to it as the working ground. The hill grows because you stay on it; if you keep climbing back to Hemingway Mountain whenever your shit-hill embarrasses you, the hill never grows.
8. Continue applying the energy probe. Some of what you produce on the new hill will still be inert. The criterion is not "stay loyal to Saunders Mountain" but "stay loyal to whatever produces the energy." If a different mode shows up later that produces more energy still, follow it.

## Signal it landed

For your current writing project you can finish two sentences. "When I write in mode X, the meter reads ___." "When I write in mode Y, the meter reads ___." You can name which mode you are writing in, and you have stopped writing in modes whose meter reads low for the sake of being the writer you originally wanted to be. At least one piece of the new work has produced an energetic response (laughter, tears, eagerness, alarm) that your old mode was not producing.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "Am I writing in this voice because it generates energy, or because it is the voice I wanted to have?" Note your answer.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if the answer becomes a specific energy reading on a specific mode, and you can name a piece of recent work whose energy output told you which mode to keep.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, you stay loyal to the writer you imagined being and produce inert work indefinitely. The work is technically defensible, often "well-crafted," and dead on the page. Editors and friends, sensing this, give the polite reactions writers dread, "interesting," "ambitious," "clearly a lot of work in there," and you take these as evidence of difficulty rather than evidence of low energy. Saunders is blunt about why this is fatal: the writer can choose what to write about, but cannot choose what they are able to make live. A writer who refuses the voice that lives, in favor of the voice they admire, is choosing inanimacy. The shit-hill problem is real (the voice that works will likely be smaller, less prestigious, embarrassing) but the alternative, climbing Hemingway Mountain to be an acolyte, is a much harder fate to recover from because you can spend decades there before you notice the meter never moved.

## Worked micro-example

In his early thirties, Saunders sees himself as a Hemingwayesque realist working out of his time in the Asian oil fields. The stories he writes are minimal, strict, efficient, lifeless, humor-free. The reactions he gets are the polite ones: "interesting," "a lot going on." On a boring conference call at his environmental engineering job, he scribbles dark Seussian poems with cartoons. He almost throws them out as not-real-writing. He brings them home, drops them on the table, walks off; from the kitchen he hears his wife laughing genuinely as she reads them, the first time in years anyone has reacted to his writing with pleasure. The next day he writes a futuristic theme-park story in the same overdriven, slightly raunchy, working-class corporate voice. He finds that in this mode he has stronger, immediate opinions about what is wrong, in the form of impulses rather than deliberations. The story becomes "The Wavemaker Falters," the first piece of *CivilWarLand in Bad Decline*. The voice is embarrassing to him; the work it produces is the best he has ever done. He names the trade explicitly: he had climbed Hemingway Mountain and could only be an acolyte at the top. Coming back down he finds a smaller hill marked "Saunders Mountain," disappointing, "kind of a shit-hill," but with his own name on it. He decides to stand on it and commit, and the hill grows because he stayed.
