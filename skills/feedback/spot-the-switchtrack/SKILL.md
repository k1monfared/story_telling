## When to reach for this

A feedback conversation is going sideways. The heat is rising rather than dropping, you and the giver seem to be repeating yourselves, and you have the sense of being misheard at every turn. On the surface you are still using the same vocabulary (the gift, the timing, the report, the kid bath time), but the encounter feels not like one disagreement but like two people insisting past each other. Reach for this before the next round.

Also reach for this when you notice the after-the-fact version: you have just walked away from a feedback exchange and find yourself venting to a third party about the giver's character, motives, delivery, or fairness, while never quite addressing what the feedback itself said. That is silent switchtracking, and it confirms the split was real.

## The move

1. Pause and ask the diagnostic question: are we using the same words to talk about two different topics? The chapter calls the artifact a switchtrack, by analogy to a railroad switch: the conversation looks like one track but has quietly become two, and you and the giver are now heading in different directions on parallel tracks.
2. Locate the giver's topic. What was the original feedback, in their words? It is rarely the topic you have been arguing on. Try to state it in one sentence: the daughter's "you treat me like an untrustworthy child," the boss's "you missed the sales numbers," the wife's "you didn't do what you promised," the pedestrian's "you're in the crosswalk."
3. Locate your topic. What is the second topic that has hooked you and pulled the conversation sideways? The chapter catalogues six common families of relationship trigger that produce these second topics. Check each:
    - **Skill or judgment**: the how, when, or where of the delivery (in front of others, at the wrong time, with the wrong tone). The feeling is "you have no business raising this this way."
    - **Credibility**: the giver lacks the expertise, background, experience, or values to speak on this. The feeling is "you have no standing to say this."
    - **Trust / motives**: the giver's intentions are suspect (jealous, threatened, controlling, building a case, checking a box). The feeling is "I cannot take this from someone who is doing this for that reason."
    - **Appreciation**: your efforts and successes feel unseen; this feedback feels unbalanced or arrives in the place where a thank-you should have been. The feeling is "after all I have done, this is what you bring me?"
    - **Autonomy**: the giver is encroaching on a decision, behavior, or domain that is yours. The feeling is "you don't get to tell me what to do here."
    - **Acceptance**: the giver is asking you to be a different person, not adjust a behavior. The feeling is "you don't accept who I am."
4. Name your second topic in one sentence using one of those six handles. The wife who reacts to "your tone" with "I'm not the dog" has switchtracked from the broken bath-time promise (giver's topic) to autonomy and delivery (her topics). Louie has switchtracked from "I don't like red roses" (Kim's topic) to "you don't appreciate me" (his appreciation topic).
5. Confirm the split is real by checking if both topics could be true. The chapter is explicit that the second topic may be at least as important as the first, and that's part of the trap: the second topic is real, you are right that it deserves attention, AND you have stopped engaging with the first topic. Both being true is the diagnostic. (The next step, signposting, is its own skill.)

## Signal it landed

You can write two sentences, one labelled "their topic" and one labelled "my topic," in different words, on different subjects, both true, both deserving conversation. You are no longer unsure why the exchange is escalating. The split is now visible to you, even if it is not yet visible to the giver.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, ask: "If I had to summarize what we are arguing about in one sentence, would the giver agree that's the topic?" If the answer is "no, they'd say we're arguing about something else" or "I'm not sure, we keep talking past each other," you are in a switchtrack and have not yet seen it.

After applying, ask the same question. The skill landed if you can write the giver's topic in their words and your topic in your words, distinctly, and identify the moment the conversation forked.

## Failure mode this prevents

Without spotting the switchtrack, the conversation becomes the loop the chapter names: two people giving feedback, no one receiving it. Each utterance gets heard through the other person's topic-filter, so the daughter's "don't you trust me" becomes evidence of ingratitude when the mother is on the gratitude track, and the wife's "you did precisely none of the things you promised" becomes evidence of bad tone when the husband is on the autonomy track. Each person hears their own topic confirmed by everything the other says, and the actual feedback (in both directions) goes nowhere. The silent version is worse: the receiver never voices the second topic at all, smiles through the conversation, and then triangulates afterward to friends or colleagues, short-circuiting learning in every direction. The chapter's framing: switchtracking does not just slow feedback; it defeats it.

## Worked micro-example

The chapter opens with Louie and Kim. Kim presents red roses she has told Louie before that she does not like, and gives him simple feedback: I don't like red roses. Louie's reaction is not "tell me more about that" but "let me critique how you just told me that." On the surface they are both talking about red roses, but they are not. Kim's topic is that Louie does not listen to her (a credibility-of-effort, autonomy-of-her-stated-preferences, and acceptance trigger underneath, but on the surface a coaching message). Louie's topic is that Kim does not appreciate his effort (an appreciation trigger). Same words, different topics. The chapter's spotting practice continues with four additional vignettes, each modelling one of the six relationship-trigger types: the daughter and mother (appreciation), the boss and salesperson (skill/judgment of timing), the husband and wife at the recital (skill/judgment plus autonomy of tone), and the pedestrian and driver (autonomy of vehicle vs. autonomy of body in crosswalk). Each scene shows the same structural artifact: same surface words, two distinct topics, both real, both being missed.
