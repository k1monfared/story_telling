## When to reach for this

You are in a workshop, a reading group, a teaching session, or a revision pass on your own draft. The conversation has drifted to what something means: what a scene symbolizes, what a character represents, what theme the story is exploring. The talk feels deep but is not changing what anyone would do to the page. Reach for this whenever the discussion is interpretive and a writer in the room needs to act on it.

## The move

1. Notice when a question or comment is interpretive: it asks what something means, what it stands for, what theme it serves.
2. Re-pose the same observation as a strategic question. Replace "what does X mean" with "what is X doing for the reader" or "why do we need X." Replace "what does X symbolize" with "what would change if X were cut."
3. Direct the strategic question at a specific element on the page: a scene, a return, a repetition, a digression, a beat. Name the element by location, not by abstraction.
4. Answer the strategic question in terms of effect on the reader as the story unfolds in time: what the element sets up, pays off, delays, escalates, or earns.
5. If the strategic question has no good answer, that element is a candidate for cutting or rewriting. Mark it.

## Signal it landed

Each question on the table now points at a specific element of the text and asks what that element is doing for the reader. The discussion produces at least one concrete decision you could apply to a draft (cut this, expand this, move this, replace this), not just a richer interpretation.

## Probe before & after

Before applying, write down the current question being discussed. Ask: "If I had a perfect answer to this question, would I know what to change in the manuscript?"

After applying, ask the same question of your re-posed question. The skill landed if the answer flips from "no, I would just understand the story better" to "yes, I would know whether to keep, cut, or rework a specific passage."

## Failure mode this prevents

Without this move, critique converges on interpretation: the room produces ever more sophisticated readings of what the story means while the writer leaves with nothing to do on Monday. Symbolism talk is especially seductive because it sounds like analysis, but it does not tell you whether the second return to the village is earning its place, whether a digression is paying off, or which sentence is doing the work of the ending. The story gets understood and stays unrevised.

## Worked micro-example

Saunders contrasts two ways of teaching the same Russian story. The academic version says something like: "Resurrection, in this context, is a metaphor for political revolution, an ongoing concern in the Russian zeitgeist." The strategic version asks: "Why do we even need that second return to the village?" The first answer enriches reading. The second one is the question a writer or reviser can act on, because answering it forces you to articulate what the second return is structurally accomplishing, and whether it is.
